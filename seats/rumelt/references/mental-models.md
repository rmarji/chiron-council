# Mental Models — Richard Rumelt

Every named model or concept the Rumelt corpus uses, defined, with origin and when it applies.

## The kernel

**Origin:** GSBS, ch. 5, "The Kernel of Good Strategy."
**Definition:** The minimal logical structure every good strategy contains: (1) a **diagnosis** that defines or explains the nature of the challenge, simplifying complexity by identifying the critical aspects of the situation; (2) a **guiding policy** — the overall approach chosen to cope with or overcome the obstacles identified in the diagnosis; (3) **coherent actions** — coordinated steps and resource commitments that carry out the guiding policy.
**When it applies:** Any time someone claims to have a strategy. If any element is missing or vague, the artifact is a goal statement, a slogan, or a budget — not a strategy. Rumelt stresses the guiding policy is *not* a vision or a goal: it rules out a vast range of actions, which is its point.

## The crux

**Origin:** *The Crux* (2022), Part I; the term is borrowed from rock climbing, where the crux is the hardest section of a route.
**Definition:** The outcome of diagnosis: the one part of the tangle of challenges that is both critical and addressable — "the aspect that is both surmountable and promises the greatest progress" (The Crux, jacket/Part I; phrasing paraphrased). Solving the crux cascades into everything downstream; failing it makes other wins irrelevant.
**When it applies:** Overwhelm, competing initiatives, gnarly multi-part problems. The discipline is to resist working the easy or familiar parts and instead concentrate on the hardest solvable part. Rumelt's touchstone example is SpaceX treating low-cost, reusable boosters as the crux of cheap space access (The Crux, Part I).

## Addressable Strategic Challenge (ASC)

**Origin:** *The Crux* (2022).
**Definition:** A challenge scored on two axes: **importance** (degree to which it threatens core values or survival, or represents a major opportunity) and **addressability** (degree to which it appears solvable with feasible action). The ASC region — high on both — is where strategy should operate.
**When it applies:** Prioritization among candidate challenges in a strategy exercise; the collect–cluster–filter sequence of the Strategy Foundry ends by selecting the ASC and locating its crux.

## Challenge-based (vs. goal-based) strategy

**Origin:** *The Crux* (2022), building on GSBS chs. 3-5.
**Definition:** Treat strategy as problem-solving, not goal-setting or visioning. Ambition, mission, and willpower are inputs; strategy is the designed response to a diagnosed challenge. Goals worth having are *deduced from* the diagnosis, not imposed before it.
**When it applies:** Whenever a planning process starts from targets ("where do we want to be in five years?"). The corpus inverts the question to "what is making progress hard right now?"

## Gnarly problems

**Origin:** *The Crux* (2022), Part I.
**Definition:** Challenges with no given alternatives, unclear or conflicting goals, tangled cause-and-effect, and no way to compute an optimum. Rumelt's point is that real strategic situations are gnarly by default, which is why deduction-from-goals fails and iterative diagnosis plus design is required.
**When it applies:** When someone expects a framework to output the answer. Gnarly problems require re-framing, analogy, and the discipline of writing down the challenge.

## Leverage

**Origin:** GSBS, ch. 6, "Using Leverage."
**Definition:** "Finding an imbalance in a situation, and exploiting it to produce a disproportionately large payoff." Strategic leverage arises from three sources: **anticipation** (foreseeing others' behavior and pivotal events), identifying a **pivot point** (where effort is magnified by the structure of the situation), and **concentration** (massing resources past threshold effects rather than dribbling them out).
**When it applies:** Resource allocation. If effort is being spread evenly "to be fair," leverage is being destroyed.

## Proximate objectives

**Origin:** GSBS, ch. 7, "Proximate Objectives."
**Definition:** A target "close enough at hand to be feasible" — one the organization can reasonably be expected to hit from its current position and capability. A good proximate objective absorbs ambiguity from above and hands the levels below a solvable problem (Kennedy's moon commitment; JPL engineer Phyllis Buwalda's lunar-surface specification, GSBS ch. 7).
**When it applies:** Whenever goals are aspirational, distant, or demotivating. The more dynamic and uncertain the situation, the more proximate the objective should be.

## Chain-link systems

**Origin:** GSBS, ch. 8, "Chain-Link Systems."
**Definition:** Systems whose performance is limited by the weakest link. Improving any non-binding link yields nothing until the limiting link is fixed; quality levels must be coordinated across links. Two faces: chain-links **stuck** (turnarounds must fix one link at a time, absorbing losses without visible payoff until several links are repaired — GSBS ch. 8's disguised turnaround case) and chain-links **mastered** (an integrated system like IKEA's design-flat-pack-showroom-logistics loop becomes extremely hard to imitate because a rival must copy the whole chain, not one link — GSBS chs. 8-9).
**When it applies:** Diagnosing stuck performance and evaluating imitability of an advantage.

## Design view of strategy

**Origin:** GSBS, ch. 9, "Using Design"; also Rumelt's dissertation-era work on fit.
**Definition:** Strategy as configuration: fitting resources and actions together into a coherent whole, with Hannibal's double-envelopment at Cannae (216 BC) as the archetype of advantage created purely by coordinated design. Trade-off: fewer resources demand tighter, cleverer coordination; abundant resources tolerate looser designs.
**When it applies:** When someone treats strategy as a single decision or a portfolio of independent bets rather than a mutually reinforcing configuration.

## Isolating mechanisms

**Origin:** "Towards a Strategic Theory of the Firm" (1984, in Lamb, ed., *Competitive Strategic Management*); operationalized in GSBS ch. 12.
**Definition:** Firm-level barriers to imitation — causal ambiguity, reputation, buyer switching costs, patents and legal restraints, learning and network effects — that protect the rents a strategy generates, analogous to entry barriers at industry level.
**When it applies:** Testing whether an advantage is durable. "Interesting" advantages (GSBS ch. 12) are ones you can deepen, broaden, or defend via isolating mechanisms.

## Inertia and entropy

**Origin:** GSBS, ch. 14, "Inertia and Entropy."
**Definition:** The two great organizational drags. **Inertia** — "an organization's unwillingness or inability to adapt to changing circumstances" — comes in three forms: inertia of routine (stale standard procedures), cultural inertia (embedded beliefs and status structures), and inertia by proxy (a firm doesn't adapt because it profits from its *customers'* inertia). **Entropy** is the drift toward disorder absent active maintenance — blurred product lines and creeping unfocus, as in GM's overlapping brand price ladders (GSBS ch. 14).
**When it applies:** Diagnosing incumbents (including yourself) and hunting rivals' weaknesses — a competitor's inertia is an exploitable asymmetry.

## Attractor states and waves of change

**Origin:** GSBS, ch. 13, "Using Dynamics."
**Definition:** An attractor state is where an industry "should" end up given demand and technology forces; waves of change (rising fixed costs, deregulation, predictable biases in forecasting, incumbent responses, attractor states) are exogenous shifts that redistribute advantage. Cisco riding the IP-networking attractor state is the corpus's exemplar.
**When it applies:** Timing entry and attack; a structural break ("Strategy in a structural break," McKinsey Quarterly 2008) is the extreme case where old formulas become liabilities.

## The panel of experts / create-destroy discipline

**Origin:** GSBS, ch. 17, "Using Your Head."
**Definition:** Because the first strategy that comes to mind is usually the herd's, Rumelt advises simulating a virtual panel of respected strategists and using them to critique and destroy your initial idea, forcing generation of alternatives. Judgment must be pre-committed in writing to be improvable.
**When it applies:** Any strategy review where consensus arrived too quickly.
