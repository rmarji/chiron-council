# Quotes — Richard Rumelt

Short, attributed excerpts only. Wording checked against the published texts and reputable transcriptions; entries whose exact wording could not be fully verified are explicitly marked **[paraphrase]**. Use sparingly and always with attribution.

## From Good Strategy Bad Strategy (2011)

- "The kernel of a strategy contains three elements: a diagnosis, a guiding policy, and coherent action." — GSBS, ch. 5.
- "Good strategy is coherent action backed up by an argument, an effective mixture of thought and action with a basic underlying structure I call the kernel." — GSBS, ch. 1.
- "The most basic idea of strategy is the application of strength against weakness." — GSBS, ch. 1.
- "Good strategy works by focusing energy and resources on one, or a very few, pivotal objectives whose accomplishment will lead to a cascade of favorable outcomes." — GSBS, ch. 1.
- "A good strategy honestly acknowledges the challenges being faced and provides an approach to overcoming them." — GSBS, ch. 3.
- "If you fail to identify and analyze the obstacles, you don't have a strategy." — GSBS, ch. 3.
- "Good strategy is design, and design is about fitting various pieces together so they work as a coherent whole." — GSBS, ch. 9.
- "A master strategist is a designer." — GSBS, ch. 9.
- "Strategy is designing a way to deal with a challenge." — GSBS, ch. 9 (wording as transcribed in published reader notes of the chapter).
- **[paraphrase]** The guiding policy directs and constrains action without fully defining it, the way guardrails direct a car without steering it. — GSBS, ch. 5.
- On Steve Jobs' plan for post-1997 Apple, as Rumelt recounts asking him what the longer-term strategy was, Jobs replied: "I am going to wait for the next big thing." — GSBS, ch. 1.

## From the McKinsey Quarterly essays

- "Bad strategy is not simply the absence of good strategy. It grows out of specific misconceptions and leadership dysfunctions." — "The perils of bad strategy," McKinsey Quarterly (2011).
- "Rare is the time when the strategic environment for a great many companies and industries undergoes a rapid, fundamental, and universally perceptible change." — "Strategy in a structural break," McKinsey Quarterly (December 2008).

## From The Crux (2022)

- **[paraphrase]** The crux is the part of the challenge that is both surmountable and, once solved, promises the greatest progress — a term Rumelt borrows from climbing, where the crux is the hardest pitch of a route. — The Crux, Part I.
- **[paraphrase]** Goals, ambitions, visions, missions, and values are not strategy; strategy is what you do about the challenge. — The Crux, Part I; consistent with GSBS ch. 3.
- **[paraphrase]** An Addressable Strategic Challenge scores high on both importance (threat to core values or existence, or major opportunity) and addressability (solvable with feasible action). — The Crux.

## From lectures and interviews

- **[paraphrase]** A strategy is a coherent mix of policy and action designed to surmount a high-stakes challenge. — Good Strategy/Bad Strategy public lecture, London School of Economics (2011); the same formulation appears in GSBS.
- **[paraphrase]** Strategy work is episodic, not an annual planning ritual; most "strategic plans" are just three-year rolling budgets. — "Strategy's strategist: An interview with Richard Rumelt," McKinsey Quarterly (2007).

## Usage notes

- Quote no more than a sentence or two at a time; the depth of the corpus lives in the paraphrased and cited reference files, not in block quotation.
- Where a claim matters to a live decision, cite book + chapter (or article + year) alongside the quote so it can be checked.
- Anything not in quotation marks in this seat is paraphrase and must be presented as analysis of the corpus, never as Rumelt's own words.
