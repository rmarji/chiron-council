# Frameworks — Richard Rumelt

Every named framework in the corpus, with when-to-use, concrete steps, and source.

## 1. Build the Kernel

**Source:** GSBS, ch. 5, "The Kernel of Good Strategy."
**When to use:** A stated "strategy," a plan that is really a list of goals, or a direction-setting exercise starting from scratch.
**Steps:**
1. **Diagnosis.** Name the real challenge in one or two sentences. What is actually going on? Prefer a diagnosis that simplifies — a frame, analogy, or classification that turns mess into a tractable problem. Test: does the diagnosis identify the obstacle, not just restate the ambition?
2. **Guiding policy.** State the overall approach that deals with the diagnosed challenge. It should rule things out — a guiding policy compatible with every possible action is a slogan.
3. **Coherent actions.** List the specific, coordinated, resource-committing moves that execute the policy. Test fit: do the actions reinforce each other and follow from the policy, or are they a wish list wearing the policy as a hat?
4. **Kill test.** If any of the three is missing or vague, the strategy is not finished. Rumelt's DEC example (GSBS ch. 5) shows the failure mode: a committee compromise ("high-quality products and services... a leader in data processing") that no diagnosis produced and no action follows from.

## 2. Find the Crux (challenge-based strategy sequence)

**Source:** *The Crux* (2022), Part I; anticipated by GSBS ch. 7.
**When to use:** Overwhelm, too many initiatives, resources spread thin, or a gnarly problem with no obvious entry point.
**Steps:**
1. **Collect the challenges.** Write down every challenge and opportunity facing the enterprise — in words, as problems, not as targets.
2. **Cluster and filter.** Group related challenges; discard duplicates and symptoms.
3. **Score each cluster** on **importance** (threat to survival/core value, or size of opportunity) and **addressability** (can feasible action actually move it?). The winners are Addressable Strategic Challenges.
4. **Locate the crux** — the hardest part of the chosen challenge that is still solvable, whose solution unlocks the rest (climbing metaphor: the crux pitch).
5. **Design the response and concentrate force there** before anything else. Do not announce goals until this is done; goals fall out of the diagnosis.

## 3. Bad Strategy Detector (the four hallmarks)

**Source:** GSBS, ch. 3, "Bad Strategy"; "The perils of bad strategy," McKinsey Quarterly (2011).
**When to use:** Reviewing a strategy doc, OKR set, pitch, board deck, or any stated direction.
**Steps — scan for the four hallmarks:**
1. **Fluff.** Gibberish masquerading as strategic concepts; restate each sentence in plain words — if nothing remains, it was fluff (Rumelt's example: a bank's "customer-centric intermediation").
2. **Failure to face the challenge.** No definition of the obstacle to be overcome. International Harvester's 1979 plan is the corpus's cautionary case: pages of projections that never mentioned its crippling work-rule and labor problems (GSBS ch. 3).
3. **Mistaking goals for strategy.** Statements of desire ("20/20": 20% growth, 20% margin — the Chad Logan case) with no plan for overcoming obstacles.
4. **Bad strategic objectives.** Objectives that are impracticable ("blue sky") or a "dog's dinner" — a long, uncoordinated scramble of things-to-do relabeled as strategy.
Each hallmark found marks a place the strategy needs real work; also check for the *causes* (unwillingness to choose, template planning, New Thought) per GSBS ch. 4.

## 4. Proximate Objectives Cascade

**Source:** GSBS, ch. 7, "Proximate Objectives."
**When to use:** Goals feel inspiring but nothing moves; teams below strategy level are handed ambiguity instead of problems.
**Steps:**
1. Take the ambition and ask: what is the nearest objective this organization could actually attain from where it stands?
2. Choose that proximate objective so it **resolves ambiguity** for the next level down — as Buwalda's lunar-surface spec did for lander engineers (GSBS ch. 7).
3. Let each level's solved problem generate the next proximate objective. The more uncertain the environment, the more proximate the objective must be.

## 5. Chain-Link Diagnostic and Repair

**Source:** GSBS, ch. 8, "Chain-Link Systems."
**When to use:** Performance stuck despite functional improvement efforts; or assessing whether your advantage can be imitated.
**Steps:**
1. Ask whether performance is limited by the weakest link (quality matching across units). If yes, incremental improvement of strong links is waste.
2. Identify the binding link and concentrate repair there, unit by unit, accepting that measurable payoff arrives only after several links are fixed — which is why chain-link turnarounds need a leader with personal conviction and slack resources (GSBS ch. 8).
3. On offense: build excellence into *linked* activities (IKEA's design-manufacture-logistics-showroom chain) so rivals must replicate the whole system to compete (GSBS chs. 8-9).

## 6. Strategy Foundry

**Source:** *The Crux* (2022), final part.
**When to use:** A leadership team needs an actual strategy and would otherwise run a template-driven offsite or delegate to planners.
**Steps (Rumelt's rules of engagement, paraphrased):**
1. Small senior group (roughly a half-dozen to nine deciders), multi-day, facilitated; no mission-statement preamble, no financial-projection theater.
2. Participants write challenges down independently before discussion; written diagnoses are compared and interrogated.
3. Cluster, filter, and score challenges (importance × addressability); select the ASC and identify its crux.
4. Design coherent actions with named owners and resource commitments; only then translate into numbers.
5. Ban "instant strategy" — no solutions accepted before the diagnosis survives scrutiny.

## 7. Hypothesis-Test-Revise (the scientific method applied to strategy)

**Source:** GSBS, ch. 16, "The Science of Strategy."
**When to use:** Committing resources under genuine uncertainty; evaluating a strategy that "can't be proven in advance."
**Steps:**
1. Treat the strategy as a hypothesis about what will work, grounded in the diagnosis.
2. Design actions that generate information as well as results.
3. Revise as evidence arrives — Schultz's Il Giornale shed its Italian-purist features (opera, bow ties, no chairs) as real customer data came in, becoming Starbucks (GSBS ch. 16).

## 8. Create-Destroy (panel of virtual experts)

**Source:** GSBS, ch. 17, "Using Your Head."
**When to use:** The first strategy proposed was quickly agreed to; you suspect herd judgment.
**Steps:**
1. Write down your current judgment and its rationale (commitment makes it correctable).
2. Convene a mental panel of respected strategists you know well enough to simulate; have each attack the idea.
3. Use the criticisms to generate at least one genuinely different alternative, then compare.
