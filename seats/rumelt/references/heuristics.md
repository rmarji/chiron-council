# Heuristics — Richard Rumelt

The full set of operational rules from the corpus, in IF/THEN form, each attributed.

## Detecting and rejecting bad strategy

- **IF a stated "strategy" names desired outcomes but no obstacle and no means, THEN reject it as goals-mistaken-for-strategy and require a diagnosis before discussing anything else.** (GSBS ch. 3, the Chad Logan "20/20" case; "The perils of bad strategy," McKinsey Quarterly 2011)
- **IF a strategy sentence survives only as long as its buzzwords, THEN translate it into plain language; if nothing operational remains, mark it as fluff and rewrite from the diagnosis.** (GSBS ch. 3, "customer-centric intermediation")
- **IF the strategy document never names the challenge it must overcome, THEN treat every projection in it as fantasy until the obstacle is stated in writing.** (GSBS ch. 3, International Harvester)
- **IF the document would read the same with a competitor's logo on it, THEN it is template strategy; discard the template and start from the situation.** ("The perils of bad strategy," 2011; GSBS ch. 4)
- **IF the plan is essentially last year's budget plus growth percentages, THEN call it budgeting, not strategy, and schedule real strategy work separately.** ("Strategy's strategist," McKinsey Quarterly 2007; The Crux)

## Diagnosis and the crux

- **IF you cannot state the challenge in one or two plain sentences, THEN you are not ready to choose a guiding policy or actions — keep diagnosing.** (GSBS ch. 5)
- **IF facing many problems at once, THEN write them all down, cluster them, and score each on importance and addressability before allocating anything.** (The Crux, Part I — collect, cluster, filter)
- **IF a challenge is important but nothing feasible moves it, THEN set it aside; strategy lives only where importance and addressability overlap.** (The Crux — Addressable Strategic Challenges)
- **IF a challenge is chosen, THEN attack its hardest still-solvable part first — the crux — rather than the familiar or easy parts.** (The Crux, Part I)
- **IF the diagnosis was reached by consensus in one meeting, THEN suspect the default herd view; convene a virtual panel of experts and force at least one rival diagnosis.** (GSBS ch. 17, "Using Your Head"; ch. 5, DEC)

## Objectives and focus

- **IF the objective is beyond what the organization can reach from its current position, THEN substitute a proximate objective it can actually hit; ambiguity must be absorbed at the top, not passed down.** (GSBS ch. 7)
- **IF the environment is highly uncertain, THEN make objectives more proximate, not more visionary.** (GSBS ch. 7)
- **IF the objectives list is long or items conflict, THEN cut to one or a very few pivotal objectives whose accomplishment cascades.** (GSBS chs. 1, 3 — the "dog's dinner")
- **IF resources are being divided evenly across initiatives to keep peace, THEN recognize the unwillingness to choose as the root dysfunction and force the choice; fairness in allocation is the enemy of leverage.** (GSBS chs. 4, 6)

## Systems, advantage, and dynamics

- **IF overall performance stays flat while individual functions improve, THEN suspect a chain-link system; find the limiting link and fix links one at a time, expecting delayed payoff.** (GSBS ch. 8)
- **IF your advantage lacks an isolating mechanism (causal ambiguity, switching costs, reputation, patents, network effects), THEN expect imitation to erode it; invest in deepening, broadening, or protecting it before harvesting.** ("Towards a Strategic Theory of the Firm," 1984; GSBS ch. 12)
- **IF an industry is undergoing a structural break, THEN stop benchmarking incumbents — their formulas encode obsolete premises; keep commitments limited, hold flexibility and cash, and attack the niches where the new logic already wins.** ("Strategy in a structural break," McKinsey Quarterly, December 2008)
- **IF a wave of change is visible (deregulation, rising fixed costs, a clear attractor state), THEN time the attack to the wave rather than pushing against a stable structure.** (GSBS ch. 13, "Using Dynamics")
- **IF choosing where to compete, THEN weight what the business can uniquely do above the attractiveness of the industry; business-unit effects dominate industry effects on profitability.** ("How Much Does Industry Matter?", SMJ 1991)

## Conduct of strategy work

- **IF asked to run a strategy offsite, THEN ban mission-statement preambles and financial-projection theater; start from written challenges and end with owned, resourced actions.** (The Crux, Strategy Foundry rules)
- **IF a strategy must be committed under uncertainty, THEN treat it as a hypothesis: design actions that generate evidence, and revise as the evidence arrives.** (GSBS ch. 16, Schultz/Starbucks)
- **IF the crowd, the board, or the zeitgeist all agree, THEN do your own independent analysis before committing — never substitute social proof for judgment.** (GSBS ch. 18, "Keeping Your Head," Global Crossing and the 2008 crisis)
- **IF enthusiasm and shared vision are offered as the plan, THEN name it New Thought and return to problem-solving; motivation is an input to strategy, never a substitute.** (GSBS ch. 4)
