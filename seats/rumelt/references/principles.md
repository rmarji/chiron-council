# Core Principles — Richard Rumelt

The full catalog of core beliefs in the Rumelt corpus, each with rationale and source. Primary texts: *Good Strategy Bad Strategy* (GSBS, 2011) and *The Crux* (2022); supporting material from his McKinsey Quarterly essays and academic work.

## 1. Strategy is a coherent response to a high-stakes challenge — not a goal, vision, or target

A target ("grow revenue 20%," "be the market leader") is an aspiration, not a strategy. Rumelt defines good strategy as "coherent action backed up by an argument" — a designed response to a specific, identified challenge (GSBS, ch. 1). The Chad Logan case makes this concrete: a graphic-arts CEO presented Rumelt his "20/20 plan" — 20% revenue growth, 20% margin — and Rumelt's verdict was that a financial target with no account of the obstacle or the means is not a strategy at all (GSBS, ch. 3). *The Crux* (2022) sharpens the same principle into "challenge-based strategy": strategy begins with the challenge, and goals emerge from the diagnosis rather than preceding it (The Crux, Part I). In his 2011 LSE lecture he compressed the definition to a coherent mix of policy and action designed to surmount a high-stakes challenge.

## 2. Every good strategy has a kernel: diagnosis, guiding policy, coherent action

The kernel is the corpus's load-bearing structure (GSBS, ch. 5). A **diagnosis** names what is really going on, simplifying messy reality into a tractable problem. A **guiding policy** is the overall approach to the diagnosed challenge — like guardrails on a highway, it directs and constrains action without fully specifying it. **Coherent actions** are coordinated, resource-committing moves that carry out the policy. Miss any one and you do not yet have a strategy: without diagnosis you have wishes, without policy you have a to-do list, without actions you have a slogan.

## 3. Diagnosis carries most of the value

"A great deal of strategy work is trying to figure out what is going on" (GSBS, ch. 5). Rumelt argues the diagnosis is where insight lives, often achieved through metaphor, analogy, or reframing — his example of a well-chosen diagnosis transforming options is Digital Equipment's 1988 "boxes vs. solutions vs. chips" debate, where each candidate diagnosis implied a completely different strategy (GSBS, ch. 5). *The Crux* extends this: diagnosis of gnarly challenges is iterative — collect, cluster, and filter the challenges, then locate the crux (The Crux, Part I). Skipping diagnosis is the most common and most expensive strategic error.

## 4. Power comes from concentration and coherence

"Good strategy works by focusing energy and resources on one, or a very few, pivotal objectives whose accomplishment will lead to a cascade of favorable outcomes" (GSBS, ch. 1). Actions must reinforce one another, not merely coexist; conflicting objectives and dispersed resources cancel out (GSBS, chs. 6, 9). The first natural advantage of good strategy, Rumelt notes, is that it is unexpected — because most rivals will not concentrate, the concentrator wins asymmetric returns (GSBS, ch. 1, citing Apple 1997 and Desert Storm 1991).

## 5. Strategy is design, not decision-making or forecasting

"A master strategist is a designer" (GSBS, ch. 9). Strategy fits pieces together so they work as a coherent whole — Rumelt's exemplar is Hannibal at Cannae, where the power lay entirely in the coordinated design of the parts, not in any single resource (GSBS, ch. 9). Design implies trade-offs: the tighter the integration and the fewer the resources, the stronger — and more fragile — the design.

## 6. The most basic idea of strategy is applying strength against weakness

Strength applied at the decisive point — or, equivalently, to the most promising opportunity (GSBS, ch. 1). This is why diagnosis and crux-finding matter: leverage exists only where an asymmetry can be exploited (GSBS, ch. 6).

## 7. Firm-specific advantage dominates industry selection

In "How Much Does Industry Matter?" (Strategic Management Journal, 1991), Rumelt decomposed the variance in business-unit profitability and found stable business-unit effects explained several times more variance than stable industry effects (roughly 46% vs. 8%), with corporate-parent effects near zero — directly contradicting Schmalensee's 1985 industry-dominant results. The strategic implication threaded through the corpus: what a business does and knows matters far more than which industry it sits in.

## 8. Sustained advantage requires isolating mechanisms

Advantage that cannot be protected is rented, not owned. In "Towards a Strategic Theory of the Firm" (1984, in Lamb, ed., *Competitive Strategic Management*), Rumelt introduced **isolating mechanisms** — firm-level analogs of entry barriers, such as causal ambiguity, reputation, switching costs, and patents — that impede imitation and let a firm keep the rents its strategy creates. GSBS ch. 12 ("Using Advantage") operationalizes this: an advantage must be deepened, broadened, or protected to remain "interesting."

## 9. Bad strategy is not the absence of good strategy — it has specific causes

"Bad strategy is not simply the absence of good strategy. It grows out of specific misconceptions and leadership dysfunctions" ("The perils of bad strategy," McKinsey Quarterly, 2011). The causes: the unwillingness or inability to choose among conflicting values and parties; template-style planning that fills in vision/mission/values boxes; and New Thought — the belief that a positive mental attitude and shared vision substitute for problem-solving (GSBS, ch. 4).

## 10. Objectives should be proximate

Leaders should hand the organization "an objective that is close enough at hand to be feasible" (GSBS, ch. 7). Kennedy's moon mission, Rumelt argues, was proximate — the Surveyor program and existing technology made it a hard but reachable engineering problem — and JPL's Phyllis Buwalda resolving the unknown lunar surface into a concrete design spec shows how a good proximate objective absorbs ambiguity for the levels below (GSBS, ch. 7).

## 11. Strategy formation is hypothesis-driven

A strategy is a hypothesis about what will work, to be tested and revised like a scientific conjecture — Rumelt's example is Howard Schultz iterating the Il Giornale espresso-bar format toward what became Starbucks, abandoning the pure Italian template as evidence came in (GSBS, ch. 16, "The Science of Strategy").

## 12. Change and structural breaks are the strategist's opportunity

Times of upheaval move value between business models. "Rare is the time when the strategic environment for a great many companies and industries undergoes a rapid, fundamental, and universally perceptible change" — and when it does, incumbent formulas encode obsolete premises, and the right posture is flexibility, cash, and selective attack rather than imitation of yesterday's winners ("Strategy in a structural break," McKinsey Quarterly, December 2008; GSBS, ch. 13, "Using Dynamics").
