# Anti-Patterns — Richard Rumelt

Everything the corpus warns against, with the reasoning and the failure each produces. Primary sources: GSBS chs. 3-4 and "The perils of bad strategy" (McKinsey Quarterly, 2011), where Rumelt names the four hallmarks of bad strategy and its three root causes.

## Goals dressed as strategy

**What it is:** Listing desired outcomes — "be number one," "grow 20% a year" — with no account of the obstacle or the means. Rumelt's canonical case is Chad Logan's "20/20 plan" (20% revenue growth, 20% profit margin), which Rumelt refused to bless as strategy because it contained only ambition (GSBS, ch. 3).
**Why it fails:** A goal is a restatement of desire. It gives no guidance on where to concentrate, imposes no coherence on action, and — when set without reference to capability — demands the impossible and demoralizes ("The perils of bad strategy," 2011).
**The counter:** Force the kernel. What challenge stands between here and the goal? What approach deals with it? What coordinated actions follow?

## Fluff

**What it is:** "A form of gibberish masquerading as strategic concepts" — abstraction, buzzwords, and restated truisms inflated to sound like insight. Rumelt's example: a major bank's core strategy of "customer-centric intermediation," which decodes to "we are a bank" (GSBS, ch. 3; McKinsey Quarterly, 2011).
**Why it fails:** Fluff hides the absence of thought, from the audience and from the authors. An organization steering by fluff has made no choices, so it disperses effort by default.
**The counter:** Translation test — restate every sentence in plain words. If nothing operational remains, it was fluff.

## Failure to face the challenge

**What it is:** A strategy that never defines the obstacle it must overcome. International Harvester's 1979 corporate plan is the corpus's exhibit: thick with financial projections and market-share aspirations, silent on the company's ruinous labor relations and work rules — the actual problem. The company was broken up within a few years (GSBS, ch. 3).
**Why it fails:** "If you fail to identify and analyze the obstacles, you don't have a strategy" (GSBS). You cannot design a response to a challenge you refuse to name; avoidance usually signals the challenge is politically painful.
**The counter:** Diagnosis first, in writing, before any goals or actions are discussed (GSBS ch. 5; The Crux).

## Bad strategic objectives — the "dog's dinner" and the "blue sky"

**What it is:** Two failure shapes (GSBS, ch. 3): the **dog's dinner** — a long, uncoordinated scramble of objectives (Rumelt describes a city planning committee whose "strategy" was 47 strategies and 178 action items) — and the **blue-sky objective** — a restatement of the desired end-state that is simply impracticable from where the organization stands, skipping over the crux.
**Why it fails:** Long lists disperse force; impracticable objectives hand subordinates ambiguity instead of a solvable problem. Both substitute volume or altitude for design.
**The counter:** Few, proximate, mutually reinforcing objectives (GSBS ch. 7).

## Template-style strategy

**What it is:** Filling in the vision / mission / values / strategies boxes of a planning template as a ritual, producing a document with no diagnosis and no real choices. Rumelt traces the template's spread through leadership and visioning fashions ("The perils of bad strategy," 2011; GSBS ch. 4).
**Why it fails:** The form replaces the thinking. Template output is interchangeable across companies — the surest tell that no situation-specific analysis occurred.
**The counter:** Start from the challenge, not the form. A strategy exercise that begins with the mission statement is already off the rails (The Crux, Strategy Foundry rules).

## New Thought: positive thinking as strategy

**What it is:** The belief that a shared vision, self-belief, and a positive mental attitude cause success — a lineage Rumelt traces from 19th-century New Thought through Norman Vincent Peale to modern vision-led leadership programs (GSBS, ch. 4).
**Why it fails:** Motivation is an input, not a design. When belief substitutes for analysis, the organization stops examining obstacles at all — the direct cause of "failure to face the challenge."
**The counter:** Respect the difficulty. Strategy is problem-solving under constraint; enthusiasm without diagnosis is exposure.

## The unwillingness or inability to choose

**What it is:** The deepest root cause: strategy requires saying no to powerful people and pet projects, so leaders avoid it by adopting umbrella statements that offend no one (GSBS ch. 4; "The perils of bad strategy," 2011 — the DEC boxes/solutions/chips compromise is the worked example).
**Why it fails:** Universal buy-in and strategic focus are mutually exclusive. A "strategy" all constituencies endorse has chosen nothing, and resources stay smeared across legacy commitments.
**The counter:** Treat choice as the test of leadership; a guiding policy earns its name by what it rules out (GSBS ch. 5).

## "Strategic planning" as budgeting

**What it is:** Annual planning cycles that produce rolling three-to-five-year budgets with market-share glide paths, relabeled "strategic plans" ("Strategy's strategist," McKinsey Quarterly, 2007; The Crux).
**Why it fails:** Real strategy work is episodic — triggered by a challenge or a shift — not calendrical. The annual ritual guarantees template output and mistakes resource spreadsheets for designed action.
**The counter:** Separate budgeting from strategy work; convene actual strategy exercises (e.g., a foundry) when the situation demands one.

## Mistaking operational effectiveness for strategy

**What it is:** Answering a strategic question with execution intensity — work harder, hit the numbers, improve everything a little.
**Why it fails:** In chain-link systems, undirected improvement of non-binding links yields nothing (GSBS ch. 8); and effort without a pivot point has no leverage (GSBS ch. 6). The corpus treats "execution problems" as frequently mis-diagnosed design problems.
**The counter:** Diagnose first; direct intensity at the crux or the limiting link.
