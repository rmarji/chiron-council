# Frameworks — Naval Ravikant

Every operational framework in the corpus, with when-to-use, concrete steps, and source.

## Leverage Audit

*Source:* "How to Get Rich" tweetstorm (May 2018); How to Get Rich podcast episodes "Fortunes Require Leverage," "Labor and Capital Are Old Leverage," "Product and Media Are New Leverage" (2019).
*When to use:* The user trades time for money, or wants output that outlives their hours.
*Steps:*
1. Inventory how the work currently creates value across the four channels: labor, capital, code, media.
2. Mark each channel permissioned (labor, capital — someone must say yes) or permissionless (code, media — nobody can stop you).
3. Shift weight toward the permissionless channels: software, writing, video, audio — "products with no marginal cost of replication" (tweetstorm, 2018).
4. Attach the user's specific knowledge to the leveraged channel so the output is differentiated, not commodity.
5. Verify the end state: an asset that produces value with zero marginal hours — "assets that earn while you sleep" (tweetstorm, 2018).

## Specific Knowledge Finder

*Source:* tweetstorm (2018); "Arm Yourself With Specific Knowledge" and adjacent episodes, How to Get Rich podcast (2019); Almanack, "Building Wealth."
*When to use:* The user is deciding what to work on, or what their real edge is.
*Steps:*
1. Ignore the prescribed path and the currently-hot market. "Specific knowledge is found by pursuing your genuine curiosity and passion rather than whatever is hot right now" (tweetstorm, 2018, near-verbatim).
2. Look for the tell: work that "will feel like play to you but will look like work to others" (tweetstorm, 2018).
3. Check trainability: if a school can teach it, society can train a cheaper replacement. Favor what is learned by doing and apprenticeship.
4. Check the intersection: uniquely good at it AND hard to outsource or automate AND some market pulls on it.
5. Productize and personalize: attach the knowledge to a product or brand that is unmistakably the user's — "no one can compete with you on being you" (Almanack).

## Build Your Luck Surface

*Source:* "Making Money Isn't About Luck," How to Get Rich podcast (2019), building on James Austin, Chase, Chance, and Creativity (1978); "luck surface area" after Jason Roberts.
*When to use:* The user is grinding in private, or waiting for opportunity to arrive.
*Steps:*
1. Classify current luck exposure against the four kinds: blind chance, hustle, awareness, character.
2. Keep hustle and awareness (they're cheap), but invest deliberately in the fourth kind: become publicly known for one specific, verifiable strength.
3. Ship and publish: do the work AND tell the story. Luck surface area = doing x telling.
4. Let inbound compound: the fourth kind of luck inverts the funnel — opportunity starts finding the user, and it arrives pre-filtered by reputation.

## Long-Term Games Filter

*Source:* tweetstorm (2018); "Play Long-Term Games With Long-Term People," How to Get Rich podcast (2019).
*When to use:* Choosing partners, industries, deals — or judging a tempting extractive move.
*Steps:*
1. Ask: is this an iterated game? "Pick an industry where you can play long term games with long term people" (tweetstorm, 2018).
2. Screen counterparties for intelligence, energy, and above all integrity — where integrity is what they do, not what they say (tweetstorm, 2018; podcast, 2019).
3. Apply the cynic exclusion: "Don't partner with cynics and pessimists. Their beliefs are self-fulfilling" (tweetstorm, 2018). Partner with rational optimists.
4. Reject any move that wins one round by burning trust — compounding requires being welcome at the table next round.
5. Once a long-term person or game is validated, concentrate: since "99% of effort is wasted" (Almanack), go deep on the compounding 1% rather than diversifying across the non-compounding 99%.

## Aspirational Hourly Rate

*Source:* "Set and enforce an aspirational personal hourly rate" (tweetstorm, 2018); "Set an Aspirational Hourly Rate," How to Get Rich podcast (2019); Almanack, "Building Wealth."
*When to use:* The user is drowning in low-value tasks, over-attending meetings, or hand-doing outsourceable work.
*Steps:*
1. Set an hourly rate that feels absurd — priced off the future self being built, not the current market. (Naval reports using $5,000/hr before he could justify it; podcast, 2019.)
2. Enforce it ruthlessly: any task cheaper to outsource, automate, or skip than the rate — outsource, automate, or skip.
3. Apply it to social overhead too: "You should be too busy to 'do coffee,' while still keeping an uncluttered calendar" (tweetstorm, 2018) — dense on priorities, empty of obligation.
4. Re-price upward as leverage grows.

## Decision Procedure (judgment under abundance)

*Source:* Almanack, "Building Judgment"; Knowledge Project #18 (2017).
*When to use:* Any non-trivial decision, especially under option overload.
*Steps:*
1. Classify reversibility. Reversible: decide fast, iterate. Irreversible: slow down, gather.
2. Apply the default-no: "If you cannot decide, the answer is no" (Almanack, near-verbatim). Modern life offers too many options for maybes.
3. For hard, evenly-split calls: "If you're evenly split on a difficult decision, take the path more painful in the short term" (Almanack) — the brain systematically overweights near-term pain.
4. Cap ruin first (Kelly criterion; podcast, 2019): no bet that can end the game, whatever the upside.
5. Decide from a calm state; the corpus treats a rested, unhurried mind as a precondition for judgment, not a luxury (Almanack, "Building Judgment").

## Productize Yourself

*Source:* "Productize Yourself," How to Get Rich podcast (2019) — the series' own summary of the entire wealth thread.
*When to use:* Career strategy at any level; the master frame the other frameworks feed.
*Steps:*
1. "Yourself" — run the Specific Knowledge Finder; ground the offering in authenticity so competition is escaped rather than fought.
2. "Productize" — run the Leverage Audit; wrap the knowledge in code or media so it replicates at zero marginal cost.
3. Take accountability publicly: ship under your own name so the market can assign you equity-level rewards (tweetstorm, 2018).
4. Iterate toward: "Apply specific knowledge, with leverage, and eventually you will get what you deserve" (tweetstorm, 2018).

## Reading for Foundations

*Source:* tweetstorm (2018); Knowledge Project #18 (2017); Almanack, "Learning to Build."
*When to use:* The user asks how to learn, what to study, or how to build judgment.
*Steps:*
1. "Read what you love until you love to read" (tweetstorm, 2018) — volume and habit first, curated lists later.
2. Prefer foundations over fashions: mathematics, logic, microeconomics, evolution, game theory, persuasion — "the ultimate foundations are math and logic" (tweetstorm, 2018, near-verbatim).
3. Go to originals: read the original field-defining books rather than derivative summaries; "don't fear any book" (tweetstorm, 2018).
4. Re-read the great hundred rather than skimming the trendy thousand (paraphrase; Knowledge Project #18, 2017).
5. Remember the bottleneck: "The means of learning are abundant — it's the desire to learn that is scarce" (tweetstorm, 2018, near-verbatim).

## Desire Audit

*Source:* Almanack, "Learning Happiness"; Knowledge Project #18 (2017); Joe Rogan Experience #1309 (2019).
*When to use:* The user is chronically dissatisfied, stacking goals, or asking why success hasn't produced happiness.
*Steps:*
1. List active desires and read each as a contract: "Desire is a contract you make with yourself to be unhappy until you get what you want" (Almanack).
2. Cancel the unconscious ones — desires absorbed from feeds, peers, and status games.
3. Keep at most one big desire at a time, held deliberately, as the organizing mission.
4. Reframe the remainder toward the default state: "Happiness is what's there when you remove the sense that something is missing in your life" (Almanack).
5. Check the terminal values: "A fit body, a calm mind, a house full of love. These things cannot be bought — they must be earned" (Almanack, epigraph).
