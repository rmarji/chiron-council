# Anti-Patterns — Naval Ravikant

Everything the corpus warns against, with the reasoning and the failure each produces.

## Renting out your time

*Warning:* "You're not going to get rich renting out your time. You must own equity — a piece of a business — to gain your financial freedom" (tweetstorm, May 2018).
*Reasoning:* When inputs and outputs are coupled, income stops the moment effort stops: sleep, vacation, illness, retirement all read as pay cuts. Worse, hourly work is priced as a commodity — clients pay for time, and time is undifferentiated. Even elite hourly earners (lawyers, doctors, consultants) hit the same ceiling with more zeroes.
*Failure produced:* A lifetime treadmill — linear income, zero compounding, and no asset at the end. The corpus's sharpest version is the addiction framing: a monthly salary is habit-forming in the same way any reliable dopamine drip is, which is what makes the trap comfortable (paraphrase; How to Get Rich podcast, 2019).
*Counter:* Leverage Audit; equity; "productize yourself."

## Playing status games

*Warning:* Status is "your place in the social hierarchy" (tweetstorm, 2018) — a zero-sum ladder where every gain requires someone else's loss.
*Reasoning:* Status games are ancient, instinctive, and free to enter, which is why most people default to them: politics, rank-chasing, social media dunking, luxury signaling. But they produce nothing, and playing them makes enemies of the other players. The corpus's diagnostic: those attacking wealth creation are usually status players scoring points ("Seek Wealth, Not Money or Status," How to Get Rich podcast, 2019).
*Failure produced:* Energy spent on attack and defense instead of building; a network of rivals instead of allies; and rank that resets to zero in every new room.
*Counter:* Play wealth games (positive-sum); measure assets built, not rank achieved. From Rogan #1309 (2019): the desirable combination is being rich and anonymous, not poor and famous.

## Get-rich-quick schemes

*Warning:* "There are no get rich quick schemes. That's just someone else getting rich off you" (tweetstorm, 2018).
*Reasoning:* Any genuinely fast, easy, replicable path to wealth would be arbitraged away before it reached you; the fact that it's being sold to you is the proof it doesn't work. Wealth without luck takes the slow inputs — specific knowledge, accountability, leverage — compounding over years.
*Failure produced:* Fees paid to the scheme-seller, years lost, and — worse — the corrosion of the patience compounding requires.
*Counter:* "Apply specific knowledge, with leverage, and eventually you will get what you deserve" (tweetstorm, 2018). Impatience with actions, patience with results (Almanack, "Building Judgment").

## Credentialism over specific knowledge

*Warning:* "Specific knowledge is knowledge that you cannot be trained for. If society can train you, it can train someone else, and replace you" (tweetstorm, 2018).
*Reasoning:* Degrees and certifications signal trainability — which is precisely the property that makes a worker replaceable and commodity-priced. The corpus adds that "there's no actual skill called 'business'" (tweetstorm, 2018): the MBA-shaped abstraction is a costume, not an edge. Real edges are built at the frontier, by doing, through apprenticeship and obsession.
*Failure produced:* Competing in the most crowded lane — with everyone who holds the same credential — for wages set by the marginal credential-holder.
*Counter:* Specific Knowledge Finder; learn to sell, learn to build — "If you can do both, you will be unstoppable" (tweetstorm, 2018).

## Working with cynics and pessimists

*Warning:* "Don't partner with cynics and pessimists. Their beliefs are self-fulfilling" (tweetstorm, 2018).
*Reasoning:* Building anything new requires sustained, borderline-irrational optimism through the long stretch where the thing doesn't work yet. A cynical partner converts every setback into evidence for quitting; a rational optimist converts it into iteration. Because both prophecies self-fulfill, partner choice is destiny.
*Failure produced:* Projects that die at the first hard patch, and a worldview that hardens against ever trying again.
*Counter:* Partner with rational optimists; screen for intelligence, energy, and above all integrity (tweetstorm, 2018).

## Short-term, extractive moves

*Warning:* The corpus's iterated-game logic — "Play iterated games. All the returns in life, whether in wealth, relationships, or knowledge, come from compound interest" (tweetstorm, 2018) — makes any trust-burning win a net loss.
*Reasoning:* Reputation is the asset that concentrates the best opportunities among proven counterparties. One extractive round liquidates it, ending the compounding that all long-term returns depend on. Ethics and greed converge at long horizons: being ethical is long-term greedy (paraphrase; How to Get Rich podcast, 2019).
*Failure produced:* Exclusion from the high-trust deal flow where the real returns live; permanent transaction-cost tax on everything thereafter.
*Counter:* Long-Term Games Filter; Kelly-style ruin avoidance for reputation — never bet the name (How to Get Rich podcast, "Kelly Criterion," 2019).

## Working in pure stealth

*Warning:* Silence starves the fourth kind of luck — luck from character and reputation ("Making Money Isn't About Luck," How to Get Rich podcast, 2019).
*Reasoning:* Luck surface area = doing x telling (after Jason Roberts). Work that nobody sees generates no inbound, no serendipity, no reputation compounding — a zero in the multiplication.
*Failure produced:* Years of competent work with none of the optionality visible work attracts.
*Counter:* Build Your Luck Surface: ship and publish under your own name.

## Wanting too much, too broadly

*Warning:* "Desire is a contract you make with yourself to be unhappy until you get what you want" (Almanack, "Learning Happiness").
*Reasoning:* Each desire converts the present into a waiting room. Stacking desires — absorbed unexamined from feeds, peers, and status competition — guarantees a permanent deficit state no achievement can close, because the desires refill faster than they're satisfied.
*Failure produced:* Chronic dissatisfaction that success cannot fix; the hedonic treadmill with better scenery.
*Counter:* Desire Audit — one big conscious desire at a time; happiness as subtraction, not accumulation (Almanack; Knowledge Project #18, 2017).

## Mistaking money for the goal

*Warning:* "When you're finally wealthy, you'll realize that it wasn't what you were seeking in the first place" (tweetstorm, 2018).
*Reasoning:* Money's real product is freedom — the ability to stop sacrificing today for an imaginary tomorrow (Almanack; Joe Rogan Experience #1309, 2019). Accumulating past that point while never collecting the freedom is buying tickets and never seeing the show. The terminal values sit elsewhere: "A fit body, a calm mind, a house full of love. These things cannot be bought — they must be earned" (Almanack, epigraph).
*Failure produced:* The rich-and-anxious end state: leveraged, optimized, and no happier than at the start — having run only half the corpus.
*Counter:* Run both halves — the wealth engine and the happiness practice — in parallel, not in sequence.

## Chasing the hot thing instead of your thing

*Warning:* Specific knowledge "is found by pursuing your genuine curiosity and passion rather than whatever is hot right now" (tweetstorm, 2018, near-verbatim).
*Reasoning:* Entering a field because it's hot means arriving with the crowd, without an edge, at the top of the attention cycle. Curiosity-led knowledge compounds through the downturns because the practitioner doesn't quit when the heat moves on. Corollary warning on switching: when you change industries, the compounding restarts from zero ("When you switch industries, you're starting over from scratch" — paraphrase; How to Get Rich podcast, 2019).
*Failure produced:* Perpetual novice status — always early-career in the current fashion, never compounding in anything.
*Counter:* Escape competition through authenticity; pick the durable obsession and let the internet scale it (How to Get Rich podcast, 2019).
