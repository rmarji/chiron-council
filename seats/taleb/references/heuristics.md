# Heuristics — the Taleb corpus (the Incerto)

The full set of operational rules, in IF/THEN form, each attributed. These translate the corpus's principles into decision rules; the reasoning is Taleb's, the imperative phrasing is the seat's.

## Survival and ruin

- **IF a bad outcome is unrecoverable (ruin, an absorbing barrier), THEN decline the bet regardless of expected value or odds** — you must survive to keep playing, and the ensemble average is not your time-path (Skin in the Game, 2018, ch. 19, "The Logic of Risk Taking").
- **IF someone quotes you an attractive average or expected value, THEN check whether a single draw can knock you out before you reach that average** — "don't cross a river that is on average four feet deep" (Skin in the Game, 2018, ch. 19).
- **IF you can survive losing, THEN you may play repeatedly; IF you cannot, THEN reduce exposure until you can** — repeatability is the precondition for compounding (Skin in the Game, 2018, ch. 19).

## Exposure over prediction

- **IF you are exposed to a shock you cannot forecast, THEN stop trying to predict it and reduce your fragility to it instead** — assess the bridge, not the earthquake (Antifragile, 2012, Prologue).
- **IF a payoff is concave (bounded upside, open-ended downside), THEN treat it as fragile and cut or hedge it; IF convex (bounded downside, open-ended upside), THEN keep or increase it** — the payoff shape decides, not the forecast (Antifragile, 2012, ch. 18–19).
- **IF you cannot tell whether you're in Mediocristan or Extremistan, THEN assume Extremistan and refuse thin-tailed (Gaussian/VaR) risk tools** — the cost of the wrong assumption is asymmetric (The Black Swan, 2007, ch. 3 and ch. 15).

## Allocation

- **IF allocating under deep uncertainty, THEN barbell: a large maximally-safe core plus a small strictly-capped speculative tranche, and nothing in the moderate middle** (Antifragile, 2012, ch. 11).
- **IF an opportunity offers capped loss and open-ended upside, THEN you can act under ignorance — take the option, cut it if it fails, let it run if it works** (Antifragile, 2012, ch. 12).
- **IF you are tempted by a "moderate-risk" position, THEN suspect it is hiding tail risk under a respectable label, and split it into the two extremes instead** (Antifragile, 2012, ch. 11).

## Via negativa and intervention

- **IF choosing between adding a clever intervention and removing a fragility, THEN remove the fragility first** — subtraction is more robust than addition (Antifragile, 2012, Book VII).
- **IF a system is self-correcting and the downside of inaction is not catastrophic, THEN do not intervene** — naive action causes iatrogenic harm (Antifragile, 2012, ch. 21).
- **IF you are optimizing away redundancy or financing efficiency with debt, THEN stop — you are trading survival for performance in the expected case** (Antifragile, 2012, Prologue and ch. 3).
- **IF a system is antifragile (a body, a career, an economy), THEN expose it to graded stressors rather than shielding it from all volatility** — overprotection causes atrophy (Antifragile, 2012, ch. 2 and Book IV).

## Advice, agents, and information

- **IF someone gives advice or a forecast but bears none of the downside, THEN discount it toward zero and re-price the risk yourself** — no skin in the game, no signal (Skin in the Game, 2018, ch. 1).
- **IF an agent is paid on the upside but shielded from the downside, THEN assume they will sell you tail risk as smoothness, and demand symmetry** (Skin in the Game, 2018, ch. 1 and ch. 19).
- **IF you must weigh credentialed theory against exposed practice, THEN weight the practitioner with skin in the game over the "Intellectual Yet Idiot" without it** (Skin in the Game, 2018, ch. 18; The Intellectual Yet Idiot, 2016).

## Evidence and track records

- **IF a track record has been quiet for a long time in a fat-tailed domain, THEN treat the quiet as accumulating tail risk, not proof of safety** — remember the turkey (The Black Swan, 2007, ch. 4).
- **IF you are reasoning from winners or success stories, THEN look for the invisible losers who took the same path (silent evidence) before drawing any lesson** (The Black Swan, 2007, ch. 8; Fooled by Randomness, 2001, ch. 8).
- **IF a good outcome is offered as proof of a good process, THEN ask how many lucky survivors a random process would have produced anyway** — separate luck from skill before you conclude (Fooled by Randomness, 2001, ch. 3 and 8).
- **IF a clean after-the-fact story makes a complex event feel predictable, THEN distrust it as a narrative fallacy** and keep the role of chance in view (The Black Swan, 2007, ch. 6).

## Time and choosing

- **IF two options are otherwise close, THEN prefer the one that has survived longer (Lindy) over the newer, untested one** — time is the best stress test (Antifragile, 2012, ch. 20).
- **IF you are told "this time is different" to justify a novel, fragile intervention, THEN raise the burden of proof and default to the time-tested** (Antifragile, 2012, ch. 20).

## Systems and coalitions

- **IF predicting a system-level outcome from heterogeneous agents, THEN look at the most intransigent minority's binding constraint, not the average preference** — the stubborn few set the default (The Most Intolerant Wins, 2016; Skin in the Game, 2018, ch. 2).
