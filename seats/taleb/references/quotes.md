# Quotes — Nassim Nicholas Taleb (the Incerto)

Verified short excerpts with attribution. Wording checked against the published books and Taleb's own INCERTO essays; lines whose exact wording varies across editions and reproductions are marked **(near-verbatim)**, and compressed restatements are marked **(paraphrase)**. Use sparingly, always inside quotation marks, always with the citation. No chapter-length excerpts (LEGAL.md rule M5).

## From Fooled by Randomness (2001)

- "Lucky fools do not know that they may be lucky; by definition, they do not know that they belong to the category." **(near-verbatim; the recurring formulation of the survivorship/luck argument, ch. 1–3)**
- "Mild success can be explainable by skills and labor. Wild success is attributable to variance." **(near-verbatim; ch. 8, on survivorship bias)**
- The turkey/track-record principle: a population entirely composed of bad managers will still, by chance, produce a small number of stellar track records — survivorship makes the lucky look skilled. **(paraphrase; ch. 8, "Too Many Millionaires")**
- Solon's warning, which frames the book: what fortune gives, fortune can take back — a good present says nothing about the unforeseen future. **(paraphrase of Taleb's use of the Solon–Croesus story, ch. 1–2)**

## From The Black Swan (2007)

- "I stop and summarize the triplet: rarity, extreme impact, and retrospective (though not prospective) predictability." (Prologue — the three attributes of a Black Swan)
- "What you know cannot really hurt you." **(near-verbatim; the point is that it is what you don't know — the unknown — that produces Black Swans; Prologue)**
- "Missing a train is only painful if you run after it." **(near-verbatim; the "On Robustness and Fragility" postscript essay, on not chasing what you cannot control)**
- The turkey: fed daily for 1,000 days, the turkey's confidence in the butcher's benevolence is highest exactly when the risk is greatest — the afternoon before Thanksgiving. **(paraphrase; ch. 4, "One Thousand and One Days, or How Not to Be a Sucker")**
- "The Bell Curve, That Great Intellectual Fraud." (chapter title, ch. 15 — the critique of Gaussian tools in Extremistan)
- Absence of evidence is not evidence of absence. **(paraphrase; the recurring epistemic maxim used throughout, esp. the discussion of silent evidence, ch. 8)**

## From The Bed of Procrustes (2010)

- "You are rich if and when money you refuse tastes better than money you accept."
- "The difference between technology and slavery is that slaves are fully aware that they are not free."
- "The person you are the most afraid to contradict is yourself." **(near-verbatim)**
- "The three most harmful addictions are heroin, carbohydrates, and a monthly salary." **(near-verbatim)**
- "An idea starts to be interesting when you get scared of taking it to its logical conclusion." **(near-verbatim)**

## From Antifragile (2012)

- "Some things benefit from shocks; they thrive and grow when exposed to volatility, randomness, disorder, and stressors and love adventure, risk, and uncertainty." (Prologue — the opening definition)
- "Wind extinguishes a candle and energizes fire. The same holds for randomness, uncertainty, chaos: you want to use them, not hide from them." **(near-verbatim; Prologue)**
- "The fragile wants tranquility, the antifragile grows from disorder, and the robust doesn't care too much." **(near-verbatim; Book II, the triad)**
- "If you see fraud and do not say fraud, you are a fraud." **(near-verbatim; Book VII, on the ethics of skin in the game)**
- "For the perishable, every additional day in its life translates into a shorter additional life expectancy. For the nonperishable, every additional day may imply a longer life expectancy." **(near-verbatim; ch. 20, "Time and Fragility" — the Lindy effect)**
- "It is far easier to figure out if something is fragile than to predict the occurrence of an event that may harm it." **(near-verbatim; Prologue — the core pivot from prediction to fragility)**

## From Skin in the Game (2018)

- "Don't tell me what you think, tell me what you have in your portfolio." **(near-verbatim; Prologue — the epistemic filter of exposure)**
- "The minority rule will show us how it all it takes is a small number of intolerant virtuous people with skin in the game, in the form of courage, for society to function properly." (ch. 2 / the essay "The Most Intolerant Wins," 2016)
- "It suffices for an intransigent minority — a certain type of intransigent minority — to reach a minutely small level, say three or four percent of the total population, for the entire population to have to submit to their preferences." **(near-verbatim; "The Most Intolerant Wins," 2016; ch. 2)**
- "You will never fully convince someone that he is wrong; only reality can." **(near-verbatim; on the corrective role of exposure over argument)**
- "Courage is the only virtue you cannot fake." **(near-verbatim; Prologue / ch. 1)**
- Ergodicity and ruin: "no individual can get an edge from the expected return" when a path can knock the player out permanently — the ensemble average is not the time average. **(paraphrase of the ergodicity argument; ch. 19, "The Logic of Risk Taking," drawing on Ole Peters)**

## From the INCERTO essays

- "The Intellectual Yet Idiot" — the credentialed class "who are telling us 1) what to do, 2) what to eat, 3) how to speak, 4) how to think… and 5) who to vote for," while bearing none of the consequences. **(near-verbatim excerpt; "The Intellectual Yet Idiot," 2016; incorporated into Skin in the Game)**
- "A Kosher (or halal) eater will never eat nonkosher (or nonhalal) food, but a nonkosher eater isn't banned from eating kosher." (the asymmetry driving the minority rule; "The Most Intolerant Wins," 2016)

## Attribution notes

- The Incerto is the collective title Taleb gives to Fooled by Randomness, The Black Swan, The Bed of Procrustes, Antifragile, and Skin in the Game (plus the technical companion). Several essays ("The Most Intolerant Wins," "The Intellectual Yet Idiot," "An Expert Called Lindy") were published on Medium/nassimtaleb.org in 2016 and folded into Skin in the Game (2018); citations note both where relevant.
- "Antifragile" is Taleb's own coined term; the word did not exist before the 2012 book (Prologue).
- The ergodicity framing in Skin in the Game credits physicist Ole Peters and the ensemble-vs-time distinction; the Kelly/Shannon/Thorp lineage is acknowledged there as getting the ruin problem right.
- Where a line's exact wording differs across the hardcover, paperback, and quote-aggregator reproductions, it is marked **(near-verbatim)**; treat those as faithful in substance, and verify against a specific edition before printing as an exact quotation.
