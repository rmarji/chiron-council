# Quotes — Marcus Aurelius, Meditations

Short attributed excerpts only. The Meditations survive only in Greek; every English wording is a translation choice, so each quote below **names its translation** — this matters, because renderings diverge sharply (the memorable "what stands in the way becomes the way" is Gregory Hays's English, not a neutral fact about the Greek). Entries whose exact phrasing varies across translations are marked **paraphrase-stable** (the idea is verbatim-solid; the wording circulates in variants). The public-domain George Long (1862) and the literal Farquharson (1944) render the same passages more plainly; Hays (2002, Modern Library) is the fluent modern version quoted here unless noted. Use sparingly, always with attribution.

## Verified wording — Gregory Hays (2002, Modern Library)

- "The impediment to action advances action. What stands in the way becomes the way." (Book V.20. Hays's rendering; the fuller context: "The mind adapts and converts to its own purposes the obstacle to our acting." Popularized further by Ryan Holiday's *The Obstacle Is the Way*.)
- "When you wake up in the morning, tell yourself: The people I deal with today will be meddling, ungrateful, arrogant, dishonest, jealous, and surly. They are like this because they can't tell good from evil." (Book II.1.)
- "We were born to work together like feet, hands, and eyes, like the two rows of teeth, upper and lower." (Book II.1.)
- "Choose not to be harmed — and you won't feel harmed. Don't feel harmed — and you haven't been." (Book IV.7.)
- "Think of yourself as dead. You have lived your life. Now take what's left and live it properly." (Book VII.56.)
- "Just that you do the right thing. The rest doesn't matter." (Book VI.2.)
- "To welcome with affection what is sent by fate." (Book III.16.)
- "To love only what happens, what was destined. No greater harmony." (Book VII.57.)
- "The mind without passion is a fortress. No place is more secure." (Book VIII.48.)
- "Leave other people's mistakes where they lie." (Book VIII.44.)
- "Practice really hearing what people say. Do your best to get inside their minds." (Book VI.53.)
- "At dawn, when you have trouble getting out of bed, tell yourself: 'I have to go to work — as a human being. What do I have to complain of, if I'm going to do what I was born for...?'" (Book V.1.)

## Paraphrase-stable (idea verbatim, wording varies by translation)

- "You could leave life right now. Let that determine what you do and say and think." (Book II.11; Hays and others render the injunction closely, wording varies.)
- "The universe is change; our life is what our thoughts make it." / "Life is opinion." (Book IV.3; George Long: "Our life is what our thoughts make it." A compressed statement of the discipline of assent.)
- "Take a view from above — look at the thousands of flocks and herds, the thousands of human ceremonies, every sort of voyage in storm or calm, the range of creation, combination, and extinction." (Book IX.30; the "view from above" exercise; wording composited across Hays/Farquharson.)
- "Turn it inside out and see what it is like — what it becomes when old, sick, or decaying." (Book VIII.21; the objective-description exercise; wording varies across translations.)
- "The body and its parts are a river, the soul a dream and mist, life is warfare and a journey far from home, and afterlife is oblivion." (Book II.17; on transience; compressed from Hays.)
- "Those who long for posthumous fame forget that the people who remember them will die too, and the memory itself is soon extinguished." (Book IV.19; on the transience of fame; paraphrase of the argument, not a single verbatim line.)
- "It is not the things themselves that disturb people, but their judgments about the things." (Attributed in the Meditations at Book VIII.47 and V.19; the doctrine is Epictetus's, *Enchiridion* 5 — "people are disturbed not by things but by their opinions about them" — and Marcus restates it.)

## From the source of the doctrine — Epictetus (recorded by Arrian)

- "Some things are up to us and some are not up to us." (Epictetus, *Enchiridion* 1; the dichotomy of control, which Marcus inherited via Rusticus — Meditations Book I.7.)
- "Men are disturbed not by the things which happen, but by the opinions about the things." (Epictetus, *Enchiridion* 5; George Long translation. The engine behind "life is opinion.")

## On the term "inner citadel"

- "Inner citadel" is **not** a phrase from the Meditations. It is the title and central image of Pierre Hadot's study *The Inner Citadel: The Meditations of Marcus Aurelius* (Harvard, 1998), naming Marcus's Stoic *hēgemonikon* (ruling faculty), drawn from passages like Book IV.3 and VIII.48. Attribute the phrase to Hadot and the doctrine to those Books.
