# Frameworks — the Meditations of Marcus Aurelius

The Meditations are not a systematic treatise; their "frameworks" are the *spiritual exercises* (Greek *askēsis*; Pierre Hadot's term "spiritual exercises") that Marcus practices in writing. Each is presented here with when-to-use, concrete steps, and source. Because these are self-address, the seat runs them *on behalf of* the user, in the third person. Quoted wordings name their translation.

## 1. The morning premeditation (praemeditatio)

**When to use:** At the start of a day, or before entering a situation with difficult people, to inoculate against anger and disappointment.
**Steps:**
1. Anticipate the difficulty plainly: "The people I deal with today will be meddling, ungrateful, arrogant, dishonest, jealous, and surly" (Book II.1, Hays).
2. Explain it without blame: they act this way "because they can't tell good from evil" — ignorance, not malice.
3. Recall the shared nature: they share reason with you; "we were born to work together." So they cannot be your enemies, and cannot implicate you in ugliness without your consent.
4. Enter the day expecting the friction and refusing the anger.
**Source:** Book II.1 (the most famous instance); the exercise recurs (V.1 for getting out of bed to do human work).
**Note:** A related but distinct exercise, the *premeditation of adversity* (rehearsing loss and death in advance so no blow lands as a surprise), runs through II.11, II.17, VII.56.

## 2. The dichotomy sort (discipline of desire and assent)

**When to use:** Whenever a user is anxious about, or trying to control, an outcome.
**Steps:**
1. Write the situation in two columns: **up to me** (my judgment, intention, effort, response) and **not up to me** (results, other people, reputation, timing, the past, my body's fate).
2. Withdraw all investment from the second column — practice deliberate indifference; these are not goods or evils, only indifferents.
3. Pour full effort into the first column, and act well there regardless of outcome.
4. When distress arises, trace it to a judgment about column two, and drop that judgment: "Choose not to be harmed — and you won't feel harmed" (Book IV.7, Hays).
**Source:** Epictetus, *Enchiridion* 1 (the doctrine); Book IV.7, V.19, VI (the application).

## 3. The obstacle turn

**When to use:** When the user is blocked, thwarted, or treating a difficulty as the end of the road.
**Steps:**
1. Name the obstacle plainly and accept that it has happened (amor fati; stop fighting the fact).
2. Ask: *which virtue does this now call for?* — patience, justice, courage, ingenuity, self-control.
3. Redirect action toward practicing that virtue; the block to one path is the material for another. "The impediment to action advances action. What stands in the way becomes the way" (Book V.20, Gregory Hays's 2002 rendering).
**Source:** Book V.20; supported by the amor fati passages (III.16, VII.57).

## 4. The view from above

**When to use:** Against envy, wounded pride, fame-hunger, or a problem that has swollen out of proportion.
**Steps:**
1. Imaginatively rise above the earth and survey the whole: crowds, ceremonies, wars, marriages, markets, "creation, combination, and extinction" (Book IX.30).
2. Extend the frame across time — the generations before you and after you, all soon forgotten.
3. Return the present concern to its true scale; watch the ego and the grievance shrink to proportion.
**Source:** Book IX.30 (the signature instance); also VII.48, XII.24.

## 5. Objective description (stripping the impression)

**When to use:** When a thing's glamour or menace is doing the user's thinking for them (luxury, a rival's success, a fearsome situation).
**Steps:**
1. Describe the object in bare physical terms, without the value-laden gloss — "turn it inside out and see what it is like: what it becomes when old, sick, or decaying" (Book VIII.21, paraphrase-stable across translations); Marcus does this to fine wine ("dead grapes"), to purple robes ("wool dyed with shellfish blood"), to sex, to fame.
2. Observe how the added judgment dissolves once the impression is seen plainly.
**Source:** Book VI.13 (the "dead fish, dead grapes, dead wool" analysis), Book VIII.21, III.11.

## 6. Retreat into the inner citadel

**When to use:** When circumstances are chaotic and the user needs an immediate place to stand.
**Steps:**
1. Recognize that no external event can enter the ruling faculty without one's own assent — "the mind without passion is a fortress" (Book VIII.48, Hays).
2. Retreat there deliberately: "nowhere can you find a more peaceful and less busy retreat than in your own soul" (Book IV.3, paraphrase).
3. Renew yourself with a few "concise and basic precepts" — the dichotomy of control, the impermanence of things, the sufficiency of virtue — and return to action calmed.
**Source:** Book IV.3, Book VIII.48. (Term "inner citadel" from Hadot, 1998.)

## 7. Memento mori (the death exercise)

**When to use:** When a user is postponing the good life, magnifying trivialities, or gripped by fear of death or loss.
**Steps:**
1. Bring death near: "Think of yourself as dead. You have lived your life. Now take what's left and live it properly" (Book VII.56, Hays).
2. Let the finitude set priorities: what is worth anger, worry, or delay if you could "leave life right now" (Book II.11)?
3. Act on the present task with the urgency and proportion that mortality confers.
**Source:** Book II.11, II.17, VII.56, IV.17.

## 8. The evening / self-examination audit

**When to use:** As a daily practice of correcting one's own judgments (the entire Meditations *are* this exercise).
**Steps:**
1. Review the day's reactions: where did I assent to a false impression, act from anger or vanity, or treat an indifferent as a good?
2. Name the specific error and the correct Stoic judgment.
3. Resolve the correction for tomorrow — "try to be a little wiser than you were when you woke up."
**Source:** The form and practice of the Meditations as a whole; the Stoic evening review descends from the Pythagoreans via Seneca (*On Anger* III.36) and Epictetus.
