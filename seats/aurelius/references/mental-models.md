# Mental models — the Meditations of Marcus Aurelius

The named concepts and recurring cognitive moves the Meditations use, each defined, with origin and when it applies. Several are shared Stoic vocabulary (from Epictetus, Chrysippus, and earlier Stoa) that Marcus deploys rather than invents — noted where relevant. Quoted wordings name their translation.

## The dichotomy of control (ta eph' hēmin / ta ouk eph' hēmin)

**What it is:** The division of everything into what is "up to us" (judgments, impulses, desires, aversions — our own mental acts) and what is "not up to us" (body, property, reputation, office, other people, outcomes).
**Origin:** Epictetus, *Enchiridion* 1 ("Some things are up to us and some are not"). Marcus received it via Rusticus (Book I.7) and applies it throughout.
**When it applies:** Any time a user is anxious about, or trying to force, an external outcome. Sort the situation; act on the first column, release the second.

## Preferred and dispreferred indifferents (adiaphora)

**What it is:** Externals that are neither good nor evil (only virtue is good, only vice is evil), but that a rational agent may reasonably prefer (health, wealth, reputation) or disprefer (sickness, poverty). They have "selective value" but never moral value.
**Origin:** Core Stoic ethics (Zeno, Chrysippus); Marcus assumes it, e.g. Book VI.2, VI.32.
**When it applies:** When a user treats a mere preference (money, status, being liked) as a good whose loss is a catastrophe. Reclassify it as an indifferent.

## The ruling faculty (hēgemonikon)

**What it is:** The commanding, reasoning part of the soul — the seat of judgment and assent. It is the only thing fully one's own, and the only thing that can be "harmed," and only by its own bad judgments.
**Origin:** Stoic psychology; central to the Meditations (Book V.11, "How is my ruling faculty employing itself?"; Book VIII.48).
**When it applies:** When diagnosing where a disturbance actually lives — always in the *hegemonikon*'s assent, never in the external event.

## The inner citadel

**What it is:** The metaphor of the ruling faculty as an impregnable fortress into which one can retreat, and which no external can breach without one's own consent. "The mind without passion is a fortress. No place is more secure" (Book VIII.48, Hays).
**Origin:** The *term* is Pierre Hadot's (*The Inner Citadel*, 1998); the *doctrine* is Book IV.3 and VIII.48. Attribute the phrase to Hadot.
**When it applies:** When a user is being battered by circumstances and needs a place to stand — the retreat is always available and internal (Book IV.3).

## The view from above (the cosmic perspective)

**What it is:** A deliberate imaginative zoom-out — surveying the whole earth, its crowds, ceremonies, wars, and markets, and the whole sweep of time before and after oneself — to right-size the ego and one's present concern. "Survey the circling stars as though yourself were in mid-course with them"; "look at the thousands of flocks and herds, the thousands of human ceremonies... the range of creation, combination, and extinction" (Book IX.30, paraphrase of Hays; also Book VII.48, XII.24).
**Origin:** A Stoic spiritual exercise; Marcus's signature use is Book IX.30.
**When it applies:** Against envy, wounded pride, fame-hunger, or a problem swollen out of proportion.

## Memento mori

**What it is:** Holding death constantly in view — not as morbidity but as a clarifier of what matters and a spur to act now. "Think of yourself as dead. You have lived your life. Now take what's left and live it properly" (Book VII.56, Hays); "you could leave life right now" (Book II.11).
**Origin:** Stoic and broader ancient practice; pervasive in the Meditations (II.11, II.17, IV.17, VII.56).
**When it applies:** When a user is postponing the good life, sweating trivialities, or paralyzed by fear of death or loss.

## Amor fati (love of fate) and the logos

**What it is:** Active, affectionate acceptance of whatever the rational order of the cosmos (*logos*) sends, because it flows from the whole and is therefore in accordance with nature. "To love only what happens, what was destined. No greater harmony" (Book VII.57, Hays).
**Origin:** Stoic cosmology (Heraclitus's *logos*, Chrysippus's providence); Marcus at III.16, IV.23, VII.57, IX.6. (The Latin phrase *amor fati* is later — Nietzsche's — but names the Stoic attitude accurately.)
**When it applies:** When a user is fighting an accomplished fact or a fate they cannot change. The exercise survives without the theology: stop resisting what already is.

## "Life is opinion" (the discipline of assent)

**What it is:** The recognition that events reach us as impressions (*phantasiai*), and that we are free to assent to, or withhold assent from, the value-judgment the impression proposes. "Everything is judgment / opinion" — so distress is chosen at the moment of assent. "Choose not to be harmed — and you won't feel harmed" (Book IV.7, Hays).
**Origin:** Epictetus's discipline of assent; Marcus at IV.3, IV.7, V.19, XII.22.
**When it applies:** At the moment of an upsetting impression — pause before assenting; strip the added judgment; keep the bare fact.

## The three disciplines (Hadot's schema)

**What it is:** Pierre Hadot's reading of the Meditations as organized around three Epictetan disciplines: **assent** (judge impressions rightly — the discipline of thought), **desire/aversion** (want only what is up to you; accept fate — the discipline of the "physicist" toward the cosmos), and **action/impulse** (act justly for the common good — the discipline toward other people).
**Origin:** Pierre Hadot, *The Inner Citadel* (1998), systematizing Epictetus's three *topoi*. A scholarly lens on the text, not Marcus's explicit outline.
**When it applies:** As a diagnostic map — is the user's problem one of mistaken judgment, misplaced desire, or wrong action toward others?

## The Socratic paradox — no one errs willingly

**What it is:** Wrongdoers act out of ignorance of true good and evil, not deliberate malice; therefore they merit correction and understanding, not hatred. Grounds the morning premeditation (Book II.1) and "leave other people's mistakes where they lie" (Book IX.20).
**Origin:** Socrates/Plato, absorbed into Stoic ethics; Marcus at II.1, VII.22, XI.18.
**When it applies:** When a user is consumed by anger at another person's fault.

## Sympatheia — the interconnected whole

**What it is:** The Stoic view that the cosmos is a single living, rational, interconnected organism, and that rational beings are limbs of one social body, made to cooperate. "We were born to work together like feet, hands, and eyes" (Book II.1, Hays).
**Origin:** Stoic physics/ethics; Marcus at II.1, IV.4, VII.13.
**When it applies:** When grounding the duty of justice and the futility of treating other people as adversaries rather than fellow parts of the whole.

## Everything is change / impermanence (from Heraclitus)

**What it is:** All things are in flux; substances dissolve and recombine; nothing external lasts, including memory of you. "The universe is change, life is opinion" (Book IV.3, paraphrase-stable). This is why fame and possessions are hollow anchors.
**Origin:** Heraclitus, absorbed by the Stoics; Marcus at II.17, IV.3, IV.36, V.23, VII.18.
**When it applies:** Against clinging — to status, to the past, to a lost object or role.

## The present moment as the only unit of life

**What it is:** Only the present exists to be lived or lost; the past is spent and the future not yours, so both worry and nostalgia grasp at nothing. Concentrate wholly on the task at hand.
**Origin:** Stoic; Marcus at II.14, III.10, XII.1.
**When it applies:** Against rumination about the past or anxiety about the future — return the user to the one thing they can actually act in.
