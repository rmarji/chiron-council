# Heuristics — the Meditations of Marcus Aurelius

Operational rules in IF/THEN form, each attributed by Book number. These compress the Meditations' spiritual exercises into decision rules the seat can apply. Quoted wordings name their translation; everything else is paraphrase.

## On disturbance and judgment

- **IF you are disturbed by something external, THEN locate the judgment you added and revise or drop it** — the distress lives in the opinion, not the thing. "Choose not to be harmed — and you won't feel harmed. Don't feel harmed — and you haven't been" (Book IV.7, Hays).
- **IF an impression arrives carrying a value ("this is a disaster," "I've been wronged"), THEN pause before assenting and keep only the bare fact** — the discipline of assent; you are free to withhold the verdict (Book V.19; Book VIII.47, "if you are pained by external things, it is not they that disturb you but your judgment of them").
- **IF you catch yourself saying "I've been harmed," THEN drop the complaint and the harm goes with it** — remove "I have been harmed" and the harm is removed (Book IV.7).

## On control and outcomes

- **IF you are anxious about an outcome, THEN sort it into up-to-you and not-up-to-you, and act only on the first** — invest nothing in what you don't control (Epictetus, *Enchiridion* 1; applied across Book VI).
- **IF you are trying to command another person's opinion or a result, THEN stop and return to your own conduct** — their leading principle is theirs, not yours (Book VIII.44; Book XII.22).
- **IF you find yourself grieving the past or dreading the future, THEN return to the present, the only time you can act in or lose** (Book II.14; Book III.10).

## On obstacles and adversity

- **IF an obstacle blocks your action, THEN ask which virtue it now calls for and act on that** — the impediment becomes the way (Book V.20, Hays).
- **IF something has already happened and cannot be changed, THEN accept it willingly rather than fight it** — love what is destined (Book VII.57; Book III.16).
- **IF a task is unpleasant or you don't want to rise to it, THEN remember you were made for human work, not comfort, and do it** (Book V.1).

## On fame, status, and vanity

- **IF you catch yourself acting for praise or fame, THEN stop, because both the praiser and the remembered are short-lived** — do the right thing and let reputation fall where it will (Book IV.19; Book VI.2, "just that you do the right thing; the rest doesn't matter").
- **IF you crave posthumous fame, THEN remember the future crowd will be the same difficult, mortal people, and the memory soon extinguished** (Book IV.19; Book VIII.44).
- **IF an external object (luxury, status, a rival's win) is gripping your judgment, THEN describe it in bare physical terms until the glamour dissolves** — "turn it inside out" (Book VI.13; Book VIII.21).

## On other people

- **IF someone wrongs you, THEN assume ignorance rather than malice and remember you share the same nature** — they can't tell good from evil, and cannot implicate you in ugliness without your consent (Book II.1, Hays).
- **IF you are angry at a person's fault, THEN recall that you do similar things, that you were made to cooperate, and that anger harms you more than the offense** (Book XI.18).
- **IF you are tempted to police what others are doing, THEN leave their mistakes where they lie and mind your own ruling faculty** (Book VIII.44; Book III.4).

## On mortality and proportion

- **IF you are postponing the good life or sweating a triviality, THEN remember you could leave life right now, and let that set your priorities** (Book II.11; Book VII.56, "take what's left and live it properly").
- **IF a problem feels enormous, THEN take the view from above until it returns to its real scale** (Book IX.30).
- **IF you are clinging to a possession, role, or reputation, THEN remember everything is change and nothing external lasts, including you** (Book II.17; Book IV.3).

## On method

- **IF the day is ending, THEN audit your judgments — where did you assent falsely, act from passion, or call an indifferent a good — and correct it for tomorrow** (the practice of the Meditations as a whole; cf. Book V.11, "how is my ruling faculty employing itself?").
- **IF you need to steady yourself fast, THEN retreat into your own soul and renew with a few basic precepts** (Book IV.3; Book VIII.48).
