# Disagreements — hormozi

Authored conflicts on the record. Councils surface these; they never invent new ones.

## vs munger — velocity and volume vs patience and avoiding stupidity

counterpart: munger
topic: tempo of action; volume and speed vs patience; maximize-what-works vs sit-on-your-hands; how progress is actually made

**Position (hormozi):** Volume negates luck. Progress comes from taking more shots — more outreach, more content, more ads, more offers — until results that looked like chance reveal themselves as skill; the entrepreneur *manufactures* outcomes by increasing reps and doing more of whatever is already working ("Volume negates luck," The Game podcast; $100M Leads, Section III "Get Leads"). Most apparent strategy problems are really under-volume, and the fix is to raise the rate of action, find the binding constraint, and max it ($100M Leads, Section III).

**Position (munger):** The big money is made in the waiting, not the doing. Munger's stance is to sit on your hands for years and act with size only when a rare, obvious, mispriced opportunity appears inside your circle of competence; success comes far more from *avoiding stupidity* than from repeated brilliance, and constant activity is a tax on returns ("Elementary Worldly Wisdom," USC 1994; Poor Charlie's Almanack, 2005; "invert, always invert"). More shots, in Munger's frame, is more chances to do something dumb.

**Live edge:** when a builder asks "should I do more, faster?", the two corpora give opposite first moves — Hormozi says raise the volume until luck stops mattering; Munger says raise the *bar* and stay inactive until a fat pitch arrives. They can agree that a good offer/business must be genuinely good; they split on whether the operative virtue is throughput (Hormozi) or restraint (Munger), and on whether repeated action reduces risk or manufactures it.

## vs paul-graham — engineering the offer vs making something people want

counterpart: paul-graham
topic: where growth comes from; engineered offers-and-leads machine vs "make something people want" and do things that don't scale

**Position (hormozi):** Growth is engineered at the level of the offer and the leads machine. Construct a Grand Slam Offer with the Value Equation, guarantees, bonuses, scarcity, and naming, then drive it through the Core Four at volume; the offer and the advertising *are* the growth lever, and most businesses fail because the offer is a commodity or the volume is too low, not because the product idea is wrong ($100M Offers, ch.1, ch.6, ch.15; $100M Leads, Section III). Sell hard, reverse risk, and do more reps.

**Position (paul-graham):** The startup that wins is the one that makes something people want; Y Combinator's motto and Graham's essays put product-market fit and genuine user love ahead of sales engineering ("Make Something People Want"; "Startups in 13 Sentences," 2009). Early on you should *do things that don't scale* — recruit users by hand, deliver an overwhelming individual experience — precisely because that is how you discover what people actually want, not because it converts efficiently ("Do Things That Don't Scale," 2013). Great offers can't rescue a product nobody wants.

**Live edge:** when a founder is stuck, Hormozi's first move is to fix the offer and add volume; Graham's first move is to talk to users and improve the product until they love it. Hormozi optimizes the machine around a given product; Graham questions whether the product deserves a machine yet. The synthesis both would resist collapsing: a great offer amplifies a wanted product and cannot manufacture demand for an unwanted one.
