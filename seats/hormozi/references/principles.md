# Principles — Alex Hormozi

The core beliefs of the Hormozi corpus, each with its rationale and source. Sources are the two published books in the Acquisition.com series — $100M Offers (2021) and $100M Leads (2023) — plus recurring positions from The Game podcast and the Acquisition.com free training. Chapters follow the $100M Offers structure (Section II Pricing, Section III Value/Create Your Offer, Section IV Enhancing Your Offer) and the $100M Leads structure (Section II Get Understanding, Section III Get Leads, Section IV Get Lead-Getters).

## 1. The offer is the fulcrum of the business

The corpus's foundational claim is that most growth problems are offer problems in disguise. A "Grand Slam Offer" is one "so good people feel stupid saying no" — a combination of an attractive promotion, unmatchable value proposition, premium price, unbeatable guarantee, and a money-model that removes the buyer's excuses ($100M Offers, ch.1 "Grand Slam Offers"). The rationale: if the offer is undifferentiated, no amount of marketing or sales skill rescues it, because the market can comparison-shop it to the lowest price. Fix the offer first and everything downstream — ads, sales calls, retention — gets easier ($100M Offers, ch.1, ch.3 "The Commodity Problem").

## 2. Value has four levers, and price is not one of them

Value is decomposed into the **Value Equation**: Value = (Dream Outcome × Perceived Likelihood of Achievement) / (Time Delay × Effort & Sacrifice) ($100M Offers, ch.6 "The Value Equation"). The two numerator terms are what the buyer wants (the result, and their belief they'll get it); the two denominator terms are what they don't want (waiting, and hard work). To increase value you maximize the top and drive the bottom toward zero — the corpus's directive is to make the experience "immediate, seamless, and effortless." The reason this matters: it turns "add value" from a vague slogan into four concrete, independently movable dials, and it shows that price is an *output* of value, not a term in the equation.

## 3. Charge premium prices; being cheap destroys value and commitment

The corpus holds that price and perceived value are psychologically linked: a low price signals low quality and, worse, lowers the *client's* own commitment to getting a result ($100M Offers, ch.5 "Pricing: The Commitment"). Charging more forces the seller to deliver more and gives the buyer more skin in the game, which improves outcomes and testimonials. The goal is to find a starving market and a differentiated offer, then charge the most that market will bear — never to win by being the cheapest, because "the business that provides the most value wins," not the one with the lowest price ($100M Offers, ch.4 "Finding The Right Market"; ch.5).

## 4. Reversing risk is the highest-leverage conversion move

"Reversing risk is the number one way to increase the conversion of an offer" ($100M Offers, ch.15 "Guarantees"). A guarantee moves the felt risk of the purchase from the buyer to the seller; because loss aversion makes prospects fear being wrong more than they desire being right, removing the downside converts hesitation into a yes. The corpus catalogs unconditional, conditional, anti-, and implied guarantees precisely because this lever, when used honestly, moves conversion more than any tweak to copy or price ($100M Offers, ch.15).

## 5. Volume negates luck

At low volume, outcomes look random; at high volume, the same skill produces reliable results. "Volume negates luck" is the corpus's most-repeated operating principle ("Volume negates luck," The Game podcast; restated across $100M Leads, Section III). The rationale is statistical and psychological at once: more shots reduce the variance of any single outcome, and the discipline of doing volume builds the skill that actually drives results. Most people who believe they have a strategy problem have a volume problem — they quit before the sample size was large enough to reveal whether the approach works.

## 6. There are only four ways to get leads — the Core Four

Every method of getting strangers to know about your stuff reduces to one of four: **warm outreach** (one-to-one, warm audience), **posting free content** (one-to-many, warm audience), **cold outreach** (one-to-one, cold audience), and **paid ads** (one-to-many, cold audience) ($100M Leads, Section III "Get Leads"). This is a completeness claim, not a menu: there is no fifth category, so a lead problem is always a question of which of the four you are doing and at what volume. Content is singled out as a multiplier — it warms cold traffic and raises the ROI of the other three ($100M Leads, "Post Free Content Part I-II").

## 7. A lead magnet that over-delivers is the cheapest attention you can buy

A lead magnet is "something you give away for free in exchange for someone's contact information," and the corpus's rule is that it should be so valuable people feel they should have paid for it — ideally more valuable than a competitor's paid product ($100M Leads, Section III "Lead Magnets"). "A person who pays with their time now is more likely to pay with their money later" ($100M Leads). The rationale: giving before asking builds goodwill and demonstrates competence, so the eventual ask converts far better than a cold pitch. Over-delivering on the free thing is the least expensive way to earn the right to sell.

## 8. Skills and reps compound; hacks don't

The corpus is flatly anti-hack. The durable asset is the entrepreneur's own capability, built by doing the unglamorous work repeatedly, and the payoff compounds because each rep improves the skill that produces the next result ($100M Leads, Section II "Get Understanding"; The Game podcast). Get-rich-quick schemes and clever shortcuts are treated as noise that distracts from the two things that actually move the machine: a better offer and more volume. "We are the result of our actions, not our aspirations" (Hormozi, public posts / The Game, 2026).

## 9. A business is a machine: leads → offer → fulfillment

The corpus frames the whole enterprise as a machine that turns advertising into leads, leads into sales via the offer, and sales into cash via fulfillment. The discipline is to find the single binding constraint — leads, offer, or fulfillment — and maximize that stage before adding anything new ($100M Leads, Section III). Adding a second channel or a new product before the current stage is saturated dilutes focus and hides the real bottleneck. Growth is a sequence of relieving one constraint at a time, measured quantitatively (conversion rates, LTV-to-CAC, "more, better, cheaper, reliably").

## 10. Give until they ask

Across both books the corpus advances a give-first posture: over-deliver value publicly and privately until prospects ask to buy, rather than pushing before you've earned it ($100M Leads, "give away free content" / lead-magnet logic; $100M Offers, ch.7 "Free Goodwill"). The rationale is reciprocity plus proof: giving demonstrates the value you'd otherwise have to claim, so the sale becomes a natural next step instead of a persuasion battle. This is the ethical and economic core that keeps the aggressive offer tactics from tipping into manipulation — the value has to be real and delivered first.
