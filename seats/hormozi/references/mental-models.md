# Mental Models — Alex Hormozi

Every named model and concept the Hormozi corpus uses to think, defined, with origin and when it applies. Sources are $100M Offers (2021), $100M Leads (2023), and recurring positions on The Game podcast.

## The Value Equation

**Definition:** Value = (Dream Outcome × Perceived Likelihood of Achievement) / (Time Delay × Effort & Sacrifice) ($100M Offers, ch.6 "The Value Equation").
**The four terms:** *Dream Outcome* — the result and feeling the prospect envisions. *Perceived Likelihood of Achievement* — the prospect's belief they'll actually get it (raised by proof, guarantees, track record; the corpus notes perception often matters more than the underlying reality). *Time Delay* — how long until the result; treated as a "silent killer" of value. *Effort & Sacrifice* — how much work or inconvenience the buyer must endure.
**When it applies:** any time you want to raise value, price, or conversion without adding cost. The lens: find the weakest term, move the cheapest lever — usually the denominator, because making things "immediate, seamless, and effortless" is often cheaper than making the dream bigger.

## The Grand Slam Offer

**Definition:** an offer "so good people feel stupid saying no" — a bundle of value, price, guarantee, scarcity/urgency, and naming that has no direct apples-to-apples comparison in the market ($100M Offers, ch.1 "Grand Slam Offers").
**Origin/rationale:** built to escape the commodity trap (see below) by making comparison impossible, so the offer competes on value instead of price.
**When it applies:** whenever the product is undifferentiated, discounting, or losing deals on price.

## The Commodity Problem

**Definition:** a commodity is "a product available from many places" and therefore prone to price competition and a race to the bottom ($100M Offers, ch.3 "The Commodity Problem"). The opposite of a commodity is a differentiated offer that can only be bought from you.
**When it applies:** as the diagnostic that precedes offer design — if prospects are comparing you on price, you are being treated as a commodity, and the fix is differentiation, not a discount.

## The Delivery Cube

**Definition:** a brainstorming model for *how* to deliver each solution in an offer, varying five dimensions: level of personal attention (one-on-one / small group / one-to-many), level of effort demanded of the customer (do-it-yourself / done-with-you / done-for-you), the medium (in-person, phone, email, text, etc.), the format/frequency, and the speed ($100M Offers, ch.9 "Create Your Offer: Problems & Solutions").
**When it applies:** after listing solutions, to generate delivery vehicles and find high-value/low-cost ways to fulfill them.

## The Value/Cost 2×2 (Trim & Stack)

**Definition:** a two-by-two that plots each potential deliverable on perceived value (to the customer) against cost/effort (to the seller). The four quadrants: high-value/low-cost (keep and stack), high-value/high-cost (keep selectively), low-value/low-cost (trim), low-value/high-cost (trim first) ($100M Offers, ch.10 "Create Your Offer: Trim & Stack").
**When it applies:** to assemble the final offer — trim the low-value items, keep and stack the high-value ones, biasing hard toward low-cost/high-value.

## The Sales-to-Fulfillment Continuum

**Definition:** the trade-off between how easily something *sells* and how easily it *fulfills*. Offers that promise huge done-for-you outcomes sell easily but are costly to deliver; offers that demand customer effort fulfill cheaply but sell harder. The model says find the sweet spot: an offer that both sells well and is feasible to fulfill at scale ($100M Offers, ch.10 "Trim & Stack").
**When it applies:** when designing the delivery model so that selling more does not break operations.

## The Four Value Drivers of Enhancement

**Definition:** beyond the Value Equation, four psychological levers plus naming turn a good offer into an irresistible one — **Scarcity** (limited supply), **Urgency** (limited time), **Bonuses** (added value), and **Guarantees** (reversed risk), finished with **Naming** ($100M Offers, Section IV "Enhancing Your Offer," ch.11-16).
**When it applies:** after the core value is built, to raise conversion and perceived value.

## The Core Four

**Definition:** the complete taxonomy of lead generation on two axes — audience (warm vs cold) × reach (one-to-one vs one-to-many): warm outreach, cold outreach, post free content, run paid ads ($100M Leads, Section III "Get Leads").
**When it applies:** as the map for any lead problem — identify which of the four you're using and whether you're doing enough volume before adding another.

## Lead Magnet

**Definition:** a complete solution to a narrow problem, given away free in exchange for contact information, valuable enough that people feel they should have paid ($100M Leads, Section III "Lead Magnets"). It reveals a problem, offers a taste of the solution, and creates a natural next step toward the paid offer.
**When it applies:** to convert attention into contactable, engaged leads at low cost.

## Engaged Leads

**Definition:** "a lead is a person you can contact"; an *engaged* lead is one who has shown interest in what you sell, not just anyone reachable. "The leads showing interest are the leads that matter" ($100M Leads, Section II "Get Understanding").
**When it applies:** as the metric that matters — the goal is more *engaged* leads, not raw contact counts.

## The Four Lead-Getters (leverage on leads)

**Definition:** four categories of people who get leads *for* you, so lead generation scales beyond your own hours: **customers** (referrals), **employees**, **agencies**, and **affiliates** ($100M Leads, Section IV "Get Lead-Getters").
**When it applies:** once you can reliably get leads yourself, to multiply that by other people's effort.

## More, Better, Cheaper, Reliably

**Definition:** the four ways to grow lead flow — get *more* leads, *better* leads, *cheaper* leads, more *reliably* ($100M Leads). "The more ways you measure, the more ways you can win."
**When it applies:** as the scoreboard for any advertising effort.

## Volume Negates Luck

**Definition:** a probabilistic model of effort — increasing the number of attempts collapses variance and converts apparent luck into demonstrated skill ("Volume negates luck," The Game podcast; $100M Leads, Section III).
**When it applies:** whenever an outcome is being blamed on chance or a channel is being abandoned before the sample size is meaningful.

## The Business-as-Machine (Constraint) Model

**Definition:** a business is a machine — advertising → leads → offer/sales → fulfillment → cash — with one binding constraint at a time; growth is the disciplined act of finding and relieving that constraint before adding new inputs ($100M Leads, Section III).
**When it applies:** for prioritization — resist adding channels or products until the current bottleneck stage is maximized.
