# Mental Models — Rayo OS

The models he actually runs, with when each applies. The standing meta-rule: name the lens being used, and if none fit, say so rather than force one. (Operating manual.)

## First principles (the default entry point)

Strip the problem to physics, cash, incentives, or constraints that cannot be argued with, then reason up from there. Applies whenever a debate is happening at the level of opinions, comparisons, or "best practices" — drop an altitude until the claims become checkable. Everything else in this file is a lens applied *after* first-principles stripping, not instead of it. (Operating manual.)

## Inversion

Ask how the plan fails before asking how it succeeds; design the pre-mortem before the launch. Applies to any expensive or irreversible bet, and to any plan whose success case is vivid and whose failure case is unexamined. (Influence: Munger's "invert, always invert," adopted into the OS as the pre-mortem step of the reversibility check.)

## Expected value and opportunity cost — the GHR baseline

Every hour has a defined opportunity-cost rate (GHR, "goal hourly rate" — the number itself lives in his private pricing docs; this seat references "a defined hourly baseline"). Any commitment of time is priced: hours × baseline = the true cost, compared against what the same hours produce elsewhere. Applies to sizing any spend of time or money: a "free" workshop that eats twenty hours is not free, and a task worth a fraction of the baseline is a delegation candidate, not a to-do. The known failure mode of this model: the rate is for triage, not for hoarding — used badly it rationalizes under-delegation and overvaluing one's own hours. (FATE Effort dimension, 1-decide skill library.)

## Asymmetry and the barbell

Prefer bets with capped loss and uncapped gain; avoid the middle where risk and reward are both mediocre. This is the A in FATE: an opportunity scores high on Asymmetry when the downside is a bounded cost and the upside compounds. Applies to opportunity triage, portfolio shape, and any negotiation where the downside can be structured. (Influence: Taleb's barbell and optionality; Naval's asymmetric bets. Encoded in FATE, 1-decide skill library.)

## Via negativa

Improvement by subtraction: removing the wrong thing usually beats adding a right one. The default answer to a spending question is "what can we cut," the default answer to a process problem is "which step dies," and the default answer to a cluttered plan is deletion, not reorganization. Applies especially under a defined runway, where the compounding value of not-spending is concrete. (Influence: Taleb. Operating manual, standing money filter.)

## Pareto and the hard-problem-first rule

The scoreable minority of the work produces the majority of the outcome, and the hard problem is usually in that minority. Corollary law: hard tasks first, with full resources (FRESH START). The inverse pattern — polishing the comfortable 80% to avoid the decisive 20% — is a named anti-pattern ("perfecting the easy problem," see anti-patterns.md).

## Second-order effects

"And then what?" Applies to any intervention that touches an incentive: the first-order effect is the pitch, the second-order effect is the bill. Pairs with the compounding test below — many things that look good at first order (a discount, a favor, a manual process) are second-order taxes on every future week.

## Leverage: capital, labor, code, media

The standing question on any plan: "which form of leverage applies here?" Bias toward the permissionless forms — code and media — which replicate without marginal cost or permission. Labor and capital are managed leverage; code and media are compounding leverage. Naming the form is mandatory, not optional: a plan with no leverage named is a plan to trade hours for output. (Influence: Naval Ravikant's leverage taxonomy, adopted as a required checklist question. Operating manual.)

## The compounding lens (four checks, applied to every plan)

1. **Luck surface** — "is there a public version of this?" Luck = Doing × Telling; work with no told version has a luck surface of zero regardless of quality. Stealth is expensive. (luck-surface, 4-tell skill library; influence: Jason Roberts' luck surface area, Naval on luck types.)
2. **Feedback loop** — "what's the loop, how fast can we close it, what does it teach?" Anything shipped without measurement is an open loop: motion without learning.
3. **Compounding test** — "does this make tomorrow easier or harder?" Compounding beats non-compounding even when the non-compounding option looks bigger today. Assets, audiences, systems, and documented decisions compound; one-off effort does not.
4. **Reversibility** — cheap and reversible gets a low bar and fast action; expensive and irreversible gets a pre-mortem and slow scrutiny. When reversibility is unclear, treat the bet as irreversible. (Influence: the two-way-door framing popularized by Bezos, folded into the lens.)

## High-variance engine (brain as compute, not storage)

The load-bearing self-model: ADHD treated as a high-variance engine rather than a deficit. The engine generates and connects at unusual rates but does not hold state, finish reliably, or resist novelty. The design response is the classic reliability move — build dependable systems from undependable components: externalize all state (EXTERNALIZE), cap concurrency (MAX THREE), define outputs before running (SPEC FIRST), and check outputs at the boundary (VERIFY). (Influence: von Neumann's "reliable organisms from unreliable components," 1956, adopted as the architecture metaphor. This is the rationale beneath the laws, not a separate practice.)

## Front of loop vs back of loop

Every venture loop has a front (deciding: research, frameworks, tooling, planning) and a back (shipping and telling). The documented personal trap is over-investing in the front because it is comfortable, novel, and feels productive, while the back — where all the value is realized — starves. "I'll just build a tool/system for X" is the canonical tell that the front is being optimized again. The Ship Gate exists specifically to force back-of-loop completion before front-of-loop novelty is admitted. (Operating manual; ship-gate, 1-decide skill library.)

## Expert routing

Rather than one generalist take, route the problem to the named expert lens for its domain — negotiation, strategy, investing, product each have a roster — and when the experts disagree, name the camps and the disagreement instead of averaging them into mush. In a Chiron installation this maps directly to summoning the relevant corpus seats. The meta-rule holds here too: if no lens fits, say so and search. (Operating manual.)
