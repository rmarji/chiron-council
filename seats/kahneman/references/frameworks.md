# Frameworks — Daniel Kahneman

The named, actionable procedures the corpus offers, each with when-to-use, concrete steps, and source. These are the operational cousins of the mental models: where `mental-models.md` explains *why* judgment errs, this file is *what to do about it*. Sources: Thinking, Fast and Slow (2011); Noise (2021).

## 1. The Outside View (reference-class forecasting)

*When to use:* Any forecast, estimate, budget, timeline, or success-rate judgment — especially one built from the specifics of the user's own case, and especially when they feel confident.

*The problem it solves:* The inside view forecasts from the vivid particulars of this plan, with unknown obstacles invisible (WYSIATI), producing the planning fallacy — estimates anchored on the best case.

*Steps:*
1. Refuse to start from the case's own narrative. Note the inside-view estimate but set it aside.
2. Define a **reference class**: a set of past cases that are genuinely similar (comparable projects, comparable teams, comparable ventures).
3. Obtain the **base-rate distribution** of outcomes for that class — how long they actually took, what fraction succeeded, the range including the failures and overruns.
4. **Anchor the forecast on the base rate**, positioning this case within the distribution.
5. Adjust away from the base rate only for genuinely *diagnostic* specifics, and adjust *insufficiently* on purpose — the instinct is to over-adjust back toward the optimistic inside view.

*Source:* Thinking, Fast and Slow, 2011, ch. 22 ("Expert Intuition") and ch. 23 ("The Outside View"); the curriculum-project anecdote that names the fallacy.

## 2. The Premortem

*When to use:* Before committing to a plan the group is coalescing around — a launch, an acquisition, a strategy.

*The problem it solves:* Group momentum and the halo of a coherent plan suppress dissent; WYSIATI hides the failure modes.

*Steps:*
1. Gather the people who know about the plan.
2. Set the frame: "Imagine we are a year into the future. We implemented the plan as it now exists. **The outcome was a disaster.** Take 5-10 minutes to write a brief history of that disaster."
3. Each person writes independently — the prospective-hindsight framing licenses doubt that the group setting normally punishes.
4. Collect and discuss the failure stories; use them to stress-test and revise the plan.

*Source:* Thinking, Fast and Slow, 2011, ch. 24; Kahneman credits Gary Klein for the technique. Its power is legitimizing dissent and countering overconfidence and groupthink.

## 3. Taming Intuitive Predictions (the regression correction)

*When to use:* Making a numerical prediction from imperfect evidence (predicting a candidate's future performance from an interview, a startup's revenue from a pitch, a student's GPA from a reading sample).

*The problem it solves:* Intuitive predictions are *non-regressive* — as extreme as the evidence — when they should be regressed toward the mean in proportion to how weak the evidence is.

*Steps:*
1. Start with the **baseline** (the average outcome / base rate for the class).
2. Form your **intuitive estimate** from the case evidence.
3. Estimate the **correlation** between your evidence and the outcome (usually low).
4. **Move from the baseline toward your intuitive estimate by the fraction equal to that correlation.** Weak evidence → stay near the baseline; strong evidence → move most of the way.

*Source:* Thinking, Fast and Slow, 2011, ch. 18 ("Taming Intuitive Predictions").

## 4. Noise Audit

*When to use:* Before diagnosing a judgment problem as "bias," and before any remedy — in any system where multiple people (or one person repeatedly) make the same kind of judgment: underwriting, sentencing, hiring, grading, medical diagnosis, forecasting.

*The problem it solves:* Noise is invisible because we see only one judgment per case. Organizations correct for bias while never measuring the scatter, which is usually the larger error.

*Steps:*
1. Construct a set of realistic cases (real or constructed) that several judges can each evaluate.
2. Have judges evaluate the **same** cases **independently**, unaware they are being measured against each other.
3. Measure the **variability** in their judgments — the disagreement on identical cases *is* the noise.
4. Decompose it: **level noise** (some judges systematically harsher), **pattern noise** (judges rank cases differently), **occasion noise** (the same judge differs across time/mood/order).
5. Report the cost of the noise to leadership — the number is almost always shockingly large, which is what motivates remediation.

*Source:* Noise, 2021, ch. 6 ("Noise Audit") and Part II.

## 5. Decision Hygiene

*When to use:* Any recurring judgment process you want to make less noisy, once an audit has shown the noise is real.

*The problem it solves:* Individual awareness doesn't reduce noise; procedure does. "Hygiene" because the measures work against unseen, unattributable error — you never know which specific bad judgment you prevented.

*Steps / techniques:*
1. **Favor rules and algorithms** over discretion where a valid formula exists.
2. **Sequence the information** — reveal facts in a deliberate order and withhold irrelevant or biasing detail until needed (limit premature coherence).
3. **Aggregate independent judgments** — average multiple judgments made *without* mutual influence ("the wisdom of crowds" works only if the estimates are independent).
4. **Get independent judgments before discussion** — collect written estimates before the meeting; discussion produces conformity and confidence, not accuracy.
5. **Use relative rather than absolute judgments** and a **shared, anchored scale** (ranking and comparison are less noisy than absolute ratings).
6. **Select better judges** — track records and "respect-experts" who are actually calibrated.

*Source:* Noise, 2021, Part V (chs. 18-21, "Decision Hygiene").

## 6. Mediating Assessments Protocol (MAP)

*When to use:* A big, multi-attribute, often one-off decision — a hire, an acquisition, a strategic choice — that would otherwise be settled by a single holistic gut feeling.

*The problem it solves:* One salient impression (the halo effect, WYSIATI) contaminates the whole judgment; the holistic intuition forms too early and then recruits the evidence to support itself.

*Steps:*
1. **Decompose** the decision into a small number (≈ 4-7) of independent **mediating assessments** — the attributes that should actually drive it.
2. Assess each attribute **separately**, on its own evidence, ideally by different people, using a structured/relative scale.
3. **Delay the holistic judgment** — do not let anyone form or voice the overall verdict until all mediating assessments are in.
4. Only then convene to form the intuitive overall judgment, now disciplined by the independent component scores.

*Source:* Noise, 2021, ch. 25 ("A Debiased Structured Approach"), building on the structured-interview finding that decomposed, delayed-holistic judgment beats the free-form interview.

## 7. Structured Interviewing (the hiring application of MAP)

*When to use:* Selection and hiring, where the unstructured interview is trusted far beyond its validity.

*Steps:*
1. Select a few (≈ 6) **independent traits** prerequisite to success, each concrete.
2. For each trait, write **factual questions** and score 1-5 on a defined behavioral scale.
3. Score each trait **as you go**, one at a time, before moving on — never form a global impression first.
4. **Add up the scores** and strongly favor the highest total; trust the sum over the closing gut feeling.

*Source:* Thinking, Fast and Slow, 2011, ch. 22 ("How to Hire"), drawing on Kahneman's own Israeli-army interview redesign; reinforced in Noise as a MAP instance.
