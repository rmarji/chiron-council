# Mental models — Daniel Kahneman

Every named model, concept, or effect the corpus uses: defined, with origin and when it applies. Sources: Thinking, Fast and Slow (2011); Noise (2021); "Judgment under Uncertainty" (Science, 1974); Prospect Theory (Econometrica, 1979); "The Framing of Decisions" (Science, 1981); Nobel lecture (2002).

## The two-system architecture

- **System 1** — fast, automatic, effortless, associative, emotional, always on; generates impressions and intuitions that become System 2's beliefs. Its operating characteristics resemble perception (Nobel lecture, 2002; TFaS, ch. 1). *Applies:* to essentially every snap judgment.
- **System 2** — slow, effortful, deliberate, serial, the seat of self-control and complex computation; the self we consciously identify with. It is *lazy*: it endorses System 1's proposals unless strongly prompted, because effort is costly (TFaS, chs. 2-3). *Applies:* whenever a judgment "feels" reasoned — check whether System 2 actually did any work.
- **Cognitive ease / cognitive strain** — when things feel easy (familiar, primed, legible, rhymed), System 1 is comfortable and truth-judgments become lax; strain recruits System 2 and sharpens scrutiny (TFaS, ch. 5). *Applies:* to why fluent, repeated, or rhyming claims feel truer.
- **Ego depletion** — self-control and deliberate thought draw on a shared, depletable resource (TFaS, ch. 3). *Caveat: this literature later failed to replicate robustly; treat as contested — see anti-patterns.md.*

## WYSIATI and the coherence machine

- **WYSIATI — "What You See Is All There Is"** — System 1 builds a coherent story from activated information and ignores what is absent; confidence tracks coherence, not evidence (TFaS, ch. 7). *Applies:* to overconfidence, framing, base-rate neglect, and the halo effect.
- **The associative machine / priming** — one idea activates related ideas, biasing subsequent judgment (TFaS, ch. 4). *Caveat: several social-priming results cited here did not replicate.*
- **Halo effect** — a single salient trait (attractiveness, one strong answer) colors the evaluation of unrelated traits, producing spurious coherence (TFaS, ch. 7). *Applies:* interviews, performance reviews, sequential evaluation.

## The judgment heuristics (and their signature biases)

- **Representativeness** — judging probability by resemblance to a prototype rather than by base rate; produces base-rate neglect, the conjunction fallacy (the "Linda problem"), and insensitivity to sample size ("Judgment under Uncertainty," Science, 1974; TFaS, chs. 14-16). *Applies:* stereotyped predictions, "this looks like a winner."
- **Availability** — estimating frequency or probability by the ease with which examples come to mind; biases toward the recent, vivid, and emotionally charged (Science, 1974; TFaS, chs. 12-13). *Applies:* risk perception after a dramatic event.
- **Anchoring and adjustment** — an initial value (even an arbitrary or irrelevant one) pulls the final estimate toward it; adjustments are typically insufficient (Science, 1974; TFaS, ch. 11). *Applies:* negotiations, pricing, quantitative estimates.
- **Affect heuristic** — letting current liking or disliking drive judgments of risk and benefit (TFaS, ch. 13). *Applies:* "I like it, so it must be low-risk."
- **Substitution** — the master mechanism: answering an easier heuristic question in place of the hard target question, unnoticed (TFaS, ch. 9). *Applies:* diagnose *which* heuristic by naming the substituted question.

## Prospect theory and its family

- **Prospect theory** — descriptive model of choice under risk: outcomes coded as gains/losses from a reference point; probabilities transformed by a weighting function that overweights small probabilities and underweights moderate-to-high ones (Prospect Theory, Econometrica, 1979). *Applies:* any risky choice, insurance, lotteries, gambles.
- **Reference dependence** — value is defined on *changes* from a reference point, not on final states (1979). *Applies:* why a $10 discount and a $10 surcharge feel different.
- **Loss aversion** — losses loom larger than equivalent gains (coefficient ~1.5-2.5); "losses loom larger than gains" (1979; JEP, 1991). *Applies:* status-quo stickiness, reluctance to sell losers, negotiation.
- **The fourfold pattern** — risk attitudes flip across the four cells of gain/loss × high/low probability: risk-averse for likely gains and unlikely losses, risk-seeking for unlikely gains (lotteries) and likely losses (TFaS, ch. 29). *Applies:* why people buy both lottery tickets and insurance.
- **Endowment effect** — merely owning a good raises its valuation (the mug experiments), a consequence of loss aversion (Kahneman, Knetsch & Thaler, JEP, 1991; TFaS, ch. 27). *Applies:* buy/sell price gaps, sunk-cost stickiness.
- **Framing effect** — logically equivalent descriptions yield different choices (Asian-disease problem) (Science, 1981; TFaS, ch. 34). *Applies:* any decision presentable as gain or loss.
- **Denominator neglect / decision weights** — vivid low-probability outcomes are overweighted; "1 in 1,000" feels different from "0.1%" (TFaS, chs. 29-30). *Applies:* risk communication.

## Prediction and confidence

- **Base-rate neglect** — ignoring the prior frequency in favor of specific, representative detail (Science, 1974; TFaS, ch. 16). *Applies:* diagnostic and predictive judgments.
- **Inside view vs. outside view** — the inside view forecasts from the case's own specifics; the outside view forecasts from a reference class of similar cases and its base rates (TFaS, chs. 22-23). *Applies:* every estimate — the outside view is the corrective.
- **Planning fallacy** — plans and forecasts are unrealistically close to best-case scenarios because they take the inside view (TFaS, ch. 23). *Applies:* budgets, timelines, project scoping.
- **Regression to the mean** — extreme observations are followed by less extreme ones; predictions must be regressed toward the mean in proportion to evidence weakness (TFaS, chs. 17-18). *Applies:* performance streaks, the "punishment works, praise backfires" illusion.
- **Illusion of validity** — unjustified confidence in predictions, sustained by coherent stories and impervious to disconfirming track records (TFaS, ch. 20). *Applies:* stock picking, pundit forecasts, interviews.
- **Illusion of skill / luck vs. skill** — where outcomes are dominated by luck, apparent skill is illusory; "a major industry appears to be built on an illusion of skill" (TFaS, ch. 20). *Applies:* active fund management, hot-hand claims.
- **Hindsight bias** — once an outcome is known, it feels as though it was predictable all along, corrupting the evaluation of past decisions (TFaS, ch. 19). *Applies:* post-mortems, blame.
- **Conditions for skilled intuition** — validity requires a regular environment plus prolonged practice with rapid feedback (TFaS, ch. 22). *Applies:* deciding whether an expert's gut is trustworthy here.

## The two selves and wellbeing

- **Experiencing self vs. remembering self** — the self that lives the moment vs. the self that keeps score and chooses; they disagree (TFaS, chs. 35-36). *Applies:* wellbeing, product experience design, memory-vs.-experience trade-offs.
- **Peak-end rule** — an episode is remembered by its most intense moment and its end (colonoscopy/cold-pressor studies) (TFaS, ch. 35). *Applies:* customer experience, service recovery.
- **Duration neglect** — the length of an experience barely affects its remembered quality (TFaS, ch. 35). *Applies:* why a long good meal and a short one rate similarly.
- **Focusing illusion** — "nothing in life is as important as you think it is while you are thinking about it"; attention inflates the felt importance of any single factor (TFaS, ch. 38). *Applies:* the imagined impact of income, a move, a purchase on happiness.

## The noise vocabulary (Noise, 2021)

- **Noise** — undesirable variability in judgments of the same case that should be identical (Noise, Part I). *Applies:* wherever a decision could have gone to a different judge.
- **Bias vs. noise** — bias is the average (systematic) error; noise is the scatter (variable error). Total error decomposes into both (the shooting-range and bathroom-scale analogies) (Noise, ch. 5). *Applies:* deciding which error you're actually fighting.
- **System noise** = **level noise** (judges differ in their general severity/leniency) + **pattern noise** (judges differ in how they rank specific cases), and **occasion noise** is the within-judge component of pattern noise (mood, weather, order, hunger) (Noise, Part II). *Applies:* diagnosing where variability enters a system.
- **Noise audit** — a controlled measurement of how much judges disagree on identical cases (Noise, ch. 6). *Applies:* the first step before any remedy.
- **Decision hygiene** — noise-reduction procedures that work invisibly, like washing hands against unseen pathogens: sequence information, aggregate independent judgments, use relative scales and guidelines, favor rules (Noise, Part V). *Applies:* any repeated judgment process.
- **Mediating assessments protocol (MAP)** — decompose a complex judgment into a few independent attributes, assess each separately and independently, and delay the holistic judgment until the end (Noise, ch. 25). *Applies:* hiring, acquisitions, big one-off decisions.
