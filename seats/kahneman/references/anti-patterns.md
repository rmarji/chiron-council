# Anti-patterns — Daniel Kahneman

Everything the corpus warns against, with its reasoning and the failure each produces. These are the errors the frameworks in `frameworks.md` exist to prevent. Sources: Thinking, Fast and Slow (2011); Noise (2021); Prospect Theory (1979); "Judgment under Uncertainty" (Science, 1974).

## Taking the inside view

*The error:* Forecasting from the vivid, specific details of your own case — your plan, your team, your reasons — while ignoring the base rate of similar cases. *Why it fails:* WYSIATI means the unknown obstacles are simply absent from the story, so the estimate clusters near the best case. This is the mechanism of the **planning fallacy**: projects run long and over budget not from bad luck but from inside-view estimation (TFaS, ch. 23). *Remedy:* the Outside View.

## Trusting confidence as if it were accuracy

*The error:* Reading a forecaster's (or your own) high confidence as evidence the forecast is right. *Why it fails:* confidence tracks the *coherence* of the story System 1 built, not the evidence behind it; in low-validity environments confidence and accuracy are nearly uncorrelated. "Declarations of high confidence mainly tell you that an individual has constructed a coherent story in his mind, not necessarily that the story is true" (TFaS, ch. 24). This is the **illusion of validity** (TFaS, ch. 20). *Failure produced:* overconfident predictions, pundits and stock-pickers trusted beyond their record.

## Believing you can debias yourself by insight

*The error:* Assuming that once you *know* a bias, you're protected from it. *Why it fails:* System 1 generates the flawed intuition before System 2 can intervene, and "System 1 is not readily educable." The corpus's own testimony is the warning: after a lifetime studying these errors, its intuitions were "just as prone to overconfidence, extreme predictions, and the planning fallacy as... before" (TFaS, Introduction and Conclusions). *Failure produced:* organizations that run bias-awareness training and change nothing. *Remedy:* procedure — decision hygiene, MAP, formulas — not willpower. This is the crux of the disagreement with the "train yourself out of it" school (see `disagreements.md`).

## Auditing for bias while ignoring noise

*The error:* Hunting systematic error while never measuring the variability between judges. *Why it fails:* noise (scatter) and bias (average offset) are independent, additive components of error; you cannot detect noise by looking at one judgment at a time, and it is usually the larger of the two. "Wherever there is judgment, there is noise — and more of it than you think" (Noise, 2021). *Failure produced:* wildly inconsistent sentencing, underwriting, and diagnosis that no bias correction touches. *Remedy:* the noise audit.

## Letting the group's judgment converge before it's independent

*The error:* Opening the meeting with a discussion, or letting a senior/loud voice speak first, then treating the resulting consensus as strong evidence. *Why it fails:* social influence produces conformity and *shared* confidence, not accuracy; it destroys the independence that makes aggregation informative and amplifies whoever spoke first (Noise, 2021, ch. 21; the informational-cascade problem). *Failure produced:* groupthink dressed up as agreement. *Remedy:* collect independent written judgments before discussion; aggregate, then deliberate.

## Neglecting base rates in favor of the representative story

*The error:* Judging probability by how well a case matches a prototype (the "Linda problem," the tech-founder who "just looks like" a winner) instead of by prior frequency. *Why it fails:* representativeness substitutes similarity for probability and ignores how rare the category is; it also drives the **conjunction fallacy**, where a more detailed (hence more representative) scenario is judged *more* likely than the general case it's a subset of (Science, 1974; TFaS, ch. 15). *Failure produced:* stereotyped, base-rate-blind prediction.

## Chasing the available anecdote

*The error:* Estimating frequency or risk from how easily a vivid example comes to mind. *Why it fails:* availability is biased by recency, drama, and media coverage, not by actual frequency; a single memorable disaster distorts risk judgment for years (Science, 1974; TFaS, chs. 12-13). *Failure produced:* misallocated attention and resources toward the memorable over the probable.

## Explaining regression with a causal story

*The error:* When an extreme performance is followed by a milder one, inventing a cause ("praise made them complacent," "the treatment worked," "the sophomore slump"). *Why it fails:* regression to the mean is a statistical inevitability wherever outcomes mix skill and luck; the causal story is spurious. Kahneman's flight-instructor case (praise "hurt," punishment "helped") is pure regression misread (TFaS, ch. 17). *Failure produced:* false theories of cause, and interventions credited or blamed for chance.

## Anchoring on an arbitrary number

*The error:* Letting an irrelevant starting value — an opening ask, a sticker price, a random figure — pull your estimate toward it. *Why it fails:* adjustment from an anchor is systematically insufficient, and anchors work even when transparently arbitrary (Science, 1974; TFaS, ch. 11). *Failure produced:* being manipulated in negotiations and estimates by whoever names the first number.

## Deciding by frame instead of by substance

*The error:* Accepting the way a decision is *described* as if it were the decision. *Why it fails:* logically equivalent gain- and loss-frames produce opposite choices because they move the reference point (the Asian-disease problem); there is often no frame-independent preference to appeal to (Science, 1981; TFaS, ch. 34). *Failure produced:* choices that reverse on wording, exploited by anyone who controls the framing. *Remedy:* restate the decision in at least two frames before choosing.

## Being ruled by loss aversion and the endowment effect

*The error:* Overweighting what you might lose relative to equivalent gains — holding losing positions, refusing sensible risks, over-valuing what you already own. *Why it fails:* losses loom ~1.5-2.5× larger than gains, and mere ownership inflates valuation (Prospect Theory, 1979; JEP, 1991; TFaS, chs. 27-28). *Failure produced:* the disposition effect, status-quo bias, stalled negotiations, sunk-cost persistence. *Note:* loss aversion is not always error — it's a description of preference — but it becomes a trap when it blocks a decision that final-state analysis would endorse.

## Choosing for the remembering self and calling it wellbeing

*The error:* Optimizing decisions for the story you'll tell (peak-end, duration-neglected) rather than for the moment-to-moment experience actually lived. *Why it fails:* the two selves diverge, and the remembering self's verdict systematically distorts anticipated experience (the focusing illusion inflates whatever you're attending to) (TFaS, chs. 35-38). *Failure produced:* life and product choices that photograph well and feel worse.

## Over-trusting the contested findings of the corpus itself

*The error:* Citing every result in Thinking, Fast and Slow with equal confidence. *Why it matters:* several priming and ego-depletion studies referenced in the book did not replicate, and the corpus itself conceded placing "too much faith in underpowered studies." *Remedy:* weight the robust core — framing, loss aversion, base-rate neglect, anchoring, the outside view, noise — above the contested social-priming periphery.
