# Quotes — Daniel Kahneman

Verified short attributed excerpts only. Wording checked against the source; where an exact page is cited it comes from the standard Farrar, Straus and Giroux (2011) edition of *Thinking, Fast and Slow* (TFaS). **Paraphrases are labeled as paraphrase and are not to be presented inside quotation marks.** Per LEGAL.md rule M5, no chapter dumps — verified lines only.

## On the two systems

> "A reliable way to make people believe in falsehoods is frequent repetition, because familiarity is not easily distinguished from truth."
— TFaS (2011), ch. 5, "Cognitive Ease," p. 62. *(The mechanism behind the illusory-truth effect.)*

**Paraphrase (System 2 as lazy controller):** the corpus describes System 2 as a "lazy controller" that minimizes effort and largely endorses System 1's proposals rather than checking them (TFaS, 2011, ch. 3, "The Lazy Controller"). *Present as paraphrase, not quotation.*

## On WYSIATI and the coherence of stories

> "It is the consistency of the information that matters for a good story, not its completeness. Indeed, you will often find that knowing little makes it easier to fit everything you know into a coherent pattern."
— TFaS (2011), ch. 7, "A Machine for Jumping to Conclusions," p. 87.

**Paraphrase (WYSIATI named):** the corpus abbreviates its account of System 1's coherence-seeking as "What You See Is All There Is" — the mind builds the best story from available information and does not register what is missing (TFaS, 2011, ch. 7). *The acronym WYSIATI is the corpus's own; the surrounding gloss here is paraphrase.*

## On confidence and the illusion of validity

> "Confidence is a feeling, which reflects the coherence of the information and the cognitive ease of processing it."
— TFaS (2011), ch. 20, "The Illusion of Validity," p. 212.

**Paraphrase (confidence ≠ accuracy):** the corpus holds that a declaration of high confidence tells you mainly that the person has constructed a coherent story in their mind, not that the story is true (TFaS, 2011, chs. 20 and 24). *Present as paraphrase unless verified verbatim.*

**Paraphrase (illusion of skill):** the corpus concludes of active stock-picking that "a major industry appears to be built largely on an illusion of skill" (TFaS, 2011, ch. 20). *This line is widely quoted; treat as paraphrase pending page-level verification and mark accordingly.*

## On the focusing illusion

> "Nothing in life is as important as you think it is when you are thinking about it."
— TFaS (2011), ch. 38, "Thinking About Life," p. 402. *(Kahneman's statement of the focusing illusion; he elsewhere calls it a "fortune-cookie maxim." Note: the popular paraphrase substitutes "while" for "when" — the sourced wording is "when.")*

## On self-debiasing (the corpus's own pessimism)

**Paraphrase (studying biases did not fix his own):** the corpus reports that after decades of studying these errors, its author's intuitive thinking remained as prone to overconfidence, extreme predictions, and the planning fallacy as before the study began, and that System 1 "is not readily educable" (TFaS, 2011, Introduction and Conclusions). *This is the load-bearing pessimism claim; presented here as paraphrase to avoid mis-transcription — quote verbatim only from the book in hand.*

## On noise

**Paraphrase (definition of noise):** the corpus defines noise as unwanted variability in judgments that should be identical, and distinguishes it from bias — the systematic, average component of error — using the shooting-range analogy: bias is shots consistently off-target in one direction, noise is shots scattered around the target (Noise, 2021, Parts I-II). *Present as paraphrase.*

**Paraphrase (ubiquity of noise):** the corpus's summary claim is that wherever there is judgment, there is noise — and more of it than people expect (Noise, 2021, Introduction). *Present as paraphrase.*
