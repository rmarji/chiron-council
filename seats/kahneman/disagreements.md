# Disagreements — kahneman

Authored conflicts on the record. Councils surface these; they never invent new ones.

## vs munger — can you train your way out of bias?

counterpart: munger
topic: debiasing; whether a disciplined individual can overcome cognitive bias through study and checklists; where the leverage for better judgment lives — the person or the process

**Position (kahneman):** No — not reliably, and not as an individual by insight alone. System 1 produces the intuition first and is "not readily educable"; System 2 is lazy and mostly ratifies rather than checks. The corpus's own testimony is the sharpest form of this claim: after decades studying these errors, "my intuitive thinking is just as prone to overconfidence, extreme predictions, and the planning fallacy as it was before I made a study of these issues" (Thinking, Fast and Slow, 2011, Introduction and Conclusions). Merely knowing about a bias does not disarm it in the moment of judgment. The durable leverage is therefore *procedural and organizational*, not personal willpower: noise audits, breaking a judgment into independent mediating assessments, collecting estimates independently before discussion, aggregating, and — where a valid formula exists — using the formula over the expert (Noise, 2021, Part V, "Decision Hygiene"). The individual can at best learn to recognize *situations* prone to error and hand the judgment to a better process.

**Position (munger):** Yes — this is largely the point of studying the biases at all. Munger built a checklist of "twenty-five standard causes of human misjudgment" precisely so a disciplined person could run every important decision against it and catch the distortions before acting ("The Psychology of Human Misjudgment," Poor Charlie's Almanack, 2005). His "two-track analysis" is an explicit individual debiasing routine: evaluate the rational facts and incentives on one track while separately auditing the psychological forces that could be misleading you on the other (Poor Charlie's Almanack, 2005). And the "latticework of mental models" — roughly a hundred models from multiple disciplines, of which "eighty or ninety... will carry about ninety percent of the freight" — is offered as a trainable apparatus that makes an individual "worldly-wise" and less foolish over a lifetime of practice ("A Lesson on Elementary, Worldly Wisdom," USC, 1994; Poor Charlie's Almanack, 2005). Inversion — study how judgment fails so you can personally avoid the failure — assumes the individual *can* improve.

**Live edge:** both corpora agree completely on the *diagnosis* — human judgment is systematically and predictably distorted — and split on the *cure*. Munger's optimism is that a sufficiently disciplined mind, armed with checklists and models, debiases itself; Kahneman's pessimism is that the mind that most needs debiasing is exactly the one running the checklist, so the fix has to sit outside the individual, in the process. A council question like "should we trust this person's trained judgment, or force a structured procedure?" lands precisely on this seam: Munger reaches for the practitioner's checklist; the Kahneman corpus reaches for the noise audit and the formula.
