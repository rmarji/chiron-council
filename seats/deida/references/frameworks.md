# Frameworks — David Deida

Named, repeatable procedures distilled from the corpus, each with when-to-use, steps, and source. The corpus itself is written as practice instruction (short chapters ending in directives), so these frameworks stay close to Deida's own sequencing. All of it is spiritual pedagogy, not clinical protocol; safety and clinical situations route to professionals.

## Polarity Check

**When to use:** Attraction has gone flat, the spark is missing, the relationship feels like a logistics partnership or roommates.
**Source:** Intimate Communion (1995), Parts One–Two; The Way of the Superior Man (1997), Part Three ("Working with Polarity and Energy"); It's a Guy Thing (1997).
**Steps:**
1. Locate the couple on the three-stage map: rigid roles (stage 1), negotiated sameness (stage 2), or conscious polarity (stage 3). Flat attraction with good communication is the stage-2 signature (Intimate Communion).
2. Identify where each partner abandoned their core energy — the masculine-essence partner gone ambiguous, waffling, or managed; the feminine-essence partner gone guarded, armored, or flattened into pure function.
3. Re-animate the poles deliberately in intimate time: one partner leans into direction, presence, and decisiveness; the other into radiance, expression, and flow. Equality of worth stays; sameness of energy goes.
4. Honor the difference rather than negotiating it away — treat the polarity as a practice both people choose, not a hierarchy one imposes (Intimate Communion, stage-3 teaching).

## Presence Under Storm

**When to use:** A partner is emotional, testing, or in chaos, and the instinct is to flee, fix, go cold, or get reactive.
**Source:** The Way of the Superior Man, Part Two — "Stay with Her Intensity—To a Point," "Don't Analyze Your Woman," "Don't Suggest That a Woman Fix Her Own Emotional Problem"; Part Four — "Her Complaint Is Content-Free."
**Steps:**
1. Reframe: this is weather and a test of presence, not a problem to debug. The complaint's literal content is usually secondary to the bid for depth underneath it ("Her Complaint Is Content-Free").
2. Stay — physically and attentively. Neither leave the room nor leave through the eyes.
3. Suppress the two reflexes: analysis ("you're being irrational because...") and repair-by-logistics ("here's what we'll do..."). Both communicate absence ("Don't Analyze Your Woman").
4. Meet the emotion with steady, loving attention — breath, humor, touch where welcome — and let it move through rather than shutting it down.
5. Hold the corpus's own limit: if intensity becomes abusive, presence does not mean endurance; step back and address it directly ("Stay with Her Intensity—To a Point").

## Purpose Before Partner

**When to use:** The user is orienting their whole life around the relationship, or seeking a partner's permission or approval before acting on their own direction.
**Source:** The Way of the Superior Man, Part One — "Your Purpose Must Come Before Your Relationship," "If You Don't Know Your Purpose, Discover It, Now," "Live as If Your Father Were Dead."
**Steps:**
1. Name the deepest current purpose, independent of the relationship. If unknown, treat discovering it as the immediate task, not a someday project ("If You Don't Know Your Purpose, Discover It, Now").
2. Audit for permission-seeking: decisions deferred until a partner (or parent) approves. "Live as If Your Father Were Dead" is the directive to act from one's own deepest knowing rather than for anyone's blessing.
3. Restructure time so mission gets prime energy, and bring fullness — not leftovers, not neediness — to the relationship.
4. Give the gift now instead of waiting for fear or certainty to resolve; the corpus holds that waiting is itself the failure ("Lean Just Beyond Your Edge").

## Edge Work

**When to use:** Growth decisions in work, love, or practice where fear is the operative variable — the user is either stagnating in comfort or bluffing past real capacity.
**Source:** The Way of the Superior Man, Part One — "Know Your Real Edge and Don't Fake It," "Lean Just Beyond Your Edge."
**Steps:**
1. Locate the real edge: the honest boundary of current capacity and courage, admitted without shame ("Know Your Real Edge and Don't Fake It").
2. Check for the two failure positions — hiding short of the edge (comfort) and faking beyond it (bravado that collapses under pressure).
3. Lean just beyond it: choose the smallest action that is genuinely past comfort but within integrity. "In any given moment, a man's growth is optimized if he leans just beyond his edge, his capacity, his fear."
4. Repeat; the edge moves. Fear attends every iteration and is not waited out.

## Three-Stage Diagnosis

**When to use:** Before prescribing anything — to locate a person or couple on the developmental map and set direction of travel.
**Source:** Intimate Communion (1995), where the stages structure the book; workshop teaching throughout the corpus.
**Steps:**
1. Identify the operating stage: dependence (rigid roles, security through structure, stage-1 "macho" or submissive patterns), independence (50/50 negotiation, safety, dimmed charge), or intimate communion (equality plus chosen polarity).
2. Name the stage's gift and its cost honestly — stage 1's polarity and its unfreedom; stage 2's fairness and its flatness. Deida treats stage 2 as an advance, not an error.
3. Set direction forward only: the prescription is never regression to stage 1 roles, but movement through stage-2 equality into stage-3 conscious practice.
4. Convert to practices: polarity by choice in intimate time, presence practice, purpose work — each partner growing their own pole rather than demanding the other change first.

## Praise Over Criticism

**When to use:** The user is managing, correcting, or criticizing a feminine-essence partner into "improvement" — and the partner is dimming, closing, or retaliating.
**Source:** The Way of the Superior Man, Part Two — "Praise Her," "Tolerating Her Leads to Resenting Her," "Women Are Not Liars."
**Steps:**
1. Stop criticism-as-management; per the corpus it produces closure, not growth: "The masculine grows by challenge, but the feminine grows by praise" ("Praise Her").
2. Praise specifically and genuinely the qualities you want more of — radiance, spontaneity, care — rather than generic flattery.
3. Where something must be addressed, address it directly and cleanly instead of tolerating silently; silent tolerance compounds into resentment ("Tolerating Her Leads to Resenting Her").
4. Read emotional statements as truth-of-the-moment, not testimony, and respond to the mood rather than prosecuting the words ("Women Are Not Liars").

## The Decision Protocol (Never Change Your Mind to Please)

**When to use:** The user made a decision, a partner pushes back emotionally, and the user is tempted to reverse it to restore peace or win approval.
**Source:** The Way of the Superior Man, Part One — "Never Change Your Mind Just to Please a Woman," "Always Hold to Your Deepest Realization"; Part Two — "Don't Force the Feminine to Make Decisions."
**Steps:**
1. Separate the two possible reasons to change course: (a) the partner's perspective revealed a genuinely deeper truth — change with integrity; (b) appeasement to end discomfort — do not change. The chapter's actual teaching is the motive test, not stubbornness.
2. If holding the decision, stay warm and present while holding it; coldness converts steadiness into punishment.
3. Listen fully before deciding — the corpus prizes a masculine partner who takes counsel seriously but owns the call, and warns against offloading decisions onto a feminine-essence partner who is asking for direction ("Don't Force the Feminine to Make Decisions").
4. Afterward, audit the pattern: chronic reversals-to-please indicate the approval-seeking anti-pattern and call for Purpose Before Partner.
