# Anti-Patterns — David Deida

Everything the corpus warns against, with Deida's reasoning and the failure each produces. His warnings are aimed mostly at the masculine-essence reader (The Way of the Superior Man's addressee), with Dear Lover (2005) supplying the feminine-side counterparts. Final section: misuses of the corpus itself.

## The approval-seeker

**Warning:** Living for permission — reversing decisions, softening direction, and shaping a life to win a partner's (or parent's) approval. **Source:** The Way of the Superior Man (1997), Part One — "Never Change Your Mind Just to Please a Woman," "Live as If Your Father Were Dead," "Always Hold to Your Deepest Realization." **Reasoning:** A person whose word bends under emotional pressure teaches their partner that nothing in them is solid; approval purchased by self-abandonment depolarizes the relationship. **Failure produced:** Erosion of trust and attraction, plus a life steered by whoever pushes hardest. The counter is the motive test — change only for deeper truth, never for peace.

## Making the partner your purpose

**Warning:** Putting the relationship at the center of life and abandoning mission. **Source:** The Way of the Superior Man, Part One — "Your Purpose Must Come Before Your Relationship." **Reasoning:** "Every man knows that his highest purpose in life cannot be reduced to any particular relationship." When direction empties out, what the partner receives is neediness disguised as devotion, and the feminine partner senses the hollow core before the man does. **Failure produced:** The relationship quietly loses the man's depth and the partner's desire simultaneously — the very outcome the orbiting was meant to prevent.

## Collapsing under the storm

**Warning:** Fleeing, going cold, analyzing, or getting reactive when a partner is emotional. **Source:** The Way of the Superior Man, Part Two — "Stay with Her Intensity—To a Point," "Don't Analyze Your Woman," "Don't Suggest That a Woman Fix Her Own Emotional Problem." **Reasoning:** Emotional intensity is usually a test of presence, and all four collapse modes — escape, iciness, logic, rage — answer the test with absence. Telling a partner to go fix her own emotional state is, in Deida's reading, a refusal of the gift she is actually asking for: steady, feeling presence. **Failure produced:** Escalating storms (the test repeats until passed) and a partner who stops bringing her depth at all.

## Neutralizing polarity (50/50 depolarization)

**Warning:** Sanding the relationship into a same-energy, fully negotiated arrangement — the stage-2 trap. **Source:** Intimate Communion (1995), the 50/50 relationship; The Way of the Superior Man, Part Three. **Reasoning:** Deida treats stage-2 equality as a real advance over stage-1 rigidity, but names its cost honestly: when both partners guard their poles and negotiate every difference away, the charge that polarity generates disappears, and the couple becomes excellent roommates. **Failure produced:** A fair, communicative, sexless partnership — and often an affair-shaped or exit-shaped crisis when the missing charge is sought elsewhere. The counter is stage 3: equality of worth with consciously chosen polarity, never regression to stage 1.

## Waiting for fear to pass

**Warning:** Holding back your gift until you feel safe, certain, or ready. **Source:** The Way of the Superior Man, Part One — "Lean Just Beyond Your Edge"; Blue Truth (2002/2005). **Reasoning:** Fear does not resolve before action; it attends every real edge permanently. Waiting is therefore not preparation but indefinite withholding. **Failure produced:** A life of postponement in which the waiting becomes the biography.

## Faking the edge

**Warning:** Bravado — performing courage beyond real capacity. **Source:** The Way of the Superior Man, Part One — "Know Your Real Edge and Don't Fake It." **Reasoning:** The inflated position collapses exactly when pressure arrives, which costs more trust than honest limitation would have. Growth requires locating the true edge first; only from there can one lean beyond it. **Failure produced:** Public collapse under load, and partners or colleagues who learn to discount the performance.

## Criticism-as-management

**Warning:** Trying to correct, critique, or program a feminine-essence partner into improvement. **Source:** The Way of the Superior Man, Part Two — "Praise Her"; companion warning in "Tolerating Her Leads to Resenting Her." **Reasoning:** "The masculine grows by challenge, but the feminine grows by praise." Criticism aimed at the feminine produces closure and dimming rather than growth; meanwhile silent tolerance is not the alternative — it ferments into resentment. **Failure produced:** A partner who is duller, more guarded, and more resented over time, with the critic mistaking the shutdown for confirmation.

## Getting lost in tasks and duties

**Warning:** Substituting busyness for purpose. **Source:** The Way of the Superior Man, Part One — "Don't Get Lost in Tasks and Duties"; "Stop Hoping for a Completion of Anything in Life." **Reasoning:** Task-completion feels like mission but is often avoidance of the deeper direction; and since life's tasks never complete, waiting for a clear desk before living fully is structural self-deception. **Failure produced:** A productive, exhausted life that never touches its actual purpose.

## Forcing the feminine to carry the decisions

**Warning:** Chronically offloading direction — "whatever you want, dear" — onto a partner who is asking for decisiveness. **Source:** The Way of the Superior Man, Part Two — "Don't Force the Feminine to Make Decisions." **Reasoning:** Deida frames habitual deferral not as generosity but as abdication of the masculine gift of direction; the feminine-essence partner ends up doing both partners' jobs and trusting the relationship less. Taking counsel remains essential; owning the call is the point. **Failure produced:** Decision fatigue on one side, diminished trust and polarity on both.

## Misusing the corpus itself

The frame can be misread to justify the opposite of its teaching. Flags, per the corpus's own standards:

- **Withdrawal dressed as purpose.** "Purpose first" used to excuse absence, avoidance of repair, or emotional unavailability contradicts "Live with an Open Heart Even If It Hurts" and "Do It for Love" (The Way of the Superior Man, Part One) — every practice is judged by whether it gives more love, not less.
- **"Holding your ground" as domination or stonewalling.** The decision teaching is a motive test (truth vs. appeasement), not a license to never listen; the corpus explicitly endorses changing your mind when a deeper truth is revealed.
- **Polarity as gender law.** Enforcing "men must lead, women must flow" on unwilling partners contradicts the essence framing (Intimate Communion, Part One; The Way of the Superior Man, introduction) and the stage-3 requirement that polarity be chosen, not imposed.
- **Endurance of abuse rebranded as presence.** "Stay with Her Intensity—To a Point" contains its own limit; and situations involving abuse, coercion, or clinical distress are outside the corpus's jurisdiction entirely — spiritual pedagogy is not therapy, and such cases route to licensed professionals.
