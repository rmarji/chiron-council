# Sources — the Paul Graham corpus

Complete bibliography for this seat, primary sources first. Provenance for every claim in SEAT.md and the reference files traces to an entry here. This is a corpus-mode seat: it is built from Paul Graham's published essays and book, not from private conversation or impersonation.

## Primary — essays authored by Paul Graham (paulgraham.com)

The essays are the canonical corpus. Each is a standalone, dated, publicly published text at paulgraham.com. Cited throughout as "essay title, year."

1. **"How to Start a Startup"** — paulgraham.com, March 2005. Adapted from a talk at the Harvard Computer Society. Source for the three-thing recipe ("good people... make something customers actually want... spend as little money as possible"), "What matters is not ideas, but the people who have them," and "get big slow."

2. **"Cities and Ambition"** — paulgraham.com, May 2008. Source for the "cities send a message" model and the claim that being around the right ambitious peers shapes what you aim at.

3. **"Maker's Schedule, Manager's Schedule"** — paulgraham.com, July 2009. Source for the two-schedules model and the cost of meetings to creative work ("A single meeting can blow a whole afternoon...").

4. **"Relentlessly Resourceful"** — paulgraham.com, March 2009. Source for the founder-trait model: relentlessly resourceful vs. hapless, "Not merely relentless," and "'Make something people want' is the destination, but 'Be relentlessly resourceful' is how you get there."

5. **"Schlep Blindness"** — paulgraham.com, January 2012. Source for the schlep model, the Stripe example, "Your unconscious won't even let you see ideas that involve painful schleps," and "A company is defined by the schleps it will undertake."

6. **"How to Get Startup Ideas"** — paulgraham.com, November 2012. Source for "look for problems, preferably problems you have yourself," the sitcom/made-up idea filter, the "three things in common," "Live in the future," and "become the sort of person who has them."

7. **"Startup = Growth"** — paulgraham.com, September 2012. Source for the definition of a startup, "The only essential thing is growth," the weekly growth-rate targets (5-7%, 10%, 1%), the growth-as-compass rule ("ipso facto right"), and "growth is a constraint much like truth."

8. **"Do Things That Don't Scale"** — paulgraham.com, July 2013. Source for the whole unscalable-early-work playbook: recruit manually, the Collison installation, delight beyond scale, contained fire, do it manually, and the "vector" framing.

9. **"Default Alive or Default Dead?"** — paulgraham.com, October 2015. Source for the default-alive/default-dead binary, the fatal pinch, and "Hiring too fast is by far the biggest killer of startups that raise money."

10. **"How to Do Great Work"** — paulgraham.com, July 2023. Source for the general theory of ambitious work: the four steps, curiosity as the engine, prestige as a trailing indicator, earnestness as intellectual honesty, and "The way to figure out what to work on is by working."

## Primary — book

11. **Paul Graham, *Hackers & Painters: Big Ideas from the Computer Age*** (O'Reilly Media, 2004). Collected essays including "How to Make Wealth," "Beating the Averages," and "The Other Road Ahead." Background for the corpus's view of makers, wealth creation through building, and the maker's mindset that underlies the startup essays. Cited as "Hackers & Painters, 2004."

## Corpus scope and context

12. **Y Combinator** — the accelerator Graham co-founded in 2005 (with Jessica Livingston, Robert Morris, and Trevor Blackwell). Many essays draw on patterns observed across YC batches, and several YC "Startup School" talks restate the essay material. The essays are the citable, dated artifacts; where a claim rests on a YC observation, the essay that reports it is cited rather than an unpublished talk.

## Citation conventions used in this seat

- Essay claims: essay title in quotation marks + year, e.g. ("Startup = Growth," 2012).
- Book claims: ("Hackers & Painters, 2004").
- Direct quotes appear only in `quotes.md`-verified wording, inside quotation marks; everything else is paraphrase and is presented as such.
- Where a quote's exact wording is load-bearing, it was verified directly against the essay text at paulgraham.com.
- The counterpart in `disagreements.md` (Peter Thiel, *Zero to One*, 2014) is cited by chapter for Thiel's positions; those citations describe Thiel's corpus, not Graham's, and are marked `counterpart: thiel`.
