# Frameworks — the Paul Graham corpus

Every named framework with when-to-use, concrete steps, and source. Attributions are to the named essay and year. Quotes are marked; the rest is paraphrase.

## The three-thing recipe for a successful startup

*Source:* "How to Start a Startup" (2005).
*When to use:* At the very start, to check that the fundamentals are in place before anything else.
*Steps:* "You need three things to create a successful startup: to start with good people, to make something customers actually want, and to spend as little money as possible." Get good people (because "What matters is not ideas, but the people who have them. Good people can fix bad ideas, but good ideas can't save bad people"). Make something customers want by getting a prototype in front of them and refining on their reactions. Stay cheap — "get big slow"; rapid hiring and lavish spend signal weakness, not strength.

## The Growth-Rate Compass

*Source:* "Startup = Growth" (2012).
*When to use:* When a founder is overwhelmed by decisions, or can't tell whether the company is actually working.
*Steps:*
1. Pick one growth metric — usually weekly revenue growth, or weekly active-user growth if pre-revenue.
2. Set a target rate: "A good growth rate during YC is 5-7% a week. If you can hit 10% a week you're doing exceptionally well. If you can only manage 1%, it's a sign you haven't yet figured out what you're doing."
3. Measure it weekly, without exception, and plot it.
4. Use it as the decision rule: "anything that gets you the growth you need is ipso facto right." Anything that doesn't move it is a distraction. Treat the rate as ground truth — "growth is a constraint much like truth."
*Caveat:* the model is scoped to companies "designed to grow fast"; it misleads for long-cycle hardware, deep-tech, or regulated businesses.

## Idea by Noticing (not brainstorming)

*Source:* "How to Get Startup Ideas" (2012); "Schlep Blindness" (2012).
*When to use:* When a founder is trying to "come up with an idea," or is holding a plausible-but-untested one.
*Steps:*
1. Stop trying to think of startup ideas. Instead "look for problems, preferably problems you have yourself."
2. Position yourself at the frontier of a fast-changing field so gaps become visible — "Live in the future and build what seems interesting."
3. Test the candidate against the sitcom filter: if people say "yeah, maybe" in casual conversation but no one badly needs it, reject it — "The most common mistake startups make is to solve problems no one has."
4. Check for a "well": is there a small group who need it intensely (like Facebook's Harvard students)?
5. Prefer ideas hidden behind schleps — ask "what problem do I wish someone else would solve?" — because "If you pick an ambitious idea, you'll have less competition."
6. Confirm the shape: the best ideas are "something the founders themselves want, that they themselves can build, and that few others realize are worth doing."

## Do Things That Don't Scale

*Source:* "Do Things That Don't Scale" (2013).
*When to use:* When a startup is pre-traction, waiting for growth, or over-investing in automation and channels before having users.
*Steps:*
1. **Recruit manually.** "You can't wait for users to come to you. You have to go out and get them." Go get the first users one at a time; for marketplaces, "expect to take heroic measures at first."
2. **Close on the spot** (the Collison installation): don't defer with "we'll send you a link" — set the user up immediately.
3. **Delight beyond scale.** Deliver "insanely great" service and hand-hold early users; "I have never once seen a startup lured down a blind alley by trying too hard to make their initial users happy."
4. **Contain the fire.** Start with a narrow segment and get it very hot before widening.
5. **Do it manually**, then automate later — launch faster with human effort behind the scenes.
6. **Treat the startup as a vector**: product + the unscalable acquisition move — "pairs of what you're going to build, plus the unscalable thing(s) you're going to do initially to get the company going."
*Caveat:* this is explicitly a phase, not a permanent operating model.

## The Default-Alive Test

*Source:* "Default Alive or Default Dead?" (2015).
*When to use:* Whenever a startup has raised money, is planning to hire, or is planning spend.
*Steps:*
1. Ask the question and write down the answer: on current revenue growth and expenses, "by default do they live or die?" — reach profitability before the money runs out, or not?
2. Ask it early. "Half the founders I talk to don't know whether they're default alive or default dead" — the danger is discovering it too late.
3. If default dead, watch for the fatal pinch: "default dead + slow growth + not enough time to fix it."
4. Fix product and growth before adding headcount — "Hiring too fast is by far the biggest killer of startups that raise money"; the product "needs to evolve more than to be 'built out.'"
5. Don't mistake the large staffs of successful companies as the cause of success rather than an effect of it.

## Recipe for Great Work (the four steps)

*Source:* "How to Do Great Work" (2023).
*When to use:* When the question is broader than one startup — choosing a field, a career, or a decade's focus.
*Steps:*
1. **Choose** work with three qualities: "something you have a natural aptitude for, that you have a deep interest in, and that offers scope to do great work."
2. **Get to the frontier:** learn enough that you're at the edge of what's known.
3. **Notice the gaps** others overlook.
4. **Explore the promising ones.** Curiosity drives all four — "Curiosity is the key to all four steps in doing great work." When stuck: "The way to figure out what to work on is by working. If you're not sure what to work on, guess." Ignore prestige ("at best a trailing indicator") and stay earnest, since "The core of being earnest is being intellectually honest."

## The Maker's/Manager's Schedule protocol

*Source:* "Maker's Schedule, Manager's Schedule" (2009).
*When to use:* When a founder-builder or a team of makers is losing deep-work time to meetings.
*Steps:* Recognize that makers need half-day blocks — "A single meeting can blow a whole afternoon, by breaking it into two pieces each too small to do anything hard in." Protect contiguous maker time; cluster necessary meetings at the edges of the day (the office-hours pattern). At minimum, get managers to "understand the cost" they impose when they schedule a maker mid-afternoon.
