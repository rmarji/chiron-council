# Anti-patterns — the Paul Graham corpus

Everything the essays warn against, with the reasoning and the failure it produces. Attributions are to the named essay and year. Quotes are marked; the rest is paraphrase.

## Solving a problem no one has (the sitcom idea)

*The failure:* You build something that sounds plausible in casual conversation but that no one actually needs, and it never gets real users. "The most common mistake startups make is to solve problems no one has" ("How to Get Startup Ideas," 2012). A "made-up" or "sitcom" idea — like "a social network for pet owners" — passes the "yeah, maybe" test but fails to attract anyone who badly wants it.
*Why it happens:* It comes from brainstorming ideas in the abstract instead of noticing a problem you actually have. The corpus's fix is to invert the whole approach: "The way to get startup ideas is not to try to think of startup ideas. It's to look for problems, preferably problems you have yourself" ("How to Get Startup Ideas," 2012).

## Schlep blindness

*The failure:* You never even see the best ideas, because your unconscious steers you away from ideas that involve tedious, unpleasant, or scary work. "Your unconscious won't even let you see ideas that involve painful schleps. That's schlep blindness" ("Schlep Blindness," 2012). Payments were "broken" for a decade while founders built recipe sites instead — until Stripe took on the banking, fraud, and regulatory schleps.
*Why it matters:* The avoidance is systematic, so schlep-heavy ideas are undervalued and under-competed — "If you pick an ambitious idea, you'll have less competition, because everyone else will have been frightened off." The fix is to ask "what problem do I wish someone else would solve?" rather than "what should I solve?"

## Waiting for growth instead of manufacturing it

*The failure:* You expect users to arrive on their own, and nothing happens. The corpus is blunt that this is backwards: "Actually startups take off because the founders make them take off," and "You can't wait for users to come to you. You have to go out and get them" ("Do Things That Don't Scale," 2013). The related mistake is being too "diffident" to close — saying "we'll send you a link" instead of setting the user up on the spot.
*Why it happens:* Founders (often engineers) are shy about manual recruiting, are lazy about it, or underestimate compound growth. The fix is the whole "do things that don't scale" playbook.

## Betting on a big launch or a partnership

*The failure:* You pin growth on a single splashy launch or a deal with a big company, and it produces little sustainable growth. On partnerships: they turn out to be "way more work than we expected, and we ended up getting practically nothing out of it" ("Do Things That Don't Scale," 2013).
*Why it matters:* Big launches don't generate durable growth; the corpus holds that "Any strategy that omits the effort" of manually getting and delighting users is "ipso facto suspect." Growth is earned user by user, not switched on by an event.

## Refusing to delight early users because it "doesn't scale"

*The failure:* You skip hand-written thank-you notes, hand-holding, and extraordinary early service because it can't scale — and you starve yourself of the feedback and loyalty that ignite a startup. The corpus is emphatic: "I have never once seen a startup lured down a blind alley by trying too hard to make their initial users happy" ("Do Things That Don't Scale," 2013).
*Why it happens:* Engineering culture treats unscalable, manual service as beneath the product. But at the earliest stage, "the experience of being your user" and the feedback from delighting a few users is "the best you ever get."

## Hiring your way out of a product problem

*The failure:* A default-dead company raises money and hires aggressively, believing headcount will fix things — and burns down faster. "Hiring too fast is by far the biggest killer of startups that raise money" ("Default Alive or Default Dead?," 2015).
*Why it happens:* Founders mistake the large staffs of already-successful companies as the *cause* of success rather than an effect of it. The real problem is usually that "the product is not appealing enough" and "needs to evolve more than to be 'built out.'" The fix: know whether you're default alive or default dead before you hire.

## Not knowing whether you're default alive or default dead

*The failure:* You drift toward the fatal pinch — "default dead + slow growth + not enough time to fix it" — without realizing it, and discover the trajectory too late to change it. "Half the founders I talk to don't know whether they're default alive or default dead" ("Default Alive or Default Dead?," 2015).
*Why it matters:* The answer changes everything; a default-dead company must fix product and growth immediately, not plan expansion. Not asking the question is itself the danger.

## Scaling before product-market fit

*The failure:* Building acquisition machinery — paid channels, headcount, partnerships — before a small group of users actually loves the product. This combines the growth, default-dead, and don't-scale warnings: you spend to grow something people don't yet want. The corpus's counter is to stay cheap ("spend as little money as possible," "How to Start a Startup," 2005) and do things that don't scale until real growth appears ("Do Things That Don't Scale," 2013).

## Raising money to figure out the idea

*The failure:* Treating funding as a substitute for having noticed a real problem. The corpus holds that ideas come from noticing problems you have and can build for — "something the founders themselves want, that they themselves can build, and that few others realize are worth doing" ("How to Get Startup Ideas," 2012) — not from funding an open-ended search. Money follows demonstrated demand, not the reverse.

## Chasing prestige instead of curiosity

*The failure:* Choosing what to work on by how impressive it sounds. "The prestige of a type of work is at best a trailing indicator and sometimes completely mistaken" ("How to Do Great Work," 2023). Following prestige corrupts judgment and pulls you off the work you'd actually be great at.
*The fix:* Let genuine curiosity choose the field, and stay earnest — "The core of being earnest is being intellectually honest."

## Letting meetings shred maker time

*The failure:* Scheduling a builder into mid-afternoon meetings and destroying their capacity for hard creative work — "A single meeting can blow a whole afternoon, by breaking it into two pieces each too small to do anything hard in" ("Maker's Schedule, Manager's Schedule," 2009). The fix is to protect contiguous blocks and cluster meetings at the day's edges.
