# Quotes — the Paul Graham corpus

Verified short excerpts with attribution. Wording was checked against the essay text at paulgraham.com. Lines whose exact phrasing varies across the essay (or that compress a longer passage) are marked **(paraphrase)** or **(variant wording)**. Use sparingly, always inside quotation marks, always with the citation.

## From "Startup = Growth" (2012)

- "A startup is a company designed to grow fast. Being newly founded does not in itself make a company a startup."
- "The only essential thing is growth. Everything else we associate with startups follows from growth."
- "A good growth rate during YC is 5-7% a week. If you can hit 10% a week you're doing exceptionally well. If you can only manage 1%, it's a sign you haven't yet figured out what you're doing."
- "you can use that target growth rate to make all your decisions for you; anything that gets you the growth you need is ipso facto right."
- "For startups, growth is a constraint much like truth."

## From "Do Things That Don't Scale" (2013)

- "Actually startups take off because the founders make them take off."
- "You can't wait for users to come to you. You have to go out and get them."
- "I have never once seen a startup lured down a blind alley by trying too hard to make their initial users happy."
- "When anyone agreed to try Stripe they'd say 'Right then, give me your laptop' and set them up on the spot." (the Collison installation)
- "Marketplaces are so hard to get rolling that you should expect to take heroic measures at first."
- "Instead we should try thinking of them as pairs of what you're going to build, plus the unscalable thing(s) you're going to do initially to get the company going." (the "vector" idea)
- "Any strategy that omits the effort... is ipso facto suspect." **(paraphrase; compresses the launch/partnership passage)**

## From "How to Get Startup Ideas" (2012)

- "The way to get startup ideas is not to try to think of startup ideas. It's to look for problems, preferably problems you have yourself."
- "The very best startup ideas tend to have three things in common: they're something the founders themselves want, that they themselves can build, and that few others realize are worth doing."
- "The most common mistake startups make is to solve problems no one has."
- "At YC we call these 'made-up' or 'sitcom' startup ideas."
- "Empirically, the way to have good startup ideas is to become the sort of person who has them."
- "Live in the future, then build what's missing." **(also appears in the variant form "Live in the future and build what seems interesting")**

## From "Schlep Blindness" (2012)

- "Schlep was originally a Yiddish word but has passed into general use in the US. It means a tedious, unpleasant task."
- "Your unconscious won't even let you see ideas that involve painful schleps. That's schlep blindness."
- "A company is defined by the schleps it will undertake."
- "If you pick an ambitious idea, you'll have less competition, because everyone else will have been frightened off."
- "It's too late now to be Stripe, but there's plenty still broken in the world, if you know how to see it."

## From "Default Alive or Default Dead?" (2015)

- "Half the founders I talk to don't know whether they're default alive or default dead."
- "The fatal pinch is default dead + slow growth + not enough time to fix it." **(near-verbatim)**
- "Hiring too fast is by far the biggest killer of startups that raise money."

## From "Relentlessly Resourceful" (2009)

- "Hapless implies passivity. To be hapless is to be battered by circumstances — to let the world have its way with you, instead of having your way with the world."
- "Not merely relentless. That's not enough to make things go your way except in a few mostly uninteresting domains."
- "'Make something people want' is the destination, but 'Be relentlessly resourceful' is how you get there."

## From "How to Start a Startup" (2005)

- "You need three things to create a successful startup: to start with good people, to make something customers actually want, and to spend as little money as possible." **(near-verbatim; the essay's three-part framing)**
- "What matters is not ideas, but the people who have them."
- "Good people can fix bad ideas, but good ideas can't save bad people."

## From "Maker's Schedule, Manager's Schedule" (2009)

- "A single meeting can blow a whole afternoon, by breaking it into two pieces each too small to do anything hard in."
- "You can't write or program well in units of an hour." **(near-verbatim)**

## From "How to Do Great Work" (2023)

- "The work you choose needs to have three qualities: it has to be something you have a natural aptitude for, that you have a deep interest in, and that offers scope to do great work."
- "Curiosity is the key to all four steps in doing great work: it will choose the field for you, get you to the frontier, cause you to notice the gaps in it, and drive you to explore them."
- "The way to figure out what to work on is by working. If you're not sure what to work on, guess."
- "The prestige of a type of work is at best a trailing indicator and sometimes completely mistaken."
- "The core of being earnest is being intellectually honest."

## From "Cities and Ambition" (2008)

- "Great cities attract ambitious people." **(near-verbatim)**
- "There are few people strong enough to keep working on something no one cares about." **(paraphrase; the essay's point about needing peers who value your kind of ambition)**
