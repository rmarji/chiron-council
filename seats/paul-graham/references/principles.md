# Principles — the Paul Graham corpus

The full catalog of core beliefs in the paulgraham.com startup essays, each with rationale and source. Every claim is attributed to a named essay and year. Direct quotes are marked with quotation marks; everything else is paraphrase.

## 1. A startup is a company designed to grow fast

The corpus's foundational reframe: startup is not a synonym for "new company" or "tech company." "A startup is a company designed to grow fast. Being newly founded does not in itself make a company a startup. Nor is it necessary for a startup to work on technology, or take venture funding, or have some sort of 'exit.' The only essential thing is growth. Everything else we associate with startups follows from growth" ("Startup = Growth," 2012). A barbershop makes something people want but can't grow fast because it can only serve people locally; a search engine can. The rationale: growth is what forces and reveals everything else — the need to make something many people want, the need for scalable ways to reach them, and the venture economics that follow ("Startup = Growth," 2012).

## 2. Make something people want

The single sentence Y Combinator prints on its literal T-shirts, and the destination of the whole exercise. In the three-part recipe: "You need three things to create a successful startup: to start with good people, to make something customers actually want, and to spend as little money as possible" ("How to Start a Startup," 2005). And in "Relentlessly Resourceful" (2009): "'Make something people want' is the destination." The rationale is that most startups fail not from running out of money but because they made something nobody wanted — demand is the binding constraint, and everything upstream (ideas, decks, PR) is subordinate to it.

## 3. People matter more than ideas

"What matters is not ideas, but the people who have them. Good people can fix bad ideas, but good ideas can't save bad people" ("How to Start a Startup," 2005). The rationale: ideas mutate constantly in the early life of a startup, so the durable asset is a team that can notice problems, adapt, and execute. Microsoft began intending to sell programming languages; the initial idea rarely survives.

## 4. Growth is the one metric — and the compass

Because growth is the essential thing, the weekly growth rate becomes the measurement and the decision tool. "A good growth rate during YC is 5-7% a week. If you can hit 10% a week you're doing exceptionally well. If you can only manage 1%, it's a sign you haven't yet figured out what you're doing" ("Startup = Growth," 2012). And it decides for you: "you can use that target growth rate to make all your decisions for you; anything that gets you the growth you need is ipso facto right." The corpus goes further and calls growth epistemically load-bearing: "For startups, growth is a constraint much like truth" ("Startup = Growth," 2012).

## 5. Startups take off because founders make them take off

The default outcome of a new company is that nothing happens; traction is not awaited but manufactured. "Actually startups take off because the founders make them take off" ("Do Things That Don't Scale," 2013). The rationale: early-stage growth is fragile and requires "extraordinary effort initially"; there is no autopilot that carries a startup from zero to momentum.

## 6. Do things that don't scale

The most counterintuitive principle: the way to start is to do labor-intensive things that could never work at scale. Recruit users manually — "You can't wait for users to come to you. You have to go out and get them." Delight them beyond reason — "I have never once seen a startup lured down a blind alley by trying too hard to make their initial users happy." Do manually what you'll later automate. And treat a startup idea not as a product alone but as "pairs of what you're going to build, plus the unscalable thing(s) you're going to do initially to get the company going" ("Do Things That Don't Scale," 2013). The rationale: unscalable effort is how a fragile early startup gets the initial users and feedback that scalable machinery can't yet produce.

## 7. Get ideas by noticing real problems, not by brainstorming

"The way to get startup ideas is not to try to think of startup ideas. It's to look for problems, preferably problems you have yourself" ("How to Get Startup Ideas," 2012). Brainstormed ideas are "sitcom" ideas — plausible in casual conversation ("yeah, maybe") but solving a problem no one actually has, which the corpus names "the most common mistake startups make." The best ideas are "something the founders themselves want, that they themselves can build, and that few others realize are worth doing." The deeper move is to live at the frontier of a fast-changing field so the missing thing becomes obvious to you, and to "become the sort of person who has them" ("How to Get Startup Ideas," 2012).

## 8. The best ideas hide behind schleps

"Schlep... means a tedious, unpleasant task." "Your unconscious won't even let you see ideas that involve painful schleps. That's schlep blindness" ("Schlep Blindness," 2012). Because most founders unconsciously avoid schlep-heavy ideas, those ideas are undervalued and under-competed: "If you pick an ambitious idea, you'll have less competition, because everyone else will have been frightened off." "A company is defined by the schleps it will undertake." The rationale: fear and tedium act as a filter that keeps good ideas open — the antidote is to ask "what problem do I wish someone else would solve?"

## 9. Be relentlessly resourceful

The trait that best predicts a good founder. Its opposite: "Hapless implies passivity. To be hapless is to be battered by circumstances — to let the world have its way with you, instead of having your way with the world" ("Relentlessly Resourceful," 2009). And it must be *relentlessly* resourceful, not merely relentless: "Not merely relentless. That's not enough to make things go your way except in a few mostly uninteresting domains." The rationale: startup obstacles are novel and unpredictable, so a fixed plan is worthless; what wins is adaptive, high-agency improvisation toward the goal.

## 10. Know whether you're default alive or default dead

Every startup should know, in writing, its survival trajectory. On current revenue growth and expenses, do you reach profitability before the money runs out? "Half the founders I talk to don't know whether they're default alive or default dead" ("Default Alive or Default Dead?," 2015). The deadly combination is "the fatal pinch... default dead + slow growth + not enough time to fix it." The rationale: knowing changes the entire conversation — a default-alive company can play offense; a default-dead one must fix product and growth before anything else, because "Hiring too fast is by far the biggest killer of startups that raise money."

## 11. Spend as little money as possible; don't scale before fit

The third leg of the recipe — "spend as little money as possible" ("How to Start a Startup," 2005) — connects directly to default-dead thinking. Founders mistake the large staffs of successful companies for the *cause* of success rather than an effect of it; the corpus warns against hiring your way out of a product problem ("Default Alive or Default Dead?," 2015). Scaling machinery (headcount, paid acquisition, partnerships) before a small group of users loves the product is the classic failure mode.

## 12. Great work at all: curiosity to the frontier, then notice the gaps

Beneath the startup layer runs a general theory of ambitious work. "The work you choose needs to have three qualities: it has to be something you have a natural aptitude for, that you have a deep interest in, and that offers scope to do great work" ("How to Do Great Work," 2023). The method: "choose a field, learn enough to get to the frontier, notice gaps, explore promising ones," and "Curiosity is the key to all four steps." Prestige misleads and earnestness ("The core of being earnest is being intellectually honest") is a source of power. The rationale connects to startup ideas: noticing a gap at the frontier is the same act as noticing a startup-worthy problem.
