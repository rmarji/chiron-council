# Anti-patterns — Peter Thiel (corpus)

Everything the corpus warns against, with the reasoning and the failure it produces. Sources: *Zero to One* (2014) by chapter; CS183 notes (2012) by class number and title. Paraphrase unless quoted.

## Competing in a crowded market

*The pattern:* Treating a bloody, undifferentiated field as a challenge to out-hustle, and taking "the market is huge" as a reason to enter.
*Why the corpus warns against it:* Perfect competition drives economic profit to zero; a company locked in competition has no profit and no room to plan long-term (Zero to One, ch. 3; "Competition Is for Losers," WSJ, Sept 12 2014).
*The failure it produces:* You win the fight and still earn nothing — a "successful" business with no durable profit. The corpus's counsel is to escape the market, not conquer it.

## 1-to-n imitation dressed as innovation

*The pattern:* Copying a proven model ("the X for Y," "the Uber of Z") and calling it a startup.
*Why:* Copying takes the world from 1 to n; it creates no secret and no monopoly. Real value comes from going 0 to 1 (Zero to One, ch. 1; CS183 Class 1).
*The failure:* A me-too product with no defensible edge, thrown straight into competition — the very state the corpus says to avoid.

## Indefinite optimism

*The pattern:* Expecting the future to improve while making no specific plan to build it; favoring optionality, process, and diversification over commitment.
*Why:* An indefinite optimist "wants to become rich but has no concrete vision for how to do so"; the mindset produces financialization and process-over-substance (Zero to One, ch. 6; "The End of the Future," National Review, Oct 3 2011).
*The failure:* Nothing definite gets built. The corpus reads this as the root of Western stagnation and of "well-rounded" résumé culture that keeps every door open and walks through none. "A bad plan is better than no plan."

## Neglecting distribution ("if you build it, they will come")

*The pattern:* Believing a superior product needs no deliberate sales or distribution.
*Why:* "Poor sales rather than bad product is the most common cause of failure"; great selling is designed to be invisible, which is why technical founders undervalue it (Zero to One, ch. 11; CS183 Class 9, "If You Build It, Will They Come?").
*The failure:* A great product nobody buys, because no channel was ever built to reach the customer.

## Lottery-ticket thinking

*The pattern:* Treating success as luck to be diversified against rather than a definite outcome to be engineered; "you can't control your destiny."
*Why:* The corpus rejects the indeterminate worldview; a founder who believes outcomes are random will never commit to a thesis (Zero to One, ch. 6; CS183 Class 13, "You Are Not A Lottery Ticket").
*The failure:* Hedged, diffuse effort that the power law punishes; the diversification that feels safe guarantees mediocrity (links to ch. 7).

## Diversifying into mediocrity (fighting the power law)

*The pattern:* Spreading capital, effort, or attention evenly across many options because it feels prudent.
*Why:* Returns follow a power law — a few bets dominate all the rest combined; only back what can be enormous (Zero to One, ch. 7, "Follow the Money"; CS183 Class 7).
*The failure:* A portfolio of half-hearted bets, none large enough to matter. The corpus says concentrate.

## Incrementalism and "no secrets left"

*The pattern:* Believing all the important things are already known, so only small improvements remain.
*Why:* The belief that there are no secrets is caused by incrementalism, risk-aversion, complacency, and flatness — and it is self-fulfilling: people who don't look for secrets don't find them (Zero to One, ch. 8, "Secrets").
*The failure:* A culture that never attempts anything genuinely new, foreclosing the 0-to-1 opportunities that build great companies.

## Entering a large market head-on

*The pattern:* Being drawn to a giant total addressable market and attacking it broadly from day one.
*Why:* A large market is usually already competitive; the corpus prescribes the opposite — "start with a big share of a small market" and expand outward (Zero to One, ch. 5).
*The failure:* A sliver of a crowded market with no monopoly economics, when a dominated niche would have given a defensible base.

## Sanding down the founder

*The pattern:* Replacing a distinctive, extreme founder with a "professional," well-rounded manager to reduce risk.
*Why:* Founders' value lies in their distinctiveness; the founder's traits sit at extremes and that is a feature (Zero to One, ch. 14; CS183 Class 18, "Founder as Victim, Founder as God").
*The failure:* The company loses the very unusualness that let it see and build something new; it regresses to managerial ordinariness.

## Consultant-nihilist culture

*The pattern:* Hiring mercenaries with no shared mission and optimizing an early team for résumé diversity.
*Why:* Strong culture sits closer to the "cult" end of the spectrum — a team obsessively aligned on a mission others don't yet see; early teams should share a purpose and want to work together ("You Should Run Your Startup Like a Cult," Wired, Sept 2014; CS183 Class 5, "The Mechanics of Mafia").
*The failure:* A team with no cohesion or conviction, unable to execute the hard, unproven 0-to-1 work a startup requires.

## Misattributing the "flying cars" line

*The pattern:* Quoting "we wanted flying cars, instead we got 140 characters" as a Peter Thiel line.
*Why:* It is the tagline of Founders Fund's 2011 manifesto "What Happened to the Future?", written by partner Bruce Gibney — associated with Thiel's stagnation thesis but not authored by him.
*The failure:* A false citation. Attribute it as a firm tagline, never as a Thiel quote.
