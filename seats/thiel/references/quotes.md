# Quotes — Peter Thiel (corpus)

Verified short attributed excerpts only. Wording is checked against the source; anything not verifiable verbatim is marked as a paraphrase and kept out of quotation marks. All page-level references are to *Zero to One* (Crown Business, 2014) by chapter, or Blake Masters' CS183 notes (Stanford, Spring 2012) by class. ## On competition and monopoly

> "All happy companies are different: each one earns a monopoly by solving a unique problem. All failed companies are the same: they failed to escape competition."
— Zero to One, ch. 3 (following the Tolstoy/Anna Karenina inversion).

> "Monopoly is the condition of every successful business."
— Zero to One, ch. 3.

*Paraphrase:* Competition is for losers — perfect competition drives profit to zero, so a great company escapes competition rather than winning it (Zero to One, ch. 3; and the title of the WSJ essay, Sept 12 2014).

## On the contrarian question and secrets

> "What important truth do very few people agree with you on?"
— Zero to One, ch. 1 (the contrarian question).

> "The most contrarian thing of all is not to oppose the crowd but to think for yourself."
— Zero to One, ch. 1.

> "Brilliant thinking is rare, but courage is in even shorter supply than genius."
— Zero to One, ch. 8, "Secrets."

*Paraphrase:* The business form of the contrarian question is "What valuable company is nobody building?" (Zero to One, ch. 1).

## On 0 to 1 and imitation

> "Every moment in business happens only once. The next Bill Gates will not build an operating system. The next Larry Page or Sergey Brin won't make a search engine. And the next Mark Zuckerberg won't create a social network. If you are copying these guys, you aren't learning from them."
— Zero to One, preface / ch. 1.

*Paraphrase:* Horizontal progress (globalization) goes from 1 to n; vertical progress (technology) goes from 0 to 1, and that is where value is created (Zero to One, ch. 1; CS183 Class 1).

## On markets

*Paraphrase:* Start with a big share of a small market, then expand outward — a dominated niche beats a sliver of a huge, competitive market (Zero to One, ch. 5).

*Paraphrase:* It is better to be the last mover — to make the last great development in a market and enjoy years of monopoly profits — than to be the first mover (Zero to One, ch. 5; CS183 Class 4, "The Last Mover Advantage").

## On the future

*Paraphrase:* A bad plan is better than no plan — the corpus's defense of definite optimism over indefinite optimism (Zero to One, ch. 6).

*Paraphrase:* You are not a lottery ticket — success is a definite outcome to be engineered, not luck to be diversified against (Zero to One, ch. 6; CS183 Class 13, "You Are Not A Lottery Ticket").

## On distribution

> "Customers won't come just because you build it."
— Zero to One, ch. 11 (on distribution; the rejection of the "if you build it, they will come" fallacy).

*Paraphrase:* Poor sales rather than bad product is the most common cause of failure; superior sales and distribution can build a monopoly even with no product differentiation (Zero to One, ch. 11; CS183 Class 9, "If You Build It, Will They Come?").

## Not a Thiel quote — attribute correctly

The line "we wanted flying cars, instead we got 140 characters" is the **tagline of Founders Fund's 2011 manifesto "What Happened to the Future?", written by partner Bruce Gibney** — associated with Thiel's stagnation thesis but NOT authored by Thiel. Never present it as a Thiel quotation; cite it as a Founders Fund firm tagline (Gibney, 2011).
