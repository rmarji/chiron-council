# Frameworks — Peter Thiel (corpus)

Every named framework the corpus uses, with when-to-use, concrete steps, and source. Sources: *Zero to One* (2014) by chapter; CS183 notes (2012) by class number and title. Paraphrase unless quoted.

## The Seven Questions (Zero to One's go/no-go test)

*Source:* Zero to One, ch. 13, "Seeing Green."
*When to use:* Deciding whether a business is worth building or backing. The chapter's context is why the cleantech bubble collapsed — most firms answered only one or two of the seven, and getting most of them wrong is fatal even if the mission is noble.
*Steps — answer all seven honestly:*
1. **Engineering** — Can you create breakthrough technology instead of incremental improvement? (10x, not 10%.)
2. **Timing** — Is now the right time to start this particular business?
3. **Monopoly** — Are you starting with a big share of a small market?
4. **People** — Do you have the right team?
5. **Distribution** — Do you have a way to not just create but deliver your product?
6. **Durability** — Will your market position be defensible 10 and 20 years into the future?
7. **Secret** — Have you identified a unique opportunity that others don't see?
*Verdict rule:* You need strong answers to most, and getting several wrong dooms the business. Tesla is the corpus's example of a company that got all seven right.

**Important attribution note:** These seven questions are from the *book*. The CS183 class notes (2012) instead organize the material around a longer list of startup elements — markets, mimesis/competition, secrets, incrementalism, durability, teams, distribution, timing, financing, and luck. Cite the book's seven questions and the class notes' elements *separately*; do not present the seven questions as a CS183 framework.

## The Monopoly Test (four traits + durability)

*Source:* Zero to One, ch. 5, "Last Mover Advantage"; CS183 Class 4.
*When to use:* Deciding whether a market position is a real monopoly or a race to the bottom.
*Steps:*
1. Check for the four traits — **proprietary technology** at least 10x better than the next option, **network effects**, **economies of scale**, **branding**.
2. Test **durability** — will the position survive a decade or two of competition and technological change?
3. Discount current growth rate: a fast grower with no durable trait is a warning, not a monopoly.
4. If a monopoly exists, disguise it (avoid scrutiny); if it doesn't, don't pretend — find or build one.

## Start Small and Monopolize

*Source:* Zero to One, ch. 5.
*When to use:* Choosing which market to enter, especially when tempted by a "huge market."
*Steps:*
1. Identify the smallest market you could plausibly dominate — a specific, underserved niche.
2. Take a big share of that small market to secure monopoly economics.
3. Expand outward into adjacent markets from the base of strength (Amazon: books → everything; Facebook: one campus → the world).
4. Treat entering a large market head-on as a red flag, not an opportunity.

## The Definite-Optimist Plan (the future grid)

*Source:* Zero to One, ch. 6; CS183 Class 13, "You Are Not A Lottery Ticket."
*When to use:* A roadmap that hedges every bet and commits to nothing.
*Steps:*
1. Plot the plan on the 2x2 — determinate/indeterminate x optimist/pessimist.
2. If it sits in indefinite optimism (expect good things, plan nothing), force a move to definite optimism.
3. Name the specific future being built and the sequenced steps to get there.
4. Replace "keep our options open" with one committed bet; remember "a bad plan is better than no plan."

## The Distribution Design (beat the "build it and they will come" fallacy)

*Source:* Zero to One, ch. 11; CS183 Class 9, "If You Build It, Will They Come?"
*When to use:* When a team assumes a superior product sells itself.
*Steps:*
1. Reject the engineer's fallacy — "poor sales rather than bad product is the most common cause of failure."
2. Estimate customer lifetime value (CLV) and pick a channel whose cost per acquisition it can support.
3. Map the channel to deal size: complex/personal sales for high-value deals; distribution/personal sales for mid-value; marketing and viral growth for low-value, high-volume.
4. Build the distribution machine as deliberately as the product; remember great selling is designed to be invisible.

## The Secret Search

*Source:* Zero to One, ch. 8, "Secrets."
*When to use:* Testing whether a business has a defensible basis, or hunting for a new idea.
*Steps:*
1. Reject the premise that there are no secrets left — that belief is caused by incrementalism, risk-aversion, complacency, and flatness.
2. Ask where people aren't looking: important fields nobody studies, and things people aren't allowed to talk about.
3. Distinguish natural secrets (about the physical world) from human secrets (about what people hide).
4. Convert the secret into a business only a few people can build, and tell it only to the people who need to know.

## The Anti-Lottery-Ticket Stance

*Source:* Zero to One, ch. 6; CS183 Class 13.
*When to use:* When success is being treated as luck to hedge against.
*Steps:*
1. Reject "you can't control your destiny" — refuse to treat outcomes as a lottery.
2. Convert an indeterminate bet into a determinate plan with a specific thesis.
3. Concentrate rather than diversify — the power law rewards the one enormous bet, not a spread of hedges (links to ch. 7).
