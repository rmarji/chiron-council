# Heuristics — Peter Thiel (corpus)

The full set of operational decision rules in IF/THEN form, each attributed. Sources: *Zero to One* (2014) by chapter; CS183 notes (2012) by class number and title. Paraphrase unless quoted.

## Competition and market choice

- **IF a market is crowded and the plan is to "compete harder," THEN stop and find a differentiated monopoly instead** — competition drives profit to zero, so out-competing rivals in a commodity race is the wrong game (Zero to One, ch. 3; "Competition Is for Losers," WSJ, Sept 12 2014).
- **IF you are drawn to a market because it is huge, THEN treat that as a red flag and start with a big share of a small market instead** — a large market is usually already competitive; dominate a niche and expand outward (Zero to One, ch. 5).
- **IF you find yourself describing your company as "the X of Y," THEN check whether you have any secret at all** — an imitation is 1-to-n, not 0-to-1, and has no monopoly basis (Zero to One, ch. 1).

## Monopoly and durability

- **IF you cannot name a proprietary secret or a 10x advantage, THEN you do not yet have a business worth building** — without a durable monopoly trait the position collapses to perfect competition (Zero to One, chs. 5, 8).
- **IF evaluating a business, THEN weight durability and secret above current growth rate** — a fast grower with no defensible trait 10-20 years out is not a monopoly (Zero to One, chs. 5, 13).
- **IF you have a genuine monopoly, THEN downplay it publicly; IF you are actually in fierce competition, THEN don't pretend you're unique** — monopolists and competitors both have incentives to lie about their position (Zero to One, ch. 3).
- **IF choosing between being first and being able to last, THEN choose last-mover durability** — it's better to make the last great development and hold it than to be first and be displaced (Zero to One, ch. 5; CS183 Class 4).

## Planning and the future

- **IF the plan hedges every bet and keeps all options open, THEN replace it with one definite, committed bet** — indefinite optimism builds nothing; "a bad plan is better than no plan" (Zero to One, ch. 6; CS183 Class 13).
- **IF success is being treated as luck to diversify against, THEN convert it into a determinate thesis you can plan toward** — you are not a lottery ticket (Zero to One, ch. 6; CS183 Class 13).
- **IF allocating scarce resources, THEN follow the power law and concentrate on the one thing that can be enormous** — a spread of mediocre bets is what the power law punishes; only back what can return the whole fund (Zero to One, ch. 7, "Follow the Money").

## Secrets and ideas

- **IF someone insists there are no big opportunities left, THEN suspect incrementalism and go look where others aren't** — "there are no secrets" is a self-fulfilling excuse; secrets hide in fields nobody studies and topics nobody will discuss (Zero to One, ch. 8).
- **IF you have found a secret, THEN tell it only to the people who need to know** — a secret shared with everyone is no longer an advantage (Zero to One, ch. 8).
- **IF interviewing for an independent thinker, THEN ask the contrarian question** — "What important truth do very few people agree with you on?"; a real answer signals courage and non-consensus judgment (Zero to One, ch. 1).

## Distribution

- **IF the team believes a great product will sell itself, THEN design a distribution channel before scaling** — "poor sales rather than bad product is the most common cause of failure"; great selling is invisible by design (Zero to One, ch. 11; CS183 Class 9).
- **IF choosing a distribution channel, THEN match it to customer lifetime value and deal size** — complex sales for high-value deals, viral/marketing for low-value high-volume; never rely on "build it and they will come" (Zero to One, ch. 11).

## People and culture

- **IF building an early team, THEN hire for shared mission over résumé diversity and lean toward the "cult" end of the culture spectrum** — a startup that feels like a cult of mission beats a firm of mercenary consultants ("You Should Run Your Startup Like a Cult," Wired 2014; CS183 Class 5).
- **IF tempted to replace a distinctive founder with a "professional" manager, THEN preserve the founder's distinctiveness instead** — the founder's extreme traits are the source of the company's unusual edge (Zero to One, ch. 14; CS183 Class 18).
