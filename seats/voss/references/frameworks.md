# Frameworks — the Voss corpus

Every named framework with when-to-use, concrete steps, and source. NSTD = *Never Split the Difference* (2016).

## Mirroring sequence
**When to use:** Early, when caught off guard, or whenever more information is needed without taking a position (NSTD Ch. 2, "Be a Mirror").
**Steps (NSTD Ch. 2 gives this as a four-step sequence):**
1. Use the late-night FM DJ voice.
2. Start with "I'm sorry..."
3. Mirror — repeat the last (or critical) one to three words with a slight upward inflection.
4. Silence. At least four seconds, to let the mirror work its magic.
5. Repeat.
NSTD Ch. 2 demonstrates it at the 1993 Chase Manhattan Bank robbery (Brooklyn), where mirrors kept hostage-taker Chris Watts's partner talking until information leaked and the surrender was negotiated; the actual call recordings are played in the MasterClass lesson "Case Study: Chase Manhattan Bank Robbery" (2019).

## Labeling procedure
**When to use:** The counterpart is emotional, defensive, guarded, or stuck (NSTD Ch. 3).
**Steps:**
1. Detect the emotion (words, tone, body language — or predict it from the situation).
2. Label it aloud beginning "It seems like... / It sounds like... / It looks like..." Never "I'm hearing that" or "I think you feel" — first person centers you and invites resistance (NSTD Ch. 3).
3. Silence. "Once you've thrown out a label, be quiet and listen" (NSTD Ch. 3).
4. Defuse negatives by naming them; reinforce positives by naming them. For anger, label the underlying interest, not the anger itself.
Fill-in labels the corpus recommends: "It seems like ___ is valuable to you," "It seems like you're reluctant to ___" (Black Swan Group training materials).

## The Accusation Audit
**When to use:** Before a hard ask, a renegotiation, or bad news — any time the counterpart plausibly holds negatives about you or your position (NSTD Ch. 4; MasterClass lesson "The Accusation Audit").
**Steps:**
1. In preparation, list every negative thing the other side could think or say about you — be fearless and exhaustive; the worst accusations first.
2. Open by voicing them yourself, slightly stronger than they would: "You're going to think I'm ungrateful. You're going to think I haven't been paying attention..."
3. Do not follow with "but"; let the audit stand, and pause.
4. When the counterpart pushes back on your self-accusations ("no, that's not it..."), the temperature drops — then make the ask.
NSTD Ch. 4 illustrates with the case of Voss's student Anna renegotiating a lucrative government contract by opening with everything the counterpart could accuse her firm of. The MasterClass adds the rule of thumb that an accusation audit almost never goes too far — understating is the common failure.

## Calibrated question construction
**When to use:** Pushed for a concession, at an impasse, needing movement without confrontation, or needing information (NSTD Ch. 7, "Create the Illusion of Control").
**Construction rules (NSTD Ch. 7):**
1. Open with "what" or "how." Avoid "why" — it reads as accusation in every language — except when the defensiveness works for you ("Why would you ever change from the way you've always done things?").
2. No closed questions (yes/no answerable), no leading questions, no verbal firing squad — one question, then silence.
3. Deliver with deference and the DJ voice; a calibrated question delivered aggressively is just a demand.
**The standard bank (NSTD Ch. 7):** "What about this is important to you?" · "How can I help to make this better for us?" · "How would you like me to proceed?" · "What is it that brought us into this situation?" · "How can we solve this problem?" · "What's the objective? What are we trying to accomplish here?" · "How am I supposed to do that?" (the signature no-in-disguise for pushing back on demands).
NSTD Ch. 7's field case: an American kidnapped in Ecuador, freed after his wife, coached to ask "How do we know he's alive?" and "How are we supposed to pay when you won't prove he's OK?", turned the kidnappers into problem-solvers.

## The "That's right" summary
**When to use:** Before any ask; whenever the counterpart is entrenched (NSTD Ch. 5).
**Steps:**
1. Effective pause and minimal encouragers ("yes," "uh-huh") while they talk.
2. Mirror, then label.
3. Paraphrase — their meaning in your words.
4. Summarize = paraphrase + label of the emotional world behind it.
5. Listen for "that's right." If you hear "you're right," you have not landed; keep going.
This is NSTD Ch. 5's rendering of the FBI Behavioral Change Stairway (active listening → empathy → rapport → influence → behavioral change), demonstrated on Abu Sayyaf's Abu Sabaya in the Jeffrey Schilling case (2000).

## The email magic one-liner
**When to use:** Being ignored (NSTD Ch. 4, closing section).
**Step:** Send the one-sentence, no-oriented email: "Have you given up on this project?" Loss framing plus the safety of "no" provokes a fast reply.

## Bend-their-reality toolkit (anchoring and framing)
**When to use:** Discussing numbers and terms (NSTD Ch. 6, "Bend Their Reality").
**Moves:**
1. Anchor their emotions first (an accusation-audit-style "I've got a bad deal for you" preface).
2. Let the other side name a price first when information is scarce — and steel against the anchor.
3. Establish a range citing an external standard ("at top-tier firms this job pays $130–170K"); people hear the favorable end but the range feels less aggressive.
4. Pivot to nonmonetary terms; ask what they could give that's cheap for them and valuable to you.
5. When numbers must be said, use odd, precise, nonround figures.
6. Surprise with an unrelated gift after an extreme anchor — reciprocity plus generosity signal.
7. Beware deadline pressure — deadlines are usually flexible, and the Friday-cash-flow rhythm of Haitian kidnappers (NSTD Ch. 6) is the corpus's proof that hidden deadlines cut both ways.

## The Ackerman model
**When to use:** Hard distributive bargaining, once discovery and empathy work are done (NSTD Ch. 9, "Bargain Hard"; attributed by Voss to Mike Ackerman, an ex-CIA kidnapping-response specialist).
**Steps, with the exact percentages:**
1. Set your target price (your goal).
2. Open at **65%** of target.
3. Plan three raises of decreasing increments: to **85%**, **95%**, and **100%** of target.
4. Before each raise, use tactical empathy and varied ways of saying "no" (calibrated questions, labels) to induce the counterpart to bid against themselves.
5. Make the final (100%) number precise and odd — e.g., $37,893 rather than $38,000 — for credibility.
6. On the final number, throw in a nonmonetary item (possibly one they don't even want) to signal you have reached your limit.
The decreasing increments exploit reciprocity and convey that you are being squeezed dry; the structure lets the counterpart feel they won (NSTD Ch. 9).

## Guarantee-execution protocol
**When to use:** After apparent agreement — because "'Yes' is nothing without 'How'" (NSTD Ch. 8, "Guarantee Execution").
**Steps:**
1. Ask calibrated implementation questions: "How will we know we're on track?" and "How will we address things if we find we're off track?"
2. Apply the **Rule of Three**: get agreement to the same thing three times (commitment, then a label/summary of it, then a "how" about implementation).
3. Watch for behind-the-table players — ask "How does this affect the rest of your team?" and "What do the people not on this call think?"
4. Read the 7-38-55 signals; label incongruence between words and tone.
5. Watch for the Pinocchio effect (wordy, third-person-heavy answers) as a deception flag.

## The Negotiation One Sheet
**When to use:** Preparation for any planned negotiation (NSTD Appendix, "Prepare a Negotiation One Sheet").
**Sections, in order:**
1. **The goal** — best-case outcome, written down (anchor yourself high but legitimately).
2. **Summary** — the known facts stated so well the other side would answer "that's right."
3. **Labels / accusation audit** — three to five prepared labels and the full list of their likely accusations.
4. **Calibrated questions** — three to five prepared "how/what" questions targeting deal-killers and behind-the-table players.
5. **Noncash offers** — the list of nonmonetary items valuable to you or cheap for them.

## Black Swan hunting
**When to use:** Continuously; formally in Ch. 10's protocol (NSTD Ch. 10, "Find the Black Swan").
**Steps:** Assume three to five unknowns exist that would change the deal. Get face time — Black Swans surface in person, not in email. Attend to unguarded moments (before and after the formal meeting, interruptions, odd remarks). When something makes no sense ("crazy" behavior), treat it as a clue: they may be ill-informed, constrained by hidden interests, or operating on other information. Work out the counterpart's "religion" (worldview) and read their similarity cues (NSTD Ch. 10; the 2003 Washington "Tractor Man" standoff is the worked case).
