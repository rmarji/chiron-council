# Heuristics — the Voss corpus

Operational IF/THEN rules extracted from the corpus, each attributed. NSTD = *Never Split the Difference* (2016).

## Opening and tone

- **IF** the exchange is tense, high-stakes, or the counterpart is agitated **THEN** drop into the late-night FM DJ voice — slow, calm, downward inflection — before saying anything of substance (NSTD Ch. 2; MasterClass "Body Language & Speech Patterns").
- **IF** unsure which voice to use **THEN** default to the positive/playful voice; a smile carries through even on the phone (NSTD Ch. 2).
- **IF** entering any planned negotiation **THEN** prepare a Negotiation One Sheet first: goal, summary, 3–5 labels, accusation audit, 3–5 calibrated questions, noncash offers (NSTD Appendix).

## Listening and discovery

- **IF** caught off guard or needing more information **THEN** mirror the last one to three words and go silent for at least four seconds (NSTD Ch. 2).
- **IF** the counterpart says something surprising or nonsensical **THEN** treat it as a Black Swan clue, not craziness — they are constrained, ill-informed, or running on hidden interests (NSTD Ch. 10).
- **IF** the negotiation matters **THEN** get face time; Black Swans surface in person and in unguarded moments before and after the formal session, not over email (NSTD Ch. 10).
- **IF** tempted to talk **THEN** remember the listener, not the talker, is in control; ask, then be quiet (NSTD Ch. 2; Lex Fridman #364, 2023).

## Emotions and empathy

- **IF** the counterpart is emotional, defensive, or entrenched **THEN** label the emotion ("It seems like...") and follow with silence — never rebut the emotion with facts (NSTD Ch. 3).
- **IF** the counterpart is angry **THEN** label the underlying interest or fear, not the anger, and never say "I understand" — demonstrate understanding instead (NSTD Ch. 3; MasterClass).
- **IF** about to deliver a hard ask, a renegotiation, or bad news **THEN** run the accusation audit first: voice their worst assumptions about you before they can (NSTD Ch. 4).
- **IF** the counterpart says "you're right" **THEN** treat it as a dismissal; re-summarize (paraphrase + label) until they say "that's right" (NSTD Ch. 5).
- **IF** you have not yet heard "that's right" **THEN** do not make the ask; the empathy work is unfinished (NSTD Ch. 5).

## "No," "yes," and questions

- **IF** you need engagement from a stalled or silent counterpart **THEN** ask a no-oriented question ("Is now a bad time?", "Is it a ridiculous idea to...?") rather than chasing yes (NSTD Ch. 4).
- **IF** an email is being ignored **THEN** send exactly one line: "Have you given up on this project?" (NSTD Ch. 4).
- **IF** given a demand or pushed for a concession **THEN** ask "How am I supposed to do that?" and let them solve your constraint (NSTD Ch. 7).
- **IF** about to ask "why" **THEN** recast it as "what" or "how" ("What made you choose that?") unless you deliberately want defensiveness (NSTD Ch. 7).
- **IF** you get a "yes" **THEN** distrust it until it survives the Rule of Three — the same commitment confirmed three ways in one conversation (NSTD Ch. 8).

## Numbers and bargaining

- **IF** information is scarce **THEN** let the other side name a number first, and brace against the anchor before it lands (NSTD Ch. 6).
- **IF** you must name a number first **THEN** anchor with a range pegged to an external standard, favorable end at your target (NSTD Ch. 6).
- **IF** hard bargaining begins **THEN** run Ackerman: 65% opening, raises to 85%, 95%, 100% of target, decreasing increments, empathy between rounds, precise odd final figure, nonmonetary item on the close (NSTD Ch. 9).
- **IF** the counterpart drops an extreme anchor **THEN** take the punch without anger, deflect ("I'm sorry, that just doesn't work for me"), and pivot to calibrated questions or nonmonetary terms (NSTD Ch. 9).
- **IF** the counterpart plays the "fair" card **THEN** never concede to it; ask them to explain how you are treating them unfairly, or preempt by inviting them to stop you if they ever feel unfairly treated (NSTD Ch. 6).
- **IF** a deadline is being used to pressure you **THEN** assume it is flexible and self-imposed until proven otherwise; deadlines rarely trigger the feared consequence (NSTD Ch. 6).
- **IF** tempted to meet in the middle to end the discomfort **THEN** stop — no deal is better than a bad deal; reopen discovery instead (NSTD Ch. 6, Ch. 9).

## Execution and verification

- **IF** the deal is "agreed" **THEN** ask "How will we know we're on track?" and "How will we address it if we're off track?" before celebrating — "yes" is nothing without "how" (NSTD Ch. 8).
- **IF** the counterpart's words and tone diverge **THEN** believe the tone (7-38-55) and label the incongruence (NSTD Ch. 8).
- **IF** answers get wordy and third-person-heavy **THEN** raise your deception estimate (Pinocchio effect, NSTD Ch. 8).
- **IF** others influence the decision from behind the table **THEN** ask "How does this affect the rest of your team?" before assuming the room's yes is final (NSTD Ch. 8).
- **IF** closing **THEN** let the counterpart feel they won something on the final round; the last impression is the lasting impression (NSTD Ch. 9).

## Diagnosis

- **IF** the counterpart is silent and methodical **THEN** treat them as an Analyst: give data, tolerate silence, skip the small talk; **IF** relationship-focused and chatty **THEN** an Accommodator: distinguish rapport from commitment; **IF** blunt and forceful **THEN** an Assertive: let them feel heard before expecting them to hear you (NSTD Ch. 9).
- **IF** you are unaware of being in a negotiation **THEN** you are losing it — "the most dangerous negotiation is the one you don't know you're in" (Voss, MasterClass, 2019; Black Swan Group materials).
