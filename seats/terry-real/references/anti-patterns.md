# Anti-Patterns — the Terry Real corpus

Everything Real warns against, with his reasoning and the failure each pattern produces. The centerpiece is the five losing strategies (*The New Rules of Marriage*, Ballantine, 2007; expanded in *Fierce Intimacy*, Sounds True, 2018) — the moves that feel completely justified in the moment and reliably destroy closeness.

## Losing strategy 1: Needing to be right

**The move:** Litigating objective reality — marshaling receipts, timelines, witnesses — to prove your account of events is the true one.
**Why it feels right:** You may actually be factually correct, and the adaptive child experiences being wronged-and-unacknowledged as intolerable.
**The failure it produces:** A verdict instead of a connection. The corpus's blunt framing: "you can be right or you can be married" (*Fierce Intimacy*, 2018). Objective reality has no place in personal relationships; the trial can be won while the marriage is lost (*The New Rules of Marriage*, 2007).
**Counter:** Shift from complaint to request (winning strategy one).

## Losing strategy 2: Controlling your partner

**The move:** Direct coercion (demands, ultimatums, monitoring) or indirect coercion (guilt, manipulation, "helpful" instruction) aimed at making the partner be different.
**Why it feels right:** Often motivated by real anxiety or a real grievance; control feels like problem-solving.
**The failure it produces:** Resistance or resentful compliance — never genuine giving. People do not experience being controlled as love, and the controller reads the resistance as proof more control is needed (*The New Rules of Marriage*, 2007).
**Counter:** Empower each other — "what can I give you to help you give me what I want?" (*The New Rules of Marriage*, 2007).

## Losing strategy 3: Unbridled self-expression

**The move:** Venting — dumping every feeling and judgment on the partner in the name of honesty or authenticity, litigating history, the kitchen-sink fight.
**Why it feels right:** Decades of pop psychology said expressing feelings is intrinsically healthy; withholding feels fake.
**The failure it produces:** The listener gets flooded and defensive; nothing lands. Real is unusually direct here: your partner is not your therapist, and "honesty" without skill or respect is just aggression with a halo (*The New Rules of Marriage*, 2007; *Fierce Intimacy*, 2018).
**Counter:** Speak out with love and savvy — one issue, feedback-wheel form.

## Losing strategy 4: Retaliation

**The move:** Getting even — the cutting remark, withholding affection or sex, the affair, telling the kids, punishing lateness with lateness.
**Why it feels right:** It restores the internal ledger; the adaptive child experiences payback as justice.
**The failure it produces:** Escalation symmetry — each act of justice is the other side's new injury. Real singles out **offending from the victim position** as the signature form: using your legitimate woundedness as a moral license to be grandiose and cruel (*Fierce Intimacy*, 2018; *Us*, 2022). Scorekeeping is retaliation's bookkeeping department — the running tally guarantees the next round.
**Counter:** Respond with generosity (*The New Rules of Marriage*, 2007).

## Losing strategy 5: Withdrawal

**The move:** Stonewalling, the silent treatment, disappearing into work or screens, emotional checkout while physically present — or the unannounced walkout mid-fight.
**Why it feels right:** It reads as maturity ("I'm not fighting, am I?") and it stops the immediate pain.
**The failure it produces:** The pursuing partner escalates to reach you; the withdrawer withdraws further — the classic pursue/withdraw death spiral. Withdrawal is not peace; it is disconnection wearing peace's clothes (*The New Rules of Marriage*, 2007; the pattern is gendered and rooted in male disconnection per *How Can I Get Through to You?*, 2002).
**Counter:** Cherish — move toward, not away; if flooded, take a responsible time-out with a return time instead (*The New Rules of Marriage*, 2007).

## "Constructive" criticism

In a love relationship there is no such thing; criticism of the person (as opposed to a feedback-wheel request about a behavior) is contempt with better lighting. The failure: the criticized partner hears character indictment, defends, and changes nothing (*The New Rules of Marriage*, 2007).

## Harshness as a virtue

The belief that yelling, sarcasm, or brutal bluntness is what finally "gets through." Real's answer: "there is nothing that harshness does that loving firmness doesn't do better" (*Us*, 2022). Harshness produces compliance-with-resentment or counter-attack; firmness inside warmth produces change.

## The one-up stance (grandiosity)

Superiority, contempt, entitlement, "I'm just being honest," correcting and instructing the partner. Real treats this as the most under-diagnosed relational pathology — nobody seeks treatment for what feels good — and traces male grandiosity to the flight from shame (*I Don't Want to Talk About It*, 1997; *Us*, 2022). Failure produced: contempt is intimacy's most reliable solvent, and the grandiose partner is usually the last to know.

## The one-down collapse (shame)

Caving, self-erasure, martyrdom, walking on eggshells, apologizing for existing. Also unrelational, just quieter: the collapsed partner is not present as an equal, and buried resentment leaks out as passive aggression or eventual explosion (*I Don't Want to Talk About It*, 1997; *How Can I Get Through to You?*, 2002 — "as women shut down their needs, they also shut down their sense of pleasure").

## Keeping the peace by avoiding conflict

Suppressing disharmony instead of building the skill of repair. Avoidance is not harmony; it is deferred rupture plus accumulating distance. Real built *Fierce Intimacy* (2018) around the opposite claim: real closeness requires standing up to each other with love.

## Waiting for the partner to change first

Standing on principle — "I'll soften when they do" — keeps the stalemate exactly where it is, since both partners are running the same program. The wise adult moves first toward repair; moving first is strength, not surrender (*Us*, 2022; RLT training stance).

## Treating the defense instead of the depression

Family and clinician focus on the drinking, the rage, the workaholism — the visible defense — while the covert depression underneath goes unaddressed, so the man swaps one defense for another. The failure: relapse and serial symptom-substitution (*I Don't Want to Talk About It*, 1997).

## Confusing walls with boundaries

Numbness, secrecy, and stonewalling framed as "having good boundaries." A wall keeps everything out, including love; a boundary filters. Walled-off is a boundary failure just as much as boundaryless is (*Fierce Intimacy*, 2018; RLT training materials).
