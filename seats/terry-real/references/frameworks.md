# Frameworks — the Terry Real corpus

Every named, operational framework in Real's published work: when to use it, the concrete steps, and the source.

## The feedback wheel

**When to use:** Any time a grievance needs to be raised — the corpus's standard delivery vehicle for complaints, replacing venting, criticism, and mind-reading.
**Origin:** Devised by family therapist Janet Hurley; Real adapted and popularized it ("I invite you to use Janet Hurley's feedback wheel" — *The New Rules of Marriage*, 2007; reprised in *Us*, 2022).
**Steps** — four sentences, spoken from the wise adult, about one specific event:
1. **"This is what I saw / heard."** Behavioral data only — camera-level, no adjectives, no patterns, no "you always."
2. **"This is what I made up about it."** Your interpretation, explicitly owned as your story, not as fact. Real treats this step as the genius of the tool: it converts accusation into disclosure.
3. **"This is how I feel about it."** Emotions (a handful of words), not judgments dressed as feelings.
4. **"This is what I would like."** A concrete, doable, future-facing request — what would help, not a rehash of the offense.
**Post-condition:** After delivering the wheel, let go of the outcome. Your partner gets to respond as a free agent; the wheel earns you a hearing, not compliance (*The New Rules of Marriage*, 2007). Real also instructs: use "and," never "but," because "'but' cancels out whatever came before it" (*The New Rules of Marriage*, 2007).

## The dead-stop contract

**When to use:** For a couple's signature repetitive spiral — the scripted fight where each partner's behavior confirms the other's core negative image (CNI).
**Origin:** *The New Rules of Marriage* (2007).
**Steps:**
1. In a calm moment, each partner identifies the CNI they trigger in the other (e.g., "bully," "flake") and the concrete behaviors that trigger it.
2. Agree on a signal. When one partner flags "you're in my CNI right now," the other comes to a **dead stop — whether or not they agree with the perception.**
3. The flagged partner stops the behavior and offers a brief repair line ("I'm sorry — I don't mean to bully you"), rather than litigating whether the perception is fair.
4. Nothing short of physical safety overrides the contract; honoring your side is unconditional, not contingent on the partner's behavior (*The New Rules of Marriage*, 2007).
**Why it works, per the corpus:** the spiral cannot survive one partner refusing to play their half; arguing about the accuracy of the CNI is itself the spiral.

## The repair protocol

**When to use:** After a rupture, blowup, or cold war — the move from disharmony back to harmony.
**Origin:** *Us* (2022), integrating *The New Rules of Marriage* (2007) tools.
**Steps:**
1. **Wait out the whoosh.** No repair is possible from the adaptive child; self-regulate first (breath, time, self-talk). "How old do I feel right now?" (*Us*, 2022).
2. **Remember love.** Deliberately reconnect with the part of you that cares about this person and wants the relationship — Real names this the first step of repair proper (*Us*, 2022).
3. **Stand "same as."** Drop one-up (righteousness, contempt) and one-down (collapse, groveling) before speaking (*Us*, 2022; *I Don't Want to Talk About It*, 1997).
4. **Own your part cleanly.** Accountability without self-erasure — a specific acknowledgment, not a global confession or a "sorry you feel that way" (*The New Rules of Marriage*, 2007).
5. **Deliver the remainder through the feedback wheel** and end on a request (*The New Rules of Marriage*, 2007).
6. **Receiving side: respond with generosity.** Real's instruction to the listening partner: give your partner what they ask for whenever you can afford to — "you have to help your partner come through for you" (*Us*, 2022; winning strategy three, *The New Rules of Marriage*, 2007).

## The five winning strategies

**When to use:** As the standing replacement set for the five losing strategies (see `anti-patterns.md`), one-for-one.
**Origin:** *The New Rules of Marriage* (2007), Part II.
**Steps / components:**
1. **Shift from complaint to request** — replaces needing to be right. Say what you want next, not what was wrong.
2. **Speak out with love and savvy** — replaces unbridled self-expression. Strategic, respectful honesty (the feedback wheel is the tool).
3. **Respond with generosity** — replaces retaliation. Listen to understand, give what you can, assume goodwill.
4. **Empower each other** — replaces control. Real's formula: "what can I give you to help you give me what I want?" (*The New Rules of Marriage*, 2007).
5. **Cherish** — replaces withdrawal. Actively nourish, appreciate, and protect what you have; gratitude as a practice, not a mood.

## Boundary work: walls vs boundaries

**When to use:** Enmeshment (no protection — the partner's moods flood straight in; agreeing to things you resent) or walled-off distance (protection so total no one gets in), and any anger that keeps escaping as contempt.
**Origin:** RLT training materials and *Fierce Intimacy* (2018), adapting Pia Mellody's boundary model; *How Can I Get Through to You?* (2002) on walls.
**Steps / components:**
1. **The protective boundary** filters what comes in: you can hear your partner's anger or opinion of you, evaluate it, take in what is true, and leave the rest — without either absorbing it (boundarylessness) or refusing all of it (a wall).
2. **The containing boundary** governs what goes out: you feel everything and say what serves the relationship, with full respect — no dumping, no contempt, no "unbridled self-expression."
3. The diagnostic: a **wall** blocks everything and kills intimacy; a **boundary** is selectively permeable and makes intimacy safe. Being boundaryless and being walled-off are both boundary failures (*Fierce Intimacy*, 2018; RLT training materials).

## The responsible time-out

**When to use:** Flooding — when either partner is too whooshed to stay in full respect.
**Origin:** *The New Rules of Marriage* (2007).
**Steps:**
1. Call it on yourself, not the partner ("I'm getting reactive; I need twenty minutes"), with a named return time — a time-out, not a walkout.
2. During the break, regulate; do not build your case.
3. Return when you said you would and re-engage. The return is what distinguishes the tool from the losing strategy of withdrawal (*The New Rules of Marriage*, 2007).

## Loving confrontation ("joining through the truth")

**When to use:** When a partner's behavior genuinely must change — addiction-adjacent behavior, chronic disrespect, broken agreements — and soft approaches have failed.
**Origin:** RLT training materials; demonstrated throughout *How Can I Get Through to You?* (2002) and Real's public sessions (e.g., The Tim Ferriss Show #810, 2025).
**Steps:** Name the behavior and its consequence bluntly; hold the frame that you say it *because* you value the person; pair every confrontation with an offered path back into connection. Accountability delivered inside warmth, not instead of it. "There is nothing that harshness does that loving firmness doesn't do better" (*Us*, 2022).
