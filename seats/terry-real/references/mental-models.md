# Mental Models — the Terry Real corpus

Every named model or concept in Real's published work, with its definition, origin, and when it applies.

## Adaptive child vs wise adult

**Definition:** The adaptive child is the part of the self built in childhood to survive an imperfect environment — the placater, the controller, the avoider, the counter-attacker. The wise adult is the present-day, prefrontal self who can hold complexity, stay warm, and choose a response. Under perceived relational threat, control passes from wise adult to adaptive child (*Us*, 2022). Real characterizes the adaptive child as black-and-white, rigid, and certain — a child's version of an adult — while the wise adult is nuanced and flexible (*Us*, 2022; The Tim Ferriss Show #810, 2025).
**Origin:** *Us* (2022), building on his earlier "inner child / functional adult" language inherited from Pia Mellody's work at The Meadows, where Real trained.
**When it applies:** Any moment of reactivity. Diagnostic question the corpus uses: "how old do I feel right now?"

## The "whoosh"

**Definition:** Real's term for the felt moment of being triggered — the fast, whole-body surge that sweeps you from the wise adult into the adaptive child before conscious thought catches up. Central claim: the whoosh never stops happening, no matter how much therapy you have; mastery is shortening the time between whoosh and recovery (*Us*, 2022).
**When it applies:** Flooding, sudden rage, sudden collapse, the urge to fire off the text. The model converts "don't get triggered" (impossible) into "catch the whoosh and wait" (learnable).

## The grandiosity–shame seesaw (one-up / one-down)

**Definition:** Self-esteem disturbance has two poles: grandiosity (one-up: superiority, contempt, entitlement) and shame (one-down: worthlessness, collapse, self-erasure). People oscillate between them — often within a single fight — and both are unrelational. Health is the level position, "same as" (*I Don't Want to Talk About It*, 1997; *Us*, 2022).
**Origin:** *I Don't Want to Talk About It* (1997), where "the flight from shame into grandiosity" is identified as the engine of male covert depression; developed via Pia Mellody's esteem framework.
**When it applies:** Whenever contempt, condescension, martyrdom, or self-flagellation shows up. Real's field-defining observation: therapy over-serves shame and under-serves grandiosity, because grandiosity feels good to its owner (*Us*, 2022; The Tim Ferriss Show #810, 2025).

## Harmony – disharmony – repair

**Definition:** The three-phase cycle all close relationships loop through. Harmony is closeness; disharmony is rupture; repair is the return. The cycle is normal and endless; dysfunction is getting stuck in disharmony or faking harmony by avoidance (*Us*, 2022; *Fierce Intimacy*, 2018).
**When it applies:** Reframing any rupture. It moves the goal from "stop fighting" to "get fluent at repair," and it normalizes the rupture itself, which lowers shame enough to make repair possible.

## The five losing strategies

**Definition:** The five moves that feel justified in conflict and reliably destroy closeness: (1) needing to be right, (2) controlling your partner, (3) unbridled self-expression, (4) retaliation, (5) withdrawal (*The New Rules of Marriage*, 2007; expanded at length in *Fierce Intimacy*, 2018).
**When it applies:** As the corpus's primary diagnostic lens on any recurring fight. Full treatment in `anti-patterns.md`; counters in `frameworks.md` (the five winning strategies).

## Core negative image (CNI)

**Definition:** The exaggerated, worst-case caricature each partner carries of the other — "a controlling tyrant," "a selfish child" — assembled from real data but distorted into a cartoon. In escalation, each partner behaves in exactly the way that confirms the other's CNI, producing the CNI-meets-CNI spiral (*The New Rules of Marriage*, 2007).
**When it applies:** Recurring fights that feel scripted. Knowing your partner's CNI of you tells you precisely which behaviors will detonate; the dead-stop contract (see `frameworks.md`) is the interrupt.

## Psychological patriarchy and the intimacy gap

**Definition:** The gender system as an internalized psychology, not just a politics: qualities coded feminine (vulnerability, tenderness, dependency) are devalued in everyone, and qualities coded masculine (invulnerability, dominance) are inflated. Boys undergo what Real calls the "normal traumatization" of being shamed out of connection; girls learn to mute honest self-assertion to preserve relationships. The result is the intimacy gap — women asking for more relationship than men were raised to deliver (*How Can I Get Through to You?*, 2002, developed with Carol Gilligan; *I Don't Want to Talk About It*, 1997).
**When it applies:** Gendered stalemates — the pursuing/critical partner and the stonewalling/avoidant partner — and any case where a man's "independence" is doing the work of disconnection.

## Covert vs overt male depression

**Definition:** Overt depression is acknowledged and felt. Covert depression is masked by externalizing defenses — alcohol, work, rage, affairs, screens — that self-medicate the mood while hiding it from the man himself. The defense is what the family sees; the depression is what drives it (*I Don't Want to Talk About It*, 1997).
**When it applies:** A partner (typically male) who "isn't depressed" but drinks, rages, or disappears into work. The model redirects attention from the behavior to the pain underneath it.

## Relational esteem vs self-esteem

**Definition:** Self-esteem is how you hold your own worth ("your capacity to recognize your worth and value" as inherent, not performance-based — *The New Rules of Marriage*, 2007). Relational esteem is a distinct capacity: prizing the relationship itself, the "us," as a thing you belong to and act on behalf of (*Us*, 2022).
**When it applies:** Decisions framed as "me vs you." The model adds a third stakeholder — the relationship — and asks what *it* needs.

## Us consciousness vs you-and-me consciousness

**Definition:** You-and-me consciousness is the adversarial, individualist default: two separate selves scoring wins. Us consciousness is ecological: you live inside the relationship the way you live inside a biosphere, so harming it is self-harm and "winning" against your partner is losing (*Us*, 2022).
**When it applies:** Score-keeping, ultimatums, zero-sum negotiations. Master reframe of *Us*: move from "me versus you" to "us versus the problem."

## Second consciousness / relational mindfulness

**Definition:** The trained observer-capacity that notices the whoosh while it is happening and interposes a choice — Real calls the practice relational mindfulness: using the relationship itself, in real time, as the meditation object (*Us*, 2022; RLT training materials).
**When it applies:** The gap between trigger and response. This is the mechanism by which every other model becomes usable under fire.

## Normal marital hatred

**Definition:** Real's deliberately shocking name for the recurrent moments in every long-term relationship when you look at your partner and feel fury, contempt, or "get me out of here." His claim: these moments are universal, survivable, and not evidence of a broken marriage — "everyone gets to go crazy in long-term relationships, but you have to take turns" (*Us*, 2022).
**When it applies:** Catastrophizing after a bad stretch ("maybe we're just wrong for each other"). Normalizing the hatred is itself a repair move.

## Relational heroism

**Definition:** The moment-level unit of change: when every fiber pulls toward the inherited, destructive move and the person deliberately performs the connecting move instead. Real frames recovery not as insight but as an accumulation of these small heroic reversals (*I Don't Want to Talk About It*, 1997).
**When it applies:** Making change concrete. The question is never "have you healed?" but "what did you do at the moment of choice?"

## Carried shame and carried feelings

**Definition:** Feelings and shame that were never the child's own — a parent's rage, a parent's disowned worthlessness — absorbed in childhood and carried into adulthood as one's own emotional weather (*I Don't Want to Talk About It*, 1997, drawing on Pia Mellody).
**When it applies:** Reactions disproportionate to the trigger; multigenerational patterns ("the unresolved pain of previous generations operates in families like emotional debt" — *I Don't Want to Talk About It*, 1997).
