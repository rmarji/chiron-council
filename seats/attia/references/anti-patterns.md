# Anti-patterns — the Peter Attia corpus

Everything the published corpus warns against, with its reasoning and the failure the pattern produces. Sources: *Outlive* (Peter Attia with Bill Gifford, Harmony, 2023) by chapter; named *Drive* episodes; peterattiamd.com. Study aid; not medical advice.

## Waiting for a diagnosis (reactive medicine)

*The pattern:* Doing nothing until symptoms or an event appear, then treating. *Why the corpus opposes it:* The Four Horsemen develop silently over decades; by the time they are symptomatic, much of the damage is done and options narrow. Medicine 2.0's reactive posture is structurally too late for chronic disease (*Outlive*, ch. 2-3). *Failure it produces:* A heart attack, a late-stage cancer, or a dementia diagnosis that a decade of earlier action might have delayed or prevented.

## Optimizing lifespan while ignoring healthspan

*The pattern:* Chasing more years without regard to whether those years are livable — or, conversely, obsessing over biomarkers while neglecting quality of life. *Why the corpus opposes it:* Longevity is the balance of lifespan and healthspan; extending one at the expense of the other misses the point (*Outlive*, ch. 1). *Failure it produces:* A long final decade spent frail, dependent, or miserable.

## Treating "normal labs" as "optimized"

*The pattern:* Accepting standard reference ranges ("your cholesterol is a little high but okay") as evidence you are fine. *Why the corpus opposes it:* Reference ranges are population norms in a sick population, not longevity targets; standard LDL-C in particular can mask atherogenic risk that apoB would reveal (Drive #229, 2022). *Failure it produces:* False reassurance while risk quietly compounds.

## One-size-fits-all diets and named-diet tribalism

*The pattern:* Adopting keto, vegan, carnivore, or any branded diet as universally correct. *Why the corpus opposes it:* The claim that one diet works for every person is "absolutely incorrect"; what matters is the individual's metabolic response, measured with their own data (*Outlive*, ch. 15). *Failure it produces:* Following a diet that harms your specific biomarkers because it worked for someone else.

## Reaching for supplements and "anti-aging" pills first

*The pattern:* Buying a supplement stack or chasing the latest longevity molecule before doing the behavioral work. *Why the corpus opposes it:* Exogenous molecules are the fifth and last tactical domain, graded by evidence; most of the hyped ones (resveratrol, many NAD precursors) lack strong human evidence, and even the promising ones (rapamycin) are unproven for healthy-human longevity (AMA #35, 2022; #272, 2023). *Failure it produces:* Spending money and attention on marginal or useless interventions while neglecting exercise, sleep, and nutrition.

## Neglecting strength and stability

*The pattern:* Doing only cardio, or no resistance training, and ignoring the foundational stability work. *Why the corpus opposes it:* Muscle mass, strength, and stability decline with age and are what protect against falls, frailty, and loss of independence in the Marginal Decade — "never has a 90-year-old wished for less muscle" (*Outlive*, ch. 11). *Failure it produces:* A frail, fall-prone final decade even in someone with decent aerobic fitness.

## Under-training relative to the far-future goal

*The pattern:* Training only to current comfort rather than back-casting from the Marginal Decade. *Why the corpus opposes it:* Because capacity falls with age, the target you set today must be high enough that a usable surplus remains in your 80s and 90s (*Outlive*, ch. 11; Drive #261, 2023). *Failure it produces:* Arriving at old age below the functional threshold for the tasks you wanted to keep doing.

## Cutting sleep to be productive

*The pattern:* Treating sleep as expendable time to trade for work. *Why the corpus opposes it:* Chronic short sleep degrades glucose regulation, cardiovascular health, immune function, and cognition, and impairs overnight clearance of beta-amyloid (Drive #47-49, 2019; #126, 2020). *Failure it produces:* Metabolic and cognitive harm that no amount of exercise or diet fully offsets.

## Ignoring emotional health

*The pattern:* Optimizing the body relentlessly while neglecting distress, relationships, and meaning. *Why the corpus opposes it:* Physical and emotional health are tightly linked, and a long, fit life lived in misery defeats the entire purpose; Attia treats emotional health as foundational and spends about as much time on it as on exercise (*Outlive*, ch. 17). *Failure it produces:* A physically healthy person whose life — and often whose relationships — collapse from within.

## False certainty and overconfident numbers

*The pattern:* Presenting individual health decisions as if the evidence gives clean, certain answers. *Why the corpus opposes it:* In biology "the best we can hope for is reducing our uncertainty"; population-average evidence rarely maps cleanly onto one person (*Outlive*, ch. 3). *Failure it produces:* Overconfident interventions that ignore individual variation and the real error bars.

## Self-prescribing as a substitute for a physician

*The pattern:* Using a framework or podcast as a replacement for clinical care — self-ordering drugs, self-interpreting labs as treatment. *Why the corpus opposes it:* Testing, screening, and treatment are decisions to make *with* a doctor who has your full context; the published work supplies questions, not prescriptions (*Outlive*, throughout). *Failure it produces:* Harm from unsupervised interventions and missed context only a clinician would catch.
