# Mental Models — Charlie Munger

Every named model in the Munger corpus: definition, origin, and when it applies. The primary sources are "A Lesson on Elementary, Worldly Wisdom" (USC Marshall, 1994; Talk Two in Poor Charlie's Almanack) and "The Psychology of Human Misjudgment" (talks 1992-1995 including Harvard Law School 1995, revised as Talk Eleven in Poor Charlie's Almanack, 2005).

## The latticework of mental models

Definition: a working inventory of the big ideas from all major disciplines, held simultaneously and checked against every problem. "80 or 90 important models will carry about 90% of the freight in making you a worldly-wise person" (USC, 1994). Origin: Munger's critique of academic siloing — "all the wisdom of the world is not to be found in one little academic department." Applies whenever a problem is being solved with the one tool the solver happens to know; the latticework is the antidote to the man-with-a-hammer tendency (USC, 1994).

## Inversion

Definition: solve problems backward — specify how to guarantee failure, then avoid it. Origin: mathematician Carl Jacobi's "man muss immer umkehren," adopted explicitly by Munger (Poor Charlie's Almanack; Harvard School commencement, 1986). Applies to goal-setting, plan review, and any question phrased "how do I succeed?" See `frameworks.md` for the procedure.

## Circle of competence and the three baskets

Definition: the honestly bounded domain of genuine understanding; everything else is sorted into "out" or, most often, "too hard." Origin: USC 1994 talk on playing games where one has an aptitude edge; restated at Caltech 2020 ("I put them in my 'too hard pile'"). Applies before any commitment of capital, time, or reputation. Corollary: knowing what you don't know is more useful than being brilliant (Poor Charlie's Almanack).

## Two-track analysis

Definition: analyze every decision twice — track one, the rational factors that genuinely govern the interests involved; track two, the subconscious psychological tendencies distorting the analyst's own conclusions and those of others. Origin: Poor Charlie's Almanack (Kaufman's synthesis of Munger's stated method). Applies to every consequential judgment; most people run only track one.

## Lollapalooza effects

Definition: extreme, nonlinear outcomes produced when several tendencies or forces act in the same direction at once — the confluence, not any single cause, does the damage or creates the result. Origin: Munger's coinage in "The Psychology of Human Misjudgment" (Talk Eleven). Canonical illustrations: Tupperware parties and open-outcry auctions (several biases stacked deliberately), Coca-Cola's global compounding (Talk Four, "Practical Thought About Practical Thought?", 1996), and cult conversions. Applies whenever an effect looks disproportionate to any visible single cause — look for stacked tendencies.

## The psychology of human misjudgment — the 25 tendencies

Munger's full catalog of standard cognitive malfunctions (Talk Eleven, Poor Charlie's Almanack, 2005 revision; earlier versions delivered 1992-1995 including Harvard Law School, 1995). Each is a model: named, predictable, exploitable by others, and checkable.

1. **Reward and Punishment Superresponse** — incentives dominate behavior and cognition; incentive-caused bias makes decent people drift into rationalized bad conduct (FedEx night shift; Xerox's Joe Wilson finding inferior machines outsold better ones because commissions favored them).
2. **Liking/Loving Tendency** — people ignore faults of, and comply with, those they like or love; admiration also distorts.
3. **Disliking/Hating Tendency** — the mirror image; distorts facts to facilitate hatred and blocks fair weighing of disliked sources.
4. **Doubt-Avoidance Tendency** — the brain resolves uncertainty by deciding quickly; puzzlement and stress trigger premature conclusions.
5. **Inconsistency-Avoidance Tendency** — the mind resists change to prior conclusions, loyalties, and identities; "the human mind is a lot like the human egg: when one sperm gets in, it shuts down" (paraphrase-stable, Poor Charlie's Almanack). Chains of habit are too light to be felt until too heavy to be broken (attributed within Talk Eleven).
6. **Curiosity Tendency** — the constructive counterforce; curiosity helps destroy the effects of other tendencies.
7. **Kantian Fairness Tendency** — expectation of, and rage at violations of, reciprocal fair conduct.
8. **Envy/Jealousy Tendency** — "it is not greed that drives the world, but envy" (Talk Eleven; restated Daily Journal, 2023).
9. **Reciprocation Tendency** — automatic returning of favors and disfavors; exploited by concession-extraction and gifts; antidote is deferring reaction.
10. **Influence-from-Mere-Association** — quality assumed from price, messengers blamed for messages, past success assumed to repeat because of associated trivia.
11. **Simple, Pain-Avoiding Psychological Denial** — reality distorted until bearable; strongest around death, addiction, and loss.
12. **Excessive Self-Regard Tendency** — overappraisal of one's own conclusions, possessions (endowment effect), and picks; "the man who can't fix it" hires badly by being sold well.
13. **Overoptimism Tendency** — "what a man wishes, that also will he believe" (Demosthenes, quoted in Talk Eleven); excess optimism is the normal state even after pain.
14. **Deprival-Superreaction Tendency** — losses hurt far more than equivalent gains please; drives gambling near-misses, New Coke rage, and doubling-down to get even.
15. **Social-Proof Tendency** — thinking and acting as observed peers do; strongest under puzzlement and stress; explains bubbles, corporate herding, and bystander inaction.
16. **Contrast-Misreaction Tendency** — judgments anchor on what was just seen, not on absolutes; small steps ratchet people into disaster (the frog-boiling error class).
17. **Stress-Influence Tendency** — light stress improves performance; heavy stress causes dysfunction and even flipped loyalties (Pavlov's flood-stressed dogs, Talk Eleven).
18. **Availability-Misweighing Tendency** — the mind overweights what is vivid, recent, and easily recalled; "an idea or a fact is not worth more merely because it is easily available" (Talk Eleven, paraphrase-stable).
19. **Use-It-or-Lose-It Tendency** — unpracticed skills attrite; the checklist and deliberate practice are the antidotes.
20. **Drug-Misinfluence Tendency** — chemical dependency degrades cognition and morals with denial attached.
21. **Senescence-Misinfluence Tendency** — age-driven decay, partially offset by continuous learning and practice.
22. **Authority-Misinfluence Tendency** — automatic deference to hierarchy, including to wrong or misunderstood orders (Milgram, and the copilot-error flight cases, Talk Eleven).
23. **Twaddle Tendency** — humans emit and tolerate meaningless talk; serious work requires keeping twaddle-prone people far from it (the honeybee dancing gibberish when the nectar is straight up, Talk Eleven).
24. **Reason-Respecting Tendency** — compliance and learning both improve when reasons are given, even weak ones (the Xerox line-cutting study cited in Talk Eleven); therefore always say why.
25. **Lollapalooza Tendency** — the confluence of multiple tendencies acting in one direction, producing extreme outcomes (see above).

## Incentive-caused bias

Singled out because Munger ranked it first in power. Definition: cognition drifts, usually unconsciously, toward conclusions that serve the thinker's incentives — the agent believes the rationalization. Origin: Talk Eleven, tendency 1. Applies to salespeople, consultants, bankers, managers presenting projections, and the analyst's own conclusions ("especially fear professional advice when it is especially good for the advisor," Talk Eleven).

## Man-with-a-hammer syndrome

Definition: "to the man with only a hammer, every problem looks like a nail" (USC, 1994). A specialist's single model gets overapplied to everything. Applies when evaluating expert advice: ask what tool the expert sells before accepting the diagnosis.

## Elementary mathematics: compound interest, permutations, decision trees

The 1994 USC talk names the hard-core mathematical base of the latticework: compound interest ("one of the most important models there is on earth," paraphrase-stable), and the elementary probability of Fermat and Pascal — permutations, combinations, and decision-tree thinking. Applies to all investing (compounding), and to any decision under uncertainty (expected value via decision trees). Munger's claim: the Fermat/Pascal system mirrors how the world actually works, and not knowing it makes one "a one-legged man in an ass-kicking contest" (USC, 1994 — Munger used the same figure for those without mental models generally).

## Accounting — and its limits

Double-entry accounting (origin credited to Venice) is a foundational business model, but the corpus insists on knowing its limits: depreciation schedules, EBITDA, and projections make figures look precise while embedding fiction (USC, 1994; Berkshire meeting remarks on EBITDA — see `anti-patterns.md`).

## Scale economics and its diseconomies

From USC 1994: advantages of scale include cost spreading (Ivory soap advertising on early TV), social-proof reinforcement (ubiquity begets trust), and geographic density (chain stores). The countervailing model: bureaucracy as a diseconomy of scale — big, slow, territorial (Munger's contrast of GM and smaller rivals, and praise of the "fanatic" small operator). Applies to competitive analysis in both directions.

## The surfing model

Definition: when a new technology or trend forms a wave, the operator who catches it early and stays on can ride for a very long time (National Cash Register under Patterson is Munger's example, USC 1994). Applies to evaluating first-mover situations — and to recognizing that missing the wave is fatal only for the surfer, not the passenger who simply avoids buying the losers.

## Moats — durable competitive advantage

Definition: structural protections (brand, scale, network, regulation, switching costs) that keep a business's high returns from being competed away. The corpus test, from Berkshire meeting remarks: explain exactly why the good results exist now, then identify the forces that could stop them. See `frameworks.md` for the evaluation procedure.

## Mr. Market

Origin: Ben Graham (The Intelligent Investor), retained in the Munger corpus as the one indispensable Graham idea even after Munger moved past cigar-butt investing: quotations are offers from a manic-depressive counterparty, to be exploited or ignored, never obeyed (Berkshire meeting remarks; Poor Charlie's Almanack).

## Opportunity cost as the universal filter

Definition: every use of capital or time is measured against the best alternative actually available; "opportunity cost is a huge filter in life" (paraphrase-stable, Berkshire meeting remarks). Applies to portfolio decisions (the best existing holding is the hurdle for any new idea), hiring, and time allocation.

## Febezzlement

Definition: Munger's extension of Galbraith's "bezzle" — the functional equivalent of embezzlement created when undisclosed value-destruction (e.g., excessive money-management costs on foundation assets) leaves everyone temporarily feeling richer. Origin: "Philanthropy Round Table" address (November 2000; Talk Seven, Poor Charlie's Almanack). Applies to any system where fees and marks create phantom wealth.

## Autocatalysis

Definition: borrowed from chemistry — a process whose output accelerates its own further output. Munger applied it to Coca-Cola's compounding (each solved problem creating the conditions for the next gain) in "Practical Thought About Practical Thought?" (1996, Talk Four). Applies when hunting for businesses whose growth feeds itself.

## Granny's Rule

Definition: force the desired behavior by sequencing — carrots after vegetables; unpleasant necessary tasks before pleasant ones. Origin: Talk Eleven (reward/punishment section), where Munger recommends applying it to oneself daily. Applies to personal productivity and to designing incentive systems.

## Backup systems and margin of safety

Definition: engineering's redundancy and breakpoint models imported into decision-making — assume components fail, build slack. Origin: USC 1994 (engineering models in the latticework) combined with Graham's margin of safety, which the corpus treats as the bedrock investing rule (Poor Charlie's Almanack, Investing Principles Checklist). Applies to position sizing, leverage decisions, and any plan whose failure is expensive.
