# Core Principles — Charlie Munger

The full catalog of core beliefs in the Munger corpus, each with its rationale and source. Third-person analytic register; direct quotations only where the wording is verified (see `quotes.md`).

## Invert, always invert

Munger borrowed the maxim from the 19th-century mathematician Carl Gustav Jacob Jacobi — "man muss immer umkehren" ("one must always invert") — and made it the organizing move of his thinking (Poor Charlie's Almanack, Talk Two, USC 1994). Many hard problems are best solved backward: failure modes are concrete and enumerable, while success factors are diffuse. His 1986 Harvard School (now Harvard-Westlake) commencement address is the canonical demonstration — instead of prescribing a good life, he prescribed guaranteed misery (envy, resentment, unreliability, ignoring the lessons of others' failures, going down and staying down after adversity, ignoring disconfirming evidence) and told graduates to avoid the prescriptions (Poor Charlie's Almanack, Talk One). The rationale: inversion converts a vague ambition into a checklist of avoidable errors. The companion epigram, "All I want to know is where I'm going to die, so I'll never go there," is attributed to Munger across Berkshire meeting lore and Poor Charlie's Almanack.

## Avoid stupidity over seeking brilliance

The corpus holds that consistently not being stupid compounds better than intermittent brilliance. Munger's verified formulation: "It is remarkable how much long-term advantage people like us have gotten by trying to be consistently not stupid, instead of trying to be very intelligent" (Poor Charlie's Almanack). At Caltech in December 2020 he restated it operationally: "I just try to avoid being stupid. I have a way of handling a lot of problems — I put them in my 'too hard pile,' and just leave them there" (Caltech Distinguished Alumnus conversation, 2020). In his final CNBC interview he generalized it to life: "Avoid crazy at all costs. Crazy is way more common than you think" (CNBC, Becky Quick, November 2023). Rationale: most damage traces to a small set of repeatable mistakes — overpaying, overconfidence, leverage, ignoring incentives, acting outside competence — and removing them is tractable in a way that manufacturing genius is not.

## Incentives rule everything

Incentive-caused bias leads Munger's catalog of psychological tendencies. In "The Psychology of Human Misjudgment" (Harvard Law School, 1995; revised as Talk Eleven, Poor Charlie's Almanack, 2005) he stated that he had been in the top 5% of his age cohort in understanding incentive power all his life, and had underestimated it all his life anyway. His verified maxim: "Never, ever, think about something else when you should be thinking about the power of incentives" (Talk Eleven). The FedEx night-shift case is his standard illustration: packages moved on time only after workers were paid per shift instead of per hour (Talk Eleven). Corollary for advice-taking: "especially fear professional advice when it is especially good for the advisor" (Talk Eleven, antidotes to incentive-caused bias). The widely quoted "Show me the incentive and I will show you the outcome" traces to the 1995 talk.

## Circle of competence

Competence that cannot be bounded is not competence. The corpus position, from the 1994 USC talk onward: "you have to figure out what your own aptitudes are... you're going to have to play within your own circle of competence" (paraphrase-stable, Talk Two). What matters is not the circle's size but knowing exactly where its edge lies — the losses come from confident action just outside the border, where things feel familiar but are not understood. The operational form is the three-basket sort Munger described repeatedly: in, out, and too hard, with "too hard" the largest basket (Berkshire meetings; Caltech, 2020).

## A latticework of mental models

From the 1994 USC Marshall talk ("A Lesson on Elementary, Worldly Wisdom"): wisdom is not isolated facts but facts hung on a latticework of theory, and "80 or 90 important models will carry about 90% of the freight in making you a worldly-wise person." The models must come from multiple disciplines — mathematics, physics, biology, psychology, engineering, economics — "because all the wisdom of the world is not to be found in one little academic department" (Talk Two). Rationale: single-model thinkers torture reality to fit their tool ("man with a hammer"); multi-model thinkers let reality select the tool.

## Quality over bargain price

A great business at a fair price is superior to a fair business at a great price (Poor Charlie's Almanack). Munger's arithmetic argument, from the 1994 USC talk: over the long run, a stock's return converges on the underlying business's return on capital — a business earning 18% on capital held for decades produces a fine result even at an expensive-looking entry price, while a 6% business produces a poor one however cheaply bought (Talk Two). Buffett credited this Munger-driven shift away from Ben Graham's cigar-butt bargains as the blueprint for Berkshire: it drove the See's Candies purchase (1972) and everything after (Buffett's shareholder letters; Poor Charlie's Almanack).

## Patience and concentration

"The big money is not in the buying and the selling, but in the waiting" (Poor Charlie's Almanack). Great opportunities are rare; when the world offers one, the wise bet heavily, and otherwise they do nothing (Talk Two, USC 1994, on the Kelly-like logic of infrequent mispriced bets). The corpus treats deliberate inactivity as a position, not a failure to act — "sit-on-your-hands investing" (Wesco meeting remarks). Munger considered three holdings sufficient diversification for a competent investor; he stated at Daily Journal meetings that his own family wealth sat substantially in three positions — Berkshire, Costco, and Li Lu's partnership (Daily Journal Q&A, 2017-2021).

## Deserved trust and reliability

From the 2007 USC Gould commencement: "the safest way to get what you want is to try and deserve what you want. It's such a simple idea — it's the golden rule." The same address holds that "the highest form which civilization can reach is a seamless web of deserved trust" — not procedure, but reliable people correctly trusting one another. Reliability is the entry-level virtue: in the 1986 Harvard School address, unreliability is listed among the surest prescriptions for misery. Faithful performance of what is undertaken, Munger argued, can carry a person of moderate gifts past brilliant but undisciplined rivals (USC Gould, 2007).

## Lifelong learning and the learning machine

"Without lifetime learning, you people are not going to do very well" (USC Gould, 2007, paraphrase-stable). Munger described Buffett as a "learning machine" whose skills visibly compounded after age 65, and prescribed going to bed each night a little wiser than that morning (USC Gould, 2007; Poor Charlie's Almanack). The mechanism is compound interest applied to knowledge: small daily increments, held for decades, dominate.

## Rationality as a moral duty

"My life has been dedicated rather intensely to rationality" (Caltech, 2020). Across Daily Journal meetings Munger framed rationality not as a technique but as an ethical obligation — a duty to be as un-fooled as one can manage, because self-deception injures others as well as oneself (Daily Journal Q&A; Poor Charlie's Almanack). Two-track analysis (rational factors first, then the subconscious distortions of one's own judgment) is the working method this duty produces.

## Equanimity under drawdown

From the 2009 BBC interview: an investor unwilling to react with equanimity to a 50% market price decline two or three times a century deserves the mediocre result they will get (paraphrase-stable; BBC, 2009). Volatility of quotation is not risk; permanent loss of capital and inadequate return are (Berkshire meeting remarks). The temperament prior is upstream of every analytical one: without it, none of the rest is usable.

## Envy is the enemy, not greed

"The world is not driven by greed. It's driven by envy" (Daily Journal annual meeting, February 2023). Munger classed envy as the one deadly sin with no upside at all — the sin you cannot possibly have any fun at (Berkshire meeting remarks, paraphrase-stable) — and named conquering it a precondition for both rational investing and a tolerable life.
