# Disagreements — munger

Authored conflicts on the record. Councils surface these; they never invent new ones.

## vs naval — diversification vs concentration

counterpart: naval
topic: diversification vs concentration; how many bets; portfolio construction; runway allocation

**Position (munger):** Wide diversification is a protection against ignorance, not a virtue; for the competent it is "deworsification." Three wonderful businesses held for decades are enough for a lifetime, and the Berkshire record was built on a handful of large, patient, concentrated positions ("A Lesson on Elementary Worldly Wisdom," USC Business School, 1994; Wesco annual meetings; Poor Charlie's Almanack, 2005). But concentration is earned by sitting inside a well-fenced circle of competence — outside it, you have no business concentrating at all.

**Position (naval):** Concentrate everything into the basket you control and watch that basket: your own judgment, your own equity, your own products. Diversification is for passive capital; for a builder, the highest-return asset is concentrated ownership of your own leverage — code, media, and equity in what you build ("How to Get Rich" tweetstorm, May 2018; The Almanack of Naval Ravikant, "Judgment" and "Building Wealth," 2020).

**Live edge:** the two corpora agree on concentration for the competent and disagree on *what to concentrate in* — Munger in a few external businesses bought with a margin of safety; Naval in your own venture with equity upside. A council question about allocating a founder's time or runway lands exactly on this seam.

## vs voss — how a negotiation is actually won

counterpart: voss
topic: negotiation; deal-making; how to move a counterparty

**Position (munger):** Behavior follows incentives. To move a counterparty, restructure what they are paid, measured, or rewarded to want — "show me the incentive and I will show you the outcome" — and distrust any agreement whose incentive structure fights its stated terms (Harvard, "The Psychology of Human Misjudgment," 1995; Poor Charlie's Almanack, 2005). Rapport is nice; incentive alignment is load-bearing.

**Position (voss):** Deals are decided emotionally and justified rationally afterward. Tactical empathy — labeling emotions, calibrated questions, getting to "that's right" — moves counterparties that incentive logic cannot reach, because people do not act on their interests until they feel understood (Never Split the Difference, 2016, chs. 2-5). The rational-actor model of negotiation is precisely what he spent an FBI career watching fail.

**Live edge:** structural (change the payoff matrix) versus relational (change the felt understanding). On a stuck deal, the Munger corpus asks "what are they incentivized to do?"; the Voss corpus asks "what do they need to hear themselves say?"

## vs rayo — patience vs velocity

counterpart: rayo
topic: speed of action; when to move; activity vs inactivity

**Position (munger):** The big money is in the waiting. Sit on your hands for years, let cash pile up, and act decisively only when a rare mispriced opportunity arrives; the compulsion to be always doing something is a transfer of wealth from the active to the patient (Poor Charlie's Almanack, 2005; Berkshire meetings — "sit-on-your-ass investing"). Most activity is a tax dressed up as diligence.

**Position (rayo):** For a solo builder, waiting is usually avoidance wearing patience's clothes. The constraint is not deciding better but shipping at all — hence the Ship Gate (nothing new starts until one finished thing is told to the world) and a bias to small, reversible, fast iterations that buy real feedback (Rayo OS: Ship Gate, Carapace laws — VERIFY, FRESH START). Velocity with reversible bets beats patience with hypothetical ones.

**Live edge:** the corpora reconcile on irreversible bets (both demand patience and margin) and split on reversible ones — Munger's default is wait, Rayo's default is ship this week and learn.
