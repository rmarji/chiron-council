# Frameworks — Robert Cialdini

Every named framework and technique with when-to-use, concrete steps, and source. These are the operational procedures the corpus documents and validates experimentally.

## The Seven Principles as a design checklist

*When to use:* Designing any request, pitch, page, ad, or campaign — the master framework.
*Steps:* Walk each principle and ask which is *truthfully* present, then lead with those.
1. **Reciprocity** — give first, meaningfully and unexpectedly, before asking (*Influence*, ch. 2).
2. **Commitment & Consistency** — secure a small, active, public, voluntary commitment first (ch. 3).
3. **Social Proof** — show what *similar* others in the *same situation* do (ch. 4).
4. **Liking** — build similarity, real compliments, and cooperation before the ask (ch. 5).
5. **Authority** — establish genuine, relevant expertise, ideally introduced by a third party (ch. 6).
6. **Scarcity** — surface the *genuine* limit and name the loss (ch. 7).
7. **Unity** — frame a shared "we" identity and co-create where possible (ch. 8, 2021; introduced in *Pre-Suasion*, 2016).
*Source:* *Influence* (2021); condensed in "Harnessing the Science of Persuasion" (HBR 2001). *Rule:* never manufacture a principle that isn't there — that is the smuggler move.

## Door-in-the-Face (reciprocal-concessions technique)

*When to use:* You need a substantial "yes" and can afford an initial refusal.
*Steps:* (1) Make a larger request you expect to be declined. (2) After the refusal, retreat to the smaller, real request. The retreat reads as a concession and triggers a reciprocal concession from the target; perceptual contrast also makes the second ask look modest.
*Evidence:* Cialdini, Vincent, Lewis, Catalan, Wheeler & Darby (1975), JPSP 31(2), 206-215. Asking students to counsel juvenile delinquents two hours a week for two years (almost all refused), then asking them to chaperone a single zoo trip, roughly tripled agreement to the zoo trip versus asking for the zoo trip alone (~50% vs ~17%). *Caveat:* the second request must be plausibly the same requester's genuine fallback, or the concession reads as a trick.

## Foot-in-the-Door

*When to use:* You can start small and escalate over time; the target's *self-image* is the lever.
*Steps:* (1) Obtain agreement to a small request the target can easily grant. (2) Later, make the larger real request. Having acted, the target now sees themselves as the kind of person who does this, and consistency carries the second yes.
*Evidence:* Freedman & Fraser (1966), "Compliance without pressure," JPSP 4(2), 195-202. (Not Cialdini's own study; he builds the commitment/consistency principle on it.) *Caveat:* the initial commitment must be active and freely chosen to shift self-image.

## Low-Ball (commitment then cost)

*When to use:* The corpus documents it and warns against it — included so it can be recognized and refused.
*Steps:* (1) Secure an active decision to act at an attractive price/terms. (2) Remove the original inducement or add cost. The commitment "grows legs" and holds even though the reason for it is gone.
*Evidence:* Cialdini, Cacioppo, Bassett & Miller (1978), JPSP 36(5), 463-476. Students asked to join a 7 a.m. experiment *after* agreeing showed higher follow-through than those told the early hour up front — but only when the initial choice felt freely made. *Ethics flag:* the corpus treats low-ball as the ethically fraught technique because the commitment is obtained under false pretenses; the detective does not use it.

## That's-Not-All

*When to use:* You are selling and can improve a standing offer before the target responds.
*Steps:* (1) State the offer. (2) Before the target answers, add a bonus or drop the price. The improvement is experienced as a concession, triggering reciprocity, and contrast makes the sweetened deal look better.
*Evidence:* Burger (1986), "Increasing compliance by improving the deal: The that's-not-all technique," JPSP 51(2), 277-283 (the cupcake-and-cookies bake-sale studies). Discussed by Cialdini; the key experiments are Burger's.

## Pre-Suasive Staging

*When to use:* You control what happens immediately before the request.
*Steps:* (1) Choose the single concept you want front-of-mind (adventurousness, safety, warmth, savings). (2) Engineer the opener that channels attention there — a question, image, number, or environment cue — because "what's focal is causal." (3) Deliver the ask inside that privileged moment.
*Evidence:* *Pre-Suasion* (2016). Cialdini's canonical illustration: prefacing a survey request with "Do you consider yourself a helpful person?" lifted agreement from 29% to 77.3% (ch. 2). A second illustration is the wine-store study where French vs. German background music shifted which country's wine sold (Adrian North et al., cited by Cialdini). *Caveat:* the prime must connect honestly to the offer.

## Crafting Normative Messages (descriptive vs. injunctive)

*When to use:* Any behavior-change or public-good message.
*Steps:* (1) Check whether the desirable behavior is currently the *majority* behavior. (2) If yes, use a descriptive norm ("most people do X"). (3) If the *undesirable* behavior is common, do NOT publicize it — switch to the injunctive norm (what people approve/disapprove of). (4) Prefer the most *provincial* true norm (similar others, same setting).
*Evidence:* Cialdini, Reno & Kallgren (1990), JPSP; Cialdini (2003), Current Directions — the Petrified Forest signs (injunctive "please don't steal" yielded 1.67% theft vs 7.92% for a descriptive "many past visitors have stolen"). Schultz et al. (2007) — energy boomerang. Goldstein, Cialdini & Griskevicius (2008) — provincial towel norms.

## Reciprocity Engineering

*When to use:* Building goodwill before any ask (sales, fundraising, relationships).
*Steps:* Give first; make the gift (1) *meaningful*, (2) *unexpected*, and (3) *customized* to the recipient — the three qualities that maximize the obligation to reciprocate.
*Evidence:* *Influence*, ch. 2; the restaurant-mint tipping studies (a small unexpected gift with the check raises tips, more so when personalized) collected in *Yes!* (2008).

## The Ethical Filter (detective, not smuggler)

*When to use:* Before deploying any of the above.
*Steps:* Ask (1) is this principle *genuinely present*, or am I manufacturing it? (2) Would the target, later understanding exactly how they were moved, feel respected or conned? If the tactic needs a false claim or targets someone against their interest, refuse it.
*Source:* *Pre-Suasion* (2016), "Ethical Use of Influence"; the bungler/smuggler/sleuth distinction (*The Influence Smugglers*, Psychology Today, 2018).
