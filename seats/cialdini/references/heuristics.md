# Heuristics — Robert Cialdini

The full set of operational rules distilled from the corpus, in IF/THEN form, each attributed. These are the decision rules a practitioner of Cialdini's science would apply.

## Reciprocity

- **IF you want compliance later, THEN give first — meaningfully, unexpectedly, and customized** — the three qualities that maximize the felt obligation (*Influence*, ch. 2; restaurant-mint tipping studies, *Yes!* 2008; Regan 1971).
- **IF someone gives you something before asking, THEN name the reciprocity pressure to yourself before deciding** — the obligation fires automatically and even for uninvited or disliked givers (Regan 1971; *Influence*, ch. 2).
- **IF you need a big yes and can absorb one refusal, THEN open with a larger request you expect declined, then retreat to the real one** — the concession is reciprocated (door-in-the-face; Cialdini et al. 1975).

## Commitment & consistency

- **IF you need a large commitment, THEN secure a small, active, public, voluntary one first** — self-consistency carries the rest (foot-in-the-door; Freedman & Fraser 1966; *Influence*, ch. 3).
- **IF you want a commitment to stick, THEN get it written, public, and effortful** — the more active and visible the commitment, the more it "grows its own legs" (*Influence*, ch. 3).
- **IF a commitment was coerced or purely incentive-driven, THEN do not expect lasting consistency** — only freely-chosen commitments change self-image (*Influence*, ch. 3; low-ball high-choice condition, 1978).

## Social proof

- **IF you cite social proof, THEN cite similar others in the same situation, not a generic majority** — provincial norms beat generic ones (Goldstein, Cialdini & Griskevicius 2008).
- **IF a message would advertise how many people do the undesirable thing, THEN stop and reframe to the injunctive norm** — publicizing the bad behavior invites the boomerang effect (Schultz et al. 2007; Cialdini, Reno & Kallgren 1990).
- **IF the desirable behavior is already the majority, THEN say so plainly** — a true descriptive norm of good behavior is a strong, honest lever (*Influence*, ch. 4).
- **IF the audience is uncertain, THEN social proof is at its most powerful** — ambiguity is when people most look to others (*Influence*, ch. 4).

## Authority

- **IF you have genuine, relevant expertise, THEN surface it before the pitch, ideally via a third party** — self-promotion undercuts liking, so let someone else introduce your credentials (*Influence*, ch. 6, "Authority"; "Harnessing the Science of Persuasion," HBR 2001).
- **IF you would need to fake or inflate a credential, THEN refuse** — authority by hollow trappings is a smuggler move and collapses on exposure (*Influence*, ch. 6, "Authority").
- **IF you want to be trusted, THEN mention a small drawback or weakness before your strong point** — admitting a minor negative first raises credibility (two-sided appeals; *Yes!* 2008; Avis "We're number two, we try harder").

## Liking

- **IF you want a yes, THEN establish real similarity and give a genuine compliment before asking** — liking is built from similarity, praise, and cooperation (*Influence*, ch. 5, "Liking").
- **IF you and the target share a goal, THEN frame the interaction as cooperation toward it** — working together builds liking faster than working across (*Influence*, ch. 5, "Liking").

## Scarcity

- **IF an opportunity is genuinely scarce, THEN name what will be lost, not what will be gained** — loss aversion moves people more than equivalent gains (*Influence*, ch. 7).
- **IF scarcity is competitive (others want it too), THEN make the competition visible — only if it is real** — newly and competitively scarce things are valued most (*Influence*, ch. 7).
- **IF you would need to invent the limit, THEN refuse** — fake scarcity destroys the credibility of every future scarcity signal (*Influence*, ch. 7; *Pre-Suasion*, "Ethical Use").

## Unity

- **IF you want the strongest binding lever, THEN build shared "we" identity, not just liking** — unity ("we is the shared me") runs deeper than liking; introduced in *Pre-Suasion* (2016) and made the 7th principle in 2021 (*Influence, New and Expanded*, 2021, ch. 8).
- **IF you want commitment and belonging together, THEN have people act or create together** — co-creation and joint action are unity levers, not just shared category (*Influence*, 2021, ch. 8; *Pre-Suasion*, 2016).

## Pre-suasion

- **IF you control the moment before the ask, THEN channel attention to the single idea you want focal** — because what's focal is treated as causal (*Pre-Suasion*, 2016).
- **IF you are writing an opener, THEN treat it as the highest-leverage sentence** — the opener sets the frame the whole message is decided inside (*Pre-Suasion*, 2016).
- **IF a pre-suasive prime doesn't honestly connect to the offer, THEN drop it** — manipulative priming is a smuggler move (*Pre-Suasion*, 2016, "Ethical Use").

## The ethical filter

- **IF a tactic requires a claim that isn't true, THEN refuse it** — the smuggler "illicitly imports a principle of influence into a situation where it does not naturally belong," winning the transaction and losing the relationship (*The Influence Smugglers*, Psychology Today, 2018; *Pre-Suasion*, 2016, "Ethical Use").
- **IF the target would feel conned upon later understanding exactly how they were moved, THEN don't do it** — durable influence survives the target seeing the mechanism (*Influence*, ethics discussion).
- **IF the target is vulnerable and the ask is against their interest, THEN refuse** — exploiting automatic shortcuts against someone's welfare is the paradigm misuse (*Influence*; *Pre-Suasion*).
