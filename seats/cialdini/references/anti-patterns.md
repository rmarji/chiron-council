# Anti-patterns — Robert Cialdini

Everything the corpus warns against, with the reasoning and the failure it produces. Two families: the *ethical* anti-patterns (smuggler moves that betray the target) and the *technical* anti-patterns (misapplying a principle so it weakens or reverses).

## Ethical anti-patterns (the smuggler's moves)

### Fabricating social proof
Fake reviews, invented "bestseller" or "#1" claims, purchased followers, staged testimonials. *Reasoning:* the smuggler imports a principle that isn't genuinely present. *Failure:* proof works only while it is believed; discovered fabrication doesn't just neutralize the tactic, it converts trust into active distrust, and the dishonesty spreads a reputation cost. *Source:* *Pre-Suasion* (2016), "Ethical Use of Influence"; the smuggler/detective distinction.

### Manufacturing scarcity
Phantom countdown timers that reset, permanent "only 2 left," fake "limited edition" claims. *Reasoning:* scarcity moves people via loss aversion, but only real scarcity survives scrutiny. *Failure:* once a customer notices the timer resets, every future scarcity signal from that brand is discounted, and the credibility loss generalizes. *Source:* *Influence*, ch. 7; *Pre-Suasion*, "Ethical Use."

### Low-balling
Securing a commitment at an attractive price, then removing the inducement or adding cost. *Reasoning:* it exploits the "grows legs" property of commitment to hold a target to a deal whose basis has vanished. *Failure:* it works transactionally but is obtained under false pretenses; the corpus flags it as the ethically fraught technique the detective does not use. *Source:* Cialdini, Cacioppo, Bassett & Miller (1978).

### Weaponizing shortcuts against the target's interest
Using the automatic "click, run" nature of the principles to move a vulnerable person toward a choice that harms them. *Reasoning:* the principles fire *because* the target isn't deliberating; exploiting that against their welfare is the paradigm misuse. *Failure:* the whole ethical and long-run economic case for influence collapses — the practitioner wins the moment and loses standing. *Source:* *Influence*, ethics discussion; *Pre-Suasion* (2016).

### Dishonesty as an organizational strategy
Building a business on any of the above. *Reasoning:* Cialdini argues dishonest organizations pay hidden costs — higher employee turnover (people don't want to work where they must deceive) and reputational damage that compounds. *Failure:* short-term compliance gains are dwarfed by long-term erosion; ethical influence is also the more profitable strategy. *Source:* *Pre-Suasion* (2016), "Ethical Use of Influence."

## Technical anti-patterns (misapplying a live principle)

### Publicizing the undesirable behavior — the boomerang effect
Telling people how *many* do the bad thing ("most guests don't recycle," "tax cheating is widespread"). *Reasoning:* a descriptive norm of the undesirable behavior normalizes it. *Failure:* it *increases* the bad behavior among those currently below the average — the message backfires. Fix: appeal to the injunctive norm (disapproval) instead, or pair the descriptive norm with an injunctive cue. *Source:* Schultz, Nolan, Cialdini, Goldstein & Griskevicius (2007); Cialdini, Reno & Kallgren (1990).

### Generic proof instead of provincial proof
"Thousands of happy customers" when you could cite the behavior of *similar* others in the *same* situation. *Reasoning:* social proof is strongest from people the target sees as like themselves, here and now. *Failure:* generic proof is weak; you leave the strongest available lever unused. *Source:* Goldstein, Cialdini & Griskevicius (2008), the provincial-norm towel study.

### Coerced or incentive-only commitments
Extracting a "commitment" via pressure, bribery, or threat and expecting lasting consistency. *Reasoning:* commitments only "grow legs" and change self-image when they are *freely chosen*; a coerced one produces no internal justification. *Failure:* behavior reverts the moment the external pressure lifts. *Source:* *Influence*, ch. 3; the low-ball finding that the effect requires high choice.

### Argument-first persuasion (ignoring the privileged moment)
Polishing the message while neglecting what the audience attends to just before it. *Reasoning:* "what's focal is causal" — the pre-message state often outweighs the content. *Failure:* a strong argument delivered into the wrong attentional frame underperforms a weaker one delivered into the right one. *Source:* *Pre-Suasion* (2016).

### Authority by trappings without substance
Leaning on titles, uniforms, and props with no genuine, relevant expertise behind them. *Reasoning:* symbols of authority move people, but the corpus's honest version requires *real* expertise. *Failure:* it is both a smuggler move and fragile — exposure of the hollow credential collapses the influence. *Source:* *Influence*, ch. 6, "Authority."

### Too many choices (the overload trap)
Offering so many options that the target defers or exits. *Reasoning:* more choice can reduce action. *Failure:* conversion drops. Cialdini and colleagues cite the jam-tasting study (Iyengar & Lepper, 2000) — a 24-jam display drew more browsers but far fewer buyers than a 6-jam display — in *Yes!* (2008) as a caution against option overload. *Source:* *Yes!* (2008).

### Ignoring the contrast that precedes your ask
Failing to control what the target compares your offer against. *Reasoning:* judgment is relative; an unmanaged prior anchor can make your offer look worse. *Failure:* you cede the framing to chance or to a competitor. *Source:* *Influence*, ch. 1 (perceptual contrast).
