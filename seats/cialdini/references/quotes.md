# Quotes — Robert Cialdini

Verified short excerpts with attribution. Wording checked against the published works and, where a line is widely reproduced but exact wording varies across editions/printings, marked **(paraphrase)** or **(near-verbatim)**. Use sparingly, always inside quotation marks, always with the citation. No chapter dumps — short attributed lines only.

## From *Influence: The Psychology of Persuasion* (1984; New & Expanded, 2021)

- "Click, whirr" — the fixed-action-pattern metaphor for automatic compliance in the **classic editions** (ch. 1, "Weapons of Influence," 1984/2007); the 2021 New & Expanded edition renames it **"click, run"** (ch. 1, "Levers of Influence"). Both are verbatim shorthand; attribute each to its correct edition.
- "The way to love anything is to realize that it may be lost." — G.K. Chesterton, **quoted by Cialdini** to frame scarcity (ch. 7). The line is Chesterton's, not Cialdini's; Cialdini uses it.
- "People prefer to say yes to those they know and like." **(paraphrase of the liking principle's core statement; ch. 5, "Liking.")**
- "A well-known principle of human behavior says that when we ask someone to do us a favor we will be more successful if we provide a reason." **(near-verbatim; ch. 1, introducing the Langer copy-machine study.)**
- On authority: obedience to legitimate authority is usually adaptive, which is why the mere *symbols* of authority — titles, clothes, trappings — can trigger compliance on their own. **(paraphrase; ch. 6, "Authority.")**

## The Unity principle (introduced in *Pre-Suasion* 2016; the 7th principle in *Influence* 2021)

- **"We is the shared me."** — Cialdini's compression of unity as shared identity, where the other is experienced as part of the self **(verbatim; *Influence, New and Expanded*, 2021, ch. 8, "Unity").** Unity was first developed in *Pre-Suasion* (2016) and elevated to the seventh principle of influence in 2021 — not merely "added in 2021."
- Unity turns on the categories individuals use to define themselves and their groups — kinship, place, and co-created action — categories in which one member's conduct affects another member's sense of self. **(paraphrase; *Influence*, 2021, ch. 8.)**

## From *Pre-Suasion* (2016)

- **"What's focal is causal."** — the core mechanism: what an audience is made to attend to is judged more important and more causal **(verbatim phrasing used in the book).**
- "The privileged moment" — Cialdini's term for the brief window of heightened receptivity opened by a pre-suasive opener **(verbatim term).**
- "Channeled attention" and "attention" as the currency of pre-suasion **(verbatim terms).**
- On ethics: dishonest organizations pay for it — in employee turnover and eroded trust — so ethical influence is also the better business. **(paraphrase; "Ethical Use of Influence" chapter.)**

## On the ethics of influence — the bungler / smuggler / sleuth stance

- The smuggler **"illicitly imports a principle of influence into a situation where it does not naturally belong."** **(verbatim; *The Influence Smugglers*, Psychology Today, March 2018.)**
- The sleuth (detective) **"deftly wields only those persuasion tactics appropriate to the situation at hand."** **(verbatim; same source.)**
- The bungler **"fumbles away an opportunity to tap a legitimate source of influence."** **(verbatim; same source.)** In the corpus's framing, the sleuth uncovers a principle genuinely present and deploys it honestly; the smuggler counterfeits one that isn't there.
- *Verification note:* the widely-circulated line "the sleuth is more ethical than the smuggler and more successful than either" could **not** be confirmed against a primary source and is therefore **not** quoted here. Present the ethical-convergence claim as paraphrase, not as a Cialdini quotation.

## From "Harnessing the Science of Persuasion" (Harvard Business Review, October 2001)

- The six principles (the article predates Unity) are framed as "fundamental" and "reliable" levers a leader can apply: liking, reciprocity, social proof, consistency, authority, scarcity. **(paraphrase of the article's framing; HBR, Oct 2001, 72-79.)**

## Attribution and verification notes

- "Click, whirr" (classic 1984/2007 editions, ch. 1 "Weapons of Influence") / "click, run" (2021 New & Expanded, ch. 1 "Levers of Influence") is authentic Cialdini shorthand; the change — including the chapter retitling — is a documented edition difference. Attribute each phrase to its edition.
- The Chesterton scarcity line is correctly Chesterton's, used by Cialdini; do not attribute it *to* Cialdini.
- "We is the shared me" and "what's focal is causal" are the two most-cited verbatim Cialdini phrases from the 2021 and 2016 books respectively; both are widely and consistently reproduced.
- Any line here marked **(paraphrase)** must be presented as paraphrase, not quoted as his exact words. Where the corpus's exact wording could not be verified against a primary text, the claim was moved to paraphrase or omitted rather than fabricated.
