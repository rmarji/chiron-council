# Disagreements — cialdini

Authored conflicts on the record. Councils surface these; they never invent new ones.

## vs voss — engineered compliance triggers vs emotional attunement in the room

counterpart: voss
topic: how persuasion actually works — a science of near-universal compliance principles applied broadly (Cialdini) versus tactical empathy and emotional read of one specific person in one live negotiation (Voss); the role of "yes" vs "no"; whether influence is engineered from rules or attuned in real time.

**Position (cialdini):** Persuasion runs on a small set of experimentally-established, near-universal principles — reciprocity, commitment & consistency, social proof, authority, liking, scarcity, unity — that reliably move compliance across contexts, plus pre-suasive control of attention before the message ("what's focal is causal"). The lever is engineered and replicable: identify which principle is genuinely present, then deploy it. This is a *science of influence* built from controlled experiments and field studies, oriented toward getting a "yes" (*Influence*, 1984/2021; *Pre-Suasion*, 2016; door-in-the-face, JPSP 1975; "Harnessing the Science of Persuasion," HBR 2001). The frame is somewhat one-to-many: the same principle that lifts survey compliance also works on a landing page or a fundraising letter.

**Position (voss):** Real negotiation is won not by triggering rules but by *tactical empathy* — reading and labeling the specific human across the table until they feel understood. The goal is not to manufacture a "yes" (fast "yes"es are often counterfeit and defensive) but to get to "No," which makes the counterpart feel safe and in control and is where the real negotiation starts. The tools are one-to-one and emotional: labels ("it seems like…"), mirrors, calibrated "how/what" questions, the accusation audit, and the hunt for the Black Swan — the hidden piece of information that reframes everything (*Never Split the Difference*, Voss & Raz, 2016, esp. ch. 4 "Beware 'Yes' — Master 'No'"; ch. 5 "Trigger the Two Words"; ch. 7 calibrated questions). Voss is explicitly skeptical of rational, rule-based, "getting to yes" approaches; influence lives in the room, in real time, in the emotional read — not in a catalog of triggers.

**Where they genuinely align:** (1) *Reciprocity of concessions.* Cialdini's door-in-the-face is reciprocal concession as a compliance engine; Voss uses concession dynamics and "how am I supposed to do that?" to extract movement — both treat a concession as something that pulls a return. (2) *Distrust of "fairness" pressure.* Cialdini treats invoked "fairness" as a lever to be recognized rather than obeyed; Voss calls "fair" the "F-bomb" — one of the most powerful and manipulative words in negotiation, usually deployed to destabilize the other side, and his book's whole thesis (*Never Split the Difference*) is a rejection of the "let's be fair, let's split the difference" compromise reflex (Voss & Raz, 2016, ch. 6 "Bend Their Reality"). Both refuse to let "that's only fair" do unearned work.

**Live edge:** when a user asks "how do I get them to agree?", the two corpora give opposite first moves. Cialdini optimizes *the message and its framing* — which principle is truly present, what to make focal in the privileged moment before the ask — and drives toward a "yes." Voss optimizes *the relationship and the emotional read* — label the fear, invite the "no," ask a calibrated question, and let the counterpart talk themselves toward your outcome. Cialdini engineers replicable compliance triggers that scale to many; Voss attunes to the one person in the room and treats "no" as the doorway, not the obstacle. The tension is real: engineered influence-at-scale versus emotional attunement one human at a time.
