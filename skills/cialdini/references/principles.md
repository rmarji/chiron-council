# Principles — Robert Cialdini

The full catalog of the corpus's core beliefs, each with rationale and source. The spine is the seven principles of influence from *Influence: The Psychology of Persuasion* (1984; New & Expanded edition, 2021), supported by the peer-reviewed compliance research. Every principle is a documented *tendency*, established experimentally, not an assertion.

## The meta-principle: influence is a testable science

The corpus's foundational belief is that persuasion is empirical. Claims about what moves people are worth only their evidence, and the evidence is controlled experiments and field studies. Cialdini famously spent three years undercover in sales, fundraising, and advertising training programs to observe which tactics working professionals actually used, then took those tactics back to the lab to test why they worked (*Influence*, Introduction). The condensed managerial statement of this is "Harnessing the Science of Persuasion" (Harvard Business Review, October 2001), which reframes the (then) six principles as reliable levers a leader can apply.

## The "click, whirr" / "click, run" shortcut (why the principles work at all)

People cannot deliberate on every request, so they rely on fixed-action patterns: a single trigger feature fires a whole sequence of behavior. Cialdini's metaphor is a tape playing — **"click, whirr"** in the classic editions (*Influence*, 1984/2007, ch. 1, "Weapons of Influence"), renamed **"click, run"** in the 2021 New & Expanded edition (ch. 1, retitled "Levers of Influence"). The seven principles are the trigger features that most reliably produce the compliant response. Langer, Blank & Chanowitz's 1978 copy-machine study is the classic demonstration: the word "because" followed by even a placebic reason ("because I have to make copies") lifted compliance almost as much as a real reason, because the *form* of a justification triggered the response (94% for a real reason and 93% for a placebic reason on the small request, versus 60% with no reason).

## The perceptual contrast principle

Judgments are relative to what preceded them. A second item looks cheaper, heavier, or smaller depending on the first. Cialdini opens with the real-estate "setup" (showing rundown houses first so the target property shines) and the clothing-store sequence (sell the suit first, then accessories look trivial by comparison) (*Influence*, ch. 1). Contrast is the mechanical basis of several techniques, including door-in-the-face.

## The seven principles

### 1. Reciprocity
People feel a strong obligation to repay what another has given them — favors, gifts, concessions, information. The rule is near-universal across cultures and fires even for uninvited and disliked givers. Rationale: reciprocity underwrites cooperative exchange and division of labor, so societies train it deeply. Evidence: Regan (1971) — a confederate who bought the subject an unrequested Coke later sold roughly twice as many raffle tickets, and the effect held even when the subject disliked him. The Hare Krishna flower-then-donation tactic and the restaurant after-dinner mint (servers who leave mints raise tips) are field illustrations (*Influence*, ch. 2). Concessions are reciprocated too, which is the engine of door-in-the-face.

### 2. Commitment & Consistency
Once people take a position, they face internal and interpersonal pressure to behave consistently with it. Commitments are most binding when active, public, effortful, and freely chosen. Rationale: consistency is a valued and adaptive shortcut — it saves reprocessing and signals reliability. Evidence: Freedman & Fraser's (1966) foot-in-the-door — homeowners who first agreed to a small request (a tiny "drive safely" window card) were far more likely later to allow a large ugly billboard on the lawn. The Amway "unlimited goal" and written-goal tactics illustrate the effortful/public forms (*Influence*, ch. 3). The corpus stresses that a commitment which was coerced does *not* produce lasting consistency — the choice must feel the target's own.

### 3. Social Proof
People determine what is correct by finding out what other people think is correct, and act accordingly — most powerfully under uncertainty and when the others are *similar* to them. Rationale: copying the many is usually a good bet and economizes on thinking. Evidence base includes conformity work (Sherif's autokinetic norms; Asch) that Cialdini situates the principle within, and his own field program on norms. Boundary conditions are central: social proof from *dissimilar* others is weak, and describing an undesirable behavior as common backfires (see the boomerang effect below) (*Influence*, ch. 4).

### 4. Liking
People prefer to say yes to those they know and like. Liking is manufacturable from a handful of factors: physical attractiveness (a halo effect), similarity, compliments, increased familiarity through repeated contact, cooperation toward shared goals, and association with good things. Rationale: we extend trust to the familiar and the similar. Evidence: the Tupperware party (the request comes from a friend, not a stranger); the "we're on the same side" cooperation framing; the good-cop/bad-cop mechanism (*Influence*, ch. 5, "Liking: The Friendly Thief").

### 5. Authority
People defer to legitimate authority and, dangerously, to the mere *symbols* of it — titles, uniforms, and trappings (the expensive car, the tailored suit). Rationale: obeying credentialed experts is usually efficient and is trained from childhood. Evidence: Cialdini cites Milgram's obedience experiments as the extreme case of deference to authority, plus findings that a title alone inflates estimates (e.g., of a person's physical height). The persuasive move is to establish *genuine, relevant* expertise — and to do it before the pitch, ideally via a third party (*Influence*, ch. 6, "Authority: Directed Deference").

*(Note on ordering: in *Influence*'s chapter sequence Liking is ch. 5 and Authority is ch. 6; they are numbered 4 and 5 in this catalog's own list of the seven principles.)*

### 6. Scarcity
Opportunities seem more valuable as they become less available. Two amplifiers: newly scarce things (freedoms just lost) are valued more than things always scarce, and scarcity bites hardest under competition. Rationale is loss aversion — people are moved more by the prospect of losing something than by gaining its equivalent. Evidence: the "limited number" and "deadline" tactics; the study where cookies rated better when the jar went from many to few, and better still when the reduction was due to others' demand (*Influence*, ch. 7). Cialdini pairs this with Chesterton's observation that "the way to love anything is to realize that it may be lost."

### 7. Unity (introduced in *Pre-Suasion* 2016; elevated to the 7th principle in *Influence* 2021)
The strongest yes comes not from someone the target merely likes, but from someone they consider *one of us*. Unity is shared identity — the "we" categories of kinship, place, ethnicity, and co-created action, where one member's conduct affects another's self-esteem. Cialdini's compression: "we is the shared me." Rationale: these categories are deeper than liking because the other is experienced as part of the self. Levers include family and family-like language, shared locality, and *acting together* (co-creation, joint effort). Cialdini first developed unity in *Pre-Suasion* (2016) and elevated it to the seventh principle of influence in the 2021 New & Expanded *Influence* — this is what moved the framework from six principles to seven, and it is more precise than saying unity was "added in 2021" (*Pre-Suasion*, 2016, ch. 12; *Influence, New and Expanded*, 2021, ch. 8, "Unity").

## The pre-suasion principle (from *Pre-Suasion*, 2016)

Beyond the seven, the corpus holds that *timing and attention* frequently outrank content. The state you create in an audience immediately *before* your message — what you make them focus on — biases how the message lands, because "what's focal is causal": whatever is salient is treated as important and as causal. The "privileged moment" is the brief window, opened by a pre-suasive opener, in which receptivity peaks. This is treated as a distinct principle from the seven: not *what* you say but *what came just before it*.

## The ethics principle

The corpus insists ethical and effective influence converge. Fabricated triggers — fake social proof, phantom scarcity — win a transaction and lose the relationship, and dishonest organizations pay in employee turnover and eroded trust (*Pre-Suasion*, 2016, "Ethical Use of Influence"). The governing distinction (three types of practitioner): the **bungler** "fumbles away an opportunity to tap a legitimate source of influence"; the **smuggler** "illicitly imports a principle of influence into a situation where it does not naturally belong"; the **sleuth (detective)** "deftly wields only those persuasion tactics appropriate to the situation at hand," uncovering a principle genuinely present and deploying it honestly (*The Influence Smugglers*, Psychology Today, March 2018 — direct quotations). The claim that ethical and effective influence converge is Cialdini's, presented here as paraphrase.
