# Anti-Patterns — Charlie Munger

Everything the corpus warns against, with Munger's reasoning and the failure each produces.

## EBITDA — "bullshit earnings"

At the 2003 Berkshire Hathaway annual meeting, Munger told shareholders that any presentation using the word EBITDA becomes intelligible if, every time the word appears, the phrase "bullshit earnings" is substituted (Berkshire meeting, 2003; wording varies slightly across transcripts). Reasoning: depreciation and interest are real costs; a metric that deletes them exists to make bad economics look good, and its users are self-selecting for promotion over truth. Failure produced: overpaying for capital-hungry businesses and trusting managements that optimize optics.

## Envy

"The world is not driven by greed. It's driven by envy" (Daily Journal annual meeting, February 2023). Munger classed envy as uniquely stupid among the deadly sins because it is the only one with no fun in it at all (Berkshire meeting remarks, paraphrase-stable), and the 1986 Harvard School address lists ingesting envy as a reliable prescription for misery (Talk One, Poor Charlie's Almanack). Failure produced: benchmark-chasing, style drift, buying what neighbors got rich on at the top — someone will always be getting richer faster, and resenting it converts a solvable problem (enough) into an unsolvable one (more than everyone).

## Self-pity

From the 2007 USC Gould commencement: self-pity is always counterproductive and, taken far enough, verges on paranoia — and paranoia is one of the hardest things to reverse. Munger's prescription was the attitude of Epictetus: treat every mischance as an opportunity to behave well and learn (USC Gould, 2007). The 1986 misery-prescription speech makes the same point by inversion: go down and stay down after each reverse if misery is the goal (Talk One). Failure produced: paralysis after losses precisely when clear thinking is worth the most.

## Heavy ideology

The 2007 USC Gould address names heavy ideology as one of the most extreme distorters of human cognition, citing crazed television preachers and political zealotry alike, and warns that pounding ideas in young heads makes the distortion permanent. Munger's iron prescription (stated at USC Gould, 2007, and repeated at Daily Journal meetings): no one is entitled to an opinion unless they can state the arguments against their position better than the people on the other side. Failure produced: intelligence placed in service of conclusions chosen in advance — the smarter the person, the better the rationalizations.

## Overdiversification

The corpus rejects wide diversification for the competent investor as an academic security blanket: at USC in 1994 Munger argued the wise bet heavily on their rare high-conviction insights, and at Daily Journal meetings he noted the Munger family's wealth sat substantially in three positions — Berkshire, Costco, and Li Lu's fund (Daily Journal Q&A, 2017-2021). Diversification, in the corpus view, is protection for those who do not know what they are doing; for those who do, it guarantees mediocrity by forcing capital into the fourth-best through fortieth-best ideas. Failure produced: closet indexing at active fees, and diluted attention across positions no one truly understands.

## Trading activity and the casino culture

"The big money is not in the buying and the selling, but in the waiting" (Poor Charlie's Almanack). At the 2021 Daily Journal meeting Munger attacked commission-free trading apps as gambling parlors dressed as brokers, and described the resulting speculation as a "wretched excess" carrying dangerous consequences (Daily Journal Q&A, 2021, paraphrase-stable). Reasoning: turnover is a tax paid to intermediaries, and the activity itself is driven by deprival superreaction and social proof, not analysis. Failure produced: fees, taxes, and whipsaw — motion mistaken for progress.

## Leverage

Buffett, at Berkshire meetings, repeatedly attributed to Munger the line that a smart man can go broke three ways: liquor, ladies, and leverage (Berkshire meeting remarks; Buffett crediting Munger). The corpus reasoning: leverage converts temporary quotation declines — which the corpus expects at 50% magnitude several times a century (BBC interview, 2009) — into permanent ruin, and ruin has no expected-value offset because the game stops. Failure produced: forced selling at the bottom; the interruption of compounding, which is the whole engine.

## Macro forecasting

Across Berkshire and Wesco meetings, Munger's stated position is that the firm makes no macroeconomic predictions: microeconomics — the specific business and its competitive position — is what gets analyzed; the macro environment is what gets endured (Berkshire meeting remarks, paraphrase-stable). Reasoning: macro forecasts are unfalsifiable in advance, sold by people paid for confidence rather than accuracy (incentive-caused bias), and unnecessary — a durable business survives the range of plausible weather. Failure produced: timing decisions built on noise, and paralysis waiting for clarity that never comes.

## Commission-shaped advice

"Especially fear professional advice when it is especially good for the advisor" (Talk Eleven, Poor Charlie's Almanack). Munger's febezzlement analysis (Philanthropy Round Table address, November 2000, Talk Seven) extends this: layered advisors and money managers extract so much in undisclosed cost that everyone feels richer while the principal is quietly poorer. Failure produced: the client's capital funds the advisor's compounding instead of the client's.

## The man with a hammer

"To the man with only a hammer, every problem looks like a nail" (USC, 1994). Single-discipline experts overapply their one tool, and their incentive is to sell that tool. Failure produced: elaborate, credentialed wrongness — the corpus's examples include efficient-market academics assigning precise wrongness to prices (USC, 1994) and consultants whose every diagnosis requires their product. The counter is the latticework.

## First-conclusion bias

The corpus's image (paraphrase-stable, Poor Charlie's Almanack): the human mind is like the human egg — once one idea gets in, it shuts off against all others. Combined with doubt-avoidance and inconsistency-avoidance (Talk Eleven, tendencies 4 and 5), first conclusions harden into identity. The named counter is Darwin's practice: give priority attention to evidence that disconfirms a hypothesis, especially a cherished one (Talk One and Talk Eleven, Poor Charlie's Almanack). Failure produced: intelligent people defending their first guess as a possession.

## Twaddle and pretended knowledge

Talk Eleven's Twaddle Tendency: humans emit confident nonsense, and organizations reward fluent emitters. Munger's honeybee illustration — the bee that, facing nectar placed straight up (a case its dance can't encode), dances an incoherent lie rather than staying silent — is the corpus's picture of forecasters and pundits (Talk Eleven, Poor Charlie's Almanack). Kept adjacent: Munger's repeated observation that people who confidently answer questions they cannot possibly answer should be ignored (Daily Journal Q&A, paraphrase-stable). Failure produced: decisions built on confident noise.

## Cryptocurrency and speculative manias

Munger's February 2023 Wall Street Journal op-ed, "Why America Should Ban Crypto," called a cryptocurrency "not a currency, not a commodity, and not a security" but "a gambling contract with a nearly 100% edge for the house" (WSJ op-ed, February 2023). At Daily Journal meetings he was harsher still, framing crypto speculation as a mass delusion mixing social proof, envy, and deprival superreaction — a live lollapalooza (Daily Journal Q&A, 2022-2023, paraphrase-stable). Failure produced: capital destruction plus the corrosion of the habit of demanding underlying value.

## Denial and drifting into "crazy"

The final CNBC interview distilled a lifetime warning: "Avoid crazy at all costs. Crazy is way more common than you think. It's easy to slip into crazy" (CNBC, Becky Quick, November 2023). Paired with Talk Eleven's pain-avoiding psychological denial, the corpus treats drift — small, contrast-blind steps (tendency 16) each individually defensible — as the standard route to ruin, in firms and lives alike. Failure produced: waking up far outside the circle, leveraged, and committed, with no single decision to blame.
