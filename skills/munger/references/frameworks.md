# Frameworks — Charlie Munger

Every named framework in the corpus, with when-to-use, concrete steps, and source.

## Inversion

**When to use:** any goal, plan, launch, or "how do I succeed?" question; also post-mortems designed in advance.
**Source:** Harvard School commencement (1986, Talk One in Poor Charlie's Almanack); Jacobi's "man muss immer umkehren" cited at USC (1994) and in "Practical Thought About Practical Thought?" (1996, Talk Four, helpful notion three).
**Steps:**
1. Restate the goal, then flip it: "how would this be guaranteed to fail?"
2. List every failure mode without flinching — the 1986 address models this with its prescriptions for misery: envy, resentment, unreliability, ignoring others' failures, staying down after adversity, and refusing disconfirming evidence.
3. Eliminate or defend against each failure mode one by one.
4. What survives is the plan. Inversion converts a vague ambition into an avoid-list.

## Two-track analysis

**When to use:** every consequential judgment, especially where the analyst has a stake in the answer.
**Source:** Poor Charlie's Almanack (Kaufman's account of Munger's method), backed by Talk Eleven's tendency catalog.
**Steps:**
1. Track one — rationality: what factors really govern the interests involved, coldly assessed (economics, incentives, base rates, structure)?
2. Track two — psychology: which subconscious tendencies (from the 25) are producing automatic, possibly wrong conclusions in the participants — and in the analyst?
3. Reconcile: where track two contaminates track one, discount the contaminated conclusions and re-run them.

## The psychology checklist

**When to use:** before finalizing any significant decision; whenever behavior (yours or others') doesn't add up; when designing anything that must influence people.
**Source:** Talk Eleven, Poor Charlie's Almanack (2005); Munger's stated engineering practice of checklist routines — the corpus repeatedly invokes the pilot's checklist as the model for avoiding omission errors (Poor Charlie's Almanack; USC Gould, 2007).
**Steps:**
1. Walk the 25 tendencies as a literal checklist against the situation.
2. Flag every tendency plausibly active; weight incentive-caused bias, social proof, commitment/consistency, and denial most heavily.
3. Check for lollapalooza: two or more tendencies pointing the same direction means the effect will be nonlinear — treat the situation as more dangerous (or more powerful) than any single factor suggests.
4. Apply the specific antidotes Munger names: defer reaction (reciprocation), demand disconfirming evidence (consistency), ignore the messenger's likability (liking), ask for the incentive (reward superresponse).

## Incentive audit

**When to use:** evaluating advice, forecasts, partnerships, valuations, or any behavior that doesn't add up — including one's own conclusions.
**Source:** Talk Eleven, tendency one, with the three named antidotes for professional advice.
**Steps:**
1. For each party, ask: how are they paid, measured, or rewarded here?
2. Assume each will rationalize toward that incentive, usually sincerely and unconsciously.
3. Re-read the situation as if incentives, not stated reasons, are the cause.
4. Apply Munger's advisor antidotes: especially fear advice that is especially good for the advisor; learn the elements of the advisor's trade well enough to check the advice; and double-check or replace advice with objective evidence (Talk Eleven).

## Circle-of-competence check (three baskets)

**When to use:** before acting on, investing in, or committing to anything.
**Source:** USC (1994) on playing where one holds an aptitude edge; Caltech conversation (2020) on the too-hard pile; Berkshire meeting remarks on in/out/too-hard sorting.
**Steps:**
1. Ask: "is this genuinely understood, or merely familiar and wanted?"
2. Draw the honest border of competence — what could be explained, with numbers, to a hostile expert?
3. Sort into three baskets: in, out, too hard. Default to too hard.
4. If the decision sits outside the circle, either decline or do the work to bring it inside before acting. Passing costs nothing; there are no called strikes.

## The five helpful notions

**When to use:** structuring the attack on any large practical problem.
**Source:** "Practical Thought About Practical Thought?" (July 1996, Talk Four, Poor Charlie's Almanack), where Munger demonstrates them by "building" Coca-Cola from scratch to a $2 trillion valuation.
**Steps (the notions, in order):**
1. Simplify by answering the big no-brainer questions first.
2. Apply numerical fluency — Galileo's point that the book of nature is written in mathematics; without numbers the analysis is mush.
3. Think through the problem forward and backward — invert.
4. Use elementary, multidisciplinary wisdom — every easy-to-learn big idea from every basic subject, not one department's toolkit.
5. Watch for lollapalooza effects — the really big outcomes come from combinations of factors, so deliberately stack them (or detect stacked risks).

## The investing principles checklist

**When to use:** end-to-end discipline for any investment decision.
**Source:** "An Investing Principles Checklist," Poor Charlie's Almanack (editorial synthesis of Munger's stated practice, published with his approval).
**Elements (condensed):** measure risk first, including reputational risk and inflation; be independent — the crowd's agreement is not evidence; prepare — read and learn continuously ("go to bed a little wiser"); admit ignorance honestly (intellectual humility); analyze rigorously with numbers and inversion; allocate capital by opportunity cost; wait — patience until the mispriced bet appears; act decisively and at scale when it does; accept change and adapt; keep focus — guard against hubris and boredom.

## Moat evaluation

**When to use:** judging whether a business's high returns will persist — the central question of quality investing.
**Source:** Berkshire Hathaway meeting remarks (paraphrase-stable across years); USC (1994) on scale advantages; Poor Charlie's Almanack on See's Candies and brand power.
**Steps:**
1. Explain precisely why the good results are occurring now — brand, scale, network, switching costs, regulation, cost position. If the cause can't be named, the moat can't be trusted.
2. Identify the forces that could stop those results — technology waves (the surfing model), competitors, changed habits, regulation.
3. Ask whether the moat is widening or narrowing each year, and whether management's capital allocation reinforces it.
4. Prefer businesses that could raise prices without losing customers — the corpus's See's Candies lesson (Poor Charlie's Almanack; Berkshire meeting remarks).

## Opportunity-cost filter

**When to use:** every allocation of money or time; portfolio additions especially.
**Source:** Berkshire meeting remarks — opportunity cost as "a huge filter in life" (paraphrase-stable).
**Steps:**
1. Identify the best alternative actually available (often: buy more of what is already owned and understood).
2. Require the new idea to beat that alternative, not merely to clear an abstract hurdle rate.
3. If nothing beats the best current holding, do nothing.

## Sit-on-your-hands patience protocol

**When to use:** normal conditions — i.e., most of the time, when nothing is clearly mispriced within the circle.
**Source:** Poor Charlie's Almanack ("the big money is not in the buying and the selling, but in the waiting"); Wesco meeting remarks; Daily Journal Q&A (2017) — the two rules of fishing: fish where the fish are, and don't forget rule number one.
**Steps:**
1. Maintain a prepared mind: keep studying businesses and models while doing nothing.
2. Hold cash without discomfort; treat inactivity as a position.
3. When the rare fat pitch arrives — a big, obvious mispricing inside the circle — bet heavily. The corpus holds that a handful of decisions account for most lifetime returns (USC, 1994, on how few insights the Buffett partnership record required).

## Deserve-what-you-want (trust framework)

**When to use:** career strategy, partnership formation, organizational design, reputation decisions.
**Source:** USC Gould School of Law commencement (2007).
**Steps:**
1. Deliver to the other side what you would want to buy if positions were reversed — the golden rule applied commercially.
2. Compound reliability: perform faithfully on every undertaking, because trust is the asset that compounds behind the scenes.
3. Build toward a seamless web of deserved trust — pick partners and institutions where procedure can be replaced by earned reliability, and be the kind of node such a web can rest on.
