# Mental models — the Holmes method (corpus)

Every named model the Sherlock Holmes canon uses as a thinking tool, each defined, with its origin story and when it applies outside crime. The subject is fictional; the canon is Arthur Conan Doyle's public-domain Holmes stories (1887-1927). Third-person; quotations short and attributed. Where a model is a real logical or scientific idea the stories dramatize, that is noted so the model can be used generally.

## See vs observe

**Definition.** A distinction between passive registration (seeing) and the disciplined extraction of data (observing). Two people face the same scene; only the observer comes away with facts. **Origin.** "A Scandal in Bohemia" (1891): Holmes asks Watson how many steps lead up from the hall — Watson has climbed them countless times and cannot say, having seen but not observed. **When it applies.** At the intake of any investigation: the first question is not "what do I think?" but "what have I actually observed, as opposed to glanced at?" Outside crime: reading logs, code review, reading a room, reading a contract.

## Data before theory (the twist-to-suit trap)

**Definition.** The rule that facts must be gathered before a hypothesis is formed, because a hypothesis held early bends incoming facts toward itself. **Origin.** "A Scandal in Bohemia" (1891): "It is a capital mistake to theorise before one has data. Insensibly one begins to twist facts to suit theories, instead of theories to suit facts." Reinforced by the demand "Data! data! data!… I can't make bricks without clay" (The Copper Beeches, 1892). **When it applies.** Any moment there is pressure to declare a cause before the evidence is in. The model names the specific failure — not "being wrong" but the silent corruption of the evidence-intake by a premature theory.

## No-guessing (inference vs guess)

**Definition.** A hard line between a conclusion supported by an observable chain (inference) and one that is not (a guess), with guesses disqualified rather than merely discouraged. **Origin.** The Sign of the Four (1890): "I never guess. It is a shocking habit—destructive to the logical faculty." **When it applies.** Whenever a candidate explanation is offered. The test: can it be traced back through observed facts, or is it floating? A floating candidate is not weak evidence; it is non-evidence, and admitting it degrades the reasoning.

## Elimination (the impossible-remainder rule)

**Definition.** A subtractive procedure: strike out every explanation that is impossible, and whatever remains — however improbable — is the truth. **Origin.** The Sign of the Four (1890): "When you have eliminated the impossible, whatever remains, however improbable, must be the truth"; the principle recurs across later stories. **When it applies.** Multi-candidate problems where direct confirmation is unavailable but disconfirmation is possible. Its distinctive move: after elimination, improbability is *not* grounds to reject the survivor. This is the deliberate opposite of the "think horses, not zebras" reflex — once the horses are ruled out, the zebra is the answer.

## Reasoning backward (analytic vs synthetic)

**Definition.** Inferring the chain of causes from a known result, rather than predicting a result from causes. The canon calls the forward mode "synthetic" and the backward mode "analytic," and holds the latter far rarer. **Origin.** A Study in Scarlet (1887, pt. 2, ch. 7): "the grand thing is to be able to reason backward… There are fifty who can reason synthetically for one who can reason analytically." **When it applies.** When the outcome already exists and the cause is unknown — a failure that happened, an artifact to be explained, a crime scene. The discipline is to reconstruct the *necessary* antecedents of exactly this result, link by checkable link.

## The dog that didn't bark (absence as data)

**Definition.** Treating the non-occurrence of an expected event as a positive clue of equal rank to any occurrence. **Origin.** Silver Blaze (1892): the stable dog did not bark in the night, which proves the midnight visitor was someone it knew — "the curious incident of the dog in the night-time." **When it applies.** When a scene appears clueless or uniformly consistent. The model prompts the question "what should be here and isn't?" — the missing alarm, the absent log line, the step nobody performed. Widely borrowed outside the canon (law, intelligence, medicine) as "the dog that didn't bark."

## Singularity as a clue (the featureless-crime problem)

**Definition.** The odd, distinctive feature of a case is information-rich and should be pursued first; a featureless case is the genuinely hard one. **Origin.** "The Boscombe Valley Mystery" (1891): "Singularity is almost invariably a clue. The more featureless and commonplace a crime is, the more difficult it is to bring it home." **When it applies.** When triaging where to look. The anomaly that resists the tidy story is the lever, not the nuisance. It pairs with the observation that difficulty and strangeness are inversely related in this method — the bizarre case usually resolves fastest.

## Bizarre ≠ mysterious

**Definition.** A counter-intuitive claim that the more outlandish a case appears, the less mysterious it usually proves, because outlandish features constrain the possible causes sharply. **Origin.** "The Red-Headed League" (1891): "As a rule, the more bizarre a thing is the less mysterious it proves to be. It is your commonplace, featureless crimes which are really puzzling." **When it applies.** As an antidote to being intimidated by a strange-looking problem, and as a warning that the boring, ordinary-looking case deserves more suspicion, not less.

## The observation of trifles

**Definition.** The practice of reading the smallest physical details — the ones a summary discards — as the load-bearing evidence. **Origin.** "The Boscombe Valley Mystery" (1891): "You know my method. It is founded upon the observation of trifles"; "A Case of Identity" (1891): "the little things are infinitely the most important." **When it applies.** Whenever a big, curated narrative is on offer. The model redirects attention from the account to the trifles the account did not think to manage — the cuff, the timestamp, the byte.

## The brain-attic (curated ordinary knowledge)

**Definition.** A model of the mind as a small attic to be stocked deliberately with useful furniture, the rest stored in a "lumber-room" library; broad familiarity with the ordinary is what lets the singular be recognized. **Origin.** A Study in Scarlet (1887) introduces the attic; The Five Orange Pips (1891) refines it ("keep his little brain-attic stocked with all the furniture that he is likely to use"). **When it applies.** As the standing investment behind the method: you cannot spot the anomalous without a baseline of the commonplace. The "ideal reasoner" (Five Orange Pips) can take "a single fact in all its bearings" and run the causal chain both backward and forward from it.

## Scientific use of the imagination (balance of probabilities)

**Definition.** Hypothesis generation treated as a controlled, imaginative act that must remain anchored to a material basis, with conclusions held at the balance of probabilities where evidence is incomplete. **Origin.** The Hound of the Baskervilles (1902): "we balance probabilities and choose the most likely. It is the scientific use of the imagination, but we have always some material basis on which to start our speculation." **When it applies.** When enumerating possibilities (imagination) and when the case cannot yet support certainty (probability). It licenses free hypothesizing and then forbids any hypothesis that has lost its evidential anchor.
