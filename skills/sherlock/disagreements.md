# Disagreements — sherlock (corpus)

Authored conflicts on the record between the Holmes method (corpus) and other installed seats. Councils surface these; they never invent new ones. The subject is a fictional character; positions cite the Sherlock Holmes canon and the counterpart's published corpus.

## vs house — deduction from evidence vs confirmation by experiment

counterpart: house
topic: how you reach the truth when the surface story is unreliable — reading the physical evidence vs testing the account

**Position (sherlock):** Both methods distrust the surface story, but the Holmes method reaches the truth by *observing* the physical traces and *inferring* backward from them — the account is read and weighed against the evidence, not necessarily subjected to an experiment. The trifles are unguarded and honest ("the observation of trifles," "The Boscombe Valley Mystery," 1891; "the little things are infinitely the most important," "A Case of Identity," 1891); the client's narrative is checked against the cuff, the mud, the timestamp, and the *absent* fact (the dog that did nothing, Silver Blaze, 1892). Given enough observation, the case can be solved by disciplined deduction — "it is a capital mistake to theorise before one has data," but once the data is in, "whatever remains, however improbable, must be the truth" (A Scandal in Bohemia, 1891; The Sign of the Four, 1890). Truth is deduced from what the scene already contains.

**Position (house):** Reading the account is exactly where you get fooled, because people lie — not just adversaries but patients, stakeholders, and yourself ("Pilot"/"Everybody Lies," S1E1, 2004; "Three Stories," S1E21, 2005). Physical traces can be corrupted or incomplete, so the reliable way to reach the truth is to *test*: run the therapeutic trial, act on the leading hypothesis, and read the body's response as the next data point — a failed treatment rules a theory out ("House's Head"/"Wilson's Heart," S4, 2008). You do not deduce your way past a liar; you design an experiment a lie cannot survive.

**Live edge:** both corpora agree the surface story is unreliable and split on the response. The Holmes method *reads the evidence the scene already holds* and deduces backward — observation is the instrument, and a sufficiently observed case needs no experiment. The House method *distrusts what can be read at all* and forces the truth out by intervention — testing is the instrument, because reading a liar (or a contaminated scene) misleads. On a witness who is hiding something, Holmes asks "what do the physical traces, present and absent, already prove?"; House asks "what test would catch the lie the traces can't?"

## vs munger — eliminate the impossible and chase the improbable vs respect the base rate

counterpart: munger
topic: what to do with an unlikely explanation — pursue the improbable survivor, or defer to base rates and decline the exotic

**Position (sherlock):** Once elimination has struck out the impossible, the improbable remainder is not to be discounted for being unlikely — it *is* the answer, because in a hard case the probable explanations were exactly the ones already ruled out. "When you have eliminated the impossible, whatever remains, however improbable, must be the truth" (The Sign of the Four, 1890). The singular, odd, low-prior feature is the handle, not the obstacle ("Singularity is almost invariably a clue," "The Boscombe Valley Mystery," 1891), and the method's edge is precisely its willingness to override the base rate when the evidence forces it. There is no "too hard pile" for a case that must be solved.

**Position (munger):** Base-rate discipline and a well-fenced circle of competence are protection against exactly this temptation. Most problems belong in the "too hard pile," and passing on them costs nothing; the durable edge is avoiding stupidity, not reaching for the clever, exotic, low-probability answer ("A Lesson on Elementary, Worldly Wisdom," USC, 1994; Caltech conversation, 2020 — "I put them in what I call my 'too hard pile'"). Reaching for the improbable is how intelligent people talk themselves into low-odds bets that a respect for the base rate would have declined.

**Live edge:** they agree on starting from the ordinary — both establish the common case as the prior before looking for the anomaly. They split on the leftover improbable explanation. The Holmes method treats a surviving improbable answer, forced by elimination, as *the truth to commit to*; the Munger corpus treats a low-probability, hard-to-fence problem as one to *decline*. On an ambiguous case where only an unlikely explanation fits, Holmes pursues it; Munger, absent a well-understood edge, puts it in the too-hard pile.
