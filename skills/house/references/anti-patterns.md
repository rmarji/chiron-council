# Anti-patterns — the House method (corpus)

Everything the *House, M.D.* corpus treats as a reasoning failure, with the corpus's rationale and the failure each produces. Third-person; the character is fictional and the series is the corpus. These are the specific errors the differential-diagnosis discipline exists to prevent.

## 1. Trusting the stated history

**What it is.** Taking the patient's — or stakeholder's, or your own — account at face value and building the diagnosis on it. **Why the corpus rejects it.** The narrative is the least reliable input in the room: people lie deliberately, by omission, and to themselves ("Pilot," S1E1, 2004; "everybody lies"). **The failure it produces.** A diagnosis anchored to a false premise; every downstream test is interpreted through a corrupted starting story, so the error is invisible from inside the frame. **The corrective.** The lie audit — mark claim vs evidence, find corroboration, test the uncorroborated before acting.

## 2. Diagnostic anchoring (first-conclusion bias)

**What it is.** Locking onto the first plausible cause and then reading all later evidence as confirmation of it. **Why the corpus rejects it.** The mind closes to competing candidates the instant it has a satisfying answer, and thereafter reads new evidence as confirmation rather than test. **The failure it produces.** The differential collapses to one candidate prematurely; contradicting findings get explained away rather than believed; the team stops looking exactly when it should look hardest. **The corrective.** Force the differential wider and name a test that could *disprove* the front-runner before committing to it.

## 3. Treating symptoms, not the cause

**What it is.** Suppressing the presenting complaint — the pain, the fever, the error message — while the underlying disease continues unaddressed. **Why the corpus rejects it.** Relief is not diagnosis; a silenced symptom removes the evidence without removing the cause, and often removes the very signal you needed to find the cause. **The failure it produces.** Apparent improvement masking a worsening reality; the disease resurfaces later, harder to diagnose because the symptom that pointed at it has been masked. **The corrective.** Treat only to diagnose (read the response) or to cure the root cause — never to merely quiet the signal.

## 4. Stopping at the first answer that fits

**What it is.** Ending the differential when one candidate merely *could* be true, before the alternatives have been eliminated. **Why the corpus rejects it.** "Could explain it" is not "is the cause"; a diagnosis is only earned by excluding the rivals (diagnosis of exclusion). **The failure it produces.** A confident wrong diagnosis that survives because it was never actually tested against its competitors — the most common way the show's teams fail before House re-opens the case. **The corrective.** Require that the surviving diagnosis be the one that outlasted elimination, not the first that appeared.

## 5. Letting empathy override evidence

**What it is.** Softening, steering, or blunting a finding to spare feelings, keep the peace, or preserve consensus. **Why the corpus rejects it.** Comfort is not a data point; a pleasant falsehood kills the patient while an unwelcome truth saves them ("Pilot," S1E1: "treating illnesses is why we became doctors"). **The failure it produces.** Conclusions shaded toward what the room wants to hear; the correct-but-uncomfortable answer never gets said, and the decision is made on a distortion. **The corrective.** Deliver the finding accurately first; manage the delivery second. Being liked is downstream of being right, not a substitute for it.

## 6. Deferring to authority and prior diagnosis

**What it is.** Accepting the previous doctor's diagnosis, the textbook answer, or the patient's own certainty as the conclusion rather than as a claim to be re-examined. **Why the corpus rejects it.** Authority is a form of reported narrative and inherits its unreliability; nearly every case in the series arrives already misdiagnosed, and the prior misdiagnosis is a *clue*, not a foundation. **The failure it produces.** A wrong answer propagates unchallenged because no one re-derived it from the evidence. **The corrective.** Re-derive from the physical findings; treat the handed-down diagnosis as an anomaly to explain.

## 7. Reaching for the zebra first (and its inverse)

**What it is.** Two symmetric errors. (a) Jumping to the rare, dramatic diagnosis before excluding the common ones. (b) Clinging to the common answer after it has been shown it cannot fit all the findings. **Why the corpus rejects both.** The base rate is a prior, not a verdict: common things are common ("it's never lupus"), but the base rate is overridden the moment the evidence demands it (the one time it *is* lupus, "You Don't Want to Know," S4E8, 2007). **The failure it produces.** (a) Exotic misdiagnoses and needless expensive tests; (b) a wrong common diagnosis that ignores a real, unexplained symptom. **The corrective.** Start common, but follow any real anomaly the common answer leaves unexplained.

## 8. Editing the data to save the theory

**What it is.** When a finding contradicts the working diagnosis, dismissing the finding as error or noise rather than dismissing the diagnosis. **Why the corpus rejects it.** Symptoms don't lie; the diagnosis does. A theory that survives only by ignoring a real, corroborated finding is already dead. **The failure it produces.** A theory that is unfalsifiable in practice because every disconfirmation is explained away — the opposite of a differential. **The corrective.** Trust corroborated findings over cherished theories; when they conflict, the theory goes. (The legitimate exception: a *reported* symptom can be a lie — but a corroborated physical finding cannot be argued away.)

## 9. Exempting your own story from the method

**What it is.** Applying the everybody-lies discipline to everyone except yourself; trusting your own account of your own reasoning and motives without testing it. **Why the corpus rejects it.** The self-narrative is the one unverified story exempted from testing by default, which makes it the most dangerous ("Both Sides Now," S5E24, 2009, where the diagnostician's own self-account is the lie). **The failure it produces.** Motivated reasoning and self-deception that no external test catches because it was never pointed inward. **The corrective.** Run the lie audit on your own account too, especially when you have a stake in a particular conclusion.
