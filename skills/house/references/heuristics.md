# Heuristics — the House method (corpus)

Operational IF/THEN rules distilled from the *House, M.D.* corpus, each attributed. Third-person; the character is fictional and the series is the corpus. These are the executable form of the principles — usable on any diagnosis-shaped problem, medical or not.

## Distrusting the narrative

- **IF** a conclusion rests mainly on someone's stated account, **THEN** treat the account as unverified, mark claim-vs-evidence, and find physical corroboration before acting — everybody lies ("Pilot," S1E1, 2004).
- **IF** a claim benefits the person making it, **THEN** raise the burden of proof on that claim specifically and ask what it would hide if it were false (the lie audit; "Three Stories," S1E21, 2005).
- **IF** you are also a witness in the case (your own decision, your own motives), **THEN** run the same distrust on your own account — it is the story most likely to be exempted from testing ("Both Sides Now," S5E24, 2009).

## Reading the evidence

- **IF** the data and your diagnosis disagree, **THEN** the diagnosis is wrong (or the data is a lie or a bad test); never edit a corroborated finding to save the theory (differential-diagnosis loop, seasons 1-8).
- **IF** a working theory survives only by explaining away one real, corroborated finding, **THEN** the theory is dead — discard it, not the finding ("symptoms don't lie").
- **IF** a reported symptom is uncorroborated, **THEN** treat it as a possible lie and seek objective confirmation before weighting it (distinguishing reported from observed evidence).

## Running the differential

- **IF** you have one plausible cause, **THEN** do not stop — force the differential wider and name at least one test that could disprove it before committing (anti-anchoring; diagnosis of exclusion).
- **IF** you must choose which test to run first, **THEN** run the cheapest test that could *eliminate* the front-runner, not the one that would merely confirm it (test-to-eliminate).
- **IF** a candidate merely "could" explain the findings, **THEN** it is not the diagnosis until the rivals are excluded — a diagnosis is what survives elimination.

## Acting under uncertainty

- **IF** no single test is decisive under time pressure, **THEN** treat the leading hypothesis and read the response as the next test — pre-committing to what "works" and "fails" each mean ("House's Head"/"Wilson's Heart," S4, 2008).
- **IF** the treatment for a diagnosis fails, **THEN** the diagnosis was wrong — re-open the differential; do not add a second treatment for the same disproven diagnosis (failed-treatment update).
- **IF** an intervention is irreversible or its failure is catastrophic, **THEN** do not use it as a diagnostic trial — reserve treat-to-diagnose for reversible or informative-enough actions (reversibility check).

## Simplicity and the anomaly

- **IF** one explanation fits every finding and a rival needs two separate causes, **THEN** prefer the one — unless a real finding stays unexplained ("Occam's Razor," S1E3, 2004).
- **IF** the simple, common answer cannot account for all the symptoms, **THEN** stop dismissing the rare cause and follow the anomaly toward the zebra ("You Don't Want to Know," S4E8, 2007).
- **IF** you are tempted by the rare, dramatic diagnosis first, **THEN** exclude the common causes before reaching for it — the base rate is a prior ("it's never lupus," most of the time).
- **IF** many symptoms appear at once, **THEN** look first for a single upstream error that could cause them all (someone "screwed up") before assuming multiple independent problems ("Occam's Razor," S1E3, 2004).

## Truth over comfort

- **IF** the truth is unwelcome and the comfortable answer is false, **THEN** deliver the truth — it serves the outcome; comfort does not ("Pilot," S1E1, 2004).
- **IF** a finding is being softened to preserve consensus or spare feelings, **THEN** state it accurately first and manage the delivery second — being liked is downstream of being right (corpus stance, "Pilot," S1E1).
- **IF** an authority or a prior diagnosis is offered as the conclusion, **THEN** treat it as a claim to re-derive from the evidence, not a foundation — most cases arrive already misdiagnosed (series format, David Shore).
