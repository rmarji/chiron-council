# Mental models — the House method (corpus)

Every named concept the *House, M.D.* corpus uses as a thinking tool, each defined, with its origin and when it applies. Third-person; the character is fictional and the show is the corpus. Where a model is a real clinical or logical idea the series dramatizes, that origin is named so the model can be used outside medicine.

## Everybody lies

**Definition.** The stated account — history, self-report, testimony, prior explanation — is unreliable by default and must be verified against physical evidence before it can be trusted. **Origin.** The pilot's organizing premise ("Pilot," S1E1, 2004), generalized in "Three Stories" (S1E21, 2005) as a "basic truth of the human condition." **When it applies.** Any time a conclusion rests mainly on what someone said rather than on what can be observed or measured. Outside medicine: incident post-mortems where the reporter's account of "what I did" is the shakiest evidence; user bug reports; any decision built on a stakeholder's narrative.

## The differential diagnosis (the whiteboard method)

**Definition.** The core procedure: enumerate every candidate cause that could produce the confirmed findings, argue them adversarially, and prune the list by testing to eliminate rather than by testing to confirm. Conducted on a whiteboard with the team so candidates are visible and attackable. **Origin.** The structural spine of every episode; taught explicitly to medical students in "Three Stories" (S1E21, 2005). It is a real clinical method the show dramatizes. **When it applies.** Any multi-cause mystery with incomplete evidence — a bug, an outage, a failing plan. The discipline is completeness (list *every* candidate, not the favorite) and falsification (the next test should try to *kill* the front-runner, not flatter it).

## Treat-to-diagnose (the therapeutic trial)

**Definition.** When no single test is decisive under time pressure, administer the treatment for the leading hypothesis and read the body's response as the next diagnostic data point. A treatment that works confirms; a treatment that fails eliminates. **Origin.** A recurring move across the series; its highest-stakes use is the Season 4 finale ("House's Head"/"Wilson's Heart," S4E15-16, 2008). The real-medicine name is the diagnostic/therapeutic trial, or *diagnosis ex juvantibus* ("from that which helps"). **When it applies.** Time-boxed, high-uncertainty problems where waiting for certainty is itself a cost, and where an intervention is reversible or informative enough that its failure is worth the information. The key reframe: a failed treatment is a *successful experiment*.

## Confirmation-by-treatment / the failed-treatment update

**Definition.** The corollary of treat-to-diagnose used as a stopping rule: if the treatment for a diagnosis fails, the diagnosis is wrong — full stop — and the differential re-opens. **Origin.** The loop dynamic across seasons 1-8; dramatized whenever the team's confident diagnosis collapses because the patient worsens on therapy. **When it applies.** Preventing the anchoring failure of "the treatment didn't work, so let's add another treatment for the same diagnosis." The corpus reads a failed fix as evidence against the theory, not as a reason to escalate the fix.

## Occam's razor (the unifying diagnosis)

**Definition.** Prefer the single cause that explains the most findings over a theory requiring multiple independent causes; fewer assumptions, one unifying mechanism. **Origin.** "Occam's Razor" (S1E3, 2004), where the corpus reframes it — the simplest explanation is the one with the fewest *assumptions*, "almost always somebody screwed up," not merely the one with the fewest diseases. **When it applies.** As a first pass on any multi-symptom problem: seek the one root cause. The corpus's refinement is that "one disease" and "simplest" are not synonyms — a single upstream error (a mis-filled prescription, a bad config) can be the parsimonious explanation for many downstream symptoms.

## Hickam's dictum (the counter-principle)

**Definition.** The real-medicine counterweight to Occam: "a patient can have as many diseases as they damn well please." Sometimes the findings genuinely require more than one cause. **Origin.** A standard clinical maxim the "Occam's Razor" episode dramatizes as the losing-then-winning side of the debate; the show repeatedly proves patients can carry two simultaneous problems. **When it applies.** When a single unifying diagnosis forces you to ignore a real finding. Occam is the default prior; Hickam is the override when parsimony can't fit all the evidence.

## The zebra (and "it's never lupus")

**Definition.** A "zebra" is a rare diagnosis; the base-rate discipline says common things are common, so don't reach for the rare cause first ("when you hear hoofbeats, think horses, not zebras"). "It's never lupus" is the corpus's running gag encoding this. **Origin.** The aphorism is real — coined by Dr. Theodore Woodward at the University of Maryland in the late 1940s; the show adopts it and subverts it in "You Don't Want to Know" (S4E8, 2007), the one time it *is* lupus. **When it applies.** As a prior, not a verdict: start with the likely cause, but when the common answer cannot explain all the findings, the anomaly is pointing at a zebra, and the corpus follows it. The tension with Woodward's rule is deliberate — the method's edge is knowing *when* the base rate has been overridden by the evidence.

## Diagnosis of exclusion

**Definition.** Reaching a diagnosis by systematically ruling out every alternative rather than by directly confirming one. **Origin.** Standard clinical reasoning the differential loop embodies; the whiteboard exists to make the exclusion visible. **When it applies.** When no single test names the cause, but a battery of tests can each eliminate a candidate. The residual — what survives every elimination — is the answer. The failure mode it guards against is declaring victory before the alternatives are actually excluded.

## First-conclusion bias (diagnostic anchoring)

**Definition.** The mind's tendency to lock onto its first plausible conclusion and then read all later evidence as confirmation, shutting out competing candidates. **Origin.** Dramatized as a recurring failure across the series — the show's teams repeatedly close the differential too early, and the case only breaks when the anchored diagnosis is re-opened; it is the standard cognitive bias the differential-diagnosis loop is built to counter. **When it applies.** The moment a satisfying answer appears. The countermeasure is to force the differential wider and to name a test that could disprove the front-runner before committing.

## Turn the method inward (self-diagnosis)

**Definition.** Applying the everybody-lies discipline reflexively: your own account of yourself is an unverified narrative and among the most dangerous, because it is exempted from testing by default. **Origin.** "Both Sides Now" (S5E24, 2009), where the diagnostician must diagnose his own hallucination and finds his self-story was the lie. **When it applies.** Whenever the analyst is also a witness — post-mortems of one's own decisions, self-assessment, any case where the reasoner has a stake in a particular conclusion.

## The Sherlock Holmes frame

**Definition.** The whole method as detective work: the patient is a case, symptoms are clues, and the diagnostician is indifferent to the client but obsessed with the puzzle. **Origin.** David Shore built the lead loosely on Arthur Conan Doyle's Sherlock Holmes (creator interviews; the Wilson-as-Watson relationship). **When it applies.** As the governing metaphor — it licenses treating a problem as a mystery to be solved from physical clues rather than a person to be pleased, and it explains why the corpus prizes being right over being liked.
