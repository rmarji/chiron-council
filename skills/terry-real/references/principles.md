# Core Principles — the Terry Real corpus

The full catalog of core beliefs running through Real's five major works and Relational Life Therapy (RLT). Each entry carries its rationale and its source.

## Relationships run on harmony, disharmony, and repair

All close relationships cycle endlessly through closeness, rupture, and return. Real's claim is that this cycle is not a defect to be engineered away but the fundamental rhythm of intimacy, and that the decisive competence is repair — moving from disharmony back to harmony — rather than conflict avoidance (*Us*, 2022; *Fierce Intimacy*, Sounds True, 2018). Couples that last are not the ones who never clash; they are the ones who repair well and often. The practical consequence: when a couple is stuck, the corpus asks "what is blocking repair?" before it asks "what caused the fight?"

## Under stress, the adaptive child takes over

Every person carries an "adaptive child" — the constellation of survival strategies (placating, controlling, attacking, withdrawing) assembled in childhood to cope with an imperfect environment. Under perceived threat, a person is "whooshed" out of the prefrontal, present-day self and into this younger self, who "sees the present through the prism of the past" (*Us*, 2022). Real's principle is that no communication technique works while the adaptive child is driving; the first move is always to restore the "wise adult." He is explicit that the adaptive child never disappears — "the whoosh" happens for life; what improves is the speed of recovery (*Us*, 2022).

## Objective reality has no place in personal relationships

Who is factually correct is the wrong axis entirely. Real teaches that insisting on the objective truth of your account is the first of the five losing strategies, and that the only workable question in intimacy is subjective and forward-looking: what will help us feel close again? (*The New Rules of Marriage*, Ballantine, 2007). His signature line to volatile couples: "You can be right or you can be married" (*Fierce Intimacy*, 2018; widely repeated in talks and RLT trainings).

## Full-respect living

The non-negotiable floor of a healthy relationship is that contempt, name-calling, shaming, and one-up/one-down positioning are off the table at all times — including when you are furious, including when you are provoked. Anger is legitimate; demeaning is not (*The New Rules of Marriage*, 2007; *Us*, 2022). Real sharpens this with "there is nothing that harshness does that loving firmness doesn't do better" (*Us*, 2022). Full-respect living applies inward too: no self-contempt, no collapse into worthlessness.

## Stand "same as": the grandiosity–shame axis

Healthy self-esteem is standing as an equal — neither superior (grandiose, one-up) nor inferior (shame-filled, one-down). Both poles are unrelational: contempt breaks connection loudly, collapse breaks it quietly (*I Don't Want to Talk About It*, Scribner, 1997; *Us*, 2022). Real's distinctive emphasis, against most of the therapy field, is that grandiosity is the more destructive and less treated pole: shame feels bad so people seek help for it; grandiosity feels good so nobody does. He wrote that "the flight from shame into grandiosity lies at the heart of male covert depression" (*I Don't Want to Talk About It*, 1997).

## Repair is a skill, not a mood

Repair has learnable mechanics — remembering love, the feedback wheel, responding with generosity, the dead-stop contract — and waiting to "feel like" repairing is itself a stall (*The New Rules of Marriage*, 2007; *Us*, 2022). The first step is always "remembering love": deliberately reconnecting with the part of you that cares about this person before you open your mouth (*Us*, 2022).

## Family pathology is transmissible — and interruptible

"Family pathology rolls from generation to generation like a fire in the woods, taking down everything in its path, until one person in one generation has the courage to turn and face the flames. That person brings peace to their ancestors and spares the children that follow" (*I Don't Want to Talk About It*, 1997 — Real calls it his most famous line). The corollary principle is "relational heroism": change happens in the specific moments when every muscle in your body pulls toward the old inherited move and you deliberately do the new, connecting one instead (*I Don't Want to Talk About It*, 1997).

## Psychological patriarchy wounds both sexes

Drawing on his collaboration with Carol Gilligan, Real holds that the traditional gender system teaches boys to amputate vulnerability and connection ("the normal traumatization of boys") and teaches girls to preserve connection at the price of honest self-assertion. Both halves produce adults who cannot sustain intimacy: he calls the resulting dynamic the intimacy gap (*How Can I Get Through to You?*, Scribner, 2002; *I Don't Want to Talk About It*, 1997). "Strength is not the absence of vulnerability" (*How Can I Get Through to You?*, 2002).

## Male depression is real, hidden, and externalized

Overt depression is felt and acknowledged; covert depression hides behind its defenses — addiction, workaholism, rage, affairs — because men are socialized to treat depression as shameful weakness. The addictive defense self-medicates the pain while the underlying condition worsens (*I Don't Want to Talk About It*, 1997). "An addict needs shame like a man dying of thirst needs salt water" (*I Don't Want to Talk About It*, 1997).

## From "you and me" to "us" consciousness

The default Western stance in conflict is individualistic and adversarial: two selves negotiating for advantage. Real's later work reframes the relationship itself as the client — an ecosystem you live inside, which you can no more win against than you can win against your own lungs. Moving from "you and me consciousness" (zero-sum) to "us consciousness" (ecological) is the master shift of *Us* (2022). Relational esteem — valuing the "us" as an entity — sits alongside self-esteem as a distinct capacity (*Us*, 2022).

## Intimacy requires standing up, not shutting up

Real rejects both the peace-at-any-price marriage and the combat marriage. "Fierce intimacy" means daring to tell your partner the truth about what hurts and what you want, with love and skill, and daring to hear the same (*Fierce Intimacy*, 2018; *How Can I Get Through to You?*, 2002). Accountability and warmth are not opposites; RLT calls holding someone accountable while staying in loving connection "joining through the truth" (RLT training materials).

## Maturity means parenting your own inner child

"Maturity comes when we tend to our inner children and don't inflict them" on the people we love (*Us*, 2022). Self-regulation is an inside job: your partner may trigger the adaptive child, but soothing it and returning to the wise adult is your work, not theirs.
