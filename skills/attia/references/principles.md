# Principles — the Peter Attia corpus

The full catalog of core beliefs in the published corpus, each with rationale and source. Claims are attributed to *Outlive: The Science and Art of Longevity* (Peter Attia with Bill Gifford, Harmony, 2023) by chapter, or to named episodes of *The Drive* podcast and articles on peterattiamd.com. This is a study aid, not medical advice.

## 1. Healthspan is the real objective, not lifespan alone

The corpus defines longevity as the balance of two things: *lifespan*, how long you live, and *healthspan*, how long you live in good health, free of chronic disease and disability (*Outlive*, ch. 1). The central complaint is that mainstream medicine has become very good at extending lifespan in the acute-crisis sense while letting the last years erode into disease and dependence. Attia's own framing is that longevity "has nothing to do with living forever" and everything to do with the quality of the years you get (*Outlive*, ch. 1-3). Rationale: a decade added at the end of life is only worth pursuing if it is a decade you would actually want to live.

## 2. Medicine 2.0 is reactive; Medicine 3.0 is proactive, personalized, and honest about risk

The corpus tells a three-era story. Medicine 1.0 was pre-scientific (humors, guesswork). Medicine 2.0 is the current evidence-based, germ-theory model that conquered acute and infectious disease but treats chronic disease reactively, waiting until it manifests (*Outlive*, ch. 2-3). Medicine 3.0, the corpus's proposal, differs in four ways: (1) it emphasizes *prevention* over treatment; (2) it treats the patient as a unique individual rather than assuming trial averages apply to everyone; (3) it reasons *explicitly about risk*, including the risk of doing nothing; and (4) it weights healthspan and quality of life as heavily as lifespan (*Outlive*, ch. 3). Rationale: the diseases that now kill us have decade-long silent runways, so a model that only acts once symptoms appear is structurally too late.

## 3. The Four Horsemen are the enemy: heart disease, cancer, neurodegeneration, metabolic disease

The corpus names four disease categories that together cause the large majority of deaths among people who do not smoke and are over 50: atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease (heart attack, stroke), cancer, neurodegenerative disease (chiefly Alzheimer's), and metabolic dysfunction (the spectrum through type 2 diabetes) (*Outlive*, ch. 3). Attia argues these should be attacked individually and early, since each has a long asymptomatic development and shared metabolic roots (*Outlive*, ch. 6-9). Rationale: if you want to extend healthspan, you must delay or prevent the specific things most likely to end it.

## 4. Metabolic health underlies the other Horsemen

The corpus treats metabolic dysfunction — insulin resistance, hyperinsulinemia, ectopic fat, poor glucose handling — as a soil condition that worsens the other three Horsemen, raising risk for cardiovascular disease, several cancers, and Alzheimer's (sometimes framed as "type 3 diabetes") (*Outlive*, ch. 6). Rationale: fixing metabolic health is a high-leverage move because it moves multiple risks at once.

## 5. For chronic disease, early and sustained action dominates

Because atherosclerosis and other chronic diseases accumulate over decades, the corpus holds that acting early and holding the intervention for a long time beats a dramatic late intervention. This is the logic behind measuring and lowering apoB in your 30s and 40s rather than waiting for an event, and behind screening for cancer earlier than default guidelines (*Outlive*, ch. 7-8; Drive #229, 2022). Rationale: risk compounds, so time-in-the-market of prevention is the dominant variable.

## 6. Exercise is the most powerful longevity tool available

The corpus repeatedly states that no other intervention — no drug, no supplement — comes close to exercise for extending both lifespan and healthspan. Cardiorespiratory fitness, measured as VO2 max, is "more strongly associated with reduced mortality risk than any other metric we know of" (Drive #217, 2022; AMA #27, 2021). Moving from the lowest fitness quartile toward average roughly halves ten-year mortality risk in the data the corpus cites (Mandsager et al. 2018, JAMA Network Open, discussed in AMA #27). Rationale: if one lever moves the outcome more than any other, it deserves to be worked first and hardest.

## 7. Muscle mass and strength are protective, especially with age

The corpus argues that strength and muscle mass protect against falls, frailty, and metabolic decline, and that they must be defended deliberately because they fall with age. Attia's memorable framing: "Never in the history of human civilization has a 90-year-old said, 'I wish I had less muscle'" (*Outlive*, ch. 11). Rationale: the Marginal Decade is lost first to loss of strength and stability, so those are non-negotiable training targets.

## 8. There is no universal perfect diet — nutrition is personal biochemistry

The corpus rejects diet tribalism. Attia prefers the term "nutritional biochemistry" and argues the claim that one diet works for every person is "absolutely incorrect" (*Outlive*, ch. 15). What matters is the individual's metabolic response, measured with tools like a continuous glucose monitor, combined with general principles (adequate protein, energy balance). Rationale: because people differ metabolically, the right question is not "which diet" but "how does *this* food affect *my* biomarkers."

## 9. Protein and energy balance are the load-bearing nutrition levers

Within nutrition, the corpus foregrounds two things: total energy balance (are you over- or under-nourished for your body composition goals) and protein intake, which Attia sets far above government RDAs — on the order of 1 gram per pound of body weight per day (roughly 2 g/kg), especially to preserve muscle with age (*Outlive*, ch. 15-16). Rationale: protein defends the muscle that protects the Marginal Decade, and energy balance drives metabolic health.

## 10. Sleep is non-negotiable infrastructure

The corpus treats sleep as foundational to metabolic, cardiovascular, immune, and cognitive health, drawing heavily on the corpus's conversations with Matthew Walker (Drive #47-49, 2019; #126, 2020). Chronic short sleep impairs glucose regulation, raises cardiovascular risk, and hampers the brain's overnight clearance of metabolic waste including beta-amyloid. Rationale: no amount of exercise or nutrition fully compensates for chronically poor sleep.

## 11. Emotional health may be the foundation of the whole enterprise

In the final chapter of *Outlive*, the corpus shifts from physiology to emotional health, arguing that a long, physically fit life is worthless if lived in misery and disconnection. Attia describes his own emotional struggles and states that he now spends roughly as much time on emotional health as on exercise, "as it should be" (*Outlive*, ch. 17). Rationale: healthspan without emotional health is a hollow victory; distress tolerance and relationships are what make the added years worth living.

## 12. Exogenous molecules come last and are graded by evidence

Drugs, hormones, and supplements are the fifth tactical domain — deployed after exercise, nutrition, sleep, and emotional health, and graded by evidence strength. The corpus rates interventions unevenly: rapamycin as promising but unproven in humans, metformin and fasting as uncertain, resveratrol as unsupported (AMA #35, 2022; Drive #272, 2023). Rationale: molecules can help at the margin, but treating them as the first move is exactly the quick-fix error the corpus warns against.

## 13. Biology permits reduced uncertainty, not certainty

The corpus is epistemically humble about medicine: "the best we can hope for is reducing our uncertainty" (*Outlive*). It favors being "evidence-informed" over rigidly "evidence-based," asking how a specific patient resembles or differs from the average trial participant before applying a result. Rationale: population-average evidence rarely maps cleanly onto one individual, so honest probabilistic reasoning beats false precision.

## 14. Decisions are made with a physician who knows your whole picture

Throughout, the corpus frames testing, screening, and treatment as decisions to be made *with a doctor* who has the individual's full context — not as self-prescribed protocols. The published work is a framework for asking better questions, not a substitute for clinical care. Rationale: individualized medical decisions require a clinical relationship the corpus explicitly does not replace.
