# Principles — the Taleb corpus (the Incerto)

The full catalog of core beliefs running through Fooled by Randomness (2001), The Black Swan (2007), The Bed of Procrustes (2010), Antifragile (2012), and Skin in the Game (2018). Each principle carries its rationale and source. Ideas are paraphrased; verbatim lines live in `quotes.md`.

## 1. The consequential is the rare, and the rare is unpredictable

The central claim of the corpus is that history and markets are dominated not by the typical but by the outlier — the Black Swan — and that these outliers are, by their nature, not forecastable from the past. A Black Swan has three attributes: it is an outlier outside the realm of regular expectations; it carries an extreme impact; and human nature makes us concoct explanations for it after the fact, rendering it explainable and predictable in hindsight only (The Black Swan, 2007, Prologue). The practical consequence: effort spent predicting the specific rare event is largely wasted; effort spent on exposure to it is not.

## 2. Randomness comes in two regimes, and confusing them is the root error

Mediocristan is the province of thin-tailed randomness — heights, weights, calorie intake — where no single observation meaningfully moves the total and the bell curve is a fair approximation. Extremistan is the province of fat tails — wealth, book sales, war casualties, market moves, pandemic deaths — where a single observation can dominate the aggregate. The dangerous statistical error is importing Mediocristan tools (the Gaussian, standard deviation, Value-at-Risk) into Extremistan, where they hide precisely the tail that matters (The Black Swan, 2007, ch. 3). Most consequential modern domains are Extremistan.

## 3. Survival is lexically prior to everything else (the ruin problem)

No expected-value calculation redeems a strategy that carries a nonzero probability of ruin, because ruin is an absorbing barrier — once you hit it, the game is over and no future upside is collectible. This is the ergodicity argument: the average outcome across a crowd of players (ensemble probability) is not the outcome experienced by one player through time (time probability) when ruin is possible. "Never cross a river that is on average four feet deep" (Skin in the Game, 2018, ch. 19, "The Logic of Risk Taking"). Rationality, in the corpus, reduces first to survival.

## 4. Fragility is measurable even when the trigger is not

You cannot predict the earthquake, the crash, or the pandemic. But you can measure whether a structure is fragile to volatility — whether it has more downside than upside from a shock (concave) or more upside than downside (convex). This is the corpus's great pivot: it moves the decision problem from forecasting (which fails) to fragility assessment (which works), because fragility is a property of the object today, not a property of the unknown future (Antifragile, 2012, Prologue and Book II).

## 5. The fragile/robust/antifragile triad

Fragile things lose from disorder and break under stress (the sword of Damocles). Robust things are indifferent to disorder and survive unchanged (the Phoenix, reborn identical). Antifragile things gain from disorder, volatility, and stressors up to a point (the Hydra, which grows two heads for each one cut off). Most of the corpus's prescriptions amount to: reduce fragility, and where possible, position for antifragility (Antifragile, 2012, ch. 2). Antifragility is formally a convex response to a stressor — positive sensitivity to increases in volatility.

## 6. Via negativa — subtraction beats addition

Knowledge of what is wrong is more robust than knowledge of what is right; we know falsehood with more confidence than truth (a Popperian inheritance). Therefore the surest improvements come from removing fragilities, harmful interventions, debt, and iatrogenics rather than from adding clever new interventions. In health, in policy, in engineering, and in life, the corpus's default is to subtract the harmful before adding the helpful (Antifragile, 2012, Book VII, "The Ethics of Fragility and Antifragility"; Book V on medicine).

## 7. Skin in the game aligns risk, ethics, and knowledge

Those who make decisions must bear a share of the harm if they are wrong. Skin in the game is at once a moral principle (symmetry — do not transfer your risk to others), an epistemic filter (people who bear downside give better information than those who don't), and an evolutionary filter (fragile risk-takers are eventually removed from the system, cleansing it). Its violation — surgeons who don't bear the patient's outcome, bankers bailed out of their own bets, forecasters with no penalty for being wrong — is the corpus's recurring villain (Skin in the Game, 2018, Prologue and ch. 1).

## 8. Optionality lets you act well without knowing more than others

An option is an asymmetry: limited, known downside and large, open-ended upside. Whoever holds optionality can be right for the wrong reasons, because the payoff structure — not the forecast — does the work. This is why tinkering, trial and error, and convex experimentation beat top-down design under uncertainty: many small bets with capped loss and unbounded payoff let you harvest positive Black Swans without predicting them (Antifragile, 2012, ch. 12, "Thales' Sweet Grapes"; ch. 13–15 on convex tinkering).

## 9. Time is the ultimate stress test — the Lindy effect

For perishable things (humans, mechanical parts), each additional day lived shortens remaining expected life. For non-perishable things (ideas, books, technologies, institutions), the opposite holds: every additional year of survival increases expected remaining life. What has survived long has been filtered by exposure to volatility no forecast can replicate; so, other things equal, prefer the old and time-tested over the new and unproven (Antifragile, 2012, ch. 20, "Time and Fragility"). This is also an epistemic humility: the Lindy filter knows things the theorist doesn't.

## 10. Absence of evidence, silent evidence, and the narrative fallacy

Not seeing evidence of a risk is not evidence that the risk is absent — the turkey has 1,000 days of evidence that the butcher loves it (The Black Swan, 2007, ch. 4). Silent evidence compounds the problem: the record we observe is filtered by survivors, so we systematically overrate the skill of winners and underrate the role of luck (Fooled by Randomness, 2001, ch. 8; The Black Swan, 2007, ch. 8). The narrative fallacy is our compulsion to impose tidy causal stories on random sequences, which further blinds us to the role of chance (The Black Swan, 2007, ch. 6).

## 11. We systematically mistake luck for skill

The founding argument of Fooled by Randomness: in any population subject to randomness, some will succeed by chance, and survivorship bias then presents the lucky survivors as the skilled. A large enough crowd of coin-flipping fund managers guarantees a few spectacular multi-year track records that carry no predictive information. Distinguishing luck from skill requires counting the invisible losers and thinking in terms of the alternative histories that could have happened but didn't (Fooled by Randomness, 2001, ch. 3 and ch. 8).

## 12. Interventionism and iatrogenics — the harm of naive action

The urge to "do something" causes harm when the system would have self-corrected. Iatrogenics — harm done by the healer — generalizes beyond medicine to policy, economics, and management: the fragilista intervenes, suppresses natural volatility, and thereby transfers small visible risks into large hidden ones (e.g., suppressing forest fires builds fuel for a catastrophic one; bailing out fragile firms breeds bigger fragility). The corpus prescribes restraint: intervene where the downside of not acting is catastrophic, otherwise leave the antifragile system to its stressors (Antifragile, 2012, ch. 21, "Medicine, Convexity, and Opacity"; Book II on the fragilista).

## 13. Modernity fragilizes by removing volatility and adding debt

Small, frequent stressors are information and nourishment for antifragile systems; suppressing them (over-stabilized economies, over-managed careers, debt-financed efficiency) removes the feedback and stores up one large, hidden, correlated blowup. Debt in particular converts a robust system into a fragile one by removing slack and redundancy — the buffers that let a system absorb a shock (Antifragile, 2012, Prologue, ch. 3 "The Cat and the Washing Machine," and the critique of "touristification").

## 14. Ethics is largely about symmetry and not transferring risk

The good, in the corpus, is bound up with symmetry: do not get the upside of a risk while others get the downside. The gravest ethical failures are asymmetries — the consultant, the bureaucrat, the interventionist who is insulated from the consequences of their prescriptions. Courage, correspondingly, is the willingness to take a personal risk for a collective good; it is the only virtue you cannot fake (Skin in the Game, 2018, Prologue, ch. 1, and the minority-rule material).

## 15. Epistemic humility and the primacy of the practitioner

The corpus trusts the doer over the talker: the artisan, the trader, the entrepreneur — people whose beliefs are disciplined by real exposure — over the credentialed expert whose errors cost them nothing (the "Intellectual Yet Idiot"). Practical, tacit, Lindy-tested knowledge (know-how) outranks theorized, articulable knowledge (know-what) precisely because it has survived contact with reality (Skin in the Game, 2018, ch. 18 "How to Be Rational about Rationality," and The Intellectual Yet Idiot, 2016).
