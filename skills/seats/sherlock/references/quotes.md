# Quotes — the Holmes method (corpus)

Short attributed excerpts from Arthur Conan Doyle's public-domain Sherlock Holmes canon (1887-1927), verified against Wikisource/Wikiquote and standard full-text editions. Wording is given verbatim where checked; lines whose exact phrasing varies across editions are marked **paraphrase-stable**. The canon is public domain, so quotation is legally free — but this seat is a reasoning lens, not a fan page: quote sparingly, always attribute to the story, and never present the fiction as a substitute for the real evidence-gathering a case requires. No scene-length excerpts.

## Observation

- "You see, but you do not observe. The distinction is clear." (Holmes to Watson; "A Scandal in Bohemia," 1891 — after Watson cannot say how many steps lead up to the sitting room he has visited hundreds of times.)
- "It has long been an axiom of mine that the little things are infinitely the most important." (Holmes; "A Case of Identity," 1891.)
- "You know my method. It is founded upon the observation of trifles." (Holmes to Watson; "The Boscombe Valley Mystery," 1891.)
- "The world is full of obvious things which nobody by any chance ever observes." (Holmes; The Hound of the Baskervilles, 1902, ch. 3.)
- "It is, of course, a trifle, but there is nothing so important as trifles." (Holmes; "The Man with the Twisted Lip," 1891 — paraphrase-stable; wording varies slightly across editions, e.g. "there is nothing so important as trifles.")

## Data before theory

- "It is a capital mistake to theorise before one has data. Insensibly one begins to twist facts to suit theories, instead of theories to suit facts." (Holmes; "A Scandal in Bohemia," 1891. In context: "I have no data yet. It is a capital mistake…")
- "Data! data! data!" he cried impatiently. "I can't make bricks without clay." (Holmes; "The Adventure of the Copper Beeches," 1892.)
- "No, no: I never guess. It is a shocking habit—destructive to the logical faculty." (Holmes to Watson; The Sign of the Four, 1890, ch. 1, "The Science of Deduction.")

## Elimination and the improbable

- "How often have I said to you that when you have eliminated the impossible, whatever remains, however improbable, must be the truth?" (Holmes to Watson; The Sign of the Four, 1890, ch. 6. The principle recurs in later stories, e.g. "The Adventure of the Beryl Coronet," 1892, and "The Adventure of the Blanched Soldier," 1926.)

## Reasoning backward

- "In solving a problem of this sort, the grand thing is to be able to reason backward. That is a very useful accomplishment, and a very easy one, but people do not practise it much. In the every-day affairs of life it is more useful to reason forward, and so the other comes to be neglected. There are fifty who can reason synthetically for one who can reason analytically." (Holmes; A Study in Scarlet, 1887, pt. 2, ch. 7, "The Conclusion.")

## Absence as data

- Inspector Gregory: "Is there any other point to which you would wish to draw my attention?" — Holmes: "To the curious incident of the dog in the night-time." — Gregory: "The dog did nothing in the night-time." — Holmes: "That was the curious incident." (Silver Blaze, 1892. The silence proves the midnight visitor was known to the dog.)

## Singularity and the commonplace

- "Singularity is almost invariably a clue. The more featureless and commonplace a crime is, the more difficult it is to bring it home." (Holmes; "The Boscombe Valley Mystery," 1891.)
- "As a rule, the more bizarre a thing is the less mysterious it proves to be. It is your commonplace, featureless crimes which are really puzzling." (Holmes; "The Red-Headed League," 1891.)
- "Depend upon it, there is nothing so unnatural as the commonplace." (Holmes; "A Case of Identity," 1891.)
- "Life is infinitely stranger than anything which the mind of man could invent." (Holmes; "A Case of Identity," 1891.)

## Imagination anchored to evidence

- "Say, rather, into the region where we balance probabilities and choose the most likely. It is the scientific use of the imagination, but we have always some material basis on which to start our speculation." (Holmes to Dr. Mortimer, who has accused him of guesswork; The Hound of the Baskervilles, 1902.)

## The stocked mind

- "A man should keep his little brain-attic stocked with all the furniture that he is likely to use, and the rest he can put away in the lumber-room of his library, where he can get it if he wants it." (Holmes; "The Five Orange Pips," 1891. The brain-attic image first appears in A Study in Scarlet, 1887.)
- "The ideal reasoner," he remarked, "would, when he had once been shown a single fact in all its bearings, deduce from it not only all the chain of events which led up to it but also all the results which would follow from it." (Holmes; "The Five Orange Pips," 1891.)
- "From a drop of water a logician could infer the possibility of an Atlantic or a Niagara without having seen or heard of one or the other." (Holmes's article "The Book of Life," read by Watson; A Study in Scarlet, 1887, ch. 2, "The Science of Deduction.")
