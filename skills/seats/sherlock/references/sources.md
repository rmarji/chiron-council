# Sources — the Holmes method (corpus)

Complete provenance for this seat. The subject is a **fictional character**, Sherlock Holmes, so the "corpus" is Arthur Conan Doyle's canon of Holmes stories: four novels and fifty-six short stories published between 1887 (*A Study in Scarlet*) and 1927 (*The Case-Book of Sherlock Holmes*). The entire canon is now in the **public domain** in the United States. This seat distills the depicted **method** of observation and reasoning, cited by story. Primary corpus (the specific works) first; notes on the public-domain status and the method's real-logic lineage second.

## Primary corpus — cited works (chronological)

- **A Study in Scarlet** (1887). The first Holmes novel. Introduces "The Science of Deduction" (ch. 2), within which Watson reads Holmes's magazine article "The Book of Life" ("From a drop of water a logician could infer the possibility of an Atlantic or a Niagara…") and Holmes states the brain-attic theory of mind. Part 2, ch. 7 ("The Conclusion") contains the reasoning-backward passage and the analytic-vs-synthetic distinction ("There are fifty who can reason synthetically for one who can reason analytically").
- **The Sign of the Four** (1890). The second Holmes novel. Ch. 1, "The Science of Deduction," contains "I never guess. It is a shocking habit—destructive to the logical faculty." Ch. 6 contains the elimination rule: "when you have eliminated the impossible, whatever remains, however improbable, must be the truth."
- **"A Scandal in Bohemia"** (July 1891, *The Strand Magazine*; collected in *The Adventures of Sherlock Holmes*, 1892). Source of "You see, but you do not observe" and the data-before-theory rule ("It is a capital mistake to theorise before one has data. Insensibly one begins to twist facts to suit theories, instead of theories to suit facts").
- **"The Red-Headed League"** (August 1891, *The Strand*; *The Adventures*). Source of "As a rule, the more bizarre a thing is the less mysterious it proves to be. It is your commonplace, featureless crimes which are really puzzling."
- **"A Case of Identity"** (September 1891, *The Strand*; *The Adventures*). Source of "the little things are infinitely the most important," "there is nothing so unnatural as the commonplace," and "life is infinitely stranger than anything which the mind of man could invent."
- **"The Boscombe Valley Mystery"** (October 1891, *The Strand*; *The Adventures*). Source of "the observation of trifles" and "Singularity is almost invariably a clue. The more featureless and commonplace a crime is, the more difficult it is to bring it home."
- **"The Five Orange Pips"** (November 1891, *The Strand*; *The Adventures*). Source of the "ideal reasoner" passage and the refined brain-attic image ("keep his little brain-attic stocked with all the furniture that he is likely to use").
- **"The Man with the Twisted Lip"** (December 1891, *The Strand*; *The Adventures*). Source of "there is nothing so important as trifles" (paraphrase-stable).
- **"The Adventure of Silver Blaze"** (December 1892, *The Strand*; collected in *The Memoirs of Sherlock Holmes*, 1894). Source of "the curious incident of the dog in the night-time" — the canonical illustration of absence as evidence.
- **"The Adventure of the Copper Beeches"** (June 1892, *The Strand*; *The Adventures*). Source of "Data! data! data!… I can't make bricks without clay."
- **The Hound of the Baskervilles** (serialized 1901-1902, *The Strand*; published as a novel 1902). Source of "we balance probabilities and choose the most likely. It is the scientific use of the imagination…" and "the world is full of obvious things which nobody by any chance ever observes."

## The recurring "eliminate the impossible" principle

The elimination maxim is stated first in *The Sign of the Four* (1890, ch. 6) and recurs, with variations, in later stories including "The Adventure of the Beryl Coronet" (1892) and "The Adventure of the Blanched Soldier" (1926). This seat cites *The Sign of the Four* as the primary source and treats the recurrences as evidence that the principle is load-bearing across the canon rather than a one-off line.

## Real-logic lineage the corpus dramatizes

- **Observation → inference** as a disciplined skill echoes the diagnostic method of Doyle's teacher **Dr. Joseph Bell** of the Edinburgh medical school, the acknowledged real-world model for Holmes's technique of reading a person's history from physical signs.
- **Reasoning backward** (analytic reconstruction of causes from a result) is a general form of abductive/retrospective inference; the canon's contribution is to name it and to claim it is far rarer than forward reasoning.
- **Absence as evidence** ("the dog that didn't bark," Silver Blaze, 1892) has been adopted widely outside fiction — in law, intelligence analysis, and clinical reasoning — as shorthand for treating an expected-but-missing fact as a positive clue.
- **Elimination** (strike out the impossible; accept the improbable remainder) is a disjunctive-syllogism discipline; the method's distinctive twist is refusing to reject the survivor for improbability once the alternatives are ruled out — a deliberate counter to the base-rate reflex.

## Provenance notes

- **Mode: corpus.** The seat speaks in the third person about "the Holmes method" and "the canon," never first-person-as-Holmes. The character is fictional; this is a reasoning lens, not detective advice, and not a substitute for gathering the real evidence a case requires.
- **License: n/a (public domain).** The Sherlock Holmes canon is in the public domain in the United States, so both the ideas (never copyrightable, 17 U.S.C. §102(b)) and the exact expression may be quoted freely. This seat nonetheless quotes short and attributed by design, to keep it a method lens rather than a text dump.
- **Alias:** "The Deductionist" — the archetype name under which this seat can be de-named if ever required.
- **Attribution accuracy:** the "dog did nothing in the night-time" line is spoken by Inspector Gregory, not Holmes; Holmes frames it ("that was the curious incident"). "The Science of Deduction" is the title of two separate chapters — A Study in Scarlet ch. 2 and The Sign of the Four ch. 1.
