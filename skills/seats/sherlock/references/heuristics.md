# Heuristics — the Holmes method (corpus)

Operational decision rules distilled from the Sherlock Holmes canon, in IF/THEN form, each attributed to a story. The subject is fictional; the canon is Arthur Conan Doyle's public-domain Holmes stories (1887-1927). Third-person; quotations short and attributed. These are rules for reading a situation from evidence, usable well beyond crime.

## Observation

- **IF** you are about to reason about a scene, **THEN** first force out the data you *observed* as distinct from what you merely *saw* — list the details a glance would skip ("A Scandal in Bohemia," 1891: "You see, but you do not observe").
- **IF** a big, salient fact and a small physical trifle disagree, **THEN** weight the trifle — the little things are "infinitely the most important" and the unguarded detail is more honest than the curated one ("A Case of Identity," 1891; "The Boscombe Valley Mystery," 1891).
- **IF** the scene looks like it has nothing worth observing, **THEN** assume the opposite and look harder — "the world is full of obvious things which nobody by any chance ever observes" (The Hound of the Baskervilles, 1902).

## Theory discipline

- **IF** the data is not yet gathered, **THEN** refuse to form a theory — a theory held early "insensibly" twists the facts that arrive later toward it ("A Scandal in Bohemia," 1891).
- **IF** you feel confident in a cause before testing anything, **THEN** treat that confidence as a warning sign of a premature theory and go collect evidence — "I can't make bricks without clay" (The Copper Beeches, 1892).
- **IF** a candidate explanation cannot be traced back through observed facts, **THEN** reject it as a guess rather than entertain it — guessing is "destructive to the logical faculty" (The Sign of the Four, 1890).

## Elimination and the improbable

- **IF** you have a set of possible explanations, **THEN** work subtractively — strike out the impossible ones by disconfirming test, not the likely ones by preference (The Sign of the Four, 1890).
- **IF** only one explanation survives elimination and it seems unlikely, **THEN** commit to it anyway — "whatever remains, however improbable, must be the truth" (The Sign of the Four, 1890).
- **IF** an investigation keeps looping over already-ruled-out probable causes, **THEN** stop and accept the improbable survivor; the probable answers were the ones you already eliminated (The Sign of the Four, 1890).

## Reasoning backward

- **IF** the outcome is known but the cause is not, **THEN** reason backward from the *exact* result to its necessary antecedents rather than forward from a plausible-sounding story (A Study in Scarlet, 1887).
- **IF** a forward narrative "explains" the result but rests on unchecked links, **THEN** treat it as synthetic guesswork and convert each link into a fact you can verify (A Study in Scarlet, 1887).

## Absence and singularity

- **IF** a scene seems clueless or perfectly consistent, **THEN** ask what expected fact is *missing* and reason from the dog that did not bark (Silver Blaze, 1892).
- **IF** one feature of a case is odd or does not fit, **THEN** pursue that singular feature first as the handle, not last as noise — "Singularity is almost invariably a clue" ("The Boscombe Valley Mystery," 1891).
- **IF** a case looks bizarre and intimidating, **THEN** expect it to resolve *more* easily, and reserve extra suspicion for the commonplace, featureless case — "the more bizarre a thing is the less mysterious it proves to be" ("The Red-Headed League," 1891).

## Sources and certainty

- **IF** a conclusion rests on someone's account, **THEN** separate the account from the evidence and re-derive it from physical traces before trusting it ("The Boscombe Valley Mystery," 1891).
- **IF** the observation is incomplete, **THEN** report the balance of probabilities and the material basis it rests on, and refuse a certainty the data cannot carry — inference is "the scientific use of the imagination," not proof (The Hound of the Baskervilles, 1902).
- **IF** you must generate explanations, **THEN** imagine freely but discard any candidate with no material basis to start from (The Hound of the Baskervilles, 1902).
- **IF** you cannot yet tell what is singular about a case, **THEN** first establish the baseline of the ordinary — the anomaly is only visible against a well-stocked stock of the commonplace (The Five Orange Pips, 1891).
