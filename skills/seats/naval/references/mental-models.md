# Mental Models — Naval Ravikant

Every named model or concept the corpus uses, defined, with origin and when it applies.

## The wealth equation

Wealth = specific knowledge x accountability x leverage (tweetstorm, May 2018; Almanack, "Building Wealth"). Multiplicative, not additive: a zero in any factor zeroes the output. Specific knowledge is the un-trainable edge; accountability is risk taken under your own name (which is why it earns equity rather than wages); leverage multiplies the result. Apply whenever diagnosing why someone works hard but doesn't compound — the answer is almost always a missing factor, not insufficient effort.

## Specific knowledge

"Knowledge that you cannot be trained for. If society can train you, it can train someone else, and replace you" (tweetstorm, 2018). Found by pursuing genuine curiosity and passion rather than whatever is hot; it "will feel like play to you but will look like work to others" (tweetstorm, 2018); it is often on the bleeding edge of technology or art, highly technical or creative, and transmitted by apprenticeship, not classroom ("Arm Yourself With Specific Knowledge," How to Get Rich podcast, 2019). Use when someone asks "what should I work on?" — the corpus's answer is never a market scan first; it is an inventory of obsessions that the market happens to value.

## The leverage taxonomy: labor, capital, code, media

Three classes of business leverage (tweetstorm, 2018; "Fortunes Require Leverage" and following episodes, How to Get Rich podcast, 2019):

- **Labor** — other people working for you. The oldest form, the most fought-over, and the most expensive to manage. The corpus is openly unimpressed by headcount as a status metric.
- **Capital** — money deployed to multiply decisions. The dominant leverage of the twentieth century; scales well but requires persuading someone to hand it over, and it decays without judgment.
- **Code and media** — "products with no marginal cost of replication" (tweetstorm, 2018). Build once, replicate infinitely.

## Permissionless leverage

The property that distinguishes code and media from labor and capital: no gatekeeper. "Code and media are permissionless leverage" (tweetstorm, 2018) — you can write software, publish essays, or record podcasts without anyone's approval, and they "work for you while you sleep" (paraphrase of the same tweet). "An army of robots is freely available — it's just packed in data centers" (tweetstorm, 2018). Apply when a user is waiting to be picked — for funding, a promotion, a platform. The corpus's move is always: stop asking, start shipping through the permissionless channels.

## Productize yourself

The two-word compression of the whole wealth thread ("Productize Yourself," How to Get Rich podcast, 2019). "Yourself" carries specific knowledge and authenticity; "productize" carries leverage and scale. The test of a career move: does it push value creation from person-shaped (hours) to product-shaped (assets)? Applies to consultants, creators, and employees alike.

## Accountability (and taking risk under your own name)

"Embrace accountability, and take business risks under your own name. Society will reward you with responsibility, equity, and leverage" (tweetstorm, 2018). Accountability is the price of equity: the market pays ownership-level returns only to those who can be publicly blamed for failure. Modern society lets you cap the downside — bankruptcy and reputational failure are survivable — while the upside of a public win compounds into the fourth kind of luck. Apply when someone hides behind a team, a brand, or anonymity and wonders why they capture none of the upside.

## Skin in the game (as the corpus uses it)

Adjacent to accountability and borrowed approvingly from Nassim Taleb: trust the practitioner whose own outcomes ride on their advice, and be one. In the corpus it appears as the reason to prefer founders over commentators, investors who eat their own cooking, and advice from people who paid for their opinions ("Take Accountability to Earn Equity," How to Get Rich podcast, 2019; Almanack). It also powers the refusal heuristic: discount forecasts from people who lose nothing by being wrong.

## The principal-agent problem

From the podcast series ("Principal-Agent Problem: Act Like an Owner," How to Get Rich podcast, 2019), with Naval citing Julius Caesar's "If you want it done, then go. If not, then send." The principal (owner) works with the intensity and care of someone spending their own money; the agent (employee) optimizes for looking good to the principal. Small companies beat large ones because they have fewer agents. Apply in two directions: as a builder, act like a principal in everything (that is what earns principal-level rewards); as an organizer, design incentives so agents become principals — equity, ownership, clear accountability.

## Status games vs wealth games

Two incompatible payoff structures (tweetstorm, 2018; "Seek Wealth, Not Money or Status," How to Get Rich podcast, 2019). Wealth games are positive-sum: makers trade and everyone can win. Status games are zero-sum: rank requires someone below you, so the moves are attack and defense. The model's diagnostic power: when someone attacks a builder, ask which game the attacker is playing. Its prescriptive power: politics, academia hierarchies, and social media dunking are all status games that consume the energy wealth games need.

## The four kinds of luck

From the podcast series ("Making Money Isn't About Luck," How to Get Rich podcast, 2019), building on James Austin's Chase, Chance, and Creativity (1978):

1. **Blind luck** — pure chance, uncontrollable.
2. **Luck from hustle** — motion stirs the pot; opportunities collide with the energetic.
3. **Luck from awareness** — the prepared mind spots what others miss.
4. **Luck from character** — you become known for something distinctive, so opportunity seeks you out. The corpus's summary: build your character so that "luck becomes your destiny" (paraphrase).

The fourth is the only kind that scales and compounds; the "luck surface area" framing (Luck = Doing x Telling, after Jason Roberts) operationalizes it.

## Compounding — in wealth, knowledge, and relationships

"All the returns in life, whether in wealth, relationships, or knowledge, come from compound interest" (tweetstorm, 2018). In relationships specifically: long-standing trust removes transaction costs, makes deals fast, and concentrates the best opportunities among people who have already proven themselves to each other over decades ("Play Long-Term Games With Long-Term People," How to Get Rich podcast, 2019). Corollary from the Almanack: "99% of effort is wasted" (Almanack, "Building Judgment") — most projects, meetings, and relationships don't compound; the discipline is finding the 1% that do and going all-in.

## Judgment and leverage as multiplier pair

"Leverage is a force multiplier for your judgment" (Almanack, "Building Judgment"). Without leverage, bad judgment is cheap; with leverage, it is catastrophic — and good judgment is priceless. This is why the corpus ranks learning-to-decide above working-more-hours, and why it treats demonstrated judgment (a public track record of good calls) as the most bankable modern credential.

## Kelly criterion / avoid ruin

Borrowed from betting mathematics ("Kelly Criterion: Avoid Ruin," How to Get Rich podcast, 2019): never make bets that can take you out of the game entirely, no matter the expected value — ruin ends the compounding that all returns depend on. In the corpus this covers jail, reputation destruction, and total financial wipeout. Apply before any high-upside decision: cap the downside first.

## Desire as suffering contract

"Desire is a contract you make with yourself to be unhappy until you get what you want" (Almanack, "Learning Happiness"; Knowledge Project #18, 2017). Desires are not free — each one converts the present into a waiting room. The model does not forbid desire; it prices it. Apply when someone stacks goals reflexively: make the contracts explicit, keep one big one, cancel the rest.

## Happiness as a skill and a default state

"Happiness is what's there when you remove the sense that something is missing in your life" (Almanack, "Learning Happiness"). Not an achievement but a subtraction — of desire, rumination, and social comparison — and trainable like fitness (Knowledge Project #18, 2017; Tim Ferriss Show #473, 2020). Companion line: "Peace is happiness at rest; happiness is peace in motion" (Almanack). The wealth game buys the freedom to do this training; it cannot substitute for it.

## Rational Buddhism

The corpus's label for its spiritual stance (Almanack, "Learning Happiness"): take the Buddhist toolkit — meditation, impermanence, the illusion of self as useful hypothesis — but verify everything against direct experience and science, discarding the mystical claims that can't be tested. "Meditation is intermittent fasting for the mind" (Almanack). Apply when a user wants the calm without the dogma.

## Retirement, redefined

"Retirement is when you stop sacrificing today for an imaginary tomorrow" (Almanack, "Learning Happiness"; discussed on Joe Rogan Experience #1309, 2019). Three routes: save enough that passive income covers burn; drive burn to zero (the monk route); or do work you love so completely that today needs no sacrifice. The model reframes "when can I retire?" into "which of the three routes am I actually on?"
