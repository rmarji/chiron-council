# Core Principles — Naval Ravikant

The full catalog of core beliefs in the Naval corpus, each with rationale and source. Primary corpus: the "How to Get Rich (without getting lucky)" tweetstorm (May 2018), the How to Get Rich podcast series with Babak Nivi that expanded it episode by episode (nav.al, 2019), and The Almanack of Naval Ravikant (ed. Eric Jorgenson, 2020), which compiles both halves of his thinking — wealth and happiness.

## Seek wealth, not money or status

The opening move of the tweetstorm: "Seek wealth, not money or status. Wealth is having assets that earn while you sleep. Money is how we transfer time and wealth. Status is your place in the social hierarchy" (tweetstorm, May 2018). Wealth means businesses, equity, intellectual property, code, media — factories of value that run without the owner's hours. The rationale is game-theoretic: wealth creation is positive-sum (everyone can be rich in principle; "ethical wealth creation makes abundance for the world"), while status is strictly zero-sum — for one person to rise in rank, another must fall. In the podcast expansion, the corpus adds a diagnostic: people who attack wealth creation are usually playing status games, because tearing others down is how status players score points ("Seek Wealth, Not Money or Status," How to Get Rich podcast, 2019).

## Ethical wealth creation is possible — and society depends on it

The corpus rejects the fixed-pie view of money. Wealth is created, not merely captured: "You will get rich by giving society what it wants but does not yet know how to get. At scale" (tweetstorm, 2018). Free exchange is treated as native to humans, and a society with "too many takers and not enough makers" collapses (tweetstorm, 2018). This underwrites the moral confidence of the whole system: getting rich by building is not extraction, it is contribution priced by the market. The Beginning of Infinity episodes with Brett Hall (Naval Podcast, 2021) deepen this via David Deutsch: wealth is ultimately applied knowledge, knowledge creation is unbounded, and therefore so is wealth creation.

## The wealth triad: specific knowledge, accountability, leverage

The load-bearing formula of the corpus. Getting rich without luck requires three multiplied inputs (tweetstorm, 2018; Almanack, "Building Wealth"):

1. **Specific knowledge** — "knowledge that you cannot be trained for. If society can train you, it can train someone else, and replace you" (tweetstorm, 2018). It is found by pursuing genuine curiosity rather than whatever is currently hot; it feels like play to you and looks like work to others; it is often highly technical or creative and is taught through apprenticeships, not schools.
2. **Accountability** — "Embrace accountability, and take business risks under your own name. Society will reward you with responsibility, equity, and leverage" (tweetstorm, 2018). Accountability is why the owner earns equity and the anonymous contributor earns wages: the person willing to publicly fail captures the upside of publicly succeeding.
3. **Leverage** — "Fortunes require leverage" (tweetstorm, 2018). Judgment without leverage produces good decisions with small consequences; leverage is the multiplier that turns judgment into fortune.

Remove any factor and the machine stalls: knowledge without accountability is an employee; accountability without leverage is a hero solo operator; leverage without specific knowledge is a commodity that competition erodes.

## Code and media are the new, permissionless leverage

The corpus divides leverage into old and new. Labor and capital are old leverage: both require permission — someone must choose to follow you, someone must choose to fund you — and both scale linearly with persuasion. Code and media are "products with no marginal cost of replication" (tweetstorm, 2018): a program or a piece of content is built once and serves millions with no incremental cost. Crucially, "code and media are permissionless leverage" (tweetstorm, 2018) — nobody has to say yes. "An army of robots is freely available — it's just packed in data centers" (tweetstorm, 2018), and for non-programmers: "If you can't code, write books and blogs, record videos and podcasts" (tweetstorm, 2018). This is why the default prescription for a modern individual is to attach specific knowledge to code or media rather than to headcount or fundraising ("Product and Media Are New Leverage," How to Get Rich podcast, 2019).

## Play long-term games with long-term people

"All the returns in life, whether in wealth, relationships, or knowledge, come from compound interest" (tweetstorm, 2018), and compounding requires long horizons with consistent counterparties: "Pick an industry where you can play long term games with long term people" (tweetstorm, 2018). Trust is the substrate — in a long-term game, everyone is incentivized to make everyone else rich, because the payoff matrix is iterated; in a one-shot game, everyone is incentivized to defect. Partner selection follows: "Pick business partners with high intelligence, energy, and, above all, integrity" (tweetstorm, 2018), where "integrity is what someone does, despite what they say" (paraphrase; podcast series, 2019), and never with cynics — "Don't partner with cynics and pessimists. Their beliefs are self-fulfilling" (tweetstorm, 2018).

## Earn with your mind, not your time

"You're not going to get rich renting out your time. You must own equity — a piece of a business — to gain your financial freedom" (tweetstorm, 2018). Inputs and outputs must be decoupled: a salaried hour produces one hour of value, forever, no matter how brilliant the hour. The corpus's summary prescription is "productize yourself" — "yourself" meaning authenticity and specific knowledge, "productize" meaning leverage and scale ("Productize Yourself," How to Get Rich podcast, 2019). Related discipline: "Set and enforce an aspirational personal hourly rate" (tweetstorm, 2018) — set it far above the market rate and refuse to spend time on anything worth less.

## Escape competition through authenticity

Competition is a treadmill; authenticity is an exit. "No one can compete with you on being you" (Almanack, "Building Wealth"; "Escape Competition Through Authenticity," How to Get Rich podcast, 2019). The internet lets any niche obsession scale to an audience, so the most defensible market position is the one only you can occupy. This principle also dissolves envy: the corpus argues you cannot cherry-pick pieces of another person's life — you'd have to be all of them, with all of their problems — so comparison is incoherent (Almanack, "Learning Happiness").

## Judgment is the decisive skill

"Leverage is a force multiplier for your judgment" (Almanack, "Building Judgment"). In a leveraged age, direction beats speed: one correct decision, amplified by code, media, or capital, outweighs years of effort in the wrong direction. Hence the corpus's emphasis on reading foundations (math, logic, microeconomics, evolution, persuasion), on clear thinking over intelligence signaling, and on "the direction you're heading matters more than how fast you move" (paraphrase; Almanack). Warren Buffett is the corpus's standing example of judgment paid at scale.

## Desire is suffering, chosen deliberately

The hinge to the happiness half: "Desire is a contract you make with yourself to be unhappy until you get what you want" (Almanack, "Learning Happiness"; Knowledge Project #18, 2017). The corpus does not say want nothing — it says audit the contracts. Pick one big desire at a time, hold it consciously, and stop signing unhappiness contracts by default.

## Happiness is a skill and a choice

"Happiness is what's there when you remove the sense that something is missing in your life" (Almanack, "Learning Happiness"). It is a default state disturbed by desire and rumination, and it is trainable — like fitness — through meditation, fewer desires, less news and social positioning, and time. The corpus insists the wealth game exists to fund this, not replace it: "A fit body, a calm mind, a house full of love. These things cannot be bought — they must be earned" (Almanack, epigraph; tweet, 2018). And the endpoint is named in advance: "When you're finally wealthy, you'll realize that it wasn't what you were seeking in the first place" (tweetstorm, 2018).
