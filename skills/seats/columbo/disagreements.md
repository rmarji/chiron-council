# Disagreements — columbo (corpus)

Authored conflicts on the record between the Columbo method (corpus) and other installed seats. Councils surface these; they never invent new ones. The subject is a fictional character; positions cite the series and the counterpart's corpus.

## vs house — draw it out vs force it out

counterpart: house
topic: how you get the truth out of someone who is lying to you — disarm and let them reveal it, or confront and test until the lie breaks

**Position (columbo):** People surrender the truth when they feel *safe and superior*, not when they feel caught. The Columbo method disarms — the rumpled coat, the fumbling, the deference — so a guilty adversary relaxes, condescends, and talks; then it worries the one nagging detail quietly, never tipping its hand, and lets the suspect's own need to explain build the cage until a parting question closes it ("Prescription: Murder," 1968: "they underestimate you, and that's where you trip them up"; "Suitable for Framing," S1E4, 1971, where the arrogant suspect over-explains into the trap). The truth is *drawn out* of a person who thinks they are winning.

**Position (house):** Everybody lies, and the reliable path to truth is not to be told it but to *catch* it — distrust the stated account, verify it against physical evidence, and design a test a lie cannot survive. The narrative is the least trustworthy input; corroboration and disconfirmation are the tools, and a comfortable answer that hasn't been tested is worthless ("Pilot"/"Everybody Lies," S1E1, 2004; treat-to-diagnose, "House's Head"/"Wilson's Heart," S4, 2008). Rapport is beside the point; the experiment settles it.

**Live edge:** both corpora start from the same premise — the offered story is unreliable and the tidy version is suspect — and split on the instrument. The Columbo method *disarms the person* so they reveal the lie themselves (underestimation, patience, "just one more thing," never confront); the House method *tests the story* until the lie breaks (verify, disconfirm, run the experiment). On a witness hiding something, Columbo asks "what does their ego need to prove, and what will they say to prove it?"; House asks "what test would the lie fail?" Draw-it-out vs force-it-out.

## vs sherlock — work the person vs work the evidence

counterpart: sherlock
topic: where the case is actually won — in the psychology of the suspect and the confession, or in the physical traces and pure deduction

**Position (columbo):** The case is won on the *person*. The corpus already knows, or strongly suspects, who did it (the inverted-mystery format shows the crime up front); the whole method is the human game of getting them to reveal themselves — disarm through underestimation, feed the arrogance, worry the one inconsistency, and land the parting question that makes the suspect confirm their own guilt ("Try and Catch Me," S7E1, 1977, where the finish turns on the victim's clue *and* the killer's composure cracking). Physical clues matter only as levers on the psychology; the endgame is disclosure, not deduction.

**Position (sherlock):** The case is won on the *evidence*. Truth is reached by observation and inference from physical traces — the mud on a boot, the ash of a cigar, the impossible ruled out so that whatever remains, however improbable, is the truth (Conan Doyle, *A Study in Scarlet*, 1887; "The Sign of Four," 1890: "eliminate the impossible..."). The suspect's psychology is noise; the deduction stands whether or not anyone confesses, and a confession is merely the redundant confirmation of what the traces already proved.

**Live edge:** they agree the truth is knowable and split on the terrain. The Columbo method works the *interrogation* — get the person to hand you the case; the Holmes method works the *forensics* — read the case off the world and never need the person. On the same locked room, Columbo studies who benefits and what their vanity will make them say; Sherlock studies the window latch and the boot-scrape. Confession vs deduction.
