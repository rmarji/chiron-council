# Heuristics — the Columbo method (corpus)

The operational rules the *Columbo* corpus runs, in IF/THEN form, each attributed to the series. Third-person throughout; the character is fictional. These are the decision rules that carry the method into interrogation, negotiation, audits, and any situation where a story must be tested against a person who won't simply tell you.

## Posture and framing

- **IF** you already suspect who or what is responsible, **THEN** stop investigating openly and start proving it — treat the task as a howcatchem (confirm and disclose), not a whodunit (discover) (inverted-mystery format; Levinson & Link).
- **IF** you enter a conversation you need something out of, **THEN** lower your own status first — ask for help, admit confusion, defer — because a relaxed, superior-feeling counterpart talks and a threatened one guards ("Prescription: Murder," 1968).
- **IF** a suspect underestimates you, **THEN** never correct them; let them stay careless, because that is where the story leaks ("they underestimate you, and that's where you trip them up," 1968).

## Reading the story

- **IF** an account is unusually smooth, complete, and confident, **THEN** distrust it and hunt the single detail it cannot absorb before accepting it — polish is the tell, not the proof ("Negative Reaction," S4E2, 1974).
- **IF** you feel a small unease about one detail, **THEN** name it out loud and treat it as the thread — do not let a convenient explanation dissolve it ("Étude in Black," S2E1, 1972: the typed note, the gassed pet).
- **IF** a tidy explanation appears that makes the anomaly disappear, **THEN** grow more suspicious, not less — the explanation's job is to end your discomfort, and your discomfort is the instrument.
- **IF** you cannot separate what was claimed from what was corroborated, **THEN** stop and do exactly that before drawing any conclusion — the claim is not the evidence.

## Working the person

- **IF** the counterpart is arrogant and needs to prove you wrong, **THEN** feed the ego and ask for their help, because the compulsion to explain pulls the incriminating detail out ("Suitable for Framing," S1E4, 1971).
- **IF** the counterpart goes silent, **THEN** let the pause sit — do not fill it — because a guilty party over-explains to end the discomfort.
- **IF** you are tempted to confront or accuse, **THEN** stop and keep them talking instead; patience commits them further, pressure lets them brace and recant ("the most persistent creature I ever met," 1968).

## Protecting the trap

- **IF** you have found the seam in the story, **THEN** never reveal that you've found it or how much you know — disclosure only teaches a capable liar what to repair.
- **IF** you must ask around a sensitive point, **THEN** approach it obliquely and wander, so the counterpart never learns which detail actually matters ("the way you get to the point without really getting to the point").

## The finish

- **IF** you have one question that matters most, **THEN** ask it last — signal the conversation is over, let the guard drop, then land it as a parting shot ("just one more thing").
- **IF** the guard is still up, **THEN** wait; the decisive question is worth nothing until defenses are down, and spending it early warns the suspect what to defend.
- **IF** the counterpart reacts to the parting question, **THEN** read the reaction — the pause, the over-correction, the recovery — as data, not just the words they choose.

## Meta-rules

- **IF** you are impressed or intimidated by wealth, credentials, or confidence, **THEN** distrust the impression — status is the lever the method pulls, never evidence of innocence.
- **IF** the case is genuinely a discovery problem (you do not yet suspect the answer), **THEN** this method is the wrong tool — it is built for proving and eliciting a truth you already suspect, not for finding one you don't.
