# Core Principles — David Deida

Framing note: throughout the corpus, Deida describes masculine and feminine as energies or "sexual essences" present in every person of any gender or orientation, not as roles assigned by sex. The Way of the Superior Man's introduction states the book is written for anyone with a masculine sexual essence, and Intimate Communion (1995) builds its entire model on essence rather than anatomy. The polarity model remains essentialist in structure and is contested by clinicians and gender scholars; the corpus is prescriptive spiritual teaching, not clinical psychology, and is best held as one lens among several.

## Polarity creates attraction

The foundational claim of Intimate Communion (1995) and The Way of the Superior Man (1997), Part Three ("Working with Polarity and Energy"): sexual and emotional charge is generated by the difference between masculine and feminine energy, the way magnetic attraction requires opposite poles. In "Your Attraction to the Feminine Is Inevitable" (The Way of the Superior Man), Deida argues that a person with a masculine essence will be drawn to feminine radiance for life, and that this pull is to be understood and channeled rather than suppressed or moralized away. The corollary is diagnostic: when two partners collapse their difference into sameness — identical schedules, negotiated equivalence, mutual guardedness — the attraction goes quiet, however good the partnership looks on paper. Deida's prescription is not to abandon equality but to hold difference consciously: partners can be equals in worth and logistics while choosing to animate opposite poles in intimate time (Intimate Communion, Part Two; It's a Guy Thing, 1997).

## Presence is the masculine gift

Across Part Two of The Way of the Superior Man ("Dealing with Women") and throughout Dear Lover (2005), the corpus holds that the deepest gift of the masculine is unwavering conscious presence — full attention that neither collapses, retaliates, nor leaves — especially when a partner is emotional or chaotic. Dear Lover, written to the feminine reader, describes the longing for a partner "you can trust to stay present with you" as the feminine's core desire (Dear Lover, Part One, paraphrased). Chapters like "Stay with Her Intensity—To a Point" and "Don't Analyze Your Woman" (The Way of the Superior Man) make the operational point: emotional intensity is met with felt presence, not analysis, problem-solving, or withdrawal. Deida treats presence as trainable — through breath, body practice, and repeated exposure to intensity (Part Seven, "Body Practices"; Enlightened Sex audio program, 2004).

## Purpose before relationship

"Every man knows that his highest purpose in life cannot be reduced to any particular relationship" opens the chapter "Your Purpose Must Come Before Your Relationship" (The Way of the Superior Man, Part One). The reasoning: a person with a masculine essence organizes around direction and mission; when the relationship becomes the mission, the core empties out, the partner senses the vacancy, and both trust and attraction decay. Deida's alternative is loving from fullness — bring an already-directed life to the relationship rather than extracting worth and direction from it. Companion chapters sharpen the demand: "If You Don't Know Your Purpose, Discover It, Now" (don't drift while awaiting revelation) and "Don't Get Lost in Tasks and Duties" (busyness is not purpose; the deep purpose must be reconsulted, not buried under productivity).

## Live at your edge

Two paired chapters in Part One define the growth mechanic. "Know Your Real Edge and Don't Fake It" demands honesty about where courage actually ends — pretending to be beyond your fear is as much a failure as hiding inside comfort. "Lean Just Beyond Your Edge" then gives the dosage: "In any given moment, a man's growth is optimized if he leans just beyond his edge, his capacity, his fear" (The Way of the Superior Man). The principle rests on a prior claim, developed in "Lean Just Beyond Your Edge" and in Blue Truth (2002/2005): fear never fully resolves. Waiting for readiness is therefore a decision to withhold your gift forever. The practice is incremental exposure at the true edge — not recklessness past it, not stagnation short of it.

## Never change your mind just to please

The chapter of that title (The Way of the Superior Man, Part One) is among the most misread in the corpus. Deida's actual teaching: a person should hold to their deepest realization ("Always Hold to Your Deepest Realization," Part One) and never reverse a decision merely to appease a partner or purchase approval — because appeasement teaches the partner that your word bends under pressure, which erodes trust. He is explicit that this is not stubbornness: if a partner's perspective genuinely reveals a deeper truth, changing your mind is integrity. The test is the motive — truth versus approval-seeking. "Live as If Your Father Were Dead" (Part One) extends the same principle upstream: stop living for any authority's permission, parental or partnered, and act from your own deepest knowing.

## The feminine is radiance and flow; meet it as weather

The corpus associates the feminine pole with life-energy, radiance, emotional movement, and flow, and the masculine pole with consciousness, witness, and direction (Blue Truth, 2002/2005; Dear Lover, 2005; Intimate Communion, Part One). Practically, this yields the weather principle: feminine emotion moves like weather — storms arrive, intensify, and pass — and the masculine practice is to be the stable riverbanks rather than to litigate the storm's content. Chapters "Women Are Not Liars" and "Her Complaint Is Content-Free" (The Way of the Superior Man, Parts Two and Four) apply it: expressions in emotional moments are true of the moment rather than binding testimony, and a complaint's literal content usually matters less than the request for depth and presence underneath it.

## Praise grows the feminine; challenge grows the masculine

From "Praise Her" (The Way of the Superior Man, Part Two): "The masculine grows by challenge, but the feminine grows by praise." Deida's operational claim is that criticism-as-management — trying to correct a feminine-essence partner into improvement — produces closure and dimming, while genuine praise of the qualities you love produces more of them. The companion warning is "Tolerating Her Leads to Resenting Her" (Part Two): silent tolerance is not kindness but deferred resentment; the alternative is engaged, loving directness plus praise.

## Intimacy is spiritual practice

The corpus's deepest frame: relationship and sexuality are a yoga — a practice ground where openness under pressure is trained, and where pleasure and difficulty alike are used to cultivate consciousness and love (Finding God Through Sex, 2002/2005; Enlightened Sex audio program, 2004; Blue Truth, 2002/2005). "Do It for Love" and "Live with an Open Heart Even If It Hurts" (The Way of the Superior Man, Part One) set the ethic: the point of every practice is to give love more fully, not to win, control, or self-protect. This is also the corpus's own guardrail — any use of these teachings that closes the heart (entitlement, withdrawal, scorekeeping) is a misuse by its own standard.
