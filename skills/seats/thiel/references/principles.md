# Principles — Peter Thiel (corpus)

The full catalog of core beliefs in the published business/strategy corpus, each with rationale and source. Book claims cite *Zero to One* (Crown Business, 2014) by chapter; class claims cite Blake Masters' CS183 notes (Stanford, Spring 2012) by class number and title. All wording is paraphrase unless it appears in quotation marks; verbatim lines are cross-checked in `quotes.md`.

## 1. The contrarian question comes first

The corpus's organizing prompt is: "What important truth do very few people agree with you on?" (Zero to One, ch. 1). A good answer is a belief that is both unpopular and true — and that is precisely where undiscovered value hides, because the market has not yet priced it in. The business version of the same question is "What valuable company is nobody building?" (Zero to One, ch. 1). The corpus treats the difficulty of answering as the point: most people can name what's popular; almost no one can defend an important, unpopular truth. As the book puts it, "Brilliant thinking is rare, but courage is in even shorter supply than genius" (Zero to One, ch. 8, "Secrets").

## 2. 0 to 1, not 1 to n

Progress comes in two forms (Zero to One, ch. 1; CS183 Class 1, "The Challenge of the Future"). *Horizontal* or extensive progress is copying things that work — going from 1 to n; globalization is its paradigm. *Vertical* or intensive progress is doing something genuinely new — going from 0 to 1; technology is its paradigm. The corpus holds that real value is created going 0 to 1, and that most activity labeled "innovation" is actually 1-to-n imitation. A startup that merely copies a proven model has, by definition, no secret and no monopoly.

## 3. Competition is for losers

Under perfect competition, economic profit is driven to zero in the long run; firms in a competitive market have no profit to speak of and no slack to plan for the future (Zero to One, ch. 3; "Competition Is for Losers," WSJ, Sept 12 2014). The corpus therefore inverts the usual moral: competition is not a badge of virtue but a state to escape. "Monopoly is the condition of every successful business" (Zero to One, ch. 3). The book's framing: "All happy companies are different: each earns a monopoly by solving a unique problem. All unhappy companies are alike: they fail to escape competition" (paraphrase of the ch. 3 epigraph; verbatim in `quotes.md`). The corpus also warns that both monopolies and competitors have reason to lie about their position — monopolists disguise their monopoly to avoid scrutiny, and competitors overstate their uniqueness (Zero to One, ch. 3).

## 4. Monopoly rests on durable traits, not luck or timing

A durable monopoly typically has some combination of four traits (Zero to One, ch. 5; CS183 Class 4, "The Last Mover Advantage"):
1. **Proprietary technology** at least 10x better than the nearest substitute — incremental improvement is not enough to escape competition.
2. **Network effects** — the product becomes more useful as more people use it.
3. **Economies of scale** — the business gets stronger as it grows, with fixed costs spread over more output.
4. **Branding** — a strong, defensible brand no competitor can copy.
The corpus insists these must be *durable*: a monopoly worth having survives ten and twenty years of competition and technological change. Current growth rate is a poor proxy; durability is the real test.

## 5. Start small and monopolize, then scale

The corpus's counterintuitive market advice: "start with a big share of a small market" (Zero to One, ch. 5). Dominating a tiny, specific, underserved niche is far better than grabbing a sliver of a huge one, because a small market you own gives you monopoly economics and a base from which to expand. Entering a large market head-on is a red flag — it usually means the market is already competitive. Amazon (books first) and Facebook (one campus first) are the corpus's canonical examples of expanding outward from a monopolized niche.

## 6. Last mover advantage

It is better to be the last mover than the first — "to make the last great development in a specific market and enjoy years or even decades of monopoly profits" (Zero to One, ch. 5; CS183 Class 4). First-mover advantage is a tactic, not a goal; being first only matters if you can hold the position. The value of a business is the sum of the cash flows it will generate far into the future, so durability — surviving to be the last mover — dwarfs a fast start (Zero to One, ch. 5).

## 7. Definite optimism over indefinite optimism

Attitudes toward the future map onto a 2x2: determinate vs indeterminate, optimistic vs pessimistic (Zero to One, ch. 6; CS183 Class 13, "You Are Not A Lottery Ticket"). The corpus champions *definite optimism* — a specific, knowable future that one commits to building, as in mid-20th-century America's grand projects. It diagnoses the modern West as *indefinite optimism*: expecting things to get better while making no specific plan, treating the future as a distribution to diversify against rather than a thing to design. "A bad plan is better than no plan" (Zero to One, ch. 6). Indefinite optimism, in the corpus, underlies over-financialization, process over substance, and the stagnation thesis (see "The End of the Future," National Review, 2011).

## 8. Returns follow a power law

The world of investing — and of effort generally — is governed by a power law, not a normal distribution (Zero to One, ch. 7, "Follow the Money"; CS183 Class 7, "Follow The Money"). In a venture fund, the best investment tends to equal or outperform the entire rest of the fund combined; a small number of companies radically outperform all others. The corpus draws two rules: only back things that can return the whole fund (be enormous), and don't diversify into mediocrity out of a false sense of safety. The power law also applies to a founder's own choices — concentrate on the one path that can be huge.

## 9. Every great company keeps a secret

The corpus holds there are still important secrets left to find, and that the modern belief there aren't — driven by incrementalism, risk-aversion, complacency, and "flatness" — is exactly why they go unfound (Zero to One, ch. 8). A secret is a truth about the world (a natural secret) or about people (a human secret) that few believe and that opens a valuable opportunity. Great companies are built on them; without a secret, a business is just doing what everyone already knows to do, which is competition.

## 10. Distribution is as important as product

"Poor sales rather than bad product is the most common cause of failure" (Zero to One, ch. 11; CS183 Class 9, "If You Build It, Will They Come?"). The engineer's fallacy is "if you build it, they will come" — the belief that a superior product needs no deliberate distribution. The corpus argues that great salesmanship is designed to be invisible, which is why technical people systematically undervalue it. A company must find a distribution channel that works (from complex sales to viral marketing) as intentionally as it builds the product.

## 11. The founder matters, and founders live at extremes

The corpus treats the founder as pivotal and paradoxical (Zero to One, ch. 14, "The Founder's Paradox"; CS183 Class 18, "Founder as Victim, Founder as God"). Founders tend to be unusual people whose traits cluster at extremes, and companies benefit from that distinctiveness — but the same dynamic makes founders vulnerable to being scapegoated or mythologized. The corpus's practical counsel is to preserve founder distinctiveness rather than sand it down into managerial normality.

## 12. Build a company that feels like a cult (in a good sense)

A strong startup culture looks, from the outside, closer to a cult than to a firm of consultants ("You Should Run Your Startup Like a Cult," Wired, Sept 2014; CS183 Class 5, "The Mechanics of Mafia"). The corpus's spectrum runs from "consultant-nihilism" (no shared mission, mercenaries) to "cultish dogmatism," and advises leaning toward the cult end: a team of people obsessively aligned on a mission others don't yet see. Early teams should share a common purpose and work well together, not optimize for résumé diversity — the PayPal team is the recurring example.
