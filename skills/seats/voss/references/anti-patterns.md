# Anti-Patterns — the Voss corpus

Everything the corpus warns against, with the reasoning and the failure each produces. NSTD = *Never Split the Difference* (2016).

## Splitting the difference
The title sin. Meeting in the middle to feel fair produces outcomes neither side wanted — Voss's image is the person who, torn between black shoes and brown shoes, compromises by wearing one of each (NSTD Ch. 6, "Bend Their Reality"). Compromise is usually driven by fear of conflict or laziness, not by the merits; the corpus's position is that "no deal is better than a bad deal" (NSTD Ch. 9). Failure produced: both sides quietly resent the outcome, and the real interests that a harder conversation would have surfaced stay buried.

## Chasing "yes" / yes-momentum selling
Stacking small yeses to build commitment — the classic sales pattern — trains counterparts to feel trapped and produces counterfeit yeses given to escape pressure (NSTD Ch. 4, "Beware 'Yes' — Master 'No'"; the three-yes typology: counterfeit, confirmation, commitment). Voss notes people have been so bombarded by yes-traps that a pursued "yes" now raises defenses by itself. Failure produced: agreements that evaporate at execution because the commitment was never real.

## Arguing rationally against emotion
Presenting logic, facts, and merits while the counterpart's unaddressed emotions veto everything silently. NSTD Ch. 1 frames this as the core error of the rational-actor school; Ch. 3's answer is to label the emotion before touching the substance. On Lex Fridman #364 (2023), Voss restates it: feelings drive decisions, not reason. Failure produced: being "right" and losing — the counterpart hardens because winning the argument makes them feel defeated, not persuaded.

## Fear of "no"
Structuring every question to avoid rejection, when "no" is protection and the true start of negotiation (NSTD Ch. 4). Avoiding no also means avoiding the questions that surface real objections. Failure produced: brittle, information-poor conversations and counterparts who feel cornered.

## Going too fast
"Going too fast is one of the mistakes all negotiators are prone to make" (NSTD Ch. 2, paraphrase of the rule; the chapter states that rushing makes people feel unheard and erodes rapport). Time pressure is usually self-imposed; deadlines are "often arbitrary, almost always flexible" (NSTD Ch. 6). The cautionary case for treating deadlines as absolute is William Griffin (Rochester, NY, 1981) — the rare, tragic exception that proves the base rate (NSTD Ch. 6). Failure produced: concessions bought by an imaginary clock, and trust never built.

## Assuming the counterpart is rational — or like you
Treating the other side as a logic machine ignores the emotional drivers and Black Swans that actually move the deal (NSTD Ch. 1, Ch. 10). The companion error is projecting your own negotiator type: an Assertive reading an Analyst's silence as agreement, or an Accommodator reading chattiness as progress (NSTD Ch. 9's Analyst/Accommodator/Assertive typology). Failure produced: systematic misreading of signals and "crazy"-labeled behavior that was actually a clue (NSTD Ch. 10: they're not crazy — they're constrained, ill-informed, or driven by hidden interests).

## Taking "you're right" as a win
"You're right" is how people get someone to stop talking; treating it as agreement is self-deception (NSTD Ch. 5). Failure produced: leaving the conversation believing you persuaded, discovering later nothing changed.

## Caving to the "fair" accusation
"We've given you a fair offer" is a manipulative use of the F-word designed to trigger defensive concession (NSTD Ch. 6). The counter is to label it and ask for evidence, not to concede or to counter-accuse. Failure produced: giving up value to soothe a manufactured guilt.

## Asking "why"
"Why" is accusatory in every language and triggers defensiveness; the corpus replaces it with "what" and "how" except when defensiveness is deliberately useful (NSTD Ch. 7). Failure produced: counterparts justifying themselves instead of solving your problem.

## Threats, anger, and getting personal
Uncontrolled aggression poisons execution and invites retaliation; negative leverage should be at most alluded to with a label, never brandished (NSTD Ch. 10 on negative leverage; Ch. 9 on taking a punch "without anger"). The person across the table is not the problem — the situation is (NSTD Ch. 9). Failure produced: a counterpart who complies minimally, sabotages quietly, or walks.

## Talking too much
The talker feels in control; the listener actually is. Pitching, over-explaining, and filling silences all surrender information and forfeit the discovery mission (NSTD Ch. 2 on silence; Lex Fridman #364 on proactive listening; MasterClass on the listener's advantage). Failure produced: the Black Swans stay hidden — in the other side's pocket.

## Bluffing what you cannot back and other bad-faith moves
The corpus's system runs on demonstrated understanding and trust; deception is both detectable (Pinocchio effect, 7-38-55 incongruence — NSTD Ch. 8) and fatal to execution when discovered. Voss's counter-model is being tough on substance while relentlessly decent in manner: "He who has learned to disagree without being disagreeable has discovered the most valuable secret of negotiation" (NSTD, verified quote). Failure produced: a won deal that dies at implementation and a reputation that taxes every future negotiation.

## Neglecting the people behind the table
Deals get killed by players who never sat at the table — bosses, spouses, teams. Ignoring them because they are invisible is a standard execution failure (NSTD Ch. 8: ask "How does this affect the rest of your team?"). Failure produced: a "yes" from the room reversed by the hallway.

## Compromising with yourself before you start
Anchoring your own goal at "realistic" instead of best-case, out of fear of seeming aggressive. The Negotiation One Sheet requires writing down the optimistic-but-legitimate goal precisely to prevent self-negotiation (NSTD Appendix). Failure produced: outcomes capped by your own opening timidity rather than by the counterpart.
