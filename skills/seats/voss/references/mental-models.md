# Mental Models — the Voss corpus

Every named model or concept in the corpus, defined, with origin and when it applies. NSTD = *Never Split the Difference* (2016).

## Tactical empathy
Deliberate, accuracy-first articulation of the other side's feelings and perspective, with no requirement of sympathy or agreement (NSTD Ch. 3; Lex Fridman #364, 2023). Origin: FBI crisis negotiation practice. Applies: always — it is the operating system the other models run on.

## The three voices
Tone sets the temperature of the exchange (NSTD Ch. 2, "Be a Mirror"; MasterClass "Body Language & Speech Patterns"):
1. **Late-night FM DJ voice** — calm, slow, downward-inflecting. Used to assert without triggering pushback, and to soothe in a crisis. Deployed at the 1993 Chase Manhattan Bank robbery in Brooklyn (NSTD Ch. 2; MasterClass case study).
2. **Positive/playful voice** — the default; a smile in the voice signals ease and cooperation and is contagious even by phone.
3. **Direct/assertive voice** — used rarely and deliberately; it signals dominance and invites backlash.

## Mirroring (isopraxism)
Repeating the last one to three words (or the critical one to three words) the counterpart said, with an upward inflection. It triggers the human mirroring instinct, signals listening, buys time, and keeps them talking without the mirrorer taking a position (NSTD Ch. 2). Voss's account of Chase Manhattan 1993: "The majority of what I'm doing is mirroring... they keep talking" (MasterClass case study). Applies: whenever caught off guard, whenever more information is needed, whenever silence would be awkward.

## Labeling
Naming the counterpart's emotion or dynamic aloud with a deflecting frame — "It seems like...", "It sounds like...", "It looks like..." — never "I think you feel," which centers the speaker (NSTD Ch. 3). Labels defuse negatives by acknowledging them and reinforce positives by naming them. Neuroscience rationale cited in NSTD Ch. 3: affect labeling (naming a fear) dampens amygdala response. "The last rule of labeling is silence. Once you've thrown out a label, be quiet and listen" (NSTD Ch. 3, verified quote). Field case: the 1998 Harlem standoff, where six hours of labels ("It seems like you don't want to come out...") brought three armed fugitives out without a shot (NSTD Ch. 3).

## Dynamic silence
The deliberate pause after a mirror or label, letting it do its work. Origin: hostage-negotiation practice; reiterated on Lex Fridman #364 (2023) as the pairing of silence with mirrors to draw people out.

## The three kinds of "yes"
Counterfeit, confirmation, commitment (NSTD Ch. 4, verified). Only commitment "yes" matters; pressure produces counterfeits. Applies: any time agreement comes fast and easy — treat it as suspect.

## "No" as protection
Saying "no" makes people feel safe and in control; it is the start of the negotiation, not the end (NSTD Ch. 4). No-oriented questions ("Is it a bad idea to...?", "Have you given up on...?") get faster, more honest responses than yes-chasing. Origin: Voss's inversion of Jim Camp's *Start with No* (2002), which NSTD Ch. 4 credits.

## "That's right" vs. "you're right"
"That's right" = the counterpart confirms your summary of their world; an epiphany of feeling understood. "You're right" = a dismissal to make you go away (NSTD Ch. 5; case: Abu Sabaya / Jeffrey Schilling, Philippines 2000). Applies: as the test of whether tactical empathy has landed before any ask is made.

## Behavioral Change Stairway Model (BCSM)
The FBI-developed sequence: active listening → empathy → rapport → influence → behavioral change (NSTD Ch. 5; developed within the FBI's crisis negotiation unit). Skipping steps — jumping to influence before listening — is why most persuasion fails. Applies: as the macro-map for any conversation intended to change behavior.

## System 1 / System 2
Kahneman's dual-process model (referenced NSTD Ch. 1, citing *Thinking, Fast and Slow*, 2011). The corpus targets System 1: shape the emotional, instinctive reaction and the rational justification follows.

## Loss aversion and prospect theory
People take greater risks to avoid losses than to secure equivalent gains (Kahneman & Tversky, cited NSTD Ch. 6). Applies: frame stakes around what the counterpart stands to lose by not acting ("I've got a bad deal for you" opener; the certainty effect makes sure losses loom largest).

## Anchoring and adjustment
First numbers warp perception of everything after them (NSTD Ch. 6). The corpus's uses: let the other side anchor first when information is scarce (absorb the punch); anchor extreme when using the Ackerman system; use ranges ("at top places, this role pays X–Y") to anchor without rigidity; beware that an extreme anchor can trigger fight-or-flight in inexperienced counterparts (NSTD Ch. 6, Ch. 9).

## Odd, precise numbers
Non-round figures ($37,893 rather than $38,000) read as calculated and immovable, and gain credibility (NSTD Ch. 9, Ackerman step). Applies: final offers.

## The 7-38-55 rule
Albert Mehrabian's finding, as used in NSTD Ch. 8 ("Guarantee Execution"): in emotionally loaded communication, 7% of a message is words, 38% tone, 55% body language. Applies: when words and delivery diverge, believe the delivery, and use a label to surface the gap ("I heard you say yes, but it seemed like there was hesitation in your voice").

## The Rule of Three
Getting the counterpart to agree to the same thing three times in one conversation — first the commitment, then a label or summary of it, then a calibrated "how" about implementation (NSTD Ch. 8). Rationale: it is hard to fake conviction three times; counterfeit yeses collapse under repetition.

## The Pinocchio Effect
Liars use more words, more third-person pronouns, and more complex sentences than truth-tellers — the corpus cites a Harvard study of lying behavior (NSTD Ch. 8). Applies: execution-verification and trust assessment.

## The Chris discount
Using one's own first name to humanize oneself ("My name is Chris. What's the Chris discount?") — self-deprecating personalization that triggers reciprocity (NSTD Ch. 8).

## Negotiator archetypes: Analyst, Accommodator, Assertive
NSTD Ch. 9's typology. Analysts are methodical, silence-comfortable, and see apologies as weakness; Accommodators prize relationship and time spent talking; Assertives prize being heard before they can listen. Rule: do not assume the counterpart mirrors your own type — "your style is your normal, and it is a mistake to project it" (paraphrase of NSTD Ch. 9). Applies: preparation and diagnosis of a counterpart's silence, chattiness, or bluntness.

## Taking a punch
Skilled bargainers open with extreme anchors to knock counterparts off balance; the model is to absorb it without anger or counter-aggression, deflect ("I'm sorry, that just doesn't work for me"), and pivot to calibrated questions (NSTD Ch. 9).

## The Black Swan
Borrowed in homage from Nassim Taleb's *The Black Swan* (2007; credited in NSTD Ch. 10): the unknown unknowns — typically three to five per negotiation — that transform the deal once surfaced. They are found by listening for the unexpected, getting face time, and observing unguarded moments (before and after formal sessions), not by direct interrogation (NSTD Ch. 10). Voss named his firm, The Black Swan Group (founded 2008), after the model.

## Three types of leverage
Positive (ability to give what they want), negative (ability to hurt — use with care, threats poison execution), normative (using the counterpart's own standards and "religion" against inconsistency) (NSTD Ch. 10).

## "Religion" — the counterpart's worldview
The organizing belief system by which the counterpart makes decisions; discovering it is the master key to influence, as in the 2003 "Tractor Man" standoff in Washington, DC, resolved by working inside the man's faith framework (NSTD Ch. 10). Paired with the **similarity principle**: people trust those they perceive as belonging to their in-group (NSTD Ch. 10).

## The illusion of control
People fight direction but follow paths they believe they chose. Calibrated questions are the mechanism; the model is the frame (NSTD Ch. 7; case: the Ecuador kidnapping run through the victim's wife asking "How do we know he's alive?").
