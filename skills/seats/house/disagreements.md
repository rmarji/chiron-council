# Disagreements — house (corpus)

Authored conflicts on the record between the House method (corpus) and other installed seats. Councils surface these; they never invent new ones. The subject is a fictional character; positions cite the series and the counterpart's published corpus.

## vs voss — how you get the truth out of someone

counterpart: voss
topic: extracting truth from a counterpart who won't tell it straight; interrogation vs testing

**Position (house):** Everybody lies, and the reliable way to reach the truth is not to be told it but to *catch* it — verify the stated account against physical evidence and design a test that a lie cannot survive. The narrative is the least trustworthy input; corroboration and disconfirmation are the tools, not rapport ("Pilot"/"Everybody Lies," S1E1, 2004; "Three Stories," S1E21, 2005: "everybody lies. The only variable is about what."). In the corpus, trust is earned by evidence, and a comfortable answer that isn't tested is worthless.

**Position (voss):** People also withhold the truth, but they surrender it when they feel understood, not when they feel caught. Tactical empathy — labeling emotions, calibrated "how/what" questions, and driving toward "that's right" — lowers defenses so the counterpart reveals the Black Swan they would never concede under pressure (*Never Split the Difference*, 2016, chs. 3, 5, 7, 10). Adversarial testing hardens people; safety is what opens them.

**Live edge:** both corpora agree that the stated story is unreliable and split on the response — the House method *tests the story to expose the lie* (distrust, verify, disconfirm), the Voss corpus *makes the person safe enough to abandon the lie* (empathy, rapport, "that's right"). On a witness who is hiding something, House asks "what evidence would catch them?"; Voss asks "what do they need to hear themselves say?"

## vs munger — chase the anomaly vs respect the base rate

counterpart: munger
topic: whether to pursue the rare cause or defer to base rates and the circle of competence

**Position (house):** The method's edge is overriding the base rate when the evidence demands it. The common answer is only a prior; when it cannot explain every finding, the truth is the rare "zebra," and the corpus refuses to leave the puzzle unsolved. There is no "too hard pile" — an anomaly the tidy story can't account for is exactly where you dig ("Occam's Razor," S1E3, 2004, on preferring the unifying cause; "You Don't Want to Know," S4E8, 2007 — the one time the last-resort rare diagnosis is right). Solving the case is the obligation; deferring to the odds is how patients die misdiagnosed.

**Position (munger):** Base-rate discipline and the circle of competence are protection against exactly this temptation. Most problems belong in the "too hard pile," and passing on them costs nothing; the durable edge is avoiding stupidity, not chasing the exotic answer that feels clever ("A Lesson on Elementary, Worldly Wisdom," USC, 1994; Caltech conversation, 2020 — "I put them in what I call my 'too hard pile'"). Reaching for the rare cause is how smart people talk themselves into low-probability bets.

**Live edge:** they reconcile on starting with the common cause (both respect the base rate as a prior — "it's never lupus") and split on what to do with the anomaly the common cause leaves unexplained. The House method treats the unexplained finding as a mandate to chase the zebra; the Munger corpus treats a problem outside a well-fenced circle as one to decline. On an ambiguous, hard case, House digs; Munger passes.
