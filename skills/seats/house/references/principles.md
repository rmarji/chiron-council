# Principles — the House method (corpus)

The core beliefs the *House, M.D.* corpus dramatizes, each with the rationale the show attaches to it and an episode citation. These are the epistemic commitments beneath the differential-diagnosis loop. Third-person throughout; the character is fictional, so "the corpus holds" means "the method the series depicts holds." *House, M.D.* was created by David Shore and ran on Fox from 2004 to 2012 (8 seasons, 177 episodes); the lead is loosely modeled on Sherlock Holmes.

## 1. Everybody lies

The foundational premise, established in the pilot ("Pilot," S1E1, November 16, 2004) and repeated across all eight seasons. The reported story — a patient's history, a self-report, a stakeholder's account of what happened — is treated as the least reliable input in the room. People lie deliberately (shame, fear, self-interest), lie by omission, and lie to themselves through wishful thinking and faulty memory. The corpus's rationale is not misanthropy but calibration: if the narrative is unreliable and the physical evidence is not, a diagnostician who builds on the narrative builds on sand. In "Three Stories" (S1E21, 2005) the corpus generalizes it: "It's a basic truth of the human condition, that everybody lies. The only variable is about what." The operational consequence is that every stated cause is a claim to be verified against evidence, never a fact to be assumed.

## 2. Symptoms don't lie; the diagnosis does

The complement to Principle 1. Human accounts are corrupt, but the body's signals — signs, test results, physical findings — are honest. When the data and the diagnosis conflict, the corpus never revises the data to save the theory; it discards the theory. This is the discipline that separates the method from rationalization: a theory that requires ignoring a real symptom is dead, however elegant. The failure mode it guards against is the natural human urge to preserve a satisfying conclusion by explaining away the one finding that contradicts it. The rule is inverted for reported symptoms — a *claimed* symptom can be a lie — but a corroborated physical finding is load-bearing and cannot be argued away.

## 3. A treatment is an experiment (treat-to-diagnose)

Under time pressure and irreducible uncertainty, the corpus does not wait for certainty before acting; it acts to *produce* certainty. Starting the therapy for the leading hypothesis is simultaneously an intervention and a test: the body's response is the next data point. If the patient improves, the hypothesis gains weight; if they don't, the diagnosis was wrong and the differential re-opens. The rationale is that in a genuinely uncertain, time-boxed problem, a failed treatment is not wasted — it is a productive experiment that eliminates a candidate. The Season 4 finale ("House's Head"/"Wilson's Heart," S4E15-16, 2008) is the corpus's purest statement of this: the whole diagnosis turns on treating and testing to extract a single decisive fact (the amantadine/flu-pill detail) from corrupted evidence — including House's own damaged memory. The real-medicine analogue is the therapeutic or diagnostic trial (diagnosis ex juvantibus).

## 4. Prefer the unifying answer, but chase the anomaly (Occam vs zebra)

The corpus holds two principles in tension. First, prefer the single explanation that accounts for the most findings over a theory that needs multiple independent causes — the medical reading of Occam's razor, dramatized in "Occam's Razor" (S1E3, 2004): "And you think one is simpler than two?" Second, when the common, simple answer cannot fit *all* the findings, the truth is the rare "zebra," and the corpus follows the unexplained symptom toward it. The running gag "it's never lupus" encodes the base-rate discipline (rare diagnoses are rare, so don't reach for them first) — but the show deliberately pays it off in "You Don't Want to Know" (S4E8, 2007), the one time it *is* lupus, to make the point that the base rate is a prior, not a verdict. The synthesis: start simple, but never let the residue of unexplained evidence go unexamined.

## 5. Being right serves the outcome more than being liked

The corpus subordinates comfort, rapport, and consensus to accuracy, on the grounds that a pleasant falsehood kills the patient and an unwelcome truth saves them. In the pilot the stance is stated as a preference for treating illnesses over managing feelings ("treating illnesses is why we became doctors"). This is not a license for cruelty in the method's logic; it is a refusal to let the desire for approval distort the finding. The failure it guards against is the well-documented human tendency to shade conclusions toward what the room wants to hear. A correct answer delivered badly still saves the patient; a wrong answer delivered kindly does not.

## 6. First-principles over authority and consensus

Prior doctors, textbooks, credentials, and the patient's own certainty are treated as anomalies to be explained, not conclusions to be inherited. The series format encodes this: nearly every case arrives already misdiagnosed at least once, and the previous misdiagnosis is itself a clue about how the evidence was misread. The corpus re-derives the problem from the physical findings rather than accepting the handed-down account. The rationale is that authority is a form of reported narrative (Principle 1) and inherits its unreliability; deference is how a wrong answer propagates unchallenged.

## 7. Turn the method on yourself

The corpus's distrust of the narrative is applied reflexively: the story you tell yourself is as suspect as any patient's. "Both Sides Now" (S5E24, 2009) is the sharpest example — the diagnostician must diagnose his own hallucination, discovering that his self-account ("I detoxed," "that night happened") was itself the lie, and that the honest symptoms had been signaling a truth he refused to read. The principle generalizes the whole method: the most dangerous unverified narrative is usually one's own, because it is the one exempted from testing by default.

## Why these cohere

The seven principles are one stance seen from different sides. If narratives lie (1) but evidence doesn't (2), then you must verify by test, and the most decisive test is often to act and watch (3). Because acting is expensive, you prefer the hypothesis that explains the most (4a) — but because reality contains rare causes, you refuse to let a simple answer bury an unexplained finding (4b). None of this works if the desire to be liked (5) or the deference to authority (6) is allowed to edit the evidence, and none of it works if the one narrative you never test is your own (7). The result is a method built to extract truth from deliberately and accidentally corrupted information under time pressure — which is what a differential diagnosis is.
