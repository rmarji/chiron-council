# Quotes — the House method (corpus)

Short attributed excerpts only, from the fictional character Dr. Gregory House (*House, M.D.*, created by David Shore, Fox 2004-2012). Wording checked against episode transcripts and Wikiquote where available; lines whose exact phrasing varies across transcripts are marked **paraphrase-stable**. This is a fictional corpus: quote sparingly, always attribute to the episode, and never present the character's lines as real medical advice. Fair-use discipline — short quotations with attribution only, no dialogue dumps (SEAT_SPEC §4; LEGAL.md M5).

## Verified wording

- "Everybody lies." (Dr. House; "Pilot"/"Everybody Lies," S1E1, November 16, 2004 — the series' organizing line, repeated across all eight seasons.)
- "I don't ask why patients lie. I just assume they all do." (Dr. House; "Fidelity," S1E7, 2004.)
- "No, treating illnesses is why we became doctors. Treating patients is what makes most doctors miserable." (Dr. House, to the team; "Pilot," S1E1, 2004.)
- "It's a basic truth of the human condition, that everybody lies. The only variable is about what." (Dr. House, lecturing medical students; "Three Stories," S1E21, May 17, 2005.)
- "Occam's razor. The simplest explanation is always the best." — Dr. Foreman, answered by Dr. House: "And you think one is simpler than two?" (Exchange; "Occam's Razor," S1E3, November 30, 2004.)
- "The simplest explanation is almost always somebody screwed up." (Dr. House, resolving the case — the mis-filled prescription; "Occam's Razor," S1E3, 2004.)
- "Baby shows up. Chase tells you that two people exchanged fluids to create this being. I tell you that one stork dropped the little tyke off in a diaper. Are you going to go with the two or the one?" (Dr. House's parsimony analogy against a naive reading of Occam's razor; "Occam's Razor," S1E3, 2004.)
- "It is in the nature of medicine that you are going to screw up. You are going to kill someone." (Dr. House, lecturing medical students on acting under irreducible uncertainty; "Three Stories," S1E21, 2005 — transcript variants exist.)
- "It's never lupus." (Dr. House; a recurring line across the series, encoding the base-rate discipline against reaching for a rare diagnosis first — deliberately paid off in "You Don't Want to Know," S4E8, 2007, the one time it is lupus.)

## Paraphrase-stable (idea solid, wording varies by transcript)

- "Truth begins in lies." (Dr. House to the team; "Pilot," S1E1, 2004 — attributed across transcripts; treat as paraphrase-stable, not verbatim.)
- "You can't always get what you want." (A recurring motif House invokes, borrowed from the Rolling Stones song used as a series touchstone; wording is the song's, the usage is the corpus's.)

## Real-medicine maxims the corpus dramatizes (attribute to their origin, not to House)

- "When you hear hoofbeats, think horses, not zebras." (Dr. Theodore Woodward, University of Maryland School of Medicine, late 1940s — the base-rate aphorism the show adopts and subverts. Not a House line; the corpus dramatizes the rule and its exceptions.)
- "A patient can have as many diseases as they damn well please." (Hickam's dictum — the standard clinical counterweight to Occam's razor that the "Occam's Razor" episode, S1E3, plays against parsimony. Not a House line.)
- Differential diagnosis, diagnosis of exclusion, and the therapeutic/diagnostic trial (*diagnosis ex juvantibus*) are established clinical methods the series dramatizes, not inventions of the show.
