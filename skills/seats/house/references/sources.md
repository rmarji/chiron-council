# Sources — the House method (corpus)

Complete provenance for this seat. The subject is a **fictional character**, Dr. Gregory House, so the "corpus" is the television series that depicts him: *House, M.D.*, created by David Shore, broadcast on Fox from November 16, 2004 to May 21, 2012 (8 seasons, 177 episodes). The seat distills the depicted diagnostic **method** and epistemic stance, cited by episode, plus the real clinical and logical frameworks the show dramatizes. Primary corpus (the series and specific episodes) first; real-method references second.

## Primary corpus — the series

- **House, M.D.**, created by David Shore. Fox Broadcasting Company, 2004-2012. 8 seasons, 177 episodes. Lead character (played by Hugh Laurie) loosely modeled on Arthur Conan Doyle's Sherlock Holmes (per Shore's stated inspiration); Dr. Wilson functions as the Watson figure. The medical-procedural format centers on a diagnostic team solving cases other doctors could not, with most patients misdiagnosed at least once before House's team re-derives the answer.

## Primary corpus — cited episodes

- **"Pilot"** (widely known as **"Everybody Lies"**), Season 1, Episode 1. Directed by Bryan Singer, written by David Shore. First aired November 16, 2004. Establishes the founding premise ("everybody lies"), the differential-diagnosis team format, and the stance that the diagnostician treats illnesses rather than manages patients.
- **"Occam's Razor,"** Season 1, Episode 3. First aired November 30, 2004. Dramatizes the parsimony debate (one cause vs two; Occam vs, implicitly, Hickam's dictum) and reframes "simplest" as fewest assumptions — "the simplest explanation is almost always somebody screwed up" (a mis-filled prescription).
- **"Three Stories,"** Season 1, Episode 21. Written by David Shore. First aired May 17, 2005. House lectures medical students on differential diagnosis using three leg-pain cases (the third is his own history). Won the Emmy for Outstanding Writing for a Drama Series (David Shore) and the Humanitas Prize. Source of "It's a basic truth of the human condition, that everybody lies."
- **"You Don't Want to Know,"** Season 4, Episode 8 (2007). The deliberate payoff of the "it's never lupus" running gag — the one case where the answer genuinely is lupus (a rare cardiac presentation), making the point that the base rate is a prior, not a verdict.
- **"House's Head"** / **"Wilson's Heart,"** Season 4, Episodes 15-16. First aired May 12 and May 19, 2008. The two-part finale in which the entire diagnosis turns on extracting one decisive fact (Amber's amantadine/flu-pill detail) from corrupted evidence, including House's own damaged memory — the corpus's highest-stakes treat-to-diagnose and the clearest statement that a recovered symptom outranks a comfortable story.
- **"Both Sides Now,"** Season 5, Episode 24. First aired May 11, 2009. The finale in which the method turns inward: House must diagnose his own hallucination (Amber) and discovers his self-account was the lie, checking himself into Mayfield Psychiatric Hospital.

## Real methods and frameworks the corpus dramatizes

- **Differential diagnosis** — the standard clinical procedure of enumerating candidate causes for a set of findings and narrowing by testing. The structural spine of every episode; the show dramatizes it, it did not invent it.
- **Diagnosis of exclusion** — reaching a diagnosis by ruling out alternatives rather than directly confirming one; embodied in the whiteboard elimination process.
- **Therapeutic / diagnostic trial (diagnosis ex juvantibus)** — inferring a diagnosis from the response to treatment; the real-medicine name for the corpus's "treat-to-diagnose."
- **"When you hear hoofbeats, think horses, not zebras"** — base-rate aphorism coined by **Dr. Theodore Woodward**, University of Maryland School of Medicine, late 1940s. The origin of the show's "zebra" language and the "it's never lupus" discipline.
- **Occam's razor vs Hickam's dictum** — the parsimony principle (fewest assumptions/one unifying cause) versus the clinical counterweight ("a patient can have as many diseases as they damn well please"). The "Occam's Razor" episode stages exactly this tension.
