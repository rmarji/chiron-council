# Mental models — Robert Cialdini

Every named model or concept the corpus uses, defined, with origin and when it applies. These are the recurring conceptual tools beneath the seven principles and the pre-suasion thesis.

## "Weapons" / "levers" of influence
The umbrella term for the reliable psychological triggers that produce automatic compliance. Cialdini's framing is that each exploits a normally-adaptive shortcut. Note the edition change: ch. 1 is titled **"Weapons of Influence"** in the classic editions (1984/2007) and was **retitled "Levers of Influence"** in the 2021 New & Expanded edition. *Applies:* the master category — the seven principles are the seven weapons/levers.

## "Click, whirr" (classic) / "click, run" (2021) — the fixed-action pattern
A single trigger feature fires a whole pre-set sequence of behavior, like a tape starting. Borrowed from ethology (fixed-action patterns in animals) and applied to human compliance: we run the "tape" because we can't analyze everything. *Origin:* *Influence*, ch. 1 — the metaphor is **"click, whirr"** in the classic editions and **"click, run"** in the 2021 edition; attribute each to its edition. *Applies:* explains *why* the principles work — they are the trigger features.

## Controlled vs. automatic (short-cut) processing
People operate on two tracks: effortful, systematic evaluation, and low-effort reliance on a single cue ("a good indicator is usually enough"). Modern life pushes more decisions onto the automatic track, which is exactly the terrain the weapons exploit. Kin to Kahneman's System 1/System 2 and Petty & Cacioppo's central/peripheral routes, which Cialdini references. *Applies:* the busier or more uncertain the target, the more the shortcuts govern.

## Perceptual contrast
Judgment is relative to the immediately preceding stimulus; the same thing seems more or less extreme by comparison. *Origin:* *Influence*, ch. 1 (real-estate "setup" houses; suit-then-accessories). *Applies:* sequencing offers, price presentation, and the mechanics of door-in-the-face.

## Reciprocal concessions (the engine of door-in-the-face)
A concession obligates a return concession. Retreating from a large request to a smaller one reads as a concession, pulling the target toward a reciprocal "yes." *Origin:* *Influence*, ch. 2; Cialdini, Vincent, Lewis, Catalan, Wheeler & Darby (1975). *Applies:* whenever the real ask can be preceded by a larger, expected-to-be-refused ask.

## Commitment consistency and the "grow legs" effect
Active, public, effortful, freely-chosen commitments generate new reasons to justify themselves — they "grow their own legs" — so people stay consistent even after the original inducement is removed. *Origin:* *Influence*, ch. 3; low-ball research (1978). *Applies:* onboarding, pledges, written goals, and (as a warning) the low-ball tactic.

## Descriptive vs. injunctive norms
A crucial distinction inside social proof. A **descriptive** norm is what people *actually do*; an **injunctive** norm is what people *approve/disapprove of*. They can point in opposite directions, and using the wrong one backfires. *Origin:* Cialdini, Reno & Kallgren (1990), "A focus theory of normative conduct" (the littering / handbill studies); Cialdini (2003). *Applies:* any behavior-change message — if the undesirable behavior is common, appeal to the injunctive norm, not the descriptive one.

## The boomerang effect
Publicizing that many people do an undesirable thing (a descriptive norm of the bad behavior) normalizes it and *increases* it among those below average. *Origin:* Schultz, Nolan, Cialdini, Goldstein & Griskevicius (2007) — household energy: telling above-average users the neighborhood average cut their use, but telling below-average users the average *raised* theirs, until an injunctive cue (a smiley/frowny face) was added. *Applies:* the single most-cited cautionary model in the corpus's applied work.

## Provincial norms
Social proof is strongest from similar others in the *same immediate situation*. *Origin:* Goldstein, Cialdini & Griskevicius (2008) — hotel towel cards: "guests in THIS room reused their towels" beat "guests at this hotel," which beat a generic environmental appeal. *Applies:* always prefer the most locally-specific true proof.

## The privileged moment
The brief window of heightened receptivity opened *before* a message by a pre-suasive opener. *Origin:* *Pre-Suasion* (2016). *Applies:* whenever you control the run-up to an ask.

## "What's focal is causal"
Whatever an audience is made to attend to is judged both more important and more causal — even when the causal link is spurious. *Origin:* *Pre-Suasion* (2016). *Applies:* the core mechanism of pre-suasion; the reason an opener, question, image, or number placed just before the ask reshapes the decision.

## Channeled attention / attentional magnetizers
Persuasion by directing attention. Some stimuli are "magnetic" (the sexual, the threatening, the novel/different) and hold focus; the pre-suasive operator chooses what to make focal. *Origin:* *Pre-Suasion* (2016). *Applies:* opener design, framing, and message ordering.

## Association (the machinery of pre-suasion)
All influence ultimately runs through association: language, metaphors, and primes link the request to a desired concept, readying the response. *Origin:* *Pre-Suasion* (2016, later chapters). *Applies:* word choice, imagery, and priming in copy and conversation.

## Loss aversion (the basis of scarcity)
People are moved more by potential loss than by equivalent gain. Cialdini imports Kahneman & Tversky's prospect-theory finding to ground the scarcity principle: frame what will be *lost*, not what will be gained. *Applies:* scarcity messaging, deadlines, and "you'll miss out" framing.

## Unity ("we is the shared me")
Shared identity where the other is experienced as part of the self — deeper than liking. Two families of levers: *being together* (kin, place, shared category) and *acting together* (co-creation, synchronous or joint action). *Origin:* introduced in *Pre-Suasion* (2016, ch. 12) and elevated to the seventh principle of influence in *Influence, New and Expanded* (2021, ch. 8, "Unity") — not merely "added in 2021." *Applies:* community-building, in-group framing, and co-created commitments.

## The commitment/consistency vs. reciprocity distinction in sequential requests
The corpus is precise that foot-in-the-door runs on *self-consistency* (I'm the kind of person who agreed before), while door-in-the-face runs on *reciprocal concession* (they backed off, so I should too). Getting the mechanism right dictates the correct technique. *Origin:* *Influence*, chs. 2-3; the 1975 and 1966 studies. *Applies:* choosing between escalate-up and retreat-down request structures.

## Bungler / smuggler / sleuth (the ethics model)
A model of the practitioner, not the target. The **sleuth (detective)** finds and honestly uses principles already present ("deftly wields only those persuasion tactics appropriate to the situation at hand"); the **smuggler** imports and fakes them ("illicitly imports a principle of influence into a situation where it does not naturally belong"); the **bungler** misses them entirely ("fumbles away an opportunity to tap a legitimate source of influence"). *Origin:* the three-type framing and quoted definitions are from *The Influence Smugglers* (Psychology Today, March 2018); the ethical-use argument is developed in *Pre-Suasion* (2016). *Applies:* every recommendation — the corpus's built-in guardrail.
