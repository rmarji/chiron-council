# Anti-patterns — Alex Hormozi

Everything the Hormozi corpus warns against, with its reasoning and the failure each produces. Sources are $100M Offers (2021), $100M Leads (2023), and recurring positions on The Game podcast.

## 1. Competing on price / discounting

**The move:** lowering price to win the deal.
**Why the corpus rejects it:** a discount lowers the *perceived value* of the offer and trains the market to wait for the next discount; it also signals that prices are negotiable, which erodes trust ($100M Offers, ch.5 "Pricing," ch.14 "Bonuses"). Worse, a low price lowers the *buyer's* commitment, which hurts their results and your testimonials ($100M Offers, ch.5).
**The failure it produces:** a race to the bottom where margin, delivery quality, and client outcomes all collapse together.
**The corpus's alternative:** stack a **bonus** of greater perceived value at the same price instead of cutting price ($100M Offers, ch.14).

## 2. Being a commodity

**The move:** selling the same thing, the same way, as everyone else.
**Why the corpus rejects it:** a commodity is "a product available from many places," so the only axis of competition left is price ($100M Offers, ch.3 "The Commodity Problem").
**The failure it produces:** price competition, thin margins, and interchangeability — the market has no reason to pick you.
**The corpus's alternative:** build a **Grand Slam Offer** with no apples-to-apples comparison ($100M Offers, ch.1).

## 3. Chasing hacks and get-rich-quick over reps

**The move:** hunting for the clever trick, the growth hack, the passive-income shortcut.
**Why the corpus rejects it:** there are no hacks — only skills and reps; the durable asset is your own capability, and it compounds only through volume ($100M Leads, Section II "Get Understanding"; The Game podcast).
**The failure it produces:** endless tactic-switching, no compounding skill, and a business that never gets past its first plateau.
**The corpus's alternative:** pick a channel, do it at volume, and let "volume negate luck."

## 4. Under-volume disguised as a strategy problem

**The move:** concluding a channel "doesn't work" after a handful of attempts and switching approaches.
**Why the corpus rejects it:** at low volume, outcomes are dominated by variance; you cannot tell signal from noise until the sample is large ("Volume negates luck," The Game podcast; $100M Leads, Section III).
**The failure it produces:** perpetual channel-hopping, where nothing is done long enough or hard enough to reveal whether it works.
**The corpus's alternative:** increase volume on the current channel until results become legible.

## 5. Adding channels or products before maxing the current one

**The move:** launching a second lead channel or a new product while the first is still un-saturated.
**Why the corpus rejects it:** a business has one binding constraint at a time; adding inputs before relieving the current bottleneck dilutes focus and hides the real problem ($100M Leads, Section III).
**The failure it produces:** shallow presence everywhere and mastery nowhere; the operator is busy but not growing.
**The corpus's alternative:** find the bottleneck stage — leads, offer, or fulfillment — and maximize it first.

## 6. A weak or absent lead magnet

**The move:** running ads or outreach straight to a paid offer with nothing given first, or giving away something thin.
**Why the corpus rejects it:** attention is expensive and cold; giving a genuinely valuable free thing earns the right to sell and warms the prospect ($100M Leads, Section III "Lead Magnets"). "A person who pays with their time now is more likely to pay with money later."
**The failure it produces:** low conversion, high acquisition cost, and a pipeline of cold, unengaged contacts.
**The corpus's alternative:** build a lead magnet so good people feel they should have paid for it.

## 7. Skipping the guarantee (leaving risk on the buyer)

**The move:** selling with no risk reversal because guarantees feel scary or costly.
**Why the corpus rejects it:** "reversing risk is the number one way to increase the conversion of an offer"; leaving the risk on the buyer leaves the single biggest conversion lever untouched ($100M Offers, ch.15).
**The failure it produces:** hesitation and lost deals that a guarantee would have closed.
**The corpus's alternative:** attach an unconditional, conditional, anti-, or implied guarantee — honestly, paired with real delivery.

## 8. Revealing bonuses before asking for the sale (in one-on-one)

**The move:** front-loading all the bonuses in the opening pitch.
**Why the corpus rejects it:** in one-on-one selling, bonuses are ammunition for handling hesitation; spent too early, they lose their power to overcome the specific objection that surfaces ($100M Offers, ch.14).
**The failure it produces:** a pitch that peaks too soon and has nothing left when the prospect stalls.
**The corpus's alternative:** ask for the sale first; reveal bonuses after, each one killing a specific objection.

## 9. Fake scarcity and manufactured urgency

**The move:** inventing a countdown, a "only 3 spots left" claim, or a deadline that isn't real.
**Why the corpus rejects it:** scarcity and urgency work because they are believed; a scarcity claim exposed as false breaks the trust the whole guarantee-and-delivery engine depends on ($100M Offers, ch.12-13, ch.15). The corpus's scarcity and urgency are *real* constraints — genuine capacity limits, real cohorts, honest deadlines.
**The failure it produces:** short-term lift followed by reputational damage and refund/chargeback risk.
**The corpus's alternative:** use real constraints; if none exist, create honest ones (limited cohorts, capacity caps).

## 10. Confusing brand/mission talk with the machine

**The move:** treating brand, vision, or "our why" as the growth lever.
**Why the corpus rejects it:** brand is a downstream *output* of a working offer delivered at scale, not an input; leading with mission while the offer is weak or the volume is low avoids the real work ($100M Leads; The Game podcast).
**The failure it produces:** a well-articulated brand attached to a business that can't reliably get leads or convert them.
**The corpus's alternative:** fix the offer and the unit economics (LTV-to-CAC), do the volume, and let brand accrue as a byproduct.
