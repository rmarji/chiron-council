# Frameworks — Alex Hormozi

Every named framework in the Hormozi corpus with when-to-use, concrete steps, and source. Sources are $100M Offers (2021) and $100M Leads (2023), chapter/section anchors as published.

## 1. The Grand Slam Offer build (the master framework)

*When to use:* the product is undifferentiated, discounting, or converting poorly on price.
*Steps* ($100M Offers, Section III-IV):
1. **Pick the right market** — a starving crowd with pain, purchasing power, easy to target, and growing ($100M Offers, ch.4).
2. **Charge a premium price** — set the price high enough to force delivery and buyer commitment ($100M Offers, ch.5).
3. **List problems and turn them into solutions** — enumerate every obstacle before and after use; convert each to a solution (ch.9).
4. **Choose delivery vehicles** with the Delivery Cube (ch.9).
5. **Trim & Stack** — score deliverables on value vs cost, trim the low-value, stack the high-value (ch.10).
6. **Enhance** — add scarcity, urgency, bonuses, guarantees, and a name (ch.11-16).
The output is an offer with no apples-to-apples comparison, so it competes on value, not price.

## 2. Problems & Solutions (Grand Slam Offer, Part I)

*When to use:* to generate the raw material of an offer.
*Steps* ($100M Offers, ch.9 "Create Your Offer: Problems & Solutions"):
1. Write down what happens **immediately before and immediately after** the prospect uses the product — the full timeline of the customer's experience.
2. List every problem, obstacle, and fear at each step (the corpus pushes for an exhaustive list, not a tidy one).
3. Convert each problem into a solution stated as a benefit ("how to ___ without ___").
4. Brainstorm *how* to deliver each solution using the **Delivery Cube**: vary level of personal attention (1-on-1 / group / one-to-many), level of customer effort (DIY / done-with-you / done-for-you), medium, format/frequency, and speed.

## 3. Trim & Stack (Grand Slam Offer, Part II)

*When to use:* to assemble the final offer from the solution list.
*Steps* ($100M Offers, ch.10 "Create Your Offer: Trim & Stack"):
1. Plot every candidate deliverable on the **value-vs-cost 2×2** (value to customer × cost/effort to you).
2. **Trim**: remove high-cost/low-value items first, then low-cost/low-value items.
3. **Stack**: keep and combine the surviving high-value items, biasing toward low-cost/high-value (especially one-to-many delivery).
4. Check against the **Sales-to-Fulfillment Continuum**: land on the sweet spot that both sells well and is feasible to fulfill at scale.

## 4. The Value Equation Audit

*When to use:* to raise value, price, or conversion without adding cost.
*Steps* ($100M Offers, ch.6):
1. Rate the current offer on Dream Outcome, Perceived Likelihood, Time Delay, Effort & Sacrifice.
2. Identify the weakest term and the cheapest lever to move it.
3. Prioritize the denominator: reduce time delay (deliver a fast first win) and effort/sacrifice (do more for the customer). Make it "immediate, seamless, and effortless."

## 5. Scarcity, Urgency, Bonuses, Guarantees, Naming (Enhancement)

*When to use:* after the core value is built, to raise conversion.
*Steps* ($100M Offers, ch.11-16):
- **Scarcity** (ch.12): limit supply honestly — capacity caps, limited cohorts, limited units.
- **Urgency** (ch.13): limit time honestly — rolling cohorts, seasonal or pricing deadlines, exploding offers.
- **Bonuses** (ch.14): add value at a constant price. Nine-point checklist — always offer them; give each a benefit-driven name; explain what it is, how it relates, and why it matters; prove its value; ascribe a dollar figure; tools/checklists beat extra trainings; each bonus should kill a specific objection; total bonus value should eclipse the core; add scarcity/urgency to the bonuses. In one-on-one selling, **ask for the sale first, reveal bonuses after**.
- **Guarantees** (ch.15): reverse risk (see next framework).
- **Naming** (ch.16): name the offer so it signals the outcome and the market — the corpus uses a make-a-magnetic-name approach built around the avatar, goal, time interval, and container.

## 6. The Guarantee Framework (four types)

*When to use:* when conversion is the bottleneck. "Reversing risk is the number one way to increase the conversion of an offer" ($100M Offers, ch.15).
*The four types:*
1. **Unconditional** — the strongest; pay-first trial, "if you're not happy, full refund, no questions."
2. **Conditional** — "if you do X and don't get Y, we do Z"; sub-types include outsized refund, "we work for free until you get the result," credit-based, and delayed-payment guarantees.
3. **Anti-guarantee** — "all sales final," used as a selling point that signals seriousness and filters buyers.
4. **Implied (performance)** — the seller's pay is tied to results: revenue share, profit share, or commission.
*Rule:* guarantees can be stacked, and they should be paired with real delivery, not used as a bluff.

## 7. The Core Four Lead Audit

*When to use:* when the user needs more leads or over-relies on one channel.
*Steps* ($100M Leads, Section III "Get Leads"):
1. Map current lead flow across **warm outreach, cold outreach, post content, paid ads**.
2. Pick the one channel the user can do *today* at higher volume.
3. Attach a **lead magnet** that over-delivers.
4. Increase reps/volume on that one channel before adding a second — "volume negates luck."
5. Use content as the multiplier that warms cold traffic and lifts the ROI of the other three.

## 8. The Lead Magnet Build

*When to use:* to convert attention into engaged, contactable leads cheaply.
*Steps* ($100M Leads, Section III "Lead Magnets"):
1. Pick a narrow problem your ideal customer has that is a *prerequisite* to your paid offer.
2. Give a *complete* solution to that narrow problem (reveal it fully; don't tease).
3. Make it valuable enough that people would have paid for it.
4. Capture contact info in exchange, then make the natural next step your paid offer.

## 9. The Four Lead-Getters (scaling leads through others)

*When to use:* once you can reliably get leads yourself and want leverage.
*Steps* ($100M Leads, Section IV "Get Lead-Getters"):
1. **Customers** — engineer referrals (make it easy, incentivized, and expected).
2. **Employees** — hire and train people to run the Core Four.
3. **Agencies** — rent expertise to run channels while you learn them.
4. **Affiliates & partners** — pay others to promote your offer to their audience.
The sequence: master a channel yourself, then multiply it through these four.
