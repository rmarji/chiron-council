# Sources — Marcus Aurelius (corpus)

Complete bibliography with dates. Primary source (the corpus itself) first, then the translations used for wording, then the doctrinal source (Epictetus) and the key scholarly framing (Hadot). `provenance.sources` in SKILL.md is drawn from this list.

## Primary corpus

- **Marcus Aurelius, *Meditations* (Greek: *Ta eis heauton*, "To Himself"), Books I-XII.** Written c. 170-180 CE, during Marcus's reign (161-180 CE), largely on military campaign on the Danube frontier. Private notebooks of Stoic spiritual exercises, not intended for publication; no title survives in the transmitted text (Book titles like "Debts and Lessons" for Book I are editorial additions — Hays's, in this case — with no warrant in the manuscript). Book I is a set of acknowledgments to family and teachers; Books II-XII are the philosophical entries. This is the entire authored corpus for this seat.

## Translations quoted or referenced (for wording)

- **Marcus Aurelius, *Meditations: A New Translation*, trans. Gregory Hays (Modern Library, 2002; often listed 2003 for later printings). ISBN 9780812968255.** The fluent, modern, beginner-friendly rendering; the default translation quoted in this seat for memorable wordings, always attributed as Hays because his English is interpretive (e.g., Book V.20, "what stands in the way becomes the way"). Introduction by Hays; some editions carry a foreword by Ryan Holiday.
- **Marcus Aurelius, *Meditations*, trans. George Long (1862).** Public domain; freely available via Project Gutenberg and the Internet Classics Archive. The classic Victorian rendering — dated diction, complete, literal enough for cross-checking Hays's phrasings.
- **Marcus Aurelius, *The Meditations of Marcus Aurelius Antoninus*, trans. A. S. L. Farquharson (Oxford, 1944).** A literal, scholarly translation with an authoritative commentary on the Greek text; the reference used to check whether a memorable English line is in the Greek or is a translator's flourish.
- **Marcus Aurelius, *Meditations, with Selected Correspondence*, trans. Robin Hard, introduction and notes by Christopher Gill (Oxford World's Classics, 2011).** Precise, well-annotated modern translation; includes surviving letters between Marcus and his tutor Fronto. Recommended as a rigorous second pass after Hays.

## Doctrinal source (the philosophy Marcus practiced)

- **Epictetus, *Discourses* and *Enchiridion* (Handbook), recorded by Arrian of Nicomedia, c. 108 CE.** Epictetus (c. 50-135 CE), a former slave turned Stoic teacher, wrote nothing himself; Arrian transcribed his lectures. The *Enchiridion* opens with the dichotomy of control (ch. 1) and states "men are disturbed not by things but by their opinions about things" (ch. 5) — the two doctrines the Meditations most rely on. Marcus was introduced to Epictetus's discourses by his Stoic teacher Junius Rusticus, who lent him his own copy (Meditations Book I.7). The seat cites Epictetus as the origin of the dichotomy of control.

## Scholarly framing (used for one term and one schema)

- **Pierre Hadot, *The Inner Citadel: The Meditations of Marcus Aurelius*, trans. Michael Chase (Harvard University Press, 1998; French original *La Citadelle intérieure*, 1992).** The source of the phrase "inner citadel" (not Marcus's own term) for the *hēgemonikon*, and of the influential reading of the Meditations as organized around Epictetus's three disciplines (assent, desire, action). Cited where the seat uses "inner citadel" or "the three disciplines."

## Note on citation convention

The seat cites the corpus by Meditations Book number in Roman numerals with the section number (e.g., Book IV.7, Book IX.30). Section numbering is standard and consistent across modern translations, so a citation locates the passage regardless of which translation a reader holds; the *wording* of any quotation, however, is translation-specific and is always attributed to a named translator.
