# Frameworks — the Taleb corpus (the Incerto)

Named, actionable frameworks with when-to-use, steps, and source. Where the corpus states a procedure informally, the steps below make it operational without adding claims it doesn't hold.

## The Fragility Audit (detection by exposure, not prediction)

*When to use:* Any exposure to a shock you cannot forecast — a portfolio, a business model, a supply chain, a career, a body, a system.

*Steps:*
1. **Stop forecasting the trigger.** Accept you cannot predict the earthquake; you can only assess the bridge (Antifragile, 2012, Prologue).
2. **Map the payoff to volatility.** Does a larger shock help or hurt you? Is your loss bounded or open-ended? Open-ended loss with capped gain is concave = fragile; the reverse is convex = antifragile (Antifragile, 2012, ch. 18–19).
3. **Find the hidden fragilities:** leverage/debt (removes slack), single points of failure, correlated exposures, suppressed volatility masking stored risk, optimization that removed all redundancy (Antifragile, 2012, ch. 3, "The Cat and the Washing Machine").
4. **Subtract (via negativa).** Remove the fragilities before adding any clever fix. Robustness is what remains once the fragilities are gone (Antifragile, 2012, Book VII).
5. **Where possible, add convexity** — redundancy, optionality, a barbell — to move from robust toward antifragile.

## The Barbell (allocation under deep uncertainty)

*When to use:* Allocating capital, career risk, time, health effort, or attention when the future is fat-tailed and unknowable.

*Steps:*
1. **Split into two extremes.** Put the large majority (e.g., 85–90%) into maximally safe, low-variance holdings so that the worst case leaves the core intact.
2. **Put a small tranche (e.g., 10–15%) into high-variance, high-upside bets whose total loss you can fully afford** — venture bets, speculative options, moonshot projects.
3. **Refuse the middle.** "Moderate-risk" positions are where fat tails hide under a respectable label; the middle gives you neither real safety nor real convex upside (Antifragile, 2012, ch. 11).
4. **Cap the loss on the risky end explicitly** and let the upside run — bounded downside, open-ended upside is the whole point.
*Result:* protected from negative Black Swans, exposed to positive ones (Antifragile, 2012, ch. 11).

## The Skin-in-the-Game Test (evaluating advice, forecasts, agents)

*When to use:* Weighing any recommendation, forecast, expert opinion, or delegated decision.

*Steps:*
1. **Ask who bears the downside if this is wrong.** Trace the loss to a person, not an institution (Skin in the Game, 2018, Prologue).
2. **If the adviser is insulated from the loss, discount the advice** and re-price the risk yourself; treat "expert with no penalty for error" as near-zero signal (Skin in the Game, 2018, ch. 1).
3. **Prefer the counterparty who eats their own cooking** — who holds what they recommend, who loses if you lose.
4. **Beware asymmetric compensation:** anyone paid on the upside while shielded from the downside is incentivized to sell you tail risk as smoothness (Skin in the Game, 2018, ch. 1 and ch. 19).

## The Optionality / Convex Tinkering framework (acting under ignorance)

*When to use:* Innovation, career moves, research, entrepreneurship — situations where you cannot know in advance which bet pays off.

*Steps:*
1. **Prefer many small bets with strictly capped loss and open-ended upside** over one large committed plan (Antifragile, 2012, ch. 12–15).
2. **Keep the option; delay commitment.** Optionality lets you be right for the wrong reasons — the payoff shape, not the forecast, does the work (Antifragile, 2012, ch. 12, "Thales' Sweet Grapes").
3. **Cut losers fast, let winners run.** Since downside is capped and upside is not, you want maximum exposure to the tail of good outcomes (this is the convexity of trial and error).
4. **Tinker rather than design top-down.** Trial and error in a convex space beats theory-first planning, because it harvests positive Black Swans no plan anticipated (Antifragile, 2012, ch. 15, "History Written by the Losers").

## The Ergodicity / Ruin filter (one-shot vs repeated bets)

*When to use:* Any decision where a bad outcome could be unrecoverable, and someone is quoting you an attractive average or expected value.

*Steps:*
1. **Ask: is this ergodic?** Would the average across many players equal my own path over time — or could a bad draw knock me out permanently? (Skin in the Game, 2018, ch. 19).
2. **If ruin is possible, ignore the expected value.** A single absorbing barrier makes the ensemble average irrelevant to you; "on average four feet deep" drowns you at the deep spot.
3. **Reduce exposure until no single outcome is unrecoverable**, then optimize within what survives.
4. **Repeat only games you can survive losing** — survival is the precondition for compounding (Skin in the Game, 2018, ch. 19).

## The Lindy screen (choosing between old and new)

*When to use:* Deciding which idea, technology, practice, or institution to trust when other factors are close.

*Steps:*
1. **For non-perishables, treat age as evidence of robustness** — time has already stress-tested the survivor (Antifragile, 2012, ch. 20).
2. **Default to the time-tested over the novel** absent a strong specific reason; the burden of proof is on the new.
3. **Distrust "this time is different"** and fashionable interventions with short track records, especially in fat-tailed domains.
4. **Use Lindy as epistemic humility, not dogma** — the screen is a prior, over-ridable by evidence, not a ban on the new.

## The minority-rule lens (predicting system-level outcomes)

*When to use:* Forecasting how a market, culture, or coalition will behave when agents have heterogeneous, asymmetric preferences.

*Steps:*
1. **Look for the intransigent minority** — the group with a hard constraint that cannot flex (allergy, faith, ideology, standard) (The Most Intolerant Wins, 2016).
2. **Note that the flexible majority will accommodate**, so a small stubborn share (as little as 3–4%) can set the system-level default.
3. **Do not predict the aggregate from "what most people want"** — predict it from the binding constraint of the most inflexible group (Skin in the Game, 2018, ch. 2).
4. **Design accordingly:** to move a system, you often move the intransigent minority, not the median.
