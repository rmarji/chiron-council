# Anti-patterns — the Taleb corpus (the Incerto)

Everything the corpus warns against, with the reasoning and the failure it produces. Paraphrased; verbatim lines in `quotes.md`.

## The ludic fallacy — modeling life on games

**What it is:** Treating real-world uncertainty as if it had the known, bounded, computable probabilities of a casino or a textbook dice problem.
**Why it fails:** In games the rules and the tails are fixed and known; in reality the rules are unknown and the tails are unbounded, so the neat model omits precisely the event that matters. The character "Dr John" reasons from the model and is blindsided; "Fat Tony" asks what's outside it (The Black Swan, 2007, ch. 9).
**Failure produced:** False confidence in risk numbers; blowups from "impossible" events that were only impossible inside the model.

## Turkey induction — mistaking a quiet record for safety

**What it is:** Concluding, from a long benign track record, that a system is safe.
**Why it fails:** The quiet can be the risk building; confidence peaks exactly when exposure peaks. A thousand fed days are the turkey's strongest evidence and its fatal error (The Black Swan, 2007, ch. 4).
**Failure produced:** Maximum position at the moment of maximum hidden risk — the classic pre-crash posture.

## Naive Gaussian / Value-at-Risk in fat-tailed domains

**What it is:** Applying the bell curve, standard deviation, and Value-at-Risk to Extremistan phenomena (markets, credit, casualties).
**Why it fails:** The Gaussian assigns negligible probability to large deviations, but in Extremistan the large deviations are where nearly all the action lives; VaR reports a comforting number precisely by ignoring the tail beyond it (The Black Swan, 2007, ch. 15, "The Bell Curve, That Great Intellectual Fraud").
**Failure produced:** Systematic underpricing of tail risk; the 2008-style blowup where "25-sigma" events recur.

## Forecasting the unforecastable

**What it is:** Producing point estimates or precise probabilities for rare, high-impact events (crash dates, "15% chance of a pandemic this year," specific geopolitical shocks).
**Why it fails:** Black Swans are unpredictable by construction, and the record of expert forecasters in fat-tailed domains is poor; worse, the forecast breeds action that increases fragility (The Black Swan, 2007, ch. 10, "The Scandal of Prediction").
**Failure produced:** Plans and leverage built on false precision, which then break on the event the forecast missed.

## The fragilista and naive interventionism (iatrogenics)

**What it is:** The compulsion to "do something," intervening in systems that would self-correct, and suppressing natural volatility.
**Why it fails:** Suppressing small stressors removes feedback and stores risk, converting many small survivable shocks into one large correlated catastrophe (suppressed forest fires, over-stabilized economies, over-medicated healthy patients) (Antifragile, 2012, ch. 21 and Book II).
**Failure produced:** Iatrogenic harm — the intervention causes a bigger problem than the one it "solved," while the interventionist bears none of it.

## No skin in the game — asymmetry between decider and bearer

**What it is:** Letting people make decisions whose downside falls on others — bailed-out bankers, consultants, forecasters with no penalty, managers optimizing their bonus window.
**Why it fails:** It corrupts both incentives (risk is offloaded) and information (the insulated give bad signals), and it prevents the evolutionary cleansing that removes fragile risk-takers (Skin in the Game, 2018, Prologue and ch. 1).
**Failure produced:** Concentrated, hidden, socialized tail risk; a system that rewards the transfer of risk rather than its ownership.

## Optimizing away redundancy — efficiency as fragility

**What it is:** Removing all slack, buffers, and duplication in the name of efficiency, and financing it with debt.
**Why it fails:** Redundancy is the buffer that absorbs shocks; removing it maximizes performance in the expected case and minimizes survival in the tail. Debt removes the slack that lets a system bend instead of break (Antifragile, 2012, Prologue and ch. 3).
**Failure produced:** A system that runs beautifully until the first real shock, then fails catastrophically for want of any buffer.

## Overprotection — starving an antifragile system of stressors

**What it is:** Shielding a system that needs stressors — an economy, a child, a body, a career — from all volatility.
**Why it fails:** Antifragile systems require graded stress to grow (hormesis); removing all stress causes atrophy and hidden weakening, so the eventual, unavoidable shock lands on a system that never learned to absorb it (Antifragile, 2012, ch. 2 and Book IV).
**Failure produced:** Weakness disguised as stability; a brittle system that has lost the capacity to adapt.

## The narrative fallacy — explaining away randomness with stories

**What it is:** Imposing tidy causal stories on random or complex sequences after the fact.
**Why it fails:** The story creates an illusion of understanding and predictability, hides the role of chance, and makes the next surprise feel like it "should have been obvious" (The Black Swan, 2007, ch. 6).
**Failure produced:** Overconfidence; misattributed causation; false lessons drawn from noise.

## Ignoring silent evidence — reasoning only from survivors

**What it is:** Learning from winners and success stories without counting the invisible losers who took the same path and were destroyed.
**Why it fails:** Survivorship bias inflates the apparent skill of survivors and the wisdom of their methods; the graveyard doesn't testify (The Black Swan, 2007, ch. 8; Fooled by Randomness, 2001, ch. 8).
**Failure produced:** Copying "what the winners did" and inheriting their risk without their luck.

## Confusing luck with skill

**What it is:** Reading a good outcome as proof of a good process, especially over short horizons in noisy domains.
**Why it fails:** Randomness alone manufactures impressive track records; without counting alternative histories and the base rate of lucky survivors, you cannot separate the two (Fooled by Randomness, 2001, ch. 3 and 8).
**Failure produced:** Promotion of the lucky, over-betting on noise, and blowups when the luck reverts.

## The Intellectual Yet Idiot (IYI) — theory without exposure

**What it is:** Deferring to the credentialed expert who prescribes to others but bears no consequences and lacks tacit, practitioner knowledge.
**Why it fails:** Without skin in the game the expert's errors are costless to them and therefore uncorrected; abstract knowledge untested by reality mistakes its own gaps for the irrationality of others (The Intellectual Yet Idiot, 2016; Skin in the Game, 2018, ch. 18).
**Failure produced:** Confident, credentialed advice that is fragile to the real world, imposed on people who will bear its costs.
