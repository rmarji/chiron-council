# Sources — Peter Thiel (corpus)

Complete bibliography for this seat, primary sources first. Every claim in SKILL.md and the reference files traces to an entry here. The seat is deliberately scoped to the published **business/strategy** corpus; Thiel's political and cultural writing is out of scope and cited only where needed to mark a boundary.

## Primary — the anchor works

1. **Zero to One: Notes on Startups, or How to Build the Future** — Peter Thiel with Blake Masters, Crown Business, 2014. The core text and the single most-cited source in this seat. Chapter anchors used throughout:
   - Ch. 1 — "The Challenge of the Future": 0 to 1 vs 1 to n; the contrarian question.
   - Ch. 2 — "Party Like It's 1999": lessons from the dot-com crash.
   - Ch. 3 — "All Happy Companies Are Different": competition vs monopoly; "competition is for losers."
   - Ch. 4 — "The Ideology of Competition."
   - Ch. 5 — "Last Mover Advantage": the four monopoly traits; start small and monopolize; durability; last mover.
   - Ch. 6 — "You Are Not a Lottery Ticket": definite vs indefinite optimism/pessimism (the future grid).
   - Ch. 7 — "Follow the Money": the power law of venture returns.
   - Ch. 8 — "Secrets": natural and human secrets; "brilliant thinking is rare, but courage is in even shorter supply than genius."
   - Ch. 11 — "If You Build It, Will They Come?": distribution and sales.
   - Ch. 13 — "Seeing Green": the seven questions (via the cleantech post-mortem and Tesla).
   - Ch. 14 — "The Founder's Paradox": the founder.

2. **CS183: Startup** — Blake Masters' notes from Thiel's Stanford class, Spring 2012, published at blakemasters.tumblr.com (19 lectures). These are class *notes*, cited separately from the book. Class anchors used:
   - Class 1 — "The Challenge of the Future."
   - Class 4 — "The Last Mover Advantage": monopoly traits, durability, last mover.
   - Class 5 — "The Mechanics of Mafia": teams and culture (guests Max Levchin, Stephen Cohen).
   - Class 7 — "Follow The Money": VC power-law economics.
   - Class 9 — "If You Build It, Will They Come?": distribution and sales.
   - Class 11 — "Secrets."
   - Class 13 — "You Are Not A Lottery Ticket": definite vs indefinite optimism.
   - Class 18 — "Founder as Victim, Founder as God": the founder.

   **Attribution note:** the *seven questions* framework (engineering, timing, monopoly, people, distribution, durability, secret) is from the **book** (Zero to One, ch. 13), not the CS183 notes. The notes organize the material around a different, longer list of startup elements (markets, mimesis/competition, secrets, incrementalism, durability, teams, distribution, timing, financing, luck). Cite the two sources separately and never conflate them.

## Primary — essays and talks (business/strategy)

3. **"Competition Is for Losers"** — The Wall Street Journal, Sept 12, 2014. Adapted from Zero to One; the canonical short statement of the competition-vs-monopoly thesis.

4. **"You Should Run Your Startup Like a Cult"** — Wired, Sept 2014. A Zero to One adaptation on culture and teams (the consultant-nihilism vs cultish-dogmatism spectrum).

5. **"The End of the Future"** — National Review, Oct 3, 2011. The technological-stagnation thesis (progress slowing from roughly the early 1970s). Used for context on why the corpus prizes definite, bold, 0-to-1 bets; shades toward the political corpus, so used sparingly.

## Interviews

6. **Conversations with Tyler (Tyler Cowen)** — recorded/released Apr 6, 2015. The strategy-relevant conversation: stagnation, definite optimism, the shape of progress. (The later 2024 CWT episode is political/theological and is out of scope for this seat.)

## Provenance and biographical facts (verified, for accurate attribution)

- Thiel **co-founded** PayPal (1998) and was CEO until the eBay sale (2002).
- Thiel was Facebook's **first outside investor** (Aug 2004, $500k for 10.2%).
- Palantir (2003) and Founders Fund (2005, with Ken Howery and Luke Nosek) are **co-founded**, not founded solo.
- The "flying cars / 140 characters" line is the **tagline of Founders Fund's 2011 manifesto "What Happened to the Future?", written by Bruce Gibney** — associated with Thiel's stagnation thesis but not authored by Thiel.

## Out of scope (deliberately excluded)

- "The Education of a Libertarian" (Cato Unbound, Apr 13, 2009) and Thiel's broader political, religious, and cultural writing. This seat covers only the published business/strategy corpus.

## Citation conventions used in this seat

- Book claims: "Zero to One, ch. N" (with the chapter title on first use).
- Class-notes claims: "CS183 Class N" (with the class title on first use).
- Essay/interview claims: title + publication + date.
- Direct quotes appear only in `quotes.md`-verified wording, inside quotation marks; everything else is paraphrase and is marked as such.
- The seven questions are always attributed to the book, never to CS183.
