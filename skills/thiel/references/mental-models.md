# Mental models — Peter Thiel (corpus)

Every named model or concept the corpus uses, defined, with origin and when it applies. Sources: *Zero to One* (2014) by chapter; CS183 notes (2012) by class number and title. Paraphrase throughout unless quoted; verbatim lines are in `quotes.md`.

## The contrarian question

*Definition:* "What important truth do very few people agree with you on?" (Zero to One, ch. 1). A device for locating value: the answer must be something you believe is true but the crowd rejects. Its business form is "What valuable company is nobody building?"
*When it applies:* At the very start of idea evaluation, and as an interview question to find independent thinkers. If a founder can't answer it, the corpus doubts there's a secret underneath the business.

## 0 to 1 vs 1 to n

*Definition:* Two kinds of progress. 1-to-n is horizontal/extensive — copying what works, spreading proven models (globalization). 0-to-1 is vertical/intensive — creating something new (technology) (Zero to One, ch. 1; CS183 Class 1).
*When it applies:* Diagnosing whether a "startup" is actually creating value or imitating. Copycats and "the Uber of X" pitches are 1-to-n; a genuinely new capability is 0-to-1.

## Competition vs monopoly

*Definition:* Two ends of a spectrum. Perfect competition drives profit to zero; monopoly (a company so good at what it does that no one can offer a close substitute) earns durable profit and can plan long-term (Zero to One, ch. 3; "Competition Is for Losers," WSJ 2014). The corpus's provocation: competition is a state to escape, not a virtue.
*When it applies:* Whenever someone frames a market as a fight to win. The model reframes the question from "how do we beat rivals?" to "how do we not have rivals?"

## The four traits of monopoly

*Definition:* Proprietary technology (10x better than substitutes), network effects, economies of scale, and branding (Zero to One, ch. 5; CS183 Class 4). A durable monopoly usually combines several.
*When it applies:* Testing whether a claimed advantage is real. If none of the four is present and durable, the position will compete away.

## Last mover advantage

*Definition:* The advantage of making the last great development in a market and holding it for years or decades, which the corpus prizes over first-mover advantage (Zero to One, ch. 5; CS183 Class 4). Rooted in the idea that a business's value is the discounted sum of far-future cash flows, so durability dominates.
*When it applies:* When founders over-index on being first. The model shifts attention to whether the lead can be held.

## The determinate/indeterminate x optimist/pessimist grid

*Definition:* A 2x2 view of the future (Zero to One, ch. 6; CS183 Class 13). Four quadrants: definite optimism (specific knowable good future you build toward — mid-century US), indefinite optimism (expect good, plan nothing — the modern West, ~1982-2007), definite pessimism (calculated decline/copying — the corpus's read of China), indefinite pessimism (drift with no direction — the corpus's read of Japan/Europe, "the worst of all worlds").
*When it applies:* Evaluating any plan or worldview. The corpus pushes everything toward the definite-optimist quadrant.

## The power law

*Definition:* Returns are distributed by a power law, not a bell curve: a few outcomes dwarf all the rest combined; in a venture fund the single best investment can outperform the entire rest of the fund (Zero to One, ch. 7, "Follow the Money"; CS183 Class 7).
*When it applies:* Allocating scarce resources — capital, career choice, effort. The model argues for concentration on the one thing that can be enormous and against diversifying into mediocrity.

## Secrets (natural and human)

*Definition:* Important truths that few people know or believe, dividing into natural secrets (facts about the physical world) and human secrets (facts about people and what they hide) (Zero to One, ch. 8). Every great business is built on a secret others miss.
*When it applies:* Testing whether a business has a defensible basis. "There are no secrets left" is, in the corpus, a self-fulfilling excuse for incrementalism.

## The seven questions

*Definition:* Engineering, Timing, Monopoly, People, Distribution, Durability, Secret — the seven questions every business must answer (Zero to One, ch. 13, "Seeing Green"). Cleantech firms answered a few and failed; Tesla answered all seven.
*When it applies:* A full go/no-go evaluation of a business. (Note: this is the *book's* framework. CS183's own list of startup elements differs and should not be conflated — see `frameworks.md`.)

## Definite vs indefinite thinking about plans

*Definition:* A definite view treats the future as knowable and plans specifically; an indefinite view treats it as unknowable and keeps options open, favoring process, optionality, and diversification (Zero to One, ch. 6).
*When it applies:* When a strategy hedges everything. The corpus reads indefinite thinking as the cause of over-financialization and "well-rounded" résumé culture, and prescribes a committed definite plan instead.

## The founder's paradox

*Definition:* Founders are unusual people whose traits sit at extremes; that distinctiveness is valuable to the company but also makes founders prone to being mythologized or scapegoated (Zero to One, ch. 14; CS183 Class 18, "Founder as Victim, Founder as God").
*When it applies:* Thinking about leadership and succession — the corpus warns against sanding founders into ordinary managers.

## The cult/mafia culture spectrum

*Definition:* Company culture runs from consultant-nihilism (mercenaries, no shared mission) to cultish dogmatism; the corpus advises leaning toward the cult end — a team obsessively aligned on a mission ("You Should Run Your Startup Like a Cult," Wired 2014; CS183 Class 5, "The Mechanics of Mafia").
*When it applies:* Building an early team. The model favors mission-alignment and people who genuinely want to work together over résumé diversity.

## Definite vs indefinite optimism as a stagnation diagnosis

*Definition:* The corpus extends the grid into a macro thesis: real technological progress slowed around the early 1970s, and indefinite optimism (plus over-reliance on finance and process) is why ("The End of the Future," National Review, Oct 3 2011). The Founders Fund manifesto tagline "we wanted flying cars, instead we got 140 characters" (written by Bruce Gibney, 2011) is associated with this thesis but is *not* a Thiel line.
*When it applies:* Contextualizing why the corpus prizes bold, definite, 0-to-1 bets over incremental ones. Use with care; this shades into the political corpus, which this seat stays out of.
