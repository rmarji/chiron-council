# Disagreements — thiel

Authored conflicts on the record. Councils surface these; they never invent new ones.

## vs rumelt — a monopoly template vs bespoke diagnosis

counterpart: rumelt
topic: where good strategy comes from; a single strategic template vs case-by-case diagnosis; competition, secrets, and definite optimism vs the strategy kernel

**Position (thiel):** Good strategy has a recognizable shape and a preferred answer. The corpus prescribes a specific template: escape competition by building a monopoly ("competition is for losers"; "monopoly is the condition of every successful business"), rest that monopoly on a differentiated secret and a durable 10x trait, start with a big share of a small market, and commit to a *definite* future rather than hedging — indefinite optimism builds nothing. Run any business through the seven questions and weight durability and secret above current growth (Zero to One, chs. 1, 3, 5, 6, 8, 13; "Competition Is for Losers," WSJ, Sept 12 2014). Direction, in this view, comes from a contrarian truth plus a monopoly thesis — a strong prior about what a great business looks like.

**Position (rumelt):** There is no universal template; good strategy is bespoke. Every real strategy is a *kernel* — an honest diagnosis of the specific challenge, a guiding policy that concentrates force at the crux, and coherent actions that reinforce each other. Most of the value is in the diagnosis: correctly naming *this* situation's obstacle. A pre-packaged answer imported before diagnosis (even "seek monopoly" or "be a definite optimist") is exactly the template-style planning and New-Thought positive-thinking Rumelt files under *bad strategy* (Good Strategy Bad Strategy, 2011, chs. 1, 3-5; "The perils of bad strategy," McKinsey Quarterly, 2011; The Crux, 2022). Strategy is diagnosis first, not a formula applied.

**The overlap (why this isn't a caricature):** Both corpora loathe the same enemy — fluffy, undifferentiated strategy: goals mistaken for plans, "compete harder" with no edge, aspiration standing in for design. Both prize concentration and focus over spreading effort thin (Thiel's power law; Rumelt's guiding policy and crux). Both would reject a "grow 20% in a huge market by out-executing incumbents" plan.

**The live edge:** they part on *method*. The Thiel corpus supplies a strong prior about the answer (monopoly, secret, definite optimism) and reasons from it; Rumelt supplies a strong prior about the *process* (diagnose the specific challenge, then design) and distrusts any dogmatic formula that arrives before the diagnosis. When a founder asks "what makes this a good strategy?", Thiel's corpus asks "where is your monopoly and your secret?"; Rumelt's asks "what exactly is the challenge here, and does this concentrate force on its crux?" One tests the plan against a template; the other insists the template is the trap.
