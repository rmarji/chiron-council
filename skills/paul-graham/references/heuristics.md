# Heuristics — the Paul Graham corpus

The full set of operational rules from the essays, in IF/THEN form, each attributed. These are decision rules, not slogans: each should change what the founder does next. Quotes are marked; the rest is paraphrase.

## Ideas

- **IF you are trying to "think of a startup idea," THEN stop and look for a problem you have yourself instead** — "The way to get startup ideas is not to try to think of startup ideas. It's to look for problems, preferably problems you have yourself" ("How to Get Startup Ideas," 2012).
- **IF an idea sounds plausible when described casually but you can't name people who badly need it, THEN treat it as a "sitcom" idea and reject it** — "The most common mistake startups make is to solve problems no one has" ("How to Get Startup Ideas," 2012).
- **IF an idea would serve a large group weakly, THEN look instead for a small group that needs it intensely** — prefer the deep "well" (like Facebook's Harvard students) over broad, shallow interest ("How to Get Startup Ideas," 2012).
- **IF you want to notice good ideas, THEN put yourself at the frontier of a fast-changing field and "live in the future"** — become "the sort of person who has them" rather than brainstorming on a weekend ("How to Get Startup Ideas," 2012).
- **IF an idea feels scary or tedious and you find yourself avoiding it, THEN check for schlep blindness — that avoidance may mark the best idea** — "If you pick an ambitious idea, you'll have less competition, because everyone else will have been frightened off" ("Schlep Blindness," 2012).
- **IF you want to surface a schlep-hidden idea, THEN ask "what problem do I wish someone else would solve?" instead of "what should I solve?"** — this routes around the unconscious filter ("Schlep Blindness," 2012).

## Getting users and traction

- **IF you are waiting for users to show up, THEN go recruit them manually, one at a time** — "You can't wait for users to come to you. You have to go out and get them" ("Do Things That Don't Scale," 2013).
- **IF a user agrees to try your product, THEN set them up on the spot rather than promising to "send a link"** — the Collison installation; don't be too diffident to close ("Do Things That Don't Scale," 2013).
- **IF an action would delight your earliest users but doesn't scale, THEN do it anyway** — "I have never once seen a startup lured down a blind alley by trying too hard to make their initial users happy" ("Do Things That Don't Scale," 2013).
- **IF you're launching into a marketplace or two-sided market, THEN expect to take heroic manual measures to get it rolling** — "Marketplaces are so hard to get rolling that you should expect to take heroic measures at first" ("Do Things That Don't Scale," 2013).
- **IF you're tempted to bet growth on a big launch or a partnership, THEN distrust it** — big launches and partnerships rarely produce sustainable growth; "Any strategy that omits the effort" of manually getting users is "ipso facto suspect" ("Do Things That Don't Scale," 2013).
- **IF you can automate something now or do it by hand, THEN do it by hand first to launch faster and learn** — automate later ("Do Things That Don't Scale," 2013).

## Measuring and deciding

- **IF you are drowning in decisions, THEN pick a weekly growth rate and let it decide — anything that gets you that growth is ipso facto right** ("Startup = Growth," 2012).
- **IF you want to know whether the startup is working, THEN look at the weekly growth rate: 5-7% is good, 10% exceptional, 1% means you haven't figured it out yet** ("Startup = Growth," 2012).
- **IF you're getting a roughly constant number of new customers each period, THEN treat it as trouble — a constant number means your growth *rate* is decreasing** ("Startup = Growth," 2012).

## Survival and spend

- **IF you have raised money and are about to hire, THEN first write down whether you're default alive or default dead** — "Hiring too fast is by far the biggest killer of startups that raise money" ("Default Alive or Default Dead?," 2015).
- **IF you don't know your survival trajectory, THEN compute it now — early — rather than discovering it too late** — "Half the founders I talk to don't know whether they're default alive or default dead" ("Default Alive or Default Dead?," 2015).
- **IF you are default dead with slow growth, THEN treat it as the fatal pinch and fix the product/growth before anything else** — "default dead + slow growth + not enough time to fix it" ("Default Alive or Default Dead?," 2015).
- **IF you're tempted to spend to grow, THEN stay cheap until users clearly want the product** — "spend as little money as possible"; get big slow ("How to Start a Startup," 2005).

## Founders and execution

- **IF a plan just broke on contact with reality, THEN adapt relentlessly rather than be battered by it** — be relentlessly resourceful, "Not merely relentless" ("Relentlessly Resourceful," 2009).
- **IF you're choosing between two equally committed, equally qualified co-founders vs. a stronger idea, THEN pick the better people** — "Good people can fix bad ideas, but good ideas can't save bad people" ("How to Start a Startup," 2005).
- **IF you're a builder and meetings are eating your afternoons, THEN protect contiguous blocks and cluster meetings at the day's edges** — "A single meeting can blow a whole afternoon" ("Maker's Schedule, Manager's Schedule," 2009).

## Choosing what to work on at all

- **IF you're deciding what to work on, THEN follow genuine curiosity to the frontier and notice the gaps** — "Curiosity is the key to all four steps in doing great work" ("How to Do Great Work," 2023).
- **IF you're not sure what to work on, THEN start working and guess** — "The way to figure out what to work on is by working. If you're not sure what to work on, guess" ("How to Do Great Work," 2023).
- **IF you're choosing work by how impressive it sounds, THEN discount prestige** — "The prestige of a type of work is at best a trailing indicator" ("How to Do Great Work," 2023).
- **IF you want sharper judgment about your work, THEN be earnest — i.e. intellectually honest** — "The core of being earnest is being intellectually honest" ("How to Do Great Work," 2023).
