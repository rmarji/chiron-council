# Mental models — the Paul Graham corpus

Every named model or concept the essays use, defined, with origin and when it applies. Attributions are to the named essay and year. Quotes are marked; the rest is paraphrase.

## Startup = growth

The definitional model. A "startup" is not a stage or a sector but a *design goal*: a company built to grow fast. "The only essential thing is growth. Everything else we associate with startups follows from growth" ("Startup = Growth," 2012). *Applies when:* classifying whether something is even a startup, and deciding whether growth-oriented advice fits. A local restaurant, however good, is outside the model.

## Growth as compass / growth as truth

Once growth is the essential thing, the weekly growth rate becomes both the primary instrument and the arbiter of decisions: "anything that gets you the growth you need is ipso facto right," and "For startups, growth is a constraint much like truth" ("Startup = Growth," 2012). *Applies when:* a founder is overwhelmed by choices or self-deceiving about progress; the growth rate cuts through both.

## The barbershop vs. the search engine

The canonical contrast for why most new companies aren't startups. A barbershop makes something people want but is bounded by geography and personal service; a search engine can reach a huge market. Growing fast requires both (a) making something many people want and (b) a scalable way to reach and serve them ("Startup = Growth," 2012). *Applies when:* assessing whether a business idea can grow fast at all, or is structurally a good small business.

## Do things that don't scale

The core early-stage model: deliberately do labor-intensive work that could never scale, because that is how a fragile startup ignites. Sub-models within it (all from "Do Things That Don't Scale," 2013):

- **Recruit (manual acquisition):** "You have to go out and get them." Get the first users one by one.
- **The Collison installation:** don't say "we'll send you a link" — set the user up on the spot. "When anyone agreed to try Stripe they'd say 'Right then, give me your laptop' and set them up on the spot."
- **Delight:** deliver "insanely great" service beyond what scales; "I have never once seen a startup lured down a blind alley by trying too hard to make their initial users happy."
- **Fire (contained fire):** start with a narrow market segment and get it very hot before expanding (Facebook began "just for Harvard students").
- **Manual:** do by hand what you'll later automate, to launch faster.
- **Vector:** think of a startup as a product plus the unscalable move to get its first users — "pairs of what you're going to build, plus the unscalable thing(s) you're going to do initially."

*Applies when:* a startup is pre-traction, waiting for growth, or over-building automation before having users.

## The prepared mind / live in the future

Ideas come to a prepared mind at the frontier of a fast-changing field: "Live in the future and build what seems interesting" ("How to Get Startup Ideas," 2012). Preparation ("Empirically, the way to have good startup ideas is to become the sort of person who has them") beats a weekend brainstorm. *Applies when:* a founder is trying to force an idea instead of positioning themselves to notice one.

## The "well" (narrow, deep demand)

Great early ideas serve a small group who need the thing intensely — a deep well — rather than a large group who want it weakly. Facebook's Harvard students needed it badly. *Applies when:* evaluating whether an idea has a beachhead of intense demand versus broad, shallow interest ("How to Get Startup Ideas," 2012).

## Sitcom ideas / solving a problem no one has

A "sitcom" or "made-up" idea sounds plausible when described casually ("a social network for pet owners" — "yeah, maybe") but no one actually needs it. "The most common mistake startups make is to solve problems no one has" ("How to Get Startup Ideas," 2012). *Applies when:* stress-testing an idea; if you can't name people who badly want it, it's likely a sitcom idea.

## Schlep blindness

A schlep is "a tedious, unpleasant task"; schlep blindness is the unconscious refusal to even see ideas that involve big schleps: "Your unconscious won't even let you see ideas that involve painful schleps" ("Schlep Blindness," 2012). Because most founders are blind this way, schlep-heavy ideas (like Stripe's payments) are undervalued and under-competed — "That scariness makes ambitious ideas doubly valuable." *Applies when:* a founder is drawn only to "clean" software ideas, or is avoiding an idea for reasons they can't articulate.

## Relentlessly resourceful vs. hapless

A two-pole model of founder temperament. Hapless = passive, "battered by circumstances." Relentlessly resourceful = adaptive, aggressive, improvising toward the goal — and it must be relentless *and* resourceful, since plans break: "Not merely relentless" ("Relentlessly Resourceful," 2009). *Applies when:* assessing founders, or coaching a founder stuck against an obstacle — the model prescribes trying new things rather than surrendering.

## Default alive / default dead

A binary describing survival trajectory: on current growth and burn, do you reach profitability before the money runs out? ("Default Alive or Default Dead?," 2015). Sub-models: the **fatal pinch** ("default dead + slow growth + not enough time to fix it") and the **hiring trap** (mistaking headcount as the cause rather than effect of success; "Hiring too fast is by far the biggest killer of startups that raise money"). *Applies when:* a startup has raised money and is deciding whether to expand or retrench.

## The maker's schedule vs. the manager's schedule

Two incompatible ways of organizing time. Managers work in one-hour slots; makers (writers, programmers) work in half-day units, so meetings are far costlier to them: "A single meeting can blow a whole afternoon, by breaking it into two pieces each too small to do anything hard in" ("Maker's Schedule, Manager's Schedule," 2009). *Applies when:* a founder-builder is losing deep-work time to meetings, or scheduling a team of makers.

## Cities send a message / ambition is contagious

Great cities transmit a dominant value that shapes what you aim at: Silicon Valley says you should be more powerful/build things, New York says make more money, Cambridge says be smarter ("Cities and Ambition," 2008). Being around the right ambitious peers matters more than founders expect. *Applies when:* a founder is deciding where to base themselves, or why an environment is discouraging their kind of ambition.

## The four-step recipe for great work

The general model beneath startup ideas: "choose a field, learn enough to get to the frontier, notice gaps, explore promising ones," with curiosity as the engine — "Curiosity is the key to all four steps in doing great work" ("How to Do Great Work," 2023). Related: prestige is "at best a trailing indicator," earnestness is a source of power, and "The way to figure out what to work on is by working." *Applies when:* the question is broader than a single startup — what to devote a career or a decade to.
