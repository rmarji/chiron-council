# Sources — the Columbo method (corpus)

Complete provenance for this seat. The subject is a **fictional character**, Lt. Columbo, so the "corpus" is the television series that depicts him: *Columbo*, created by Richard Levinson and William Link, broadcast on NBC (September 15, 1971 – May 13, 1978) and revived on ABC (February 6, 1989 – January 30, 2003), from a 1968 TV-movie pilot. The seat distills the depicted investigation/interrogation **method** and epistemic stance, cited by episode, plus the real interrogation and rhetorical techniques the show dramatizes and later trainers adopted. Primary corpus (the series and specific episodes) first; format and real-method references second.

## Primary corpus — the series

- **Columbo**, created by Richard Levinson and William Link. Universal Television, for NBC (1971-1978, as part of the *NBC Mystery Movie* wheel) and ABC (1989-2003). Peter Falk played the title character across the run. The show is the defining example of the **inverted mystery** ("howcatchem"): the audience sees the murder and the murderer up front, and the drama is Columbo proving it and getting the killer to reveal themselves. The character — rumpled beige raincoat, cigar, battered Peugeot 403, a basset hound named "Dog," and a constantly-cited but never-seen wife — deliberately projects harmlessness. His first name is never canonically revealed.

## Primary corpus — origins and pilots

- **"Enough Rope,"** *The Chevy Mystery Show* (NBC anthology), 1960 — the character's first television appearance (played by Bert Freed), adapted by Levinson and Link from their short story "May I Come In?" (published as "Dear Corpus Delicti," *Alfred Hitchcock's Mystery Magazine*, March 1960).
- **Prescription: Murder** (stage play), premiered San Francisco, January 2, 1962, with Thomas Mitchell as Columbo — the character's first extended treatment.
- **"Prescription: Murder,"** NBC TV-movie pilot, first aired **February 20, 1968**. Gene Barry as Dr. Ray Flemming, a psychiatrist who murders his wife with his mistress's help and a manufactured alibi; Peter Falk's first appearance as Columbo. Establishes the method: the suspect underestimates the "sly little elf," and Columbo's relentless patience and a final trap break the confident killer. Source of the method-naming line, "they underestimate you, and that's where you trip them up."
- **"Ransom for a Dead Man,"** second TV-movie pilot, 1971 (Lee Grant), which sold the series.

## Primary corpus — cited episodes

- **"Murder by the Book,"** Season 1, Episode 1. Directed by **Steven Spielberg**, written by **Steven Bochco**. First aired **September 15, 1971**. Mystery-writer Ken Franklin (Jack Cassidy) murders his writing partner and stages an alibi; Columbo's suspicion catches on small oddities — driving instead of flying back, a $15,000 withdrawal returned the next day — and a manuscript page.
- **"Suitable for Framing,"** Season 1, Episode 4 (1971). Art critic Dale Kingston (Ross Martin) kills his uncle for the art collection and frames his aunt. Caught by the **glove/fingerprint reversal**: Columbo, having secretly worn gloves throughout, lets the arrogant Kingston over-explain and accuse him of planting prints — which is impossible. The corpus's clearest demonstration of feeding a suspect's arrogance until it convicts them.
- **"Étude in Black,"** Season 2, Episode 1 (1972). Conductor (John Cassavetes) stages his mistress's murder as suicide. The case turns on an accumulation of **"little things"** that don't fit — a typed suicide note, a gassed pet, wrong car mileage — the corpus's model of the nagging inconsistency.
- **"Negative Reaction,"** Season 4, Episode 2 (1974). Photographer Paul Galesko (Dick Van Dyke) fakes a kidnapping to cover a murder. Caught on a single detail — a staged photo whose clock reads the wrong time, plus the illogic of an ex-con getting a driver's license the day of the supposed kidnapping. Columbo's sleepless fixation on the small wrong note is on full display.
- **"Try and Catch Me,"** Season 7, Episode 1 (1977). Mystery writer Abigail Mitchell (Ruth Gordon) locks her niece's husband (her nephew-in-law) in a wall safe. Caught partly by the **victim's dying clue** (a scraped manuscript title page) and partly by the killer's composure finally cracking under Columbo's courteous persistence.

## Format and real techniques the corpus dramatizes

- **The inverted mystery ("howcatchem")** — the structural frame Levinson and Link popularized: crime and criminal shown up front, so the work is proof and disclosure, not discovery. Distinguished from the whodunit.
- **Literary lineage** — the writers cited **Porfiry Petrovich** (Dostoevsky, *Crime and Punishment*, 1866), who unnerves Raskolnikov through patient, indirect pressure, and **Father Brown** (G. K. Chesterton), the unassuming cleric-detective, as inspirations; Inspector Fichet from *Les Diaboliques* (1955) is also cited.
- **The "Columbo technique / Columbo close"** — a real adaptation of the character's disarming, "just one more thing," lower-your-status elicitation into interrogation, sales, and negotiation training (numerous negotiation and sales-training sources). The method's transfer beyond crime is the reason this seat exists.
