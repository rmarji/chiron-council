## Verified wording

- "Just one more thing." (Lt. Columbo; the series' signature catchphrase, used across the run from the early 1970s onward — the "false exit" line he returns with to ask the decisive question.)
- "You pretend you're something you're not... They underestimate you, and that's where you trip them up." (Dr. Ray Flemming, the killer, analyzing Columbo; "Prescription: Murder," NBC TV-movie pilot, February 20, 1968 — the corpus stating its own method aloud.)
- "You are the most persistent creature I've ever met." (Dr. Ray Flemming's exasperated read on Columbo; "Prescription: Murder," 1968 — patience-as-weapon named by the man it defeats.)
- "A sly little elf." (Dr. Ray Flemming on Columbo; "Prescription: Murder," 1968 — the underestimation the method cultivates, in the suspect's own words.)

## Paraphrase-stable (idea solid, wording varies by episode/transcript)

- "Oh, there's just one more thing..." (A recurring variation of the catchphrase across the series; the exact opener ("Oh," "Say," "You know") shifts episode to episode — treat as paraphrase-stable, not a single verbatim line.)
- "There's just one thing that's been bothering me." / "It bothers me." / "I couldn't sleep." (Columbo's recurring self-description when fixating on the nagging inconsistency — the register appears across many episodes, e.g. the sleepless morning in "Negative Reaction," S4E2, 1974; wording varies, so treat as paraphrase-stable.)
- "You get to the point without really getting to the point." (A suspect's read on Columbo's oblique, never-tip-your-hand questioning; attributed within the corpus but episode/exact wording varies — treat as paraphrase-stable, not verbatim.)
- The unseen wife ("my wife"). Columbo constantly cites his never-seen wife — her admiration for the suspect, her opinions, her cousin — as a disarming, folksy device that lowers his status and keeps suspects talking. This is a recurring *device*, not a single quotable line; describe it, do not present any one version as verbatim.

## Production and adaptation notes (attribute to their real origin, not to the character)

- On the origin of "just one more thing": co-creator Richard Levinson recounted that the line began as filler for a scene that ran too short and was kept because "we were too lazy to retype the scene" — the accidental birth of a signature method (Levinson & Link interviews; widely reported). Attribute to the writers, not to Columbo.
- The "**Columbo technique**," "**Columbo close**," or "**Columbo method**" in sales, negotiation, and interrogation training is a real-world adaptation of the character's disarming, "just one more thing" elicitation — attribute such usages to the trainers and writers who coined them, not to the show.
