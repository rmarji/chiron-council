# Frameworks — the Columbo method (corpus)

The named procedures the *Columbo* corpus runs, each with when-to-use, concrete steps, and an episode citation. Third-person throughout; the character is fictional. These generalize the "howcatchem" into a repeatable investigation/interrogation discipline usable well beyond crime.

## The Columbo method (the full procedure)

*When to use:* You suspect the truth and the job is to confirm it and get a resistant person to disclose it — an interrogation, a negotiation, an audit, a due-diligence or reference call, a post-mortem where the convenient account is being accepted too fast.

*Steps:*
1. **Appear harmless.** Lower your status deliberately. Ask for help, admit confusion, defer to the counterpart's expertise, let them feel superior. A relaxed, condescending adversary talks; a threatened one guards ("Prescription: Murder," 1968: "they underestimate you, and that's where you trip them up").
2. **Let them talk.** Keep the counterpart explaining. The more of the story they commit to, the more surface it has to catch on, and the harder it becomes to walk back. Ask open, wandering questions; resist filling silences yourself.
3. **Find the one detail that doesn't fit.** Separate what was *claimed* from what is *corroborated*. Catalogue discrepancies privately and mark the single anomaly the tidy story cannot absorb ("Étude in Black," S2E1, 1972 — the typed note, the gassed pet, the mileage).
4. **Worry it, don't reveal it.** Return to that detail politely and repeatedly. Never tell the counterpart what you know or how much; keep your real read private so they cannot repair the seam ("the way you get to the point without really getting to the point").
5. **Land the parting question.** Once they believe it's over and the guard is down, come back — "just one more thing" — and ask the one question the true story survives and the false one cannot ("Negative Reaction," S4E2, 1974, where the decisive contradiction is delivered at the end).

*Why it holds:* each step sets up the next — harmlessness buys talk, talk produces the seam, concealment protects the seam, and the false exit discharges it when defenses are lowest.

## The nagging-inconsistency audit

*When to use:* When an account is being accepted because it is smooth, complete, and confidently delivered.

*Steps:*
1. Ask the diagnostic question out loud: "what one thing here still doesn't sit right?"
2. Name the anomaly explicitly rather than letting it stay a vague unease.
3. Refuse the convenient explanation — the reason that would make the discomfort disappear. Treat the fact that a tidy explanation exists as suspicious in itself.
4. Pull on the thread: gather the corroboration the story assumes but hasn't shown, and watch whether it holds.

*Source:* the corpus wins case after case on the small wrong note nobody else worried — the ransom photo whose clock reads the wrong time ("Negative Reaction," 1974); the details that keep Columbo from sleeping. The discipline is temperamental as much as analytic: being unable to let the one thing go.

## The false exit ("just one more thing")

*When to use:* When a counterpart is guarded and you have a single question that matters most.

*Steps:*
1. Signal closure convincingly — thank them, gather your things, move toward the door. Let the tension release.
2. Wait for the guard to drop; the relief of a conversation ending is when defenses are lowest.
3. Return almost as an afterthought and ask the one decisive question, aimed at the exposed inconsistency.
4. Read the reaction as data — the pause, the over-explanation, the recovery — not just the words.

*Source:* the series' signature move and catchphrase; the sales world adapts it as the "Columbo close" (the small clarifying ask after the meeting feels over). Timing does the work that force cannot.

## Feed-the-ego elicitation

*When to use:* With a confident, high-status counterpart who needs to be seen as the smartest in the room.

*Steps:*
1. Position yourself below them — ask for their guidance on something in their domain.
2. Introduce a mild discrepancy without accusation: "I'm probably getting this wrong, but this one thing confuses me..."
3. Let their compulsion to correct you and demonstrate superiority pull the elaboration out. The need to win the exchange overrides the instinct to guard.
4. Note what the correction reveals; the incriminating detail usually arrives inside the explanation, not the answer.

*Source:* "Suitable for Framing" (S1E4, 1971), where the arrogant art critic over-explains straight into the fingerprint trap Columbo has set. Arrogance is the lever; the display leaks the truth.

## When to reach for which

- Start with **the Columbo method** whenever you already suspect the answer and must get a person to confirm it — it is the master procedure and the others are its components.
- Reach for **the nagging-inconsistency audit** the moment an account is being accepted for its smoothness; it is the fastest way to locate the seam.
- Deploy **the false exit** when you have exactly one question that matters and a guarded counterpart; misuse dilutes it, so save it.
- Use **feed-the-ego elicitation** specifically against high-status, arrogant counterparts; it is wasted on the humble and backfires as flattery if the discrepancy is clumsy.
