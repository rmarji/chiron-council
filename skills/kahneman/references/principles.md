# Principles — Daniel Kahneman

The core beliefs of the corpus, each with rationale and source. Primary sources: Thinking, Fast and Slow (2011); Noise (2021); Prospect Theory (Econometrica, 1979); "Judgment under Uncertainty" (Science, 1974); the Nobel lecture (2002).

## 1. The mind runs two systems, and System 1 goes first.

The corpus's organizing metaphor: **System 1** operates automatically and quickly, with little or no effort and no sense of voluntary control; **System 2** allocates attention to effortful mental activities, and is the one we identify with — the conscious, reasoning self. The trouble is the division of labor. System 1 continuously generates impressions, intuitions, and intentions; System 2 is meant to monitor and correct them but is "lazy" and typically endorses System 1's proposals with little modification. So most judgments are System 1 intuitions that System 2 waved through (Thinking, Fast and Slow, 2011, chs. 1-3; Nobel lecture, 2002, which frames System 1 as having the operating characteristics of *perception*). The two systems are a useful fiction, not brain regions — the corpus is explicit that they are characters in a story that makes the psychology memorable.

## 2. What You See Is All There Is (WYSIATI).

System 1 builds the most coherent story it can from *whatever information is activated*, and crucially does not represent the information it lacks. Confidence in a judgment is determined by the coherence of the story System 1 has constructed, not by the quantity or quality of the evidence — "you build the best possible story from the information available to you, and if it is a good story, you believe it" (Thinking, Fast and Slow, 2011, ch. 7, "A Machine for Jumping to Conclusions"). WYSIATI explains overconfidence (we don't feel the weight of what we don't know), framing effects (different information is activated by different frames), and base-rate neglect (the specific story crowds out the statistics). It is arguably the master principle from which many named biases descend.

## 3. Judgment substitutes an easier question for a harder one.

When faced with a difficult target question ("What is the probability?", "How much should I pay?", "Is this a good CEO?"), System 1 answers a related but easier **heuristic question** ("How similar is this to a prototype?", "How available is an example?", "Do I like this person?") and maps the answer back onto the original — usually without the person noticing the swap (Thinking, Fast and Slow, 2011, ch. 9, "Answering an Easier Question"; the mechanism formalizes the 1974 heuristics). Substitution is the corpus's unifying account of *why* the heuristics produce biases: representativeness substitutes similarity for probability; availability substitutes ease-of-recall for frequency; the affect heuristic substitutes liking for judgment. Naming the question actually being answered is the first debiasing move.

## 4. Outcomes are evaluated as changes from a reference point, and losses loom larger than gains.

Against expected-utility theory, which evaluates final states of wealth, **prospect theory** holds that people evaluate *gains and losses relative to a reference point*; the value function is concave for gains (risk-averse), convex for losses (risk-seeking to avoid a sure loss), and steeper for losses than gains — **loss aversion**, with a coefficient generally estimated between 1.5 and 2.5 (Prospect Theory, Econometrica 47(2), 1979; Nobel lecture, 2002). Because the reference point is set by how a problem is described, the *same* decision can reverse under a gain frame versus a loss frame (see principle 5). Loss aversion also generates the endowment effect and status-quo bias (Kahneman, Knetsch & Thaler, JEP, 1991).

## 5. Framing determines choice, so preferences are constructed, not revealed.

Because choice is reference-dependent, logically equivalent descriptions of the same options produce different choices. The canonical demonstration is the "Asian disease" problem: a program described in terms of lives *saved* is chosen when its equivalent described in terms of lives *lost* is rejected (Tversky & Kahneman, "The Framing of Decisions," Science 211, 1981; Thinking, Fast and Slow, 2011, ch. 34, "Frames and Reality"). The implication is deep: there is often no frame-independent "true preference" to recover — preferences are partly manufactured by the description, which is why the corpus insists on restating a decision in more than one frame before trusting it.

## 6. Confidence is a feeling, not a measure of accuracy.

Subjective confidence reflects the *coherence* of the story System 1 has assembled and the *ease* with which it came to mind — not the amount or reliability of the underlying evidence. In low-validity environments, the correlation between an expert's confidence and their accuracy can be near zero; the "illusion of validity" persists even after people are shown their predictions are worthless (Thinking, Fast and Slow, 2011, ch. 20, "The Illusion of Validity"; ch. 24, "The Engine of Capitalism"). The corollary is the corpus's most quoted practical warning: do not treat a confident forecaster as an accurate one.

## 7. Skilled intuition is real but conditional.

The corpus is not anti-intuition wholesale. Genuine expert intuition develops, but only under two conditions: an environment that is **sufficiently regular to be predictable**, and an **opportunity to learn these regularities through prolonged practice with rapid, unambiguous feedback** (chess, firefighting, anesthesiology qualify; stock picking and long-range political forecasting do not). Where those conditions fail, "intuition" is just overconfident pattern-completion and should be distrusted (Thinking, Fast and Slow, 2011, ch. 22, "Expert Intuition: When Can We Trust It?", drawing on the Kahneman-Klein "adversarial collaboration"). This is the principle that keeps the corpus from collapsing into blanket skepticism.

## 8. Prediction should be regressive; extreme forecasts are almost always overconfident.

Because most outcomes are part signal, part luck, an extreme observation will tend to be followed by a less extreme one — **regression to the mean** — and predictions should be regressed toward the base-rate mean in proportion to how weak the evidence is. Most causal stories about "what caused the change" (the sophomore slump, the effect of praise or punishment) are regression mistaken for causation (Thinking, Fast and Slow, 2011, ch. 17, "Regression to the Mean"; ch. 18, "Taming Intuitive Predictions"). Non-regressive, inside-view prediction is the deep root of the planning fallacy and the illusion of validity.

## 9. The remembering self and the experiencing self are different, and the remembering self decides.

Wellbeing has two selves that do not agree. The **experiencing self** lives moment to moment; the **remembering self** keeps score and makes decisions, and it evaluates episodes by the **peak-end rule** and **duration neglect** — an experience is remembered mostly by its most intense moment and its ending, almost regardless of how long it lasted (the cold-pressor and colonoscopy studies) (Thinking, Fast and Slow, 2011, chs. 35-36, "Two Selves"/"Life as a Story"). The **focusing illusion** — "nothing in life is as important as you think it is while you are thinking about it" — falls out of the same account (ch. 38). The consequence: we systematically choose for the memory rather than for the experience.

## 10. Bias is only half the error; noise is the other half, and it is larger than you think.

The late-career principle. Where bias is the *systematic* component of error (shots landing consistently off-target), **noise** is the *unwanted variability* — different judges, or the same judge on different occasions, reaching different verdicts on identical cases (the scatter of the shots). Noise is separate from bias, additive to total error, ubiquitous wherever there is judgment, and — critically — *invisible* unless deliberately measured, because we only ever see one judgment per case (Noise, 2021, Parts I-II; the shooting-range and bathroom-scale analogies). Organizations obsess over bias and never audit noise, leaving the larger and more tractable error untouched.

## 11. The fix for flawed judgment is process, not insight.

The corpus's normative conclusion, and the load-bearing point of its disagreement with optimists about self-debiasing. Because System 1's errors are not disarmed by awareness — "System 1 is not readily educable" and the corpus's own intuitions never improved despite a lifetime of study (Thinking, Fast and Slow, 2011, Introduction and Conclusions) — the leverage lies in **decision hygiene**: sequencing information, breaking judgments into independent components (the **mediating assessments protocol**), collecting judgments independently before discussion, aggregating, using shared relative scales, and preferring simple valid formulas to expert intuition where they exist (Noise, 2021, Parts IV-V; on formulas vs. clinical judgment, Thinking, Fast and Slow, 2011, ch. 21). Better judgment is engineered into the process and the organization, not exhorted into the individual.
