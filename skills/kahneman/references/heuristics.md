# Heuristics — Daniel Kahneman

The full set of operational decision rules the corpus supports, in IF/THEN form, each attributed. These are prescriptions the corpus's findings imply — how to act given that judgment is systematically distorted. Sources: Thinking, Fast and Slow (2011); Noise (2021); Prospect Theory (1979); "Judgment under Uncertainty" (Science, 1974); "The Framing of Decisions" (Science, 1981).

## Forecasting and estimation

- **IF you are forecasting a timeline, cost, or success rate, THEN take the outside view first** — build the estimate from the base-rate distribution of a reference class of similar past cases before you consider the specifics of this one (TFaS, 2011, ch. 23, "The Outside View").
- **IF your estimate is built from the specifics of your own plan, THEN treat it as a best case and expect the real outcome to be worse** — the inside view drives the planning fallacy (TFaS, 2011, ch. 23).
- **IF you are predicting an outcome from weak evidence, THEN regress the prediction toward the baseline** — move from the base rate toward your intuition only by the fraction equal to the evidence's correlation with the outcome (TFaS, 2011, ch. 18, "Taming Intuitive Predictions").
- **IF an extreme result follows an extreme one, THEN expect regression to the mean before inventing a cause** — most "the intervention worked / the star faltered" stories are regression, not causation (TFaS, 2011, ch. 17).
- **IF a plan feels complete and coherent, THEN run a premortem** — imagine it has failed a year out and write the history of the failure, independently, before committing (TFaS, 2011, ch. 24).

## Intuition, confidence, and expertise

- **IF a judgment feels easy and confident, THEN suspect substitution** — ask which harder question System 1 quietly replaced with an easier one, and answer the original question (TFaS, 2011, ch. 9; Science, 1974).
- **IF you are tempted to trust an intuition, THEN first ask whether the environment is high- or low-validity** — trust the gut only in a regular environment learned through prolonged practice with rapid feedback; otherwise distrust it (TFaS, 2011, ch. 22, "Expert Intuition: When Can We Trust It?").
- **IF someone expresses high confidence in a prediction, THEN do not treat confidence as evidence of accuracy** — confidence reports the coherence of a story, not its truth (TFaS, 2011, ch. 20, "The Illusion of Validity").
- **IF a valid formula or simple rule exists for the prediction, THEN prefer it to expert intuition in the low-validity case** — clinical judgment loses to the checklist where the environment is noisy and feedback poor (TFaS, 2011, ch. 21, "Intuitions vs. Formulas").
- **IF you are evaluating a past decision after knowing its outcome, THEN correct for hindsight bias** — judge the decision by what was knowable at the time, not by how it turned out (TFaS, 2011, ch. 19).

## Framing, loss, and value

- **IF the same choice reverses when framed as a gain versus a loss, THEN neither frame is the truth** — restate the decision in reference-free, final-state terms before deciding (Prospect Theory, 1979; Science, 1981; TFaS, ch. 34).
- **IF you find yourself holding a losing position or clinging to a sunk cost, THEN check whether loss aversion is overriding a final-state analysis** — losses loom ~1.5-2.5× larger than equivalent gains (Prospect Theory, 1979; TFaS, chs. 27-28, 32).
- **IF you are valuing something you already own, THEN correct for the endowment effect** — ownership inflates valuation; ask what you'd pay for it if you didn't have it (Kahneman, Knetsch & Thaler, JEP, 1991; TFaS, ch. 27).
- **IF a low-probability outcome is vivid and emotionally charged, THEN check whether you are overweighting it** — decision weights inflate rare, salient events (TFaS, 2011, chs. 29-30).

## Base rates, availability, and anchors

- **IF you are about to trust a vivid, easily recalled example, THEN check the base rate instead** — availability makes the memorable feel frequent (TFaS, 2011, chs. 12-13; Science, 1974).
- **IF a case strongly resembles a prototype, THEN anchor on the base rate before the resemblance** — representativeness ignores prior probability and drives the conjunction fallacy (TFaS, 2011, chs. 14-16; Science, 1974).
- **IF a number has been put on the table before you estimate, THEN treat it as a contaminating anchor** — adjustment from anchors is insufficient, even when the anchor is arbitrary (TFaS, 2011, ch. 11; Science, 1974).

## Noise and group judgment (Noise, 2021)

- **IF several people make the same kind of judgment, THEN run a noise audit before you blame bias** — measure the disagreement on identical cases; it is usually large and invisible until measured (Noise, 2021, ch. 6; Part II).
- **IF a group must reach a judgment, THEN collect independent estimates BEFORE any discussion** — discussion breeds conformity and confidence, not accuracy; aggregate first, deliberate second (Noise, 2021, ch. 21, "Decision Hygiene").
- **IF a big multi-attribute decision is looming, THEN decompose it into independent mediating assessments and delay the holistic judgment** — score each attribute separately before forming the overall verdict (Noise, 2021, ch. 25, "A Debiased Structured Approach").
- **IF you can aggregate independent judgments, THEN do so** — averaging independent estimates reduces noise, but only if the estimates were formed without mutual influence (Noise, 2021, Part V).
- **IF you must choose between an absolute rating and a relative ranking, THEN prefer the relative judgment** — comparisons on a shared, anchored scale are less noisy than absolute ratings (Noise, 2021, Part V).

## The meta-rule

- **IF you want to improve a judgment, THEN change the process, not just the person** — awareness of a bias does not disarm it; the durable fix is procedural (decision hygiene, formulas, structured decomposition), because System 1 is not readily educable (TFaS, 2011, Conclusions; Noise, 2021, Part V).
